# Phase 6 · 7.3.1 Business glossary — 100 self-test questions

Q: What is a business glossary in one sentence?
A: A business glossary is a controlled, governed vocabulary that records the agreed meaning of business concepts such as "NAV" or "AUM" together with their owners, status, and links to the data that carries them. It exists so that terms mean the same thing across teams instead of drifting per report or per database. In a catalog like OpenMetadata it is a first-class entity you build, approve, and connect to physical columns.

Q: Why does the lesson say "NAV" and "AUM" mean different things until the glossary pins them?
A: The same English word maps to several distinct calculations depending on who computes it: NAV might mean per-share NAV, total fund NAV, or NAV before/after the swing, and AUM might or might not include committed-but-undrawn capital. Until one authoritative definition is written down, owned, and linked, two reports labelled "AUM" can legitimately disagree and nobody can adjudicate which is right. The glossary removes that ambiguity by making one definition canonical and attaching it to the data.

Q: What does "glossary-to-asset linkage makes the catalog business-readable" mean?
A: It means a compliance officer or front-office user can search for a business term like "dealing cut-off" and immediately see every table and column that physically carries it, without reading a single technical schema. The link turns abstract definitions into navigable connections between meaning and storage. That bridge is what lets non-engineers trust and use the catalog for audits and reporting.

Q: In OpenMetadata, what is the difference between a glossary and a glossary term?
A: A glossary is the top-level controlled vocabulary or container (for example a "Fund Operations" glossary), while a glossary term is an individual concept inside it such as "NAV" or "Swing Price". Terms live in a hierarchy with parent-child relationships under the glossary. You create the glossary first, then populate it with terms that each get their own status, owner, and asset links.

Q: List the OpenMetadata glossary term status values.
A: The status of a glossary term must be one of `Draft`, `In Review`, `Approved`, `Deprecated`, or `Rejected`. `Draft` is being defined, `In Review` is undergoing the reviewer workflow, `Approved` is reviewed and accepted, `Deprecated` is retired but kept for history, and `Rejected` did not pass review. Knowing the exact set matters because the catalog's filters and workflow logic key off these literal values.

Q: What is the normal term lifecycle from creation to use?
A: A term is created in `Draft`, moves to `In Review` when the approval workflow starts, and lands on `Approved` once reviewers accept it; if rejected it becomes `Rejected`, and when it falls out of use it is set `Deprecated`. Only an approved term should be treated as authoritative for reporting and compliance. This draft-to-approved progression is exactly the lifecycle the Learn bullet asks you to master.

Q: Who is the "owner" of a glossary term and what are they responsible for?
A: The owner is the person or team accountable for the term's definition being correct and current; they answer "what does this mean and why" and they sponsor changes. Ownership is what turns a definition from anonymous wiki text into something with a name attached when an auditor asks who decides. In OpenMetadata the owner is set per term and is distinct from the reviewers who approve changes.

Q: What is a reviewer (steward) in the glossary approval workflow?
A: A reviewer is a user assigned to a term who must review and accept proposed changes before the term advances out of `In Review` to `Approved`. Reviewers act as the data stewards who enforce governance, ensuring no definition becomes authoritative without sign-off. A term can have a set of reviewers, and the workflow only completes when they accept.

Q: How does the glossary approval workflow get started in OpenMetadata?
A: The approval workflow starts when a new term is created via the UI, or when terms are created via Bulk Import, by assigning reviewers and putting the term in `Draft` status. Setting reviewers plus draft status triggers the review process so the term cannot silently become approved. If no reviewers are assigned, there is nothing to approve and the workflow does not engage.

Q: Why distinguish the owner from the reviewer rather than collapsing them into one role?
A: Separating them creates a basic control: the person who proposes or owns a definition is not the only person who blesses it, which prevents a single individual from unilaterally redefining a regulated term like NAV. It mirrors maker-checker controls auditors expect in fund administration. Practically, the owner drives content and the reviewers (stewards) gate its approval.

Q: What is "term-to-asset linkage"?
A: Term-to-asset linkage is connecting a glossary term to every physical column or table that carries that concept, so the meaning and the data are explicitly joined in the catalog. For example, linking the term "NAV" to `gold.fund_nav.nav_per_share` and to any other column holding a NAV value. This is the linkage the Done-when criterion tests when you click "NAV" and see every asset that carries it.

Q: In OpenMetadata, how is a glossary term applied to a column in practice?
A: You add the glossary term to the asset much like a tag: from the table or column page you attach the term, or from the term you add the asset, and OpenMetadata records the association both ways. The term then appears on the column and the column appears in the term's list of linked assets. This bidirectional link is what makes search-from-meaning and search-from-data both work.

Q: A column holds NAV but searching the term "NAV" does not list it — what is the first thing to check?
A: Check whether the term has actually been linked to that column, because an unlinked column will never show up under the term no matter how it is named. Open the column page and confirm the glossary term is attached, not merely that the column is called `nav`. Naming similarity does not create a link; only an explicit association does.

Q: Why is linking the term to "every column that carries it" stricter than linking it once?
A: NAV may be stored redundantly across bronze, silver, and gold or across several fund tables, and a partial linkage gives a false sense of completeness — an auditor following the term would miss assets. Linking every carrying column guarantees the impact and lineage view for that concept is complete. That completeness is the difference between a glossary that looks governed and one that actually is.

Q: What does the Done-when "click NAV and see its definition, owner, and every asset that carries it" verify?
A: It verifies three things at once: the term exists with an approved definition, an accountable owner is assigned, and the term-to-asset links are in place and complete. If any of the three is missing the click reveals the gap immediately. It is a single end-to-end smoke test of the whole lesson's deliverable.

Q: Define a data dictionary and how it differs from a business glossary.
A: A data dictionary documents the technical structure of data — table and column names, data types, nullability, and constraints — describing how data is stored, not what it means to the business. A business glossary instead captures business meaning, ownership, and approval status independent of any physical schema. The glossary answers "what is NAV", the dictionary answers "the column `nav_per_share` is a `numeric(18,6)` not-null".

Q: What is a semantic layer and how does it differ from a glossary?
A: A semantic layer is an executable abstraction (in tools like a metrics/BI layer) that turns business concepts into queryable, computed definitions — it actually returns the AUM number when you query it. A glossary is descriptive: it states what AUM means but does not compute it. The glossary is the agreed prose definition; the semantic layer is the runnable formula that should conform to it.

Q: Glossary, data dictionary, semantic layer — summarise the one-line distinction between all three.
A: The glossary defines what a concept means in business terms, the data dictionary describes how data is physically structured and typed, and the semantic layer makes those concepts computable as live metrics. Meaning, structure, and computation respectively. Mature governance keeps all three consistent so the prose definition, the stored column, and the metric calculation agree.

Q: What is the "coming convergence" of glossary, dictionary, and semantic layer that DAMA references?
A: The convergence is the trend toward a single governed metadata fabric where a business term's definition, its physical column mappings, and its executable metric are linked rather than living in three disconnected tools. The aim is that approving a definition in the glossary flows through to the dictionary mapping and the semantic-layer formula automatically. Catalogs adding metrics and semantic features alongside glossaries are an early form of this convergence.

Q: Why is convergence valuable in a regulated fund context specifically?
A: Because regulators want to see that the AUM figure in a filing traces back through a stored column to an approved, owned definition — convergence makes that trace a single navigable chain instead of three reconciliations. It collapses the risk that the BI metric quietly diverges from the written definition. For DORA/CSSF-style scrutiny, a unified meaning-to-number path is far easier to evidence.

Q: What does "seeding from regulation" mean for a glossary?
A: It means taking authoritative definitions straight from regulatory texts — for example AIFMD or PRIIPs — and using them as the canonical source for the corresponding glossary terms, rather than inventing in-house wording. The regulation becomes the cited authority behind the definition. This makes the term defensible: when asked who decided what NAV means, you point to the regulatory source.

Q: What is AIFMD and why is it a good source for glossary definitions?
A: AIFMD is the EU Alternative Investment Fund Managers Directive, governing managers of alternative funds, and it defines concepts like "AIF", "leverage", and "net asset value" in a legally binding way. Seeding those definitions from AIFMD anchors your terms to an authority a Luxembourg fund administrator must already comply with. It removes argument because the definition's provenance is the directive itself.

Q: What is PRIIPs and which kind of definitions does it authoritatively provide?
A: PRIIPs is the EU Packaged Retail and Insurance-based Investment Products regulation, which standardises pre-contractual disclosure via the Key Information Document and prescribes how figures like risk indicators, performance scenarios, and costs are defined and computed. Those cost and risk definitions are exactly the kind of regulated terms worth seeding into a glossary. Citing PRIIPs makes a "summary cost indicator" term authoritative rather than a local interpretation.

Q: Why seed at least three definitions from regulation rather than writing them all in-house?
A: Regulatory seeding gives a subset of your terms unimpeachable provenance, which matters most for the figures that appear in filings and investor disclosures. It also reduces drift, because the in-house wording cannot quietly diverge from the legal definition it must satisfy. The Do step requires three such seeds to prove you can connect the glossary to authoritative sources, not just internal convention.

Q: What is the practical first step of the lesson's Do list?
A: Create a 25-term fund glossary — including NAV, swing price, AUM, dealing cut-off, and similar concepts — each with a written definition. This establishes the controlled vocabulary before any linking or approval. Starting with breadth (25 terms) forces you to confront the real ambiguity across fund operations rather than defining one easy term.

Q: After creating the 25 terms, what are the remaining three Do steps?
A: Assign an owner to each term, link every gold-table column that carries one of those terms to its glossary entry, and seed at least three definitions from regulation (AIFMD/PRIIPs). Owners give accountability, links give business-readability, and regulatory seeds give provenance. Together they turn a list of definitions into a governed, navigable glossary.

Q: Define NAV as a glossary term would.
A: NAV (Net Asset Value) is the value of a fund's assets minus its liabilities, usually expressed per share or per unit as of a valuation point. As a glossary term it must state the grain (per-share vs total), the valuation timing, and whether it is pre- or post-swing, because those choices change the number. Pinning that detail is precisely why NAV belongs in a governed glossary.

Q: Define swing price as a glossary term.
A: Swing pricing adjusts a fund's NAV up or down by a swing factor when net subscriptions or redemptions exceed a threshold, so transacting investors bear the dealing costs rather than the standing investors. The glossary term should record both the mechanism and the threshold/factor concepts it depends on. Capturing it prevents "NAV" and "swung NAV" from being conflated in reports.

Q: Define dealing cut-off as a glossary term.
A: The dealing cut-off is the deadline by which a subscription or redemption order must be received to be executed at a given dealing day's price; orders after it roll to the next dealing point. The glossary entry must fix the time, time zone, and which dealing calendar applies. Ambiguity here directly causes orders to be priced on the wrong NAV.

Q: Why is "AUM" a classic example of a term that needs an owned definition?
A: Because AUM (Assets Under Management) can include or exclude committed-but-undrawn capital, leverage, or affiliated assets, and different teams legitimately use different boundaries. Without an owned, approved definition, marketing AUM and regulatory AUM diverge and both call themselves "AUM". Pinning one canonical definition with an owner is what lets the firm state a single defensible figure.

Q: How does ISIN relate to a business glossary even though it is an identifier, not a metric?
A: "ISIN" is still a business term worth a glossary entry: it should define that an ISIN is the 12-character ISO 6166 security identifier and clarify how it is used as a key across fund and security data. The term can then be linked to every column holding an ISIN so users find them all. It shows the glossary covers identifiers and reference data, not only computed measures.

Q: How would you use the glossary to link the term "LEI" across the estate?
A: Create an "LEI" term defining it as the 20-character ISO 17442 Legal Entity Identifier issued under GLEIF, assign an owner from the entity-data team, then link it to every column that stores an LEI in entity, counterparty, and fund tables. Searching "LEI" then returns every asset carrying it. This is identical in mechanics to linking NAV, just for a reference-data attribute.

Q: A fund report shows two different AUM figures — how does the glossary help diagnose it?
A: Open the AUM term, read its approved definition and follow its asset links to see which columns feed each report; the divergence usually traces to one report using a column linked to the approved definition and the other using an unlinked column with a different boundary. The glossary turns a "which AUM is right" argument into a factual lineage check. The fix is to relink or correct the offending source to the approved definition.

Q: Why should a glossary term be approved before it is cited in a regulatory filing?
A: Because an `Approved` status means reviewers (stewards) have signed off, giving the definition governance standing; a `Draft` term is provisional and could change or be rejected. Citing only approved terms ensures the firm relies on definitions that passed maker-checker control. Auditors can then trust the status field as evidence of due process.

Q: What happens governance-wise when a regulated definition such as a PRIIPs cost figure changes?
A: The owner proposes the updated definition, the term re-enters the review workflow, and reviewers must approve it before it is authoritative again; the old version should be `Deprecated` rather than deleted to preserve history. This keeps an auditable trail of how the meaning evolved. Linked assets remain attached so the impact of the change is visible immediately.

Q: Why prefer `Deprecated` over deleting an obsolete term?
A: Deprecation retains the term and its history so past reports that referenced it remain explicable, whereas deletion erases the audit trail and breaks lineage for historical filings. Regulators may ask what a figure meant three years ago, and a deprecated term still answers that. Soft-retiring also lets you redirect users to the replacement term.

Q: What is the risk of naming a column `nav` and assuming that is enough governance?
A: A name is not a definition: `nav` could be per-share or total, pre- or post-swing, and nothing about the column name resolves that. Without an explicit link to an approved glossary term, the meaning is implicit and unverifiable. Governance requires the explicit term link, which carries the definition and owner, not just a suggestive column name.

Q: How does term-to-asset linkage improve impact analysis when a definition changes?
A: Because every column carrying the concept is linked to the term, opening the term shows the full set of assets affected by a definition change, so you can assess and communicate impact precisely. Without the links you would have to guess which tables hold NAV. The linkage converts "we think it touches these tables" into a definitive list.

Q: Scenario — the Done-when says all 25 terms must have owners; one term has none. What is the consequence?
A: An ownerless term has no accountable party, so when its definition is questioned during an audit there is nobody to defend or correct it, and it cannot properly enter a review workflow. It fails the lesson's completion criterion. The fix is to assign an owner before considering the glossary done.

Q: What does "at least one linked asset where applicable" allow for?
A: It acknowledges that some terms are conceptual or process-level (for example "dealing cut-off" as a policy) and may not map to a single stored column, so they are exempt from the linkage requirement. Terms that do correspond to stored values, like NAV or AUM, must be linked. The phrasing prevents forcing artificial links on non-data concepts.

Q: How does a glossary support compliance and front office "alike", as the Why states?
A: Compliance uses it to prove every reported concept has an owned, approved, sourced definition and to trace figures to their data, while the front office uses the same linked vocabulary to understand reports without reading schemas. One governed vocabulary serves both because meaning is centralised. That shared source of truth is the point of building it.

Q: What is a controlled vocabulary and why is a glossary one?
A: A controlled vocabulary is a curated, restricted set of approved terms with agreed definitions, as opposed to free-text that anyone can vary. A glossary is exactly this: terms are governed, owned, and approved rather than ad hoc. Control is what stops "AUM", "Assets u/m", and "total assets" from proliferating as inconsistent synonyms.

Q: How can synonyms be handled so users find a term by alternate names?
A: A glossary term can record synonyms (equivalent relationships), so searching an alternate name like "Net Assets" still surfaces the canonical "NAV" term. This guides users to the single approved definition regardless of the phrasing they know. It reduces the chance someone defines a duplicate term because they could not find the existing one.

Q: What are related terms and how do they help in a fund glossary?
A: Related terms are associative links between concepts that are connected but not synonymous, such as "NAV" related to "Swing Price" and "Dealing Cut-off". They let a user exploring one concept discover the adjacent ones that affect it. In funds this is valuable because the pricing concepts are tightly interdependent.

Q: How does a hierarchical (parent-child) term structure help organise a fund glossary?
A: Hierarchy lets you nest specific terms under broader ones — for example "Per-Share NAV" and "Total NAV" under a parent "NAV" — so the vocabulary reflects how concepts decompose. It keeps related definitions together and clarifies which is the umbrella concept. This structure scales better than a flat list as the glossary grows beyond the initial 25 terms.

Q: Why might you mark two glossary terms as mutually exclusive on an asset?
A: Mutual exclusivity prevents a single column from being tagged with two terms that cannot both be true, for example "Pre-Swing NAV" and "Post-Swing NAV" on the same column. It enforces a constraint at tagging time so the link stays coherent. This guards against contradictory metadata that would mislead anyone trusting the catalog.

Q: How does the glossary relate to data lineage?
A: Lineage shows how data flows between assets, and term links overlay business meaning onto that flow, so you can follow "NAV" from its gold source back through the pipeline. Together they answer both "where does this number come from" and "what does it mean". A glossary without lineage explains meaning but not provenance; combined they give the full picture.

Q: What is the difference between a tag and a glossary term in OpenMetadata?
A: A tag is a lightweight classification label (often for things like PII or tiering) drawn from a classification, whereas a glossary term carries a full business definition, owner, status, and lifecycle. Both can be attached to assets, but the glossary term is the governed, defined concept. Use tags for categorisation and glossary terms for meaning.

Q: Why is it dangerous to let engineers define business terms unilaterally in a data dictionary?
A: Engineers naturally describe storage ("`nav_per_share numeric(18,6)`") rather than business intent, and a definition written only from the technical side may not match the regulated meaning of NAV. The business owner and stewards must define and approve the term so meaning is set by accountable parties. The dictionary should then map to the glossary, not replace it.

Q: A new "Swing Price" term is stuck in `In Review` for weeks — what is the first thing to check?
A: Check who the assigned reviewers are and whether they have been notified or are unavailable, because `In Review` only advances when reviewers accept the change. A term with no active reviewer, or reviewers who left the org, will stall indefinitely. Reassigning a current steward unblocks the workflow.

Q: Why should the glossary definition of NAV state its valuation point explicitly?
A: Because the same fund can have different NAVs at different valuation points (for example a daily dealing NAV versus a month-end accounting NAV), and a definition silent on timing is ambiguous. Stating the valuation point makes clear which number the term refers to. This precision is what prevents two correct-looking NAVs from disagreeing.

Q: How would you decide which gold columns to link to the NAV term?
A: Inspect the gold layer for every column whose values represent a NAV — typically per-share NAV and total NAV columns across fund tables — and link each to the term, excluding columns that merely reference NAV indirectly. The goal is completeness of carrying columns, not catching every mention of the word. Reviewing the data dictionary's types and the lineage helps confirm which columns actually hold the value.

Q: What is the relationship between the glossary and a data catalog?
A: The glossary is a module within the data catalog that holds business definitions, while the catalog as a whole also indexes assets, lineage, quality, and ownership. The glossary makes the catalog business-readable by connecting meaning to those indexed assets. Without it, the catalog is a technical inventory; with it, it answers business questions.

Q: Why does the lesson emphasise linking to "gold" tables specifically?
A: Gold tables are the curated, business-facing layer that reporting and the front office actually consume, so linking terms there makes the most-used data business-readable first. Bronze and silver are intermediate and less directly cited in filings. Concentrating linkage on gold maximises governance value for the effort.

Q: How does a glossary reduce onboarding time for a new analyst?
A: A new analyst can read the approved definition of "dealing cut-off" or "AUM" and see exactly which columns carry them, instead of asking colleagues and risking inconsistent folklore. The glossary encodes institutional knowledge in a discoverable, owned form. This shortens the path from "I see a column called `aum`" to "I understand and trust this number".

Q: What governance gap does an unapproved but widely linked term create?
A: If a term is linked to many assets while still in `Draft`, users may rely on a definition that has not been reviewed and could still be rejected or changed, propagating an unvetted meaning across the estate. The status warns that it is provisional. Best practice is to approve before broad linkage, or at least surface the draft status prominently.

Q: How can Bulk Import help when building a 25-term glossary?
A: Bulk Import lets you create many terms at once from a structured file, setting names, definitions, owners, and reviewers in one operation rather than clicking each term in the UI. For a 25-term seed it is far faster and more consistent. Setting reviewers and `Draft` status in the import also kicks off the approval workflow for each term.

Q: Why is consistency of definitions across reports a regulatory concern, not just a tidiness concern?
A: Regulators and auditors expect that a figure reported to them is computed from a single, defensible definition; inconsistent AUM or NAV across reports suggests weak controls and can itself be a finding. The glossary provides the evidence that one approved definition governs the figure. Consistency here is a compliance posture, not cosmetic neatness.

Q: What is the danger of having a definition in the glossary that the semantic layer does not actually implement?
A: The prose says AUM excludes undrawn commitments but the BI metric includes them, so users trusting the glossary get a number that contradicts the definition. This silent divergence is exactly what the convergence trend aims to eliminate. Until convergence, you must manually keep the glossary definition and the metric formula aligned and reconciled.

Q: How would you prove to an auditor that "NAV" is governed?
A: Open the NAV term and show its approved status, its named owner and reviewers, the regulatory or internal source of its definition, and the complete list of linked gold columns. That single view evidences accountability, sign-off, provenance, and data linkage at once. It is the Done-when click turned into audit evidence.

Q: Why might "Total NAV" and "NAV per share" be separate glossary terms?
A: They are different measures — one is the fund-level value, the other divides it by shares outstanding — so collapsing them invites the exact confusion the glossary exists to prevent. Separate terms let each have its own definition, owner, and linked columns. A parent "NAV" term can relate them while keeping the distinction explicit.

Q: What does it mean to assign a "steward" versus an "owner" in practical fund-data governance?
A: The owner is accountable for the term's correctness and sponsors its definition, while the steward (reviewer) operationally curates and approves changes and keeps linkages healthy. In OpenMetadata the steward role maps to the reviewers in the approval workflow. Both are needed: accountability plus active curation.

Q: How does a glossary help reconcile a discrepancy between the TA system and the accounting NAV?
A: By giving each system's figure a defined, linked term you can see whether they are even measuring the same concept (for example dealing NAV versus accounting NAV) before treating the difference as an error. Many "discrepancies" dissolve once the glossary reveals two different but legitimate definitions. If they should match, the link tells you precisely which columns to reconcile.

Q: Why is free-text documentation in a wiki an inferior substitute for a governed glossary?
A: A wiki page has no enforced status, no required owner, no approval gate, and no machine-readable link to the columns it describes, so it drifts and cannot be audited. A glossary term carries lifecycle, accountability, and asset linkage as structured metadata. The difference is governance you can prove versus documentation you merely hope is current.

Q: What completion check confirms the glossary is actually linked, not just defined?
A: Clicking a term such as "NAV" and seeing the list of every asset that carries it confirms the links exist; an approved definition with an empty asset list would mean it is defined but not connected. The Done-when criteria pair the definition view with the asset list deliberately. Linkage, not just authorship, is the proof of completion.

Q: How does seeding from AIFMD change the wording you put in a term?
A: You quote or closely paraphrase the directive's own definition and cite AIFMD as the source, so the term reads as the regulatory definition rather than a local rewrite that could subtly differ. The citation is part of the term's metadata. This makes the definition defensible and traceable to the legal text.

Q: Why is "who is the authoritative source" a field worth capturing on a term?
A: Recording the authoritative source — a directive like PRIIPs, an internal policy, or a standards body — lets anyone challenge or verify the definition by going to the cited authority. It distinguishes regulator-mandated definitions from internal conventions. For audit, the source field answers "why is this the definition" without further investigation.

Q: A column is linked to "Swing Price" but the data is actually unswung NAV — how is this caught and fixed?
A: It surfaces when someone follows the term to its assets and notices the values do not behave like a swing adjustment, or when a data-quality rule tied to the term fails; the fix is to relink the column to the correct term such as "NAV". Mutually exclusive term constraints can prevent the wrong combination in the first place. The lesson is that links must be verified against the data, not assumed.

Q: How does a glossary support the front office's self-service analytics?
A: Front-office users can browse approved terms, read definitions, and jump straight to the linked gold columns to build their own queries with confidence in the meaning. They no longer depend on engineers to explain what each field represents. The glossary turns the catalog into a self-service business dictionary backed by real data links.

Q: Why does the convergence of glossary and semantic layer matter for "single source of truth" claims?
A: A single source of truth requires that the defined meaning and the computed value cannot diverge, which only holds when the glossary definition and the semantic-layer metric are linked and governed together. Convergence makes the approved definition the contract the metric must honour. Without it, the "single source" is two sources hoping to agree.

Q: What is the minimum metadata a fund glossary term should carry to be useful for compliance?
A: At minimum a clear definition, an accountable owner, a status (ideally `Approved`), a source/authority for the definition, and links to the assets that carry it. Those five elements let compliance answer what it means, who owns it, whether it is sanctioned, where it came from, and where the data lives. Anything less leaves an audit question unanswered.

Q: How would you handle a term whose definition legitimately differs between two fund ranges?
A: Either create distinct terms scoped to each range (for example "AUM (UCITS)" versus "AUM (AIF)") or use the hierarchy with a parent concept and qualified children, so each precise meaning has its own owned, linked definition. Forcing one definition onto genuinely different concepts reintroduces the ambiguity the glossary removes. Explicit scoping keeps each number defensible.

Q: Why is the glossary considered a governance artefact rather than a documentation artefact?
A: Because it enforces accountability (owners), control (approval workflow and statuses), and traceability (asset links and sources) — properties that define governance — rather than merely recording information. Documentation describes; governance controls and proves. The lifecycle and reviewer workflow are what make the glossary a control, not just a reference.

Q: What is the consequence of skipping the owner-assignment step but doing the linking?
A: You get a glossary where users can find definitions and assets but nobody is accountable for keeping the definitions correct, so they decay and there is no one to defend them under audit. Linking without ownership is half-governance. The lesson's Done-when requires owners on all 25 terms precisely to avoid this gap.

Q: How does a glossary term's status field assist catalog filtering and discovery?
A: Because the status is a structured value (`Draft`, `In Review`, `Approved`, `Deprecated`, `Rejected`), you can filter the catalog to show only `Approved` terms for trusted use or surface `In Review` ones for stewards to action. It turns lifecycle state into a queryable property. This is also why knowing the exact literal values matters when scripting or filtering.

Q: Why must "dealing cut-off" be defined with a time zone in a Luxembourg fund context?
A: Orders and pricing depend on a precise deadline, and Luxembourg funds deal with global investors and administrators across time zones, so a cut-off stated without a zone (for example "12:00") is ambiguous and can mis-price orders. The glossary definition must pin the time zone and dealing calendar. This is a concrete example of a term where vagueness has direct financial consequences.

Q: How does linking glossary terms support data-quality rule design?
A: Once a column is linked to a term like "NAV", you can attach quality expectations (positivity, valuation-point freshness) that follow from the concept's definition, making the rules semantically grounded rather than arbitrary. The shared meaning drives consistent rules across every linked column. This is part of why glossary and DQ programmes reinforce each other.

Q: What problem does a glossary solve that lineage alone cannot?
A: Lineage tells you where data flows but not what it means, so two columns can have clean lineage and still be mislabelled or misunderstood; the glossary supplies the agreed business meaning lineage lacks. You need both: provenance from lineage and meaning from the glossary. The Done-when click combines them into a single trustworthy view.

Q: Why is a 25-term scope a deliberate teaching choice rather than an arbitrary number?
A: Twenty-five terms is large enough to surface real cross-team disagreement and force genuine definition work across NAV, pricing, AUM, and dealing concepts, yet small enough to complete the owner-assignment and linkage in the lesson's three-hour budget. It teaches the full workflow at meaningful scale. One or two terms would not reveal the conflicts the glossary exists to resolve.

Q: How would you justify spending governance effort on a glossary to a sceptical engineering team?
A: Point to the concrete failure mode it prevents — two reports labelled "AUM" disagreeing with no way to adjudicate — and the audit time saved when every reported figure traces to an owned, approved, sourced definition with linked columns. The glossary turns recurring definitional arguments into a one-time governed decision. It is cheaper than repeatedly reconciling divergent numbers.

Q: What is the difference between deprecating a term and rejecting one?
A: A `Rejected` term failed the approval workflow and was never sanctioned, whereas a `Deprecated` term was previously valid and is now retired but retained for history. The distinction matters for audit: rejected means "we decided against this definition", deprecated means "this used to be authoritative". Both keep a record rather than silently disappearing.

Q: How does the glossary help when migrating reports from a legacy system?
A: By mapping each legacy field to a glossary term, you make explicit which concept it represents, so the new system can link to the same approved definitions and the migration preserves meaning rather than just moving bytes. Discrepancies in legacy definitions surface as conflicting links to the same term. The glossary becomes the meaning contract the migration must satisfy.

Q: Why might a "summary cost indicator" term be seeded specifically from PRIIPs?
A: Because PRIIPs prescribes how summary cost figures are defined and presented in the Key Information Document, so the regulation is the authoritative source for what that indicator means and how it is computed. Seeding from PRIIPs makes the term's definition match the disclosure obligation exactly. Any in-house wording risks diverging from the mandated methodology.

Q: What does it mean that the catalog becomes "business-readable", in concrete terms?
A: It means a non-technical user can search a word they understand ("NAV"), read a plain-language approved definition, see who owns it, and click straight to the data that holds it — all without SQL or schema knowledge. Readability is the ability of the business, not just engineers, to navigate the catalog. The glossary's definitions and links are what provide it.

Q: A reviewer was changed after a term entered `Draft` — why can this be problematic?
A: Reviewer changes once a term is already in draft can leave the approval workflow in an inconsistent state, where the new reviewer may not be wired into the in-flight review, a known edge case in OpenMetadata. The practical guidance is to set reviewers before drafting and avoid swapping mid-review. If you must change them, verify the term still advances correctly.

Q: How does a business glossary interact with the firm's regulatory reporting obligations?
A: Regulatory reports cite figures like NAV and AUM whose definitions must match the regulation, so the glossary's approved, regulation-seeded terms become the controlled definitions those reports draw on. Linking the report's source columns to those terms evidences the chain from filing to definition to data. This is governance that directly supports DORA/UCITS/AIFMD reporting integrity.

Q: Why is "approved" status alone insufficient without linked assets for a data-carrying term?
A: An approved-but-unlinked term means the firm has agreed what NAV means but cannot point to where NAV actually lives, so the definition floats free of the data and cannot support lineage or impact analysis. Governance needs the connection to be actionable. For data-carrying concepts, approval plus linkage is the complete state.

Q: How can mutually exclusive glossary terms encode a real fund-data rule?
A: For example, a column representing a single NAV cannot simultaneously be "Pre-Swing NAV" and "Post-Swing NAV", so marking those terms mutually exclusive prevents a tagging mistake that would misrepresent the value. The constraint encodes domain truth into the metadata layer. It stops contradictory links from ever being saved.

Q: What is the value of recording who proposed a definition versus who approved it?
A: Recording both gives a maker-checker trail: the proposer (owner) is accountable for content and the approver (reviewer) is accountable for sign-off, so responsibility is clear and segregated. Auditors can see the definition was not self-approved. This separation is a basic control that the approval workflow operationalises.

Q: How does a glossary mitigate the risk of key-person knowledge loss?
A: When the meaning of "dealing cut-off" lives only in one operator's head, their departure creates risk; capturing it as an owned, approved, sourced glossary term makes the knowledge institutional and discoverable. The definition survives staff turnover. This resilience is a quiet but important governance benefit.

Q: Why should the glossary, dictionary, and semantic layer be kept consistent even before full convergence?
A: Until tooling links them automatically, divergence between the prose definition, the physical column, and the computed metric reintroduces the very inconsistency the glossary exists to remove, so manual alignment and reconciliation are required. Consistency across the three is the substance of the "single meaning" promise. Convergence just makes maintaining it cheaper, not optional.

Q: What is the first thing to check if clicking "NAV" shows the definition and owner but no assets?
A: Check whether any columns have actually been linked to the term, because a populated definition and owner with an empty asset list means the linking step was skipped or the links point at a different term. Confirm on a known NAV column that the term is attached. Until links exist, the term is defined but not connected to data.

Q: How does the glossary help adjudicate when front office and compliance disagree on a figure?
A: Both sides can be pointed to the single approved, owned definition and its linked source columns, turning an opinion dispute into a factual check of "is the figure computed from the approved definition's data". The glossary is the neutral arbiter because the definition is governed, not owned by either side. Disagreements collapse to "is this term being applied correctly".

Q: Why is it important that a glossary term's definition is independent of any single physical schema?
A: Because the same concept may be stored in many places and schemas change over time, tying the definition to one table would make it fragile and parochial; an independent definition can be linked to whatever columns carry it now or later. Meaning outlives storage. This independence is exactly what separates the glossary (meaning) from the data dictionary (structure).

Q: How does seeding a UCITS-related term differ from inventing one internally?
A: A UCITS-seeded term draws its definition from the UCITS directive's framework, so concepts like "eligible asset" or fund-level disclosures match the regulatory regime the fund operates under, giving the definition external authority. An internally invented one risks subtly diverging from what UCITS actually requires. Citing UCITS as the source makes the term defensible during a CSSF review.

Q: Why should a glossary term carry an example or context note alongside its definition?
A: A concise example (for example showing how swing pricing adjusts a sample NAV) disambiguates edge cases that prose alone leaves unclear, helping non-specialists apply the term correctly. It reduces the chance two readers interpret the same definition differently. The example complements, but does not replace, the authoritative definition and its source.

Q: What is the risk of linking a term to a silver-layer column that is later dropped?
A: If the underlying column is removed and the link is not maintained, the term points at a non-existent asset, leaving a stale link that misleads anyone trusting the catalog. This is why linking is usually concentrated on stable gold tables and why link maintenance is part of stewardship. A periodic review catches and repairs such broken links.

Q: How does a glossary support recertification or periodic review of definitions?
A: Owners and reviewers can be prompted to re-confirm each term on a schedule, re-running the approval workflow so definitions do not silently go stale as regulation or business practice evolves. The status field shows whether a term is currently sanctioned. Recertification is an audit staple that the glossary's lifecycle and reviewer roles directly enable.

Q: Why is it useful to relate the glossary term "ISIN" to the term "LEI" rather than leave them isolated?
A: Both are identifiers a fund's reference data depends on, and relating them helps a user exploring security identification discover the connected legal-entity identifier, reflecting how securities and issuers link in practice. The associative relationship guides navigation without implying they are synonyms. It makes the reference-data corner of the glossary coherent rather than a flat list of codes.

Q: Summarise why the business glossary is foundational to a data architect's governance stack.
A: It establishes a single, owned, approved, regulation-anchored vocabulary and binds that meaning to the physical data through term-to-asset links, so every business concept is defined once, accountable to someone, and traceable to its columns. This makes the catalog business-readable and audit-defensible for compliance and usable for the front office. Everything else — lineage, quality, semantic metrics — gains business meaning by referencing it.
