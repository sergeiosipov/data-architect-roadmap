# Phase 7 · 6.8.2 LLM evaluation (Ragas, golden sets) — 150 self-test questions

Q: What is an LLM "golden set" and why does it sit at the centre of a defensible extraction workflow?
A: A golden set is a curated collection of inputs paired with human-verified correct outputs (the "ground truth" or reference) that you measure your pipeline against. It is the control that turns a demo into a system: without it you have no objective way to say a prompt change made things better or worse, only vibes. In a regulated fund context, it is the artefact you point an auditor to when asked how you know your ISIN extraction is accurate.

Q: Name the three eval layers the syllabus distinguishes, and which one matters most for KID field extraction.
A: The three layers are retrieval metrics (precision/recall@k — did you fetch the right context), generation metrics (faithfulness, answer relevancy — is the generated text grounded and on-topic), and task metrics (field-level extraction accuracy vs a golden set). For this workload the task metric is the one that matters: you ultimately care whether the extracted SRI, ISIN, or ongoing-cost figure is correct, not whether the prose around it reads well.

Q: Why is field-level extraction accuracy the "primary" metric for a KID/EMT extraction pipeline rather than a generic generation score?
A: Field-level accuracy directly measures the business outcome — is the `recommended_holding_period` or `ISIN` value correct — which is exactly what a transfer agent or regulator cares about. Generation scores like answer relevancy can be high while a specific field is still wrong, so they are necessary but not sufficient. You can and should defend field accuracy as primary because it is the only metric whose failure maps one-to-one onto a wrong number reaching a downstream system.

Q: What does precision@k measure in a retrieval eval?
A: Precision@k is the fraction of the top-k retrieved chunks that are actually relevant to the query. If you retrieve 5 chunks and 3 are relevant, precision@5 is 0.6. It tells you how much noise your retriever is mixing into the context window, which matters because irrelevant context both wastes tokens and can distract the generator into wrong answers.

Q: What does recall@k measure, and how does it differ from precision@k?
A: Recall@k is the fraction of all relevant chunks in the corpus that appear in your top-k results — did you find the evidence you needed. Precision asks "of what I fetched, how much was good"; recall asks "of what was good, how much did I fetch". They trade off: raising k usually improves recall but hurts precision, so you tune k against the metric that matters for your task.

Q: In Ragas, which metric class measures whether the generated answer is grounded in the retrieved context, and what is its formula?
A: The `Faithfulness` metric. It is computed as the number of claims in the response that are supported by the retrieved context divided by the total number of claims extracted from the response. So a response that makes 4 claims, 3 of which the context supports, scores 0.75; the score penalises hallucinated or unsupported statements.

Q: What three inputs does the Ragas `Faithfulness` metric require?
A: It requires `user_input` (the original question), `response` (the generated answer being evaluated), and `retrieved_contexts` (the list of supporting documents). It does not need a reference/ground-truth answer, because faithfulness measures grounding against the retrieved context, not correctness against a golden label. That is an important distinction: faithfulness can be perfect while the answer is still factually wrong if the context itself was wrong.

Q: What is `FaithfulnesswithHHEM` in Ragas and why might you choose it over the default `Faithfulness`?
A: `FaithfulnesswithHHEM` computes the faithfulness score using Vectara's HHEM-2.1-Open hallucination-detection model (a small T5-based classifier) instead of an LLM doing claim verification. You might choose it because it is free, small, open-source, and far cheaper to run at scale than calling a judge LLM for every claim. The trade-off is that a specialised classifier may generalise less well to your domain than a strong general LLM judge that you have aligned to your data.

Q: What does Ragas "answer relevancy" (Response Relevancy) measure, and what is a key blind spot?
A: Response Relevancy measures how well the generated answer addresses the actual question asked, typically by generating questions from the answer and comparing them to the original query. Its blind spot is that it rewards on-topic, complete-sounding answers regardless of factual correctness — an answer can be perfectly relevant and confidently wrong. So it catches evasive or partial answers but will not catch a relevant answer that states the wrong cost figure.

Q: Distinguish Ragas `ContextPrecision` from `ContextRecall`.
A: `ContextPrecision` measures whether the relevant items in the retrieved context are ranked highly — it penalises useful chunks buried below junk. `ContextRecall` measures whether all the information needed to produce the ground-truth answer is actually present in the retrieved context. Precision is about ranking quality of what you fetched; recall is about completeness of the evidence relative to the reference answer.

Q: What is "faithfulness can score high while the extracted field is still wrong" trying to warn you about?
A: It warns that faithfulness only checks that the answer is grounded in the retrieved context, not that the context (or the extraction) is correct. If your retriever surfaced the wrong table and the model faithfully read the wrong ongoing-cost figure from it, faithfulness is high but the field is wrong. This is why you cannot substitute a generation metric for a field-accuracy check against a golden set.

Q: Define "LLM-as-judge" and where it fits in an eval stack.
A: LLM-as-judge means using a (usually strong) LLM to grade the output of your pipeline — scoring faithfulness, relevancy, or pass/fail against a rubric — instead of, or alongside, exact-match checks. It fits where outputs are free-form and exact string match is too brittle, such as grading a generated summary. It is powerful but unreliable until calibrated, so it sits as a measured component you validate, not a black box you trust.

Q: What are the three LLM-as-judge caveats the syllabus calls out?
A: Bias, drift, and cost. Bias means the judge has systematic preferences (e.g. for longer answers, its own family of models, or the first option presented). Drift means a judge's behaviour changes when the underlying model is silently updated. Cost means each judged sample is an extra LLM call, which adds up fast over a large eval set run in CI.

Q: Before you trust an LLM judge, what must you do, per Hamel Husain's guidance?
A: Align the judge against human labels — measure how often the judge's verdicts agree with a set of human-graded examples before relying on its scores. Concretely you label a sample by hand, run the judge over the same sample, and compute agreement (e.g. accuracy, or correlation for scores). Only once agreement is acceptable do you let the judge stand in for humans; otherwise you are measuring the judge's bias, not your system.

Q: Give a concrete example of position bias in an LLM judge and how to mitigate it.
A: Position bias is when a pairwise judge favours whichever answer is shown first (or second) regardless of quality. To mitigate it, run each comparison twice with the order swapped and only count a "win" if the judge prefers the same answer in both orders; ties or order-dependent verdicts are discarded or flagged. This swap-and-check technique exposes how much of your score is real preference versus positional artefact.

Q: What is "judge drift" and why is it especially dangerous with a hosted model API?
A: Judge drift is a change in the judge's scoring behaviour over time, often because the provider silently updated the model behind a moving alias. It is dangerous because your eval baseline shifts without any change to your own code, so a "regression" or "improvement" may be an artefact of the judge, not your pipeline. The defence is to pin a specific model version for the judge and to periodically re-validate the judge against your human-labelled set.

Q: Why pin an explicit model version (not a floating alias) for both the system-under-test and the judge in CI?
A: Pinning a specific version makes eval results reproducible: a score change can be attributed to your prompt or data change, not to an upstream model swap. A floating alias like "latest" can be re-pointed by the provider at any time, silently invalidating your baseline and triggering phantom regressions. In a regulated workflow, reproducibility of the evidence is itself part of the control.

Q: What is a "rubric" in the context of LLM-as-judge grading, and why use one?
A: A rubric is an explicit scoring guide — the criteria and the meaning of each score level — that you give the judge so its grading is consistent and inspectable. Ragas exposes this via rubric-based scoring metrics. Using a rubric reduces variance between runs and makes disagreements debuggable, because you can point at the criterion the judge applied rather than guessing why it scored a 3.

Q: When building a golden dataset, why does the syllabus call it "your BA skills, weaponized"?
A: Because the hard part of a golden set is the same discipline a business analyst already has: defining unambiguous field specifications, sampling representative cases, and labelling them consistently against a spec. The LLM/eval tooling is the easy part; the value is in the rigorous, opinionated labelling. A domain expert who knows what a correct SRI or ongoing-cost value looks like is exactly who should build the golden set.

Q: What is "labeling discipline" and why does it determine the ceiling on your eval quality?
A: Labelling discipline means having a written labelling guide, consistent conventions, and ideally inter-annotator agreement so the same input always gets the same gold label. It sets the ceiling because your pipeline can never measure as "better than" the noise in your labels — if humans disagree on the gold value, the metric is meaningless. Disagreements should drive guideline refinement, not be averaged away.

Q: What is synthetic testset generation and what role does it play relative to a hand-built golden set?
A: Synthetic testset generation uses an LLM to generate question/context/reference triples from your own documents, giving you many test cases quickly. Its role is a bootstrap: it gets you coverage fast when you have no labelled data, but the syntheticly generated references still need human review before they are trusted as gold. It complements, not replaces, the carefully hand-labelled core golden set.

Q: In Ragas synthetic testset generation, what data structure is built from your documents first?
A: A knowledge graph. Ragas chunks your documents into `Node` objects (holding properties like `page_content`), runs extractors over them, and builds `Relationship` edges between nodes — for example linking nodes that share named entities. Queries are then synthesised by traversing this graph, which is what lets it generate both single-hop and multi-hop questions.

Q: In Ragas testset generation, what is the difference between a single-hop and a multi-hop query?
A: A single-hop query can be answered from one node/source, while a multi-hop query requires reasoning across multiple connected nodes in the knowledge graph. Multi-hop queries test whether your retriever can assemble evidence spread across documents and whether the generator can synthesise it. Mixing both in your testset ensures you do not only measure easy lookups.

Q: What is the role of extractors like `NERExtractor` or `KeyphraseExtractor` in Ragas testset generation?
A: Extractors pull structured information out of each node — named entities, keyphrases — and store it as node properties. Those properties are then used to build relationships between nodes (e.g. a `JaccardSimilarityBuilder` links nodes with overlapping entities). Richer extracted properties produce a denser knowledge graph and therefore more interesting multi-hop questions.

Q: What does `apply_transforms()` do in the Ragas testset-generation pipeline?
A: `apply_transforms()` runs a sequence of transformation steps (splitters, extractors, relationship builders) over the knowledge graph to enrich it before query synthesis. A `Parallel` wrapper lets independent extractors run concurrently for speed. The output is a populated knowledge graph from which `QuerySynthesizer` objects can sample scenarios.

Q: What does a Ragas `QuerySynthesizer` produce, and what is a `SingleTurnSample`?
A: A `QuerySynthesizer` generates scenarios by combining nodes, query length/style preferences, and personas, then turns each scenario into a sample. A `SingleTurnSample` is the resulting record holding the synthesised `user_input`, the `reference_contexts`, and a `reference` answer. These samples are the rows of your synthetic testset that you then run your pipeline against.

Q: Why must synthetically generated golden answers still be human-reviewed before use?
A: Because the same class of model that you are evaluating generated the references, so any systematic error or hallucination in generation gets baked into your "ground truth", making the eval circular and over-optimistic. Human review catches references that are wrong, ambiguous, or unanswerable from the given context. The synthetic set is a draft golden set, not a final one.

Q: What is "eval-in-CI" and what does it protect against?
A: Eval-in-CI means running your evals automatically on every commit or pull request, with a threshold gate that fails the build if quality drops. It protects against silent regressions: a prompt tweak or model bump that quietly lowers ISIN extraction accuracy is caught at merge time instead of weeks later by a transfer agent. It treats eval failures exactly like a failing unit test.

Q: How does DeepEval plug into pytest to run evals as tests?
A: DeepEval provides `assert_test(test_case, metrics)` which evaluates an `LLMTestCase` against a list of metrics and raises an assertion failure if any metric scores below its threshold, so it behaves like a normal pytest assertion. You write ordinary `def test_...()` functions and run them with the `deepeval test run test_file.py` command (or via pytest discovery). Failures surface in pytest's standard reporting, so CI treats them like any other test failure.

Q: In DeepEval, what is the `threshold` parameter on a metric and what range do scores use?
A: Every DeepEval metric produces a score in the 0–1 range, and `threshold` (e.g. `threshold=0.5`) is the cut-off: the metric passes only if its score is greater than or equal to the threshold. Setting the threshold is how you encode "good enough" for that metric. If the score falls below it, `assert_test` fails and the build goes red.

Q: What is a DeepEval `Golden` and how does it relate to an `LLMTestCase`?
A: A `Golden` is a stored, reusable test input (and optional expected output) — the persisted ground-truth record — typically collected in an `EvaluationDataset`. At eval time you turn goldens into `LLMTestCase` objects by running your pipeline to fill in the `actual_output`, then score them. The separation lets you version the goldens independently from the runtime outputs they are compared against.

Q: What is the exact CLI command to run a DeepEval test file, and how does it differ from invoking pytest directly?
A: The command is `deepeval test run test_file.py` (or `deepeval test run` to run a directory). It wraps pytest but adds DeepEval's metric orchestration, caching, and report generation on top of standard test discovery. You can still run the same files under bare `pytest`, but `deepeval test run` gives you the DeepEval-specific run summary and integrations.

Q: Walk through a minimal eval-in-CI GitHub Actions setup for an extraction pipeline.
A: A workflow triggers on pull requests, checks out the code, installs dependencies, sets the model API key from a secret, and runs `deepeval test run tests/` (or `pytest`) as a required step. Each test asserts field-accuracy and faithfulness metrics with explicit thresholds, so a below-threshold run exits non-zero and fails the job. Because the job is a required status check, the PR cannot merge until evals pass.

Q: Why should the eval threshold in CI be set deliberately rather than at the current passing score?
A: A threshold pinned at exactly today's score makes the gate brittle — normal sampling noise in LLM judges will flip it red on unrelated changes. You set it slightly below the current stable level to give headroom for variance while still catching a real drop. The threshold encodes the minimum quality you are willing to ship, which is a product/risk decision, not just the latest number.

Q: An eval suite passes locally but fails in CI with different scores each run. First thing to check?
A: First check that the model version and `temperature` (and any judge model) are pinned and that `temperature=0` or a fixed seed is used where supported, because non-deterministic generation produces score jitter. Also confirm the same metric thresholds and the same dataset version are used in both environments. Flaky thresholds set too tight against noisy LLM-judge scores are the usual culprit once determinism is ruled out.

Q: What is "human-in-the-loop sampling design" and what three things must it specify?
A: It is the deliberate design of where humans review model output in production. It must specify what humans review (which fields or which flagged cases), at what rate (e.g. a fixed percentage sample plus all low-confidence cases), and where the outcome is recorded (an audit log or labelling store that feeds back into the golden set). Without all three, human review is ad hoc and produces no usable signal.

Q: Why record the outcome of every human review, not just the corrections?
A: Recording both confirmations and corrections gives you an unbiased estimate of true accuracy and a growing labelled dataset; recording only corrections inflates your error rate and discards the "model was right" signal. The recorded outcomes also become new golden examples, so the review loop continuously improves the eval set. In a regulated context the record is also your evidence trail that review actually happened.

Q: What is a sensible trigger for escalating a sample from spot-check to full human review?
A: Escalate when the model's confidence is low, when automated checks fail (e.g. an extracted ISIN fails checksum validation), or when the field feeds a high-impact downstream use such as a published NAV. You define these triggers explicitly so escalation is rule-based and auditable, not arbitrary. The default sampled rate covers business-as-usual; triggers catch the risky tail.

Q: State a defensible human-review sampling rate and justify the structure.
A: A common defensible design is "review 100% of cases that fail a hard validation or fall below a confidence threshold, plus a random N% (say 5%) of the rest." The 100%-of-flagged part bounds your worst-case risk on known-suspicious items, while the random sample gives an unbiased accuracy estimate over the silent majority. You then tune N against your error budget and reviewer capacity.

Q: Why is exact string match too brittle as the only field-accuracy check, and what is the fix?
A: Exact match fails on harmless formatting differences — `EUR` vs `Euro`, `1.50%` vs `0.015`, trailing whitespace — so it under-reports true accuracy. The fix is normalised match: canonicalise both predicted and gold values (parse the number, uppercase the currency code, strip whitespace) before comparing. You reserve exact match for fields like ISIN where the canonical form is unambiguous and any deviation is genuinely wrong.

Q: For an ISIN field specifically, what extra check beyond match against the golden value is cheap and valuable?
A: Validate the ISIN's check digit using the Luhn-based mod-10 algorithm over the 12-character code, independently of the golden set. This catches structurally impossible ISINs even on inputs not in your golden set, so it acts as a free production guardrail. A faithfulness or accuracy metric will not flag a malformed ISIN on its own, but a checksum will.

Q: How would you structure a field-accuracy metric that reports per-field, not just an overall number?
A: Compute a pass/fail per field per document, then aggregate to a per-field accuracy (e.g. SRI 0.98, ISIN 1.00, ongoing-costs 0.91) as well as an overall figure. Per-field reporting is essential because an overall 0.95 can hide one critical field at 0.70. When CI fails, the per-field breakdown immediately identifies which extraction instruction regressed.

Q: The syllabus "Do" step degrades the prompt by dropping one extraction instruction. What should CI then show?
A: CI should fail, and the per-field accuracy breakdown should localise the failure to exactly the field whose instruction was dropped — e.g. `recommended_holding_period` falls while other fields hold. This demonstrates both that the gate works and that your reporting is granular enough to be actionable. If CI stayed green or only the overall number moved without identifying the field, your eval is too coarse.

Q: Why store the golden set as versioned YAML/CSV beside the code rather than in a database?
A: Keeping it in the repo means it is versioned with Git, reviewed via pull request, and diffable, so every change to ground truth has an author, a timestamp, and a rationale — which is exactly the provenance an auditor wants. It also pins the exact golden set to each commit, making eval results reproducible. A database makes the gold values mutable and harder to tie to a specific code state.

Q: What goes wrong if your golden set is not versioned alongside the code?
A: You lose reproducibility: a past eval result can no longer be reproduced because the gold values may have changed underneath it, so you cannot tell whether a score moved due to code or due to relabelling. You also lose the audit trail of who changed which label and why. Versioning the golden set is what makes "the eval said 0.97 on commit abc123" a meaningful, defensible statement.

Q: How do you pick the sample of documents for a golden set so it is representative?
A: Use stratified sampling across the dimensions that drive variation — fund type (UCITS vs AIF), document template/version, language, share class, and known edge cases like multi-share-class KIDs. Pure random sampling can miss rare-but-important formats. The goal is that the golden set's distribution mirrors (or deliberately over-weights) the production distribution and its failure modes.

Q: Why deliberately over-sample edge cases in a golden set even though they are rare in production?
A: Because rare formats are exactly where extraction breaks, and a proportionally-sampled set would contain too few of them to detect a regression on them. Over-weighting edge cases (negative numbers, missing fields, unusual layouts) makes the eval sensitive to the failures that hurt most. You can still report a separately-weighted overall accuracy if you need a production-representative headline number.

Q: What is the difference between an "offline eval" and an "online eval", and where does each apply here?
A: An offline eval runs against a fixed golden set in CI before deployment, giving a controlled, reproducible regression gate. An online eval observes live production traffic — sampling outputs for human review, tracking faithfulness on real queries, watching for drift. You need both: offline gates the release, online catches the distribution shifts and novel inputs the golden set never anticipated.

Q: Why is faithfulness still useful even though it does not check field correctness?
A: Faithfulness catches a distinct failure mode — the model inventing content not present in the source — which a field-match metric may miss when there is no gold value for a free-text answer. In an extraction-plus-summary pipeline, a high faithfulness score gives confidence the narrative is grounded, while field accuracy covers the structured values. They are complementary controls, not substitutes.

Q: A faithfulness eval scores low but spot-checked answers look correct. What is the likely cause?
A: The likely cause is that the judge's claim-decomposition or the `retrieved_contexts` you passed do not match what actually grounded the answer — for example you logged a truncated context, or the answer draws on parametric knowledge that is correct but absent from the provided context. Faithfulness measures grounding in the supplied context, so correct-but-ungrounded answers score low by design. Check that the contexts passed to the metric are the real ones the model saw.

Q: Why does running an LLM-judge eval suite cost real money, and how do you keep CI cost bounded?
A: Each judged sample is one or more extra LLM API calls, so a 500-row faithfulness eval can be hundreds of calls per CI run, multiplied across PRs. Keep cost bounded by running the full set nightly or on merge to main while running a small smoke subset on every PR, by caching judge results for unchanged inputs, and by using cheaper judges (or HHEM) where adequate. The point is to gate without bankrupting the pipeline.

Q: What is the danger of optimising your prompt directly against the same golden set you report on?
A: It is overfitting: you can tune the prompt to ace the specific gold examples while real-world accuracy stagnates or drops, because you have leaked the test set into development. The fix is to hold out a separate test split that you never look at during prompt iteration, and only report on the held-out set. This is the same train/validation/test hygiene as classical ML.

Q: How should you split a golden set into dev/test for prompt engineering?
A: Reserve a held-out test split that is used only for the final CI gate and reporting, and iterate prompts against a separate dev split. Keep the split deterministic (e.g. by document id hash) so it is stable across runs and commits. This prevents the test set from leaking into your tuning loop and keeps the reported number honest.

Q: What is inter-annotator agreement and why measure it when building a golden set?
A: Inter-annotator agreement quantifies how often two or more human labellers assign the same gold value to the same input (e.g. via percentage agreement or Cohen's kappa). Low agreement means your field is ambiguously specified, so any model score against those labels is noisy. Measuring it early forces you to sharpen the labelling guide before you waste effort evaluating against unreliable ground truth.

Q: Your overall field accuracy is 0.96 but a transfer agent reports frequent ongoing-cost errors. What does this reveal?
A: It reveals that an aggregate metric is hiding a low-performing field: ongoing-costs may be at 0.80 while other fields near 1.00 pull the average up. The fix is per-field reporting and possibly per-field thresholds in CI so the weak field cannot be masked. It also suggests your golden set may under-represent the cost-format variations causing the production errors.

Q: Why might you set different CI thresholds for different fields?
A: Because fields differ in both difficulty and business risk: a wrong ISIN is unacceptable and warrants a near-1.0 threshold, while a free-text "objectives" summary may tolerate more variation. Uniform thresholds either over-constrain easy-but-low-stakes fields or under-protect critical ones. Per-field thresholds let the gate reflect actual risk tolerance.

Q: What is "answer correctness" vs "answer relevancy" in Ragas, and which needs a reference?
A: Answer relevancy measures whether the response addresses the question and does not need a ground-truth answer. Answer/factual correctness compares the response against a reference answer and therefore requires the golden label. They answer different questions — "is it on topic" versus "is it right" — and conflating them is a common mistake; correctness is what maps to your task accuracy.

Q: How does normalisation of a percentage field like "ongoing costs" affect your accuracy metric?
A: Different documents express the same value as `0.0150`, `1.50%`, or `1,50 %` (European decimal comma), so you must parse to a canonical numeric (e.g. a `Decimal` of 0.015) before comparing to gold. Without normalisation, exact match would mark correct extractions as failures and tank your accuracy. The gotcha is locale: Luxembourg KIDs often use comma decimal separators, which naive parsing mishandles.

Q: Why is a `Decimal`/parsed comparison with a tolerance sometimes needed for numeric fields, and what is the risk?
A: Rounding and representation differences mean two correct numbers may differ in the last digit, so a small absolute or relative tolerance avoids false failures. The risk is that too loose a tolerance hides genuine errors — e.g. accepting 1.5% when the truth is 1.6%. Set the tolerance to the field's meaningful precision (often zero tolerance for regulated cost figures) and document the choice.

Q: In the EMT/EPT context, how would a golden set help validate an extraction-to-EMT pipeline?
A: You build golden records of the correct EMT field values (costs, SRI, target market) for a sample of source documents, then measure field-level accuracy of your extraction against them before the EMT file is generated. This gates the pipeline so a regression cannot quietly produce wrong EMT values distributed to distributors. It also gives you per-field evidence that the EMT-relevant fields specifically are accurate.

Q: Why is LEI extraction a good candidate for an independent structural validation alongside golden-set matching?
A: An LEI is a 20-character ISO 17442 code with a defined structure and a mod-97-10 check digit, so you can validate it independently of any golden value, catching malformed extractions on unseen inputs. Like the ISIN checksum, this is a cheap production guardrail that complements accuracy measurement. Accuracy tells you "vs known truth"; the structural check tells you "structurally impossible" even with no truth available.

Q: What does it mean for a metric to be "reference-free" versus "reference-based", with an example of each?
A: A reference-free metric scores an output without a ground-truth answer — Ragas faithfulness and answer relevancy are reference-free, judging grounding and topicality. A reference-based metric compares against a gold label — factual correctness and field-accuracy are reference-based. Reference-free metrics scale to live traffic where you have no labels; reference-based metrics need a golden set but measure true correctness.

Q: Why are reference-free metrics especially valuable for online/production monitoring?
A: Because production traffic arrives without ground-truth labels, you cannot compute reference-based accuracy on it in real time. Reference-free metrics like faithfulness and relevancy can be computed on any live output, giving an early warning of degradation (e.g. a sudden faithfulness drop after a model update). They are the production smoke alarm; the golden set is the lab bench.

Q: What is "claim decomposition" in faithfulness scoring and why can it be a source of judge variance?
A: Claim decomposition is the step where the judge LLM breaks the response into atomic factual claims before checking each against the context. It is a source of variance because different runs (or temperatures) may split the text into different claims, changing the denominator and thus the score. Pinning the judge model and using deterministic settings reduces this jitter.

Q: Why should the judge LLM ideally be a different model (or at least independent) from the one generating the answers?
A: Using the same model to generate and to judge invites self-preference bias — models tend to rate their own style of output favourably — inflating scores. An independent judge gives a less correlated, more honest assessment. At minimum, validate any judge against human labels so you can quantify and correct for residual bias regardless of which model you use.

Q: What is a "smoke" eval and why run a small one on every PR?
A: A smoke eval is a tiny, fast subset of your suite (a handful of representative cases) that runs in seconds on every pull request to catch gross breakage cheaply. The full, expensive suite runs less often (nightly or on merge). This two-tier design keeps PR feedback fast and CI bills low while still gating obvious regressions immediately.

Q: How do you make an LLM-judge eval reproducible enough to use as a CI gate?
A: Pin the judge model version, set `temperature=0` (and a seed where supported), fix the prompt and rubric, and pin the dataset version. Then accept that small residual non-determinism exists and set thresholds with headroom rather than at the exact passing score. Reproducibility plus threshold headroom is what stops the gate flapping.

Q: A new model version raises your faithfulness score but field accuracy falls. Which do you trust and why?
A: Trust field accuracy, because it is the reference-based task metric tied to the business outcome, while faithfulness only measures grounding. A model can become more fluent and better-grounded in its prose while extracting a specific value less accurately. This is precisely why the syllabus insists field-level accuracy is the primary metric for this workload.

Q: What is the purpose of writing "one paragraph per metric on its blind spot" as in the Do step?
A: It forces you to articulate, for each metric, the failure it cannot see — faithfulness ignores context correctness, relevancy ignores factual accuracy, accuracy ignores out-of-distribution inputs not in the golden set. Documenting blind spots prevents over-trusting any single number and guides which metrics must be combined. It is also exactly the kind of reasoned defence an auditor or model-risk reviewer will ask for.

Q: State the blind spot of field-level exact-match accuracy.
A: Its blind spot is anything outside the golden set: it says nothing about inputs, formats, or fund types you did not sample, and it is only as correct as your labels. It also can be brittle to formatting unless normalised, and it gives no signal on free-text quality. So a high accuracy on a narrow golden set can mask poor real-world generalisation.

Q: State the blind spot of retrieval recall@k.
A: High recall@k tells you the right evidence was fetched but says nothing about whether the generator then used it correctly or whether it also pulled in distracting irrelevant chunks (that is precision's job). You can have perfect recall and still produce a wrong answer because the generation step failed. Recall is necessary upstream insurance, not a guarantee of a correct final field.

Q: How does the "evaluation is the control that makes LLM extraction defensible" framing connect to DORA or model-risk expectations?
A: Regulators and frameworks like DORA expect that critical automated processes have controls, monitoring, and evidence of effectiveness; an eval suite with a versioned golden set, CI gates, and a recorded human-review loop is exactly that control plus its audit trail. It turns "we use an LLM" into "we measure, gate, and monitor the LLM with documented thresholds". Without it, the process is operationally fragile and hard to defend under scrutiny.

Q: Why is "no golden set and no CI gate" described as the failure mode where a transfer agent discovers your regression?
A: Because without an automated gate, a quality drop ships silently and only surfaces when a downstream consumer — a transfer agent reconciling NAV or fund data — notices wrong values, often weeks later. By then the bad data has propagated and the incident is external. The golden set plus CI gate moves the discovery to merge time, internal and cheap.

Q: What is the difference between evaluating the retrieval step and evaluating the end-to-end pipeline, and why do both?
A: Component evaluation scores retrieval (precision/recall@k) in isolation to localise where quality lives, while end-to-end evaluation scores the final extracted fields the user actually receives. You do both because an end-to-end failure does not tell you whether retrieval or generation broke, and a good retrieval score does not guarantee a good final answer. Component metrics aid debugging; end-to-end metrics measure outcome.

Q: How would you use Ragas synthetic testset generation to bootstrap evals for a brand-new KID corpus with no labels?
A: Point `TestsetGenerator` at the KID documents, let it build a knowledge graph and synthesise a mix of single- and multi-hop questions with reference answers, then have a domain expert review and correct the references to form a draft golden set. You iterate, discarding nonsensical or unanswerable items, until you trust a core subset. This gets you a usable eval quickly while the hand-labelled gold set grows.

Q: What is `generate_with_langchain_docs` used for in Ragas?
A: It is a convenience method on `TestsetGenerator` that takes LangChain `Document` objects and produces a synthetic testset in one call, handling the knowledge-graph build and query synthesis internally. It is the quick on-ramp for testset generation when your documents are already loaded as LangChain docs. You still review the generated references before trusting them.

Q: Why is "the discovery will be made by a transfer agent, not by you" a statement about feedback latency?
A: It contrasts the long latency of downstream human discovery (weeks, via a complaint) with the near-zero latency of a CI gate (minutes, at merge). Eval-in-CI compresses the feedback loop so the person best placed to fix the regression — you — finds it first and cheapest. Long feedback latency is what lets a small prompt change cause a large, late, external incident.

Q: How do you decide which fields belong in the golden set's scope?
A: Include the fields that are extracted and consumed downstream and whose errors carry real consequence — for a KID that is SRI, ongoing costs, ISIN, currency, and recommended holding period, as in the Do step. Prioritise by downstream impact and error likelihood. Fields no one consumes do not need golden coverage; high-impact regulated fields need it most.

Q: What is "data leakage" between your golden set and your retrieval corpus, and why does it inflate scores?
A: Leakage occurs when the exact passages used to write the gold answers are also the ones trivially retrievable, so retrieval looks artificially easy and accuracy is overstated relative to messier production inputs. It inflates scores because the eval no longer reflects real retrieval difficulty. Mitigate by sampling realistic queries and ensuring the test distribution matches production, not the construction process.

Q: When an LLM judge disagrees with a human on a sample, what should happen to that disagreement?
A: It should be triaged: either the human label is corrected (improving the golden set), or the judge prompt/rubric is refined to reduce the systematic disagreement, or the case is flagged as genuinely ambiguous. Disagreements are signal, not noise — averaging them away hides a calibration problem. Tracking the human-judge agreement rate over time tells you whether the judge is still trustworthy.

Q: Why is `temperature` set to 0 (or low) for both the system-under-test and the judge during evaluation?
A: A low temperature reduces output randomness, making both the pipeline's extractions and the judge's verdicts more deterministic and therefore more comparable across runs. This shrinks score variance, which is essential when a threshold gate must not flap on noise alone. Note it does not fully eliminate non-determinism with some hosted APIs, so headroom on thresholds is still needed.

Q: What does it mean to "align the judge against human labels" in measurable terms?
A: It means computing an agreement statistic between judge verdicts and human verdicts on a held-out labelled sample — e.g. accuracy/F1 for pass-fail judging, or correlation for graded scores — and only deploying the judge once that agreement clears a bar you set. You also re-run this periodically to detect drift. Alignment turns "we use an LLM judge" into "our judge agrees with humans 92% of the time on our data".

Q: Why might you prefer a cheaper classifier like HHEM over an LLM judge for high-volume faithfulness checks in CI?
A: Because at high volume the per-call cost and latency of an LLM judge dominate, whereas a small open-source classifier like HHEM runs locally, fast, and free. For faithfulness specifically, `FaithfulnesswithHHEM` gives you a scalable grounding signal without an API bill per claim. The trade-off is potentially weaker domain generalisation, which you check by validating it against your labelled set.

Q: What is the consequence of a golden set that is too small, and how do you detect it?
A: A too-small golden set produces high-variance, low-power metrics: a real regression may not move the number beyond noise, and confidence intervals on accuracy are wide. You detect it by computing confidence intervals or by seeing scores swing wildly on trivial changes. The fix is to grow the set, prioritising the fields and edge cases where you need to detect small changes.

Q: How can you express a target market or risk field (like SRI) so it is unambiguously gradable?
A: Define it as a constrained value — SRI is an integer 1–7 — and grade with exact match on the integer, rejecting anything outside the allowed range as invalid regardless of the gold value. A constrained schema removes labelling ambiguity and makes structural validation trivial. Free-text fields, by contrast, need rubric-based or reference-based grading because exact match is too brittle.

Q: A prompt change improves accuracy on the dev split but CI fails on the test split. What is the most likely explanation?
A: You have overfit the prompt to the dev split — the change exploited quirks of the dev examples that do not generalise, so the untouched test split regressed. This is exactly why the test split is held out: it is the honest signal. Roll back or rework the change and re-validate on the held-out set before merging.

Q: Why is it dangerous to let the same engineer write both the extraction prompt and the golden labels without review?
A: They may unconsciously align the labels to what the prompt happens to produce, biasing the golden set toward the current system and masking real errors. Independent labelling (or peer review of labels) keeps the ground truth honest. In a regulated setting, separation of the "builder" and the "labeller/reviewer" roles is also a control-integrity point.

Q: What metric would you watch in online monitoring to detect a silent model upgrade by your API provider?
A: Watch reference-free metrics like faithfulness and answer relevancy on live traffic, plus structural pass rates (ISIN/LEI checksum success), for a step change with no corresponding code deploy. A sudden shift uncorrelated with your releases is the signature of an upstream model swap or drift. You then re-run your golden-set eval to quantify the impact and decide whether to pin or roll back.

Q: How do you keep an LLM eval suite fast enough not to block developers while still being thorough?
A: Tier it: a fast smoke subset gates every PR in seconds, the full golden-set eval runs on merge to main or nightly, and expensive judge-based metrics are cached on unchanged inputs and parallelised. You trade exhaustiveness on every commit for fast feedback, accepting that the full gate runs slightly later. Caching and parallelism (e.g. Ragas/DeepEval async scoring) cut wall-clock time substantially.

Q: What is the relationship between the golden set and the human-review loop over time?
A: The human-review loop continuously feeds new verified labels — both corrections and confirmations — back into the golden set, so it grows and stays representative of evolving production inputs. The golden set in turn defines what "correct" means for the reviewers. This virtuous cycle is what keeps the eval honest as fund templates, regulations, and document formats change.

Q: Why might answer relevancy be misleading for a strictly extractive task, and what should you use instead?
A: For pure extraction the "answer" is a structured value, not prose, so relevancy (designed for question-answering text) adds little and can score a wrong-but-on-topic value as relevant. You should use reference-based field accuracy as the primary metric and reserve relevancy/faithfulness for any free-text or summary components. Matching the metric to the task shape is the key judgement.

Q: Your CI eval gate is green but production accuracy is poor. What gap does this most likely indicate?
A: It indicates a distribution gap: your golden set does not represent the production inputs, so the gate passes on easy/known cases while real traffic contains formats or fund types you never sampled. The fix is to sample failing production cases (via the human-review loop) into the golden set, closing the gap. A green gate only certifies the distribution it was built on.

Q: How would you weight a "production-representative" accuracy number when your golden set deliberately over-samples edge cases?
A: Re-weight each stratum (fund type, format) by its true production frequency when computing the headline number, so over-sampled edge cases do not distort it, while still keeping the unweighted per-stratum scores to monitor hard cases. This gives you two honest views: a realistic production estimate and a sensitive edge-case detector. Reporting both prevents either the headline or the tail from being misleading.

Q: What is the role of a confidence threshold in routing samples to human review?
A: A confidence threshold (from model logprobs, a calibrated score, or a verifier) decides which low-confidence extractions are auto-escalated to a human, concentrating review effort where the model is least sure. Combined with validation-failure triggers, it gives a risk-proportionate review policy. Calibrating the threshold against actual error rates is what makes it trustworthy rather than arbitrary.

Q: Why must the human-review recording be auditable in a fund-administration context?
A: Because the record is the evidence that the control operated — who reviewed, when, what they decided, and what changed — which is what an auditor, internal control function, or DORA-style operational-resilience review will request. An unrecorded review provides no assurance and cannot be relied upon. Auditable logging turns human-in-the-loop from a hope into a demonstrable control.

Q: What is the difference between `score()` and `ascore()` on a Ragas metric, and why does it matter for a large eval run?
A: `score()` runs the metric synchronously on one sample, while `ascore()` is the async variant that lets you evaluate many samples concurrently without blocking on each LLM call. For a large golden set this matters because metric scoring is I/O-bound on API latency, so async concurrency cuts wall-clock time dramatically. The gotcha is to bound concurrency so you do not trip the provider's rate limits and turn speed-ups into retries.

Q: Why is reporting a single accuracy point estimate without an uncertainty range a weak way to defend an eval result?
A: A point estimate like 0.95 hides how much it could move under resampling; on a small golden set the true accuracy might plausibly be anywhere from 0.88 to 0.99. Reporting a confidence interval (or at least the sample size) tells the reviewer how much to trust the number and whether a 0.01 movement is signal or noise. In a regulated review, quantified uncertainty is part of showing the metric is fit for purpose.

Q: How would you eval a multi-share-class KID where one document yields several ISINs, in your golden set?
A: Model the gold value as a set of expected ISINs per document and score with set-level matching (e.g. exact set equality, or precision/recall over the ISIN set) rather than a single-value match. This correctly penalises both a missed share-class ISIN and an invented extra one. Treating a multi-valued field as single-valued would silently mark partially-correct extractions as fully right or fully wrong, distorting accuracy.

Q: In a regression triage, CI fails and the per-field report shows only `currency` dropped. What are your first two hypotheses?
A: First, that the recent prompt or parsing change altered how currency is normalised — e.g. it now returns `Euro` where gold expects the ISO code `EUR`, a normalisation mismatch rather than a true extraction error. Second, that an upstream model or document-template change shifted how the currency is presented in the source. You confirm by inspecting the specific failing rows' raw outputs versus gold, which the per-field, per-document reporting makes immediate.

Q: Summarise the end-to-end defence you would give for "how do you know your LLM extraction is accurate enough to ship".
A: I maintain a versioned golden set of human-verified field values, sampled and stratified across fund types and edge cases, and I measure field-level accuracy (primary), with faithfulness and relevancy as supporting reference-free checks. Those metrics run as a CI gate with explicit per-field thresholds, so a regression fails the build before merge, and a designed human-review loop samples production at a stated rate with checksum and confidence triggers feeding corrections back. Each metric's blind spot is documented, the judge is aligned against human labels, and every review outcome is recorded for audit.

Q: In Ragas, which reference-based context-precision metric uses a ground-truth answer to judge ranking, and what is the non-LLM alternative?
A: `LLMContextPrecisionWithReference` uses an LLM and the `reference` answer to decide whether each retrieved chunk was useful for producing that answer, ranking-weighted. The non-LLM alternative is `LLMContextPrecisionWithoutReference`, which judges usefulness against the `response` instead, or `NonLLMContextPrecisionWithReference`, which compares retrieved contexts to reference contexts by string distance with no LLM call. The reference-based variant is the one you use against a golden set; the non-LLM ones are cheaper and deterministic for CI.

Q: What does the `mode` argument on Ragas `FactualCorrectness` control, and which mode best matches a KID field-extraction task?
A: `FactualCorrectness(mode=...)` selects how claim overlap between response and reference is scored: `precision` (of the claims you made, how many are in the reference), `recall` (of the reference's claims, how many you covered), or `f1` (the harmonic mean, the default). For strict extraction you often want `recall` or `f1` so that omitting a required field is penalised, not just inventing wrong ones. The gotcha is that `precision` mode alone can reward a model that safely says less.

Q: You want a deterministic, zero-API string-overlap metric in Ragas for CI. Which metric and what does it return?
A: Use `NonLLMStringSimilarity`, which compares the `response` against the `reference` using a classic string-distance measure (Levenshtein-family) and returns a 0–1 similarity with no LLM call. It is fully deterministic and free, so it never flaps and never adds API cost to a PR gate. The trade-off is that it is surface-level — `EUR` vs `Euro` scores low even when both are correct — so you normalise first or reserve it for canonical fields.

Q: In DeepEval, what does `GEval` let you build, and which two arguments define the rubric?
A: `GEval` lets you define a custom LLM-judged metric for any criterion (e.g. "is the extracted ongoing-cost figure correct and in the right unit"). The rubric comes from `criteria` (a one-line natural-language description) or, more controllably, `evaluation_steps` (an explicit ordered list of grading steps), combined with `evaluation_params` listing which `LLMTestCaseParams` the judge may look at. Using `evaluation_steps` rather than a free-text `criteria` reduces judge variance because the grading procedure is pinned.

Q: Which `LLMTestCaseParams` would you put in a DeepEval `GEval` for grading extraction, and what does including `EXPECTED_OUTPUT` change?
A: You would include `INPUT`, `ACTUAL_OUTPUT`, and `EXPECTED_OUTPUT` (the gold value), and optionally `RETRIEVAL_CONTEXT`. Including `EXPECTED_OUTPUT` makes the `GEval` reference-based — the judge compares against gold rather than just judging the output in isolation — which is what you want for correctness. Omitting it makes the same metric reference-free, judging only relevancy/quality of the output, which is the wrong shape for a field-accuracy check.

Q: What is the difference between DeepEval's `evaluate()` function and the `deepeval test run` CLI, and when do you use each?
A: `evaluate(test_cases, metrics)` is a Python call that scores a list of `LLMTestCase` objects and returns results in-process — handy in notebooks, scripts, or batch online evals. `deepeval test run test_file.py` is the pytest-wrapping CLI that turns `assert_test` failures into red builds with run summaries and caching. You use `evaluate()` for ad-hoc or production scoring and `deepeval test run` for the CI regression gate.

Q: A DeepEval metric raises an error about a missing `LLMTestCase` parameter rather than failing the threshold. What happened?
A: The metric required a field you did not populate — e.g. `ContextualRecallMetric` needs `expected_output` and `retrieval_context`, and `FaithfulnessMetric` needs `retrieval_context` — so it errored at construction/measurement rather than scoring. This is a wiring bug, not a quality regression: different metrics demand different `LLMTestCase` parameters. The fix is to fill the required fields (run the pipeline to capture `actual_output` and the real `retrieval_context`) before scoring.

Q: How do you pass a custom `RunConfig` to Ragas `evaluate()`, and which parameter bounds concurrency?
A: You construct `RunConfig(max_workers=N, timeout=..., max_retries=...)` and pass it as `evaluate(dataset, metrics=[...], run_config=run_config)`. `max_workers` (default `16`) bounds how many metric/LLM calls run concurrently, which is the knob you lower to stay under a provider's rate limit and raise to speed up a big golden-set run. Leaving it too high is the usual cause of `429` rate-limit errors mid-evaluation.

Q: Ragas evaluation keeps failing partway with timeouts on a slow local judge. Which `RunConfig` fields do you adjust?
A: Raise `timeout` (default `180` seconds) so a slow per-call response is not aborted, and tune `max_retries` (default `10`) and `max_wait` so transient failures back off rather than crash the run. You may also lower `max_workers` so the local model is not overwhelmed by concurrency, which can itself cause timeouts. These live on `RunConfig` precisely so a slow self-hosted judge does not make the whole eval non-deterministic via partial failures.

Q: Why is a 12-character ISIN's checksum computed with Luhn mod-10 while a 20-character LEI uses mod-97-10, and what does each catch?
A: ISIN uses the Luhn (mod-10) algorithm over its first 11 characters (letters expanded to digits) to produce the 12th check digit; LEI uses ISO/IEC 7064 mod-97-10 over its first 18 characters to produce the two check digits at positions 19–20. Both catch single-digit typos and most adjacent transpositions, but mod-97 over two digits has a larger residue space and so catches a slightly broader class of errors. Either way the check is independent of your golden set, so it flags structurally impossible identifiers on unseen inputs.

Q: An extracted LEI matches the golden value but a downstream system rejects it. What structural property would you verify first?
A: Verify the two trailing check digits (positions 19–20) recompute correctly under ISO/IEC 7064 mod-97-10 over the first 18 characters, and that the code is exactly 20 alphanumeric characters with the format `[A-Z0-9]{18}[0-9]{2}`. A golden-set match only proves you reproduced the label; if the label itself was mistyped, both your extraction and your gold can be wrong together. The independent checksum is what catches a bad gold value that pure matching cannot.

Q: How would you eval an extracted ISIN where the country prefix is the risk, e.g. `LU` for a Luxembourg-domiciled fund?
A: Beyond exact match against gold and the Luhn check digit, validate that the first two characters are a valid ISO 3166-1 alpha-2 country code and, if the fund's domicile is known, that they equal the expected prefix (`LU` for a Luxembourg fund). A model can emit a checksum-valid ISIN with the wrong country prefix; matching gold catches it only if that exact case is in your set. The prefix rule is a cheap, golden-set-independent guardrail for a domicile-sensitive field.

Q: For a percentage field, the model outputs `1,50 %` and gold is `0.015`. Walk through the normalisation that makes these match.
A: Strip the trailing `%` and surrounding whitespace, replace the European decimal comma with a dot to get `1.50`, parse to a `Decimal('1.50')`, then divide by 100 because the source was a percentage, yielding `Decimal('0.015')`, which equals gold. The locale step is the trap: a naive `float('1,50')` raises `ValueError`, and `float('1.50')/100` is fine but `Decimal` avoids binary-float drift. Luxembourg KIDs frequently use the comma separator, so this path must be explicit, not assumed.

Q: A numeric-field eval marks `0.015` and `0.0150` as different. What is the root cause and the fix?
A: The comparison is doing string equality instead of numeric equality, so a trailing zero or differing precision counts as a mismatch even though the values are equal. The fix is to parse both sides to `Decimal` (or a scaled integer) and compare numerically, optionally with a documented tolerance. Reserve string comparison for fields whose canonical textual form is fixed, like an ISIN, where any character difference is genuinely wrong.

Q: Your field-accuracy harness reports SRI accuracy of 1.0 but a reviewer found a wrong SRI in production. What sampling flaw does this expose?
A: It exposes that your golden set does not contain the document format or share class where the SRI extraction breaks, so the metric is perfect on the cases you sampled and blind to the one that failed. A 1.0 only certifies the sampled distribution. The fix is to feed the failing production case (via the review loop) into the golden set and re-run, which will then surface and gate the regression.

Q: How do you grade SRI extraction so that an out-of-range value fails even if it happens to match a corrupted gold label?
A: Validate against the schema first — SRI must be an integer in `1`–`7` — and treat any value outside that range as a hard failure regardless of the gold label, then exact-match the integer for in-range values. This layering means a corrupted gold of `8` cannot make a wrong-but-matching extraction pass. Schema validation is a structural control that sits in front of, not behind, golden-set comparison.

Q: What is the difference between micro-averaging and macro-averaging field accuracy across a golden set, and which hides a weak field?
A: Micro-average pools every field-document cell and divides total correct by total checked, so fields with many rows dominate; macro-average computes per-field accuracy then averages the fields equally, giving each field equal weight. Micro-averaging hides a weak low-volume field because the high-volume fields swamp it, whereas macro-averaging (or per-field reporting) surfaces it. For risk-weighted reporting you often want per-field numbers plus a deliberately weighted headline, not a single micro-average.

Q: When would you report precision and recall for a field instead of a single accuracy, and give a fund example.
A: When the field is multi-valued or extraction can both miss and invent values — e.g. the set of share-class ISINs in a multi-class KID — a single accuracy is ill-defined, so you report precision (invented ISINs hurt) and recall (missed ISINs hurt) over the predicted vs gold set. Accuracy assumes one prediction per gold; precision/recall handle the set case correctly. The F1 then gives a single comparable number while still distinguishing the two failure modes.

Q: A faithfulness metric returns the same low score on every sample, even ones you know are grounded. First thing to check?
A: Check that `retrieved_contexts` is actually populated and non-empty for each sample — a common wiring bug is passing an empty list or the wrong key, so the judge finds no support for any claim and scores everything near zero. Faithfulness divides supported claims by total claims, and with no context nothing is supported. Confirm the contexts logged are the real ones the generator saw, not a truncated or placeholder value.

Q: How do you cache LLM-judge results so an unchanged golden row does not pay for re-scoring every CI run?
A: Key the cache on a hash of the exact inputs that determine the score — judge model version, prompt/rubric, metric, and the sample's `user_input`/`response`/`contexts` — and reuse the stored score when the key is unchanged. DeepEval offers result caching via `deepeval test run --use-cache`/cached runs, and you can implement the same keying yourself. The hash must include the judge version so a deliberate judge upgrade correctly invalidates and re-scores.

Q: A golden-set eval that was green yesterday is red today with no code change. Triage order?
A: First check whether the judge or system model alias was silently updated upstream (compare pinned versions), since that shifts scores with no commit; second, confirm the golden-set version and thresholds are unchanged; third, look for non-determinism (is `temperature=0` and a seed set), then for rate-limit-induced partial failures lowering scores. The signature of upstream drift is a uniform shift across many rows with identical inputs. Pinning versions and dataset is what makes "no code change" actually mean no change.

Q: Why can two consecutive identical Ragas faithfulness runs still differ, and how do you bound the effect?
A: Even at `temperature=0`, hosted models are not perfectly deterministic, and claim decomposition can split the response into a different set of atomic claims between runs, changing the denominator and thus the score. Bound it by pinning the judge model version, fixing the prompt, averaging over a sufficiently large set, and setting thresholds with headroom rather than at the exact passing value. Treat the metric as having a small standard error, not a single exact number.

Q: How would you estimate the uncertainty on a 0.95 accuracy from a 200-row golden set?
A: Treat correctness as a binomial and compute a confidence interval — a normal approximation gives roughly `±1.96·sqrt(0.95·0.05/200) ≈ ±0.030`, so about `0.92`–`0.98`, and a Wilson interval is more accurate at the extremes. You can also bootstrap by resampling rows. The point is to report the interval (or at least n) so a reviewer knows a `0.01` move is within noise on this sample size.

Q: You need to detect a 2-point accuracy drop reliably. What does that imply for golden-set size?
A: Detecting a small effect requires enough rows that the metric's standard error is well under the effect — for proportions near `0.95`, the SE is `sqrt(p(1-p)/n)`, so reliably distinguishing `0.95` from `0.93` needs hundreds of rows, not dozens. A 30-row set has an SE around `0.04`, larger than the effect you want to catch, so the regression hides in noise. Either grow the set or accept you can only gate on larger drops.

Q: How do you keep a held-out test split stable across commits as the golden set grows?
A: Assign each row to dev/test deterministically by hashing a stable key (e.g. `sha256(document_id) % 100 < 20` for a 20% test split) rather than random shuffling, so adding new rows never reshuffles existing assignments. This keeps the test set's membership reproducible and prevents a previously-seen test case from leaking into dev later. Storing the split as a committed column is even more explicit and auditable.

Q: A prompt change passes the dev split and the held-out test split, but you still distrust it. What additional check guards against test-set leakage over time?
A: Even a held-out test set degrades into a dev set if you iterate against it repeatedly, so guard against "test-set burn-in" by reserving a fresh, never-touched holdout (or rotating in newly labelled production cases) for the final sign-off. Each time you peek at the test split to make a decision, you leak a little information into your process. Periodically refreshing the holdout from the review loop keeps the reported number honest.

Q: In stratified sampling for a Luxembourg golden set, name four strata that drive extraction variance and why each matters.
A: Domicile/regulatory regime (UCITS vs AIF) changes which fields and templates appear; language (EN/FR/DE) changes parsing and number formatting; document template/version changes table layout the extractor keys on; and share-class structure (single vs multi-class) changes whether a field is single- or multi-valued. Each is a dimension where the extractor can behave differently, so under-sampling any one leaves a blind spot. Stratifying ensures the set mirrors — or deliberately over-weights — the variation that breaks pipelines.

Q: How do you build a production-representative headline accuracy when your golden set over-samples FR-language and multi-class KIDs?
A: Compute per-stratum accuracy, then weight each stratum by its true production frequency (not its golden-set frequency) when forming the headline, so the over-sampled hard strata do not drag the number down. Keep the unweighted per-stratum scores alongside as the sensitive edge-case detector. Reporting both a re-weighted headline and raw per-stratum numbers prevents either view from misleading.

Q: A reviewer can only label 40 documents this sprint. How do you allocate them between dev and test and across strata for maximum eval value?
A: Allocate enough to the held-out test split to give it usable power (roughly the larger share if detection on the final gate matters most), stratify both splits so each contains the key fund types and at least a few edge cases, and spend marginal labels on the strata where you currently have least coverage or highest production volume. The goal is balanced strata in both splits, not a single big undifferentiated pool. Document the allocation so the resulting numbers are interpretable.

Q: What is "label-pipeline coupling" and how does it silently inflate accuracy?
A: It is when the labeller reads the model's output while creating the gold value (or labels with the same tool that runs the pipeline), so the gold drifts toward whatever the current model produces. Accuracy then looks high because you are comparing the model to a label it influenced, not to independent truth. The fix is blind labelling — label from the source document only, without seeing model output — and peer review of labels.

Q: Describe a concrete inter-annotator-agreement workflow before a golden set is trusted, including a number to compute.
A: Have two annotators independently label the same N documents from the source, compute percentage agreement and Cohen's kappa per field, and treat any field with low kappa (say below `0.6`) as under-specified — sharpen its labelling guide and re-label. Only once agreement clears your bar do you scale labelling to one annotator. Kappa, not raw agreement, is the number to track because it corrects for chance agreement on imbalanced fields.

Q: How do you align an LLM judge against humans in measurable terms, and what threshold lets you deploy it?
A: Take a held-out set of human-graded examples, run the judge over the same items, and compute agreement — accuracy or F1 for pass/fail judging, or a correlation (Spearman/Pearson) for graded scores — then deploy only when it clears a pre-set bar (e.g. ≥ `0.9` agreement on your data). Re-run this periodically to detect drift. The output is a defensible statement like "our judge agrees with reviewers `92%` of the time on Luxembourg KID cost fields".

Q: You suspect your pairwise judge has position bias. Describe the exact experiment and the decision rule.
A: Run every A-vs-B comparison twice, once as `(A,B)` and once swapped as `(B,A)`, and only record a decisive win when the judge picks the same underlying answer in both orders; order-dependent verdicts are counted as ties or discarded. The rate of order-flips quantifies the bias. If flips are frequent, the judge is unreliable for ranking and you either fix the prompt, randomise order with swap-checking, or fall back to reference-based scoring.

Q: Why prefer an independent judge model over self-judging, and what do you do when only one strong model is available?
A: Self-judging invites self-preference bias — models rate their own style favourably — inflating scores when the generator and judge are the same family. When only one strong model exists, you mitigate by aligning the judge against human labels to quantify and subtract the bias, using a strict rubric with `evaluation_steps`, and cross-checking a sample with a cheaper independent model or a non-LLM metric. Measured bias you can correct for is better than an unexamined assumption of neutrality.

Q: What two-tier CI design keeps PR feedback fast while still running the full golden-set eval, and where does each run?
A: A small "smoke" subset (a handful of representative, cheap cases) runs on every pull request in seconds as a required check, while the full golden-set eval — including expensive LLM-judge metrics — runs on merge to `main` or nightly. The smoke tier catches gross breakage immediately; the full tier provides the authoritative regression number without taxing every commit. Caching and concurrency keep the full tier's wall-clock and bill bounded.

Q: A GitHub Actions eval job fails only in CI with `RateLimitError`/`429` but passes locally. Most likely cause and fix?
A: CI runs the full suite with high concurrency against the provider, so parallel judge calls exceed the rate limit that your slower local run never hit. Lower Ragas `RunConfig(max_workers=...)` (or DeepEval's concurrency), add retries with backoff, or shard the suite, and ensure the API key/tier in CI has adequate quota. It is an infrastructure/concurrency problem, not a quality regression — the scores themselves are fine once the calls succeed.

Q: How would you store an API key for the judge model in GitHub Actions securely, and what is the failure if it is misconfigured?
A: Store it as an encrypted repository or environment secret and reference it as `${{ secrets.OPENAI_API_KEY }}`, exposing it to the step via `env:` so it never appears in logs. If it is missing or misnamed, the eval step fails with an authentication error (`401`) before any metric is scored, which looks like a red build but is a config failure, not a regression. Never echo the secret or commit it to the repo, since logs and history are retained.

Q: Why version the golden set in the repo as YAML/CSV rather than a notebook output or a mutable database table?
A: A repo-tracked YAML/CSV is diffable, code-reviewed, attributable (author + timestamp via Git), and pinned to each commit, so an eval result like "0.97 on commit `abc123`" is reproducible and auditable. A notebook output is not reviewed line-by-line and a database table is mutable, breaking the tie between a past score and the exact gold it was measured against. Reproducible, reviewable provenance is itself the regulated-industry control.

Q: A teammate edits three gold values in the same PR that changes the prompt, and accuracy jumps. Why is this a review red flag?
A: Mixing gold-label edits with a prompt change in one PR makes it impossible to attribute the accuracy jump — it could be a genuine improvement or the labels being bent to fit the new prompt. Gold-set changes should land in separate, independently-reviewed PRs with a stated rationale, so the test is never quietly moved to meet the system. This separation of "change the system" from "change the truth" is a control-integrity point an auditor will probe.

Q: What is the danger of synthetic testset references being generated by the same model you evaluate, and how do you break the circularity?
A: If the model under test (or its family) wrote the reference answers, its systematic errors are baked into your "ground truth", so the eval rewards the very mistakes it should catch — over-optimistic and circular. Break it by having a domain expert review and correct every synthetic reference before trusting it, and ideally generate references with a different, stronger model. The synthetic set is a draft golden set, never a final one.

Q: In Ragas synthetic generation, how do single-hop vs multi-hop queries stress different parts of your KID pipeline?
A: Single-hop queries (answerable from one node) mainly test whether retrieval finds the right chunk and the generator reads one fact correctly — good for field lookups like "what is the SRI". Multi-hop queries require traversing multiple connected nodes, stressing whether retrieval assembles evidence spread across sections and whether the generator synthesises it — e.g. relating costs across share classes. Mixing both ensures you are not only measuring easy single-field lookups.

Q: How do node extractors and relationship builders in Ragas affect the quality of generated multi-hop questions?
A: Extractors like `NERExtractor`/`KeyphraseExtractor` enrich each `Node` with entities/keyphrases, and relationship builders (e.g. a Jaccard-overlap builder) connect nodes sharing those properties; a denser, better-connected knowledge graph yields more and more meaningful multi-hop questions. Sparse extraction produces an under-connected graph and degenerate or trivial multi-hop queries. So tuning the transforms is what makes synthetic multi-hop tests actually exercise cross-document reasoning.

Q: An online faithfulness monitor drops sharply on live traffic with no deploy. What sequence of actions do you take?
A: Confirm it is not a logging artefact (are real `retrieved_contexts` still being captured), then check for an upstream model/alias change since reference-free metrics are sensitive to it, and re-run the golden-set eval to quantify the impact on field accuracy. If accuracy is hit, pin or roll back the model version and notify downstream consumers. The reference-free monitor is the early alarm; the golden-set re-run is the measurement that decides the response.

Q: Why are reference-free metrics the right tool for production monitoring but the wrong primary gate for release?
A: Production traffic has no gold labels, so only reference-free metrics (faithfulness, relevancy, structural pass rates) can be computed live as an early-warning alarm. But they do not measure correctness against truth — an answer can be grounded and on-topic yet contain the wrong cost figure — so they cannot certify a release. The release gate must be the reference-based field accuracy on a held-out golden set; reference-free metrics complement it, not replace it.

Q: Design the escalation rules that route a production extraction to full human review, combining structural and confidence signals.
A: Auto-escalate any extraction whose identifier fails a structural check (ISIN Luhn or LEI mod-97-10), whose SRI is outside `1`–`7`, whose model/verifier confidence is below a calibrated threshold, or which feeds a high-impact downstream value like a published NAV; everything else is covered by a random N% spot-check. Each rule is explicit and logged so escalation is auditable, not arbitrary. The flagged-100% part bounds worst-case risk while the random sample gives an unbiased accuracy estimate of the silent majority.

Q: Why must you calibrate a model-confidence threshold against observed error rates before using it to route reviews?
A: Raw model confidence (logprobs or a self-reported score) is often poorly calibrated — high confidence does not reliably mean low error — so an uncalibrated threshold either floods reviewers or lets errors through. You calibrate by binning predictions by confidence and measuring actual error per bin (a reliability check), then set the routing threshold where error exceeds your tolerance. Only a calibrated threshold makes "low confidence" a trustworthy escalation trigger rather than an arbitrary cut-off.

Q: Why record confirmations as well as corrections from human review, and what does recording only corrections do to your metrics?
A: Recording both gives an unbiased accuracy estimate and grows the golden set with verified-correct as well as verified-wrong examples; recording only corrections discards the "model was right" signal, inflating apparent error rate and skewing any retraining or threshold tuning toward failures. The full record is also the audit evidence that review occurred and what was decided. In a fund-administration context, the unbiased estimate and the audit trail both depend on logging every outcome.

Q: A model upgrade raises faithfulness and answer relevancy but field accuracy on the held-out golden set falls. Ship or hold, and why?
A: Hold — field accuracy is the reference-based task metric tied to the business outcome (a correct cost or ISIN reaching a transfer agent), and it regressed, while faithfulness and relevancy only confirm the prose is grounded and on-topic. A model can become more fluent yet extract a specific value less accurately. The whole point of designating field accuracy as primary is that it overrides improvements in supporting reference-free scores when they conflict.

Q: Tie the 6.8.2 control stack to a DORA-style operational-resilience review: what four artefacts demonstrate the control operates?
A: A versioned golden set in the repo (reproducible ground truth with provenance), a CI gate config with explicit per-field thresholds (the automated control), the judge-vs-human alignment record (evidence the measurement is trustworthy), and the human-review log with confirmations, corrections, timestamps, and reviewers (evidence the loop runs). Together they turn "we use an LLM" into a documented, measured, monitored, and evidenced control. Under DORA-style scrutiny it is the existence and operation of these artefacts — not the model choice — that is assessed.

Q: In Ragas, how do you turn your committed YAML/CSV goldens into something `evaluate()` can score, and what must each row contain?
A: Load each gold row into a `SingleTurnSample` (carrying `user_input`, `response` filled by running your pipeline, `retrieved_contexts`, and the `reference`/gold answer) and collect them into an `EvaluationDataset`, which you pass to `evaluate(dataset, metrics=[...])`. The required keys per row depend on the metrics chosen — a reference-based metric needs `reference`, faithfulness needs `retrieved_contexts` — so missing a key errors at scoring time. Building the dataset from the committed file (not a live query) is what keeps the eval pinned to the versioned gold.
