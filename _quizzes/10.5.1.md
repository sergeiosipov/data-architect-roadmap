# Phase 6 · 10.5.1 Master data management — 100 self-test questions

Q: What is master data, and how does it differ from transaction data?
A: Master data is the relatively stable, high-value reference about the core business entities — securities, funds, counterparties, legal entities — that transactions point at, whereas transaction data records events like trades, subscriptions, or NAV strikes. The distinction matters because one wrong master record (say a mispriced instrument's currency) silently corrupts every transaction that references it. In fund administration the security/fund/counterparty master is the single most reused dataset on the platform, so its quality has outsized blast radius.

Q: Define master data management (MDM) in one sentence.
A: MDM is the discipline — people, process, and technology — of creating and maintaining a single trusted "golden record" for each real-world entity by reconciling data from multiple sources. It combines matching duplicates, merging them, applying survivorship rules, and publishing the result. The goal is that everyone downstream consumes one authoritative version of "this fund" or "this instrument" rather than several conflicting copies.

Q: What is a "golden record" in MDM terms?
A: A golden record is the single, best-available, reconciled version of an entity assembled from all contributing sources after matching and survivorship are applied. It is not necessarily any one source verbatim; each attribute may be sourced from whichever feed wins under the survivorship rules. For a security master, the golden record might take the ISIN from one vendor, the issuer name from another, and the coupon from a third.

Q: Why does the architect care about MDM even when the MDM tool is bought rather than built?
A: Because the tool does not decide your match rules, survivorship precedence, or hierarchy model — those are design choices the architect owns regardless of vendor. A purchased Informatica or Semarchy stack will happily merge the wrong records or pick the wrong winner if you misconfigure it. Buying software does not buy the data-design judgement that prevents two vendor records for one instrument from double-counting a position.

Q: Name the four classic MDM architecture styles from DAMA-DMBOK.
A: The four styles are registry, consolidation, coexistence, and centralized (also called transaction). They sit on a spectrum from lightest-touch (registry, which only stores cross-references and leaves data in source systems) to heaviest (centralized, where the MDM hub is the authoring system of record). Each trades implementation cost and intrusiveness against the strength of the golden record it can guarantee.

Q: Describe the registry MDM style and its main trade-off.
A: In the registry style the hub stores only the keys and cross-references that link matching records across source systems, computing the golden record virtually on read rather than persisting it. It is the cheapest and least intrusive to deploy because sources keep authoring their own data. The trade-off is a thin, read-only golden record with no centrally cleansed values and limited ability to enforce data quality.

Q: Describe the consolidation MDM style.
A: In the consolidation style data is pulled from sources into the hub where it is matched and merged into a physical golden record, but the hub is read-only and does not push changes back to sources. It is well suited to feeding analytics and reporting from a clean master. Its limitation is that operational systems keep diverging because corrections live only in the hub, not in the originating applications.

Q: Describe the coexistence MDM style.
A: In coexistence the hub holds a physical golden record like consolidation, but changes can flow bidirectionally — the hub publishes cleansed data back to participating source systems so they converge over time. It is a pragmatic middle ground that improves source data without forcing a single authoring system. The cost is the synchronisation complexity of keeping the hub and multiple sources consistent.

Q: Describe the centralized (transaction) MDM style.
A: In the centralized style the MDM hub is the authoring system of record: master data is created and edited directly in the hub, which then distributes it to all consumers. It gives the strongest, most consistent golden record and the tightest governance. The cost is the highest, because you must re-platform authoring workflows and retrain users away from the old source systems.

Q: For a Luxembourg fund administrator, why might registry be defensible over centralized for a first MDM build?
A: Registry is defensible because it is low-cost, non-invasive, and leaves the existing transfer-agency and accounting systems as authors while still giving a cross-reference layer that ends the "which fund record is right" arguments. A regulated TA cannot easily re-platform its book-of-record systems, so a centralized hub that becomes the new authoring system is a large, risky change. The honest defence is that registry buys cross-referencing and de-duplication fast, with a migration path to coexistence later.

Q: What organisational cost rises sharply as you move from registry toward centralized MDM?
A: The cost of change management and stewardship rises sharply: centralized MDM relocates authoring into the hub, which means retraining users, rewiring operational workflows, and standing up governance to approve every master change. Registry barely touches the organisation, while centralized reshapes how people do daily work. Underestimating this people-cost is the classic reason centralized MDM programmes overrun.

Q: What is the difference between match and merge in MDM?
A: Matching is deciding whether two records refer to the same real-world entity; merging is combining the records judged to match into one consolidated entity. Match is the probabilistic or rule-based decision; merge is the mechanical consolidation that follows. Getting match wrong over-merges distinct instruments or fails to link true duplicates, and merge then propagates that error into the golden record.

Q: What is deterministic matching?
A: Deterministic matching links records using exact (or exact-after-standardisation) agreement on chosen keys — for example, two records match if their ISINs are identical. It is simple, fast, and fully explainable, which auditors like. Its weakness is brittleness: a single typo, formatting difference, or missing identifier causes a true match to be missed entirely.

Q: What is probabilistic matching, and when do you reach for it?
A: Probabilistic matching scores the overall likelihood that two records refer to the same entity by weighing agreement and disagreement across many attributes, rather than demanding exact key equality. You reach for it when reliable shared keys are absent or dirty — common with counterparty and issuer names that arrive spelled differently across vendors. It tolerates typos and partial information at the cost of needing thresholds and tuning.

Q: What is the Fellegi-Sunter model the intuition behind probabilistic linkage?
A: Fellegi-Sunter is the statistical framework that frames record linkage as a decision based on the ratio of two probabilities for each compared field: how likely that agreement is among true matches versus among true non-matches. Summing the evidence across fields yields a total match weight that maps to a match probability. Splink implements this model, which is why its tutorials reference Fellegi-Sunter directly.

Q: In Fellegi-Sunter, what is the `m` probability?
A: The `m` probability is the probability that a field agrees (falls into a given comparison level) given that the two records genuinely are a match. It essentially measures data quality and noise — for example, names might agree only 95% of the time among true matches because of typos and aliases. A high `m` means agreement on that field is expected when records truly match.

Q: In Fellegi-Sunter, what is the `u` probability?
A: The `u` probability is the probability that a field agrees given that the two records are NOT a match — i.e. agreement by pure coincidence. It reflects cardinality and rarity: a common surname coincides often, a rare ISIN almost never. A low `u` means coincidental agreement is unlikely, so agreement on that field is strong evidence of a true match.

Q: How is a per-field match weight computed from `m` and `u`?
A: The match weight for a comparison level is `log2(m/u)` — the base-2 logarithm of the ratio of the match probability to the non-match probability. Agreement on a high-`m`, low-`u` field (rare and reliable) yields a large positive weight, while disagreement contributes a negative weight. Because they are logs, the per-field weights are additive across independent fields.

Q: How does Splink turn a total match weight into a final match probability?
A: It applies the logistic transform `2^M / (1 + 2^M)`, where `M` is the summed match weight including the prior. A total match weight of 0 corresponds to a 50% match probability, and each additional unit of weight pushes the probability nonlinearly toward 1. This is why Splink can report both a raw match weight and an intuitive 0-to-1 probability.

Q: What is `lambda` (the prior) in the Fellegi-Sunter setup, and why does it matter?
A: `lambda` is the prior probability that any two randomly chosen records are a match, set by the overall overlap and duplicate rate of the data. It enters as a baseline component of the total match weight before any field evidence is added. If you mis-set it, every pair's probability is biased, so a tiny-overlap dataset with a high assumed prior will over-merge.

Q: What is blocking, and why is it essential in record linkage?
A: Blocking restricts the comparison to candidate pairs that share some blocking key (e.g. same issuer country, or same first three characters of name), instead of comparing every record against every other. It exists because the full cartesian product is quadratic and infeasible — a million records yield roughly 500 billion pairs. Blocking trades a small risk of missing matches that share no blocking key for an enormous reduction in compute.

Q: What is the risk if your blocking rules are too tight?
A: Too-tight blocking excludes true matches that happen not to agree on the blocking key, so they never even get scored and are silently lost as false negatives. For example, blocking on exact ISIN would drop any genuine duplicate where one feed lacks the ISIN. The defence is to use several complementary blocking rules so a pair only needs to satisfy one of them to be compared.

Q: What is the risk if your blocking rules are too loose?
A: Too-loose blocking generates an enormous number of candidate pairs, which blows up runtime and memory and can make the job intractable. A blocking rule that matches on a single common attribute may reproduce most of the cartesian product. Splink guidance is that a good blocking rule yields a blocked set where true matches are roughly between 1% and 99% of the pairs.

Q: How do multiple blocking rules combine in a linkage job?
A: They combine as a union: a pair becomes a candidate if it satisfies ANY one of the blocking rules, not all of them. This lets each rule catch matches the others miss — one rule blocks on name, another on identifier, another on date — so a typo in one field is rescued by agreement in another. The deduplicated union of all candidate pairs is then scored.

Q: What is a matching threshold and how do you choose it?
A: A threshold is the match-weight or match-probability cut-off above which a pair is auto-accepted as the same entity. You choose it by examining the score distribution and labelled examples, balancing precision (avoid wrong merges) against recall (avoid missed merges). In a fund master you bias toward precision, because an erroneous merge that double-counts a position is costlier than leaving two records unlinked.

Q: Why is it common to use two thresholds rather than one?
A: Two thresholds create three zones: auto-merge above the high threshold, auto-reject below the low threshold, and a clerical-review band in between for human stewards. This concentrates scarce steward effort on genuinely ambiguous pairs rather than the obvious ones. It is the practical bridge between fully automated matching and manual review.

Q: How does Splink estimate the `u` probabilities by default?
A: Splink estimates `u` using random sampling: it samples records, generates pairwise comparisons among them, and treats those pairs as non-matches because random pairs from a large dataset are almost never true matches. The `max_pairs` parameter controls sample size — more pairs give more accurate `u` values, with at least ten million recommended for large data. The estimation can be made reproducible by setting the `seed` parameter.

Q: How does Splink typically estimate `m` probabilities?
A: Splink estimates `m` with an expectation-maximisation (EM) procedure, run on records blocked by a rule so that the blocked set is enriched with likely matches. EM iteratively assigns match probabilities and re-estimates parameters until they converge. By default `m` parameters are estimated for the comparisons not fixed by the blocking rule used in that EM session, which is why several EM sessions on different blocking rules are combined.

Q: What is survivorship in MDM?
A: Survivorship is the set of rules that decide, attribute by attribute, which source value wins and is written to the golden record when matched records disagree. It is applied after matching and merging have grouped the duplicates. Good survivorship is per-attribute and justified: the ISIN might come from one vendor while the issuer domicile comes from another, each by an explicit rule.

Q: Give three common kinds of survivorship rule.
A: Common rules include source precedence (trust Bloomberg over Refinitiv for pricing), recency (take the most recently updated value), and completeness or quality (prefer the non-null, longest, or validated value). Others include most-frequent-value (voting) and rules keyed to data type. The architect chooses per attribute, because the "best" source differs by field.

Q: Why must survivorship be defined per attribute rather than per record?
A: Because no single source is best at everything: one vendor may have the most reliable identifiers while another has better corporate-action timing or better names. Picking one whole record as the winner throws away the better attributes scattered across the others. Per-attribute survivorship assembles a golden record that is better than any single contributing source.

Q: When a steward asks "why did this golden record show this issuer name?", what must the system be able to tell them?
A: It must name the survivorship rule that selected that value and the source it came from — for example, "issuer name from Vendor A because Vendor A has source precedence for descriptive fields." Provenance per attribute is the explainability that makes MDM auditable. Without it, a golden attribute is an unexplainable assertion, which fails in a regulated context.

Q: Why is "most recent value wins" a dangerous default survivorship rule on its own?
A: Because the most recent update is not always the most correct — a recent erroneous vendor feed can overwrite a long-standing correct value, and timestamps may reflect load time rather than true change time. Recency also rewards whichever system writes most often, not which is most accurate. It is usually combined with source precedence and quality checks rather than used alone.

Q: What is hierarchy management in MDM?
A: Hierarchy management maintains the parent-child relationships among master entities — which sub-funds belong to which umbrella fund, which share classes belong to which sub-fund, and which subsidiaries roll up to which issuer. The hierarchy is master data in its own right, not just attributes. Getting it wrong mis-aggregates positions and exposures up the tree.

Q: Sketch the typical fund hierarchy the master must model.
A: A common Luxembourg structure is umbrella fund (e.g. a SICAV) → sub-fund (compartment) → share class. The umbrella is the legal entity, each sub-fund is a separately-invested pool with its own NAV, and each share class differs by currency, fees, distribution policy, or investor type. The master must keep these levels distinct because NAV is struck per sub-fund and reported per share class.

Q: Why is the share-class level critical to model correctly in the fund hierarchy?
A: Because share classes of the same sub-fund share the underlying portfolio but differ in fee load, currency hedging, and distribution, so their per-share NAVs and ISINs differ even though the sub-fund NAV is one number. Collapsing classes into the sub-fund would misreport investor-level holdings and fees. Each share class typically carries its own ISIN, which is the identifier investors actually transact on.

Q: What is an issuer tree, and why does the master maintain one?
A: An issuer tree models the corporate ownership hierarchy — ultimate parent, intermediate holding companies, and issuing subsidiaries — so exposures can be aggregated to the legal entity that actually bears the risk. The master maintains it because risk and concentration limits apply at the parent level, not per issuing subsidiary. Without it, two bonds from related subsidiaries look like unrelated exposures.

Q: What is a stewardship workflow in MDM?
A: A stewardship workflow is the human-in-the-loop process by which data stewards review and resolve cases the automated rules cannot confidently decide — chiefly the ambiguous matches in the clerical-review band. It includes review queues, approve/reject actions, and an audit trail of who decided what and why. It exists because no match algorithm is perfect and some decisions need domain judgement.

Q: What populates a steward's review queue?
A: The review queue is populated by candidate pairs whose match score falls between the auto-reject and auto-merge thresholds — the genuinely uncertain ones. It may also include flagged survivorship conflicts or new sources pending mapping. Routing only the ambiguous cases to humans keeps the queue small enough to be worked, instead of asking stewards to review every pair.

Q: Why is recording the steward's decision (and reason) part of the workflow, not optional?
A: Because the decision becomes training data and an audit record: labelled match/non-match outcomes feed back to improve thresholds and parameters, and the reasoning satisfies regulators who ask why two records were merged. In a fund context an unexplained merge that later proves wrong is a control failure. Capturing who/when/why turns ad-hoc judgement into a governed, repeatable process.

Q: What does "MDM as a publisher, not a silo" mean as an integration pattern?
A: It means the MDM hub should actively publish the golden record and cross-references out to every consumer — the lakehouse, reporting marts, operational systems — rather than being a separate database people must remember to query. If MDM is a silo, downstream systems keep their own divergent copies and the golden record's value evaporates. Publishing (events, feeds, or shared tables) is what makes one master actually authoritative everywhere.

Q: What is a cross-reference (xref) table and why publish it alongside the golden record?
A: A cross-reference table maps each source record's key to the golden-record key it contributed to, so consumers can translate any vendor identifier into the canonical one and trace lineage back to sources. You publish it with the golden record because downstream systems still receive data keyed by source identifiers and need to resolve them. It is also the audit backbone for proving how the master was assembled.

Q: How should MDM publish to a lakehouse so consumers stay current?
A: Publish the golden record and xref as governed, versioned tables (or change feeds) in the lakehouse on a defined cadence, with the master as the upstream owner and the lakehouse tables as read-only downstream copies. Consumers then read one place rather than re-deriving the master. Treating the published table as an output contract, versioned and documented, prevents silent drift between the hub and what analysts query.

Q: What goes wrong if MDM is treated as a silo that systems pull from on demand?
A: Systems that forget to query it, or cache it and never refresh, fall back to stale or local copies, so the "single" master fragments into many again. Pull-only also gives no signal when the master changes, so downstream data ages silently. The publisher pattern fixes this by pushing updates, making consumption the default rather than an extra step someone may skip.

Q: What is the identifier cascade in a security master?
A: The identifier cascade is the ordered preference of identifiers used to match and key instruments — typically ISIN first, then a fallback such as FIGI or CUSIP/SEDOL, then an internal surrogate identifier when no public one exists. The cascade matters because not every instrument or every vendor record carries every identifier. It defines a deterministic path from the most authoritative public key down to an internal one.

Q: Why can't a security master rely on ISIN alone as the matching key?
A: Because some instruments lack an ISIN (certain OTC derivatives, loans, or not-yet-issued securities), an ISIN can be reused or reassigned over time, and a single ISIN can correspond to instruments that trade on multiple venues. Relying on ISIN alone misses real instruments and conflates venue-specific records. The cascade adds FIGI and internal identifiers precisely to cover these gaps.

Q: What is an ISIN and what does its structure tell you?
A: An ISIN (ISO 6166) is a 12-character International Securities Identification Number: a two-letter country/issuance prefix, a nine-character national security identifier (NSIN), and one check digit. The check digit (a Luhn-style modulus-10 computation) lets you validate an ISIN locally before trusting it. Validating the check digit is a cheap first defence against a transposed or mistyped ISIN entering the master.

Q: What is a FIGI and why include it in the identifier cascade?
A: A FIGI (Financial Instrument Global Identifier) is an open, twelve-character alphanumeric identifier that, unlike ISIN, is issued at the venue/instrument level and is freely redistributable. Including it gives a fallback key for instruments without an ISIN and a way to distinguish the same security trading on different exchanges. Its openness avoids the licensing constraints that some vendor identifiers carry.

Q: What is an internal surrogate identifier and why does the master mint one?
A: An internal surrogate is a master-owned, never-reused key assigned to every golden record regardless of which public identifiers exist. The master mints it so there is always a stable join key even for instruments that lack or later change their ISIN/FIGI. It also insulates downstream systems from external identifier churn, because the internal key never moves once assigned.

Q: What is vendor precedence in a security master, and give the canonical example?
A: Vendor precedence is the survivorship ordering that says, per attribute, which data vendor to trust when they disagree — the canonical example being a choice between Bloomberg and Refinitiv (LSEG) for fields like pricing, ratings, or descriptive terms. You might trust one vendor for prices and the other for corporate-action timing. The point is that precedence is set deliberately and per field, not "whichever loaded last."

Q: How does corporate-action survivorship differ from ordinary attribute survivorship?
A: Corporate-action survivorship governs which vendor's version of an event — a split, merger, coupon change, or symbol change — is applied to the golden instrument, and it is time-sensitive because actions have effective dates. Getting it wrong applies the action to the wrong record or at the wrong time, corrupting every downstream position and report. It often needs both source precedence and effective-date alignment, not a simple latest-value rule.

Q: Why does the lesson say "every NAV error story ends at reference data"?
A: Because the NAV calculation multiplies positions by prices and applies corporate actions, all of which are keyed on the security and fund master — so a wrong identifier, price source, or applied action surfaces as a wrong NAV. The visible symptom is a pricing error, but the root cause is usually a master/reference-data defect. This is why fund administrators invest heavily in security-master quality.

Q: Concretely, how can a match/merge error double-count a fund position?
A: If two vendor records for the same instrument fail to match, the master keeps them as two distinct golden records, and a holding reported against each is summed as if there were two different securities — inflating the position. The portfolio then over-states exposure and the NAV per share is wrong. The fix is correct matching so both vendor records resolve to one golden instrument keyed once.

Q: What is the danger of over-merging two genuinely different instruments?
A: Over-merging collapses two distinct securities into one golden record, so positions, prices, and corporate actions intended for one get applied to both. A corporate action on the wrong golden record then corrupts every downstream report that references it. Because the master is so widely reused, an over-merge propagates the error platform-wide, which is why fund masters bias toward precision.

Q: A counterparty appears as "Deutsche Bank AG", "DEUTSCHE BANK A.G.", and "Deutsche Bk AG" across three feeds. Which matching approach fits and why?
A: Probabilistic matching fits, because there is no shared exact key and the strings differ by case, punctuation, and abbreviation that deterministic equality would reject. Fellegi-Sunter scoring with name comparisons (and ideally a shared LEI as a strong agreement field) would link them above threshold. You would standardise case and punctuation first, then let the probabilistic model handle the residual variation.

Q: What is an LEI and why is it a powerful field in counterparty matching?
A: An LEI (Legal Entity Identifier, ISO 17442) is a globally unique 20-character code identifying a legal entity in financial transactions. It is powerful in matching because, when present, it is a rare, authoritative, low-`u` field — agreement on LEI is near-conclusive evidence two counterparty records are the same entity. It also links to issuer-tree data via the LEI relationship records that capture parent ownership.

Q: How does the LEI relationship data help with issuer-tree (hierarchy) management?
A: The Global LEI system publishes "level 2" relationship records that declare an entity's direct and ultimate accounting parents, which seed the issuer ownership tree without you guessing it. Using that data, the master can roll subsidiary exposures up to the ultimate parent for concentration limits. It turns hierarchy management from manual research into reconciliation against an authoritative external source.

Q: Why is Postgres a reasonable choice for the MDM hub in a hands-on build?
A: Postgres gives transactional integrity, rich SQL for expressing survivorship logic, constraints to enforce the cross-reference relationships, and easy ingestion of multiple vendor feeds into staging tables. It is free and ubiquitous, so the design choices are portable to commercial hubs later. The hub's job here is to hold sources, candidate matches, and golden records — all of which relational tables model cleanly.

Q: At a high level, what are the four steps of the lesson's hands-on MDM build?
A: First, ingest two overlapping vendor feeds with conflicting attributes into Postgres staging. Second, run Splink match/merge with explicit blocking rules and tuned thresholds. Third, express survivorship rules in SQL to assemble the golden record. Fourth, publish the golden record plus a cross-reference table to the lakehouse for consumers.

Q: When ingesting two overlapping vendor feeds, why land them in staging before matching?
A: Staging preserves each source verbatim with its provenance (which vendor, which load, which timestamp) before any transformation, so you can always trace a golden value back to its origin and re-run matching after rule changes. It also separates ingestion failures from matching failures. Matching directly on transformed-in-place data would destroy the lineage that survivorship and audit depend on.

Q: How would you express a source-precedence survivorship rule in SQL?
A: You assign each source a numeric priority, then for each entity pick the attribute value from the row with the best (lowest) priority that is non-null — commonly with a window function such as `row_number() over (partition by golden_id order by source_priority)` filtered to the first row per attribute, or `coalesce` across sources in precedence order. The result is one chosen value per attribute with the rule encoded as the ordering. Keeping the chosen source alongside the value gives the explainability stewards need.

Q: Why must the published golden record carry per-attribute lineage to its source?
A: Because regulators and stewards must be able to ask of any field "where did this come from and why," and only per-attribute lineage answers that after a multi-source merge. It also lets you debug a bad value by isolating which feed and which rule produced it. Lineage is the difference between a defensible golden record and an unaccountable one.

Q: A new vendor feed is onboarded and golden records suddenly fragment into duplicates. What is the first thing to check?
A: First check the blocking rules and standardisation for the new feed: if its identifiers or name formats differ, true matches may not share any blocking key and so are never compared, producing duplicate golden records. Confirm the new source's keys are being standardised the same way as existing feeds. The symptom (fragmentation) almost always traces to matches that were never even scored.

Q: After tuning, your match recall improved but several distinct bonds now share one golden record. What likely changed and how do you respond?
A: You likely lowered the merge threshold (or weakened a comparison) too far, so borderline non-matches now exceed the auto-merge cut-off and over-merge. Respond by raising the high threshold or widening the clerical-review band so those borderline pairs go to a steward instead of auto-merging. In a fund master, precision usually outranks recall, so err back toward fewer automatic merges.

Q: A steward asks you to justify a specific merge. What artefacts do you show?
A: You show the match weight and the per-comparison contributions — which fields agreed, the `m`/`u`-derived weight each contributed, and the total that crossed the threshold — ideally via Splink's waterfall chart. You also show the blocking rule that brought the pair together. Together these explain both why the pair was even compared and why the evidence summed above the merge line.

Q: A golden record shows a clearly wrong issuer domicile. Where do you investigate first?
A: First inspect the survivorship rule for the domicile attribute and the source it selected: the wrong value usually means precedence chose a feed whose domicile is stale or erroneous. Check whether the winning source's value is itself wrong (a source-data problem) or whether the rule picked the wrong source (a rule problem). The per-attribute lineage tells you which of the two it is.

Q: Why is standardisation (cleansing) a prerequisite step before matching?
A: Because matching compares attribute values, and unstandardised values — mixed case, punctuation, legal-suffix variants, leading zeros on identifiers — make true matches look different and inflate disagreement. Standardising first (uppercase, strip punctuation, normalise "AG"/"A.G.", validate identifier formats) raises the signal the matcher works on. Skipping it forces the probabilistic model to absorb noise it should never have seen.

Q: What is the difference between a data dictionary and the MDM golden record?
A: A data dictionary describes the structure and meaning of fields (metadata about columns), whereas the golden record is the reconciled instance data for a specific entity. The dictionary tells you what "issuer_domicile" means; the golden record tells you that this particular bond's issuer domicile is Luxembourg. MDM produces trusted values; the dictionary documents what the values represent.

Q: How do reference data and master data relate, and why are they often discussed together?
A: Reference data is the controlled code lists (currencies, countries, MICs) that master data attributes draw their permitted values from; master data is the richer entity records (instruments, funds) that consume those codes. They are discussed together because both are non-transactional, low-volume, high-impact data governed centrally. A wrong currency code (reference) corrupts a fund master record just as a bad match (master) does.

Q: Why does DAMA-DMBOK place reference and master data in the same chapter (ch. 10)?
A: Because they share the same core problem — establishing and maintaining a single authoritative source of shared, non-transactional, widely-reused data — and the same governance, versioning, and distribution disciplines. The mechanics differ (code-list versioning vs entity match/merge) but the architecture goals overlap. Treating them as one domain avoids two separate, inconsistent governance regimes.

Q: What is the role of effective dating in a security master?
A: Effective dating records when each value was valid, so the master can answer "what was this instrument's coupon on a past date" and apply corporate actions at their correct effective time rather than overwriting history. It is essential for restating a NAV as-of a prior date and for auditing. Without it, a corporate action overwrites the prior state and you lose the ability to reproduce historical reports.

Q: How does the SCD Type 2 pattern relate to master data quality?
A: SCD Type 2 keeps full change history by closing the old row and inserting a new one with validity dates rather than updating in place, which is exactly how a master preserves the history needed for as-of queries and audit. Applying it to golden records means you never lose the prior trusted value when survivorship picks a new one. It is the storage discipline that makes effective dating real.

Q: What distinguishes a "trusted source" from the golden record itself?
A: A trusted source is a high-quality input feed you weight heavily in survivorship, but it is still one contributing source; the golden record is the assembled result that may take different attributes from different trusted sources. Even your most trusted vendor can be beaten field-by-field. Conflating "trusted source" with "golden record" leads people to skip survivorship and just copy one feed.

Q: Why is governance, not technology, usually the binding constraint in an MDM programme?
A: Because the hard, contested decisions — who owns which entity, which source wins each attribute, who approves changes — are organisational, and no tool resolves them for you. Technology can match and merge once those rules exist; without agreed ownership and stewardship, the hub merges according to nobody's agreed intent. MDM programmes stall on data ownership disputes far more often than on software.

Q: What is the relationship between blocking rules and the comparisons (comparison columns) in Splink?
A: Blocking rules decide which record pairs are even generated for scoring, while comparisons define how each field is evaluated (its levels and `m`/`u` weights) once a pair exists. They are orthogonal: a pair must first survive blocking, then it is scored across all comparisons. Confusing them leads people to put match logic in blocking rules, which silently drops pairs instead of scoring them.

Q: Why can the same blocking rule used for EM training bias the `m` estimate for that field?
A: Because records blocked on a field necessarily agree on it, so within that blocked set there is no variation to learn that field's `m` from — its agreement is forced. That is why Splink, by default, does not estimate `m` for the comparison fixed by the blocking rule in a given EM session. You run multiple EM sessions blocking on different fields and combine them so every field's `m` is estimated from data where it could vary.

Q: What is term-frequency adjustment in probabilistic matching, and why does it matter for names?
A: Term-frequency adjustment varies the match weight by how common a specific value is, so agreement on a rare value (surname "Schwarzenegger") counts far more than agreement on a common one ("Smith"). It matters for names and issuer terms because treating all agreements equally over-credits coincidental matches on common values. It refines the single `u` per field into a value-sensitive one.

Q: How would you decide between deterministic and probabilistic matching for ISIN-keyed instruments versus name-keyed counterparties?
A: For ISIN-keyed instruments, deterministic matching on the (check-digit-validated) ISIN is appropriate where ISINs are present, because exact agreement on a clean unique key is conclusive and explainable. For name-keyed counterparties lacking reliable shared keys, probabilistic matching is needed to absorb spelling and formatting variation. Real masters use a hybrid: deterministic on strong identifiers, probabilistic to rescue the records those identifiers miss.

Q: What does "blocking on first three characters of name plus issuer country" achieve?
A: It groups together records that are plausibly the same entity (similar name start, same country) so only those are compared, slashing the pair count while keeping most true matches in scope. It tolerates differences later in the name because only the prefix is blocked. Combined with other blocking rules, it catches matches that pure exact-name blocking would miss.

Q: Why publish the cross-reference table to the lakehouse and not just the golden record?
A: Because downstream systems and historical data are keyed by source vendor identifiers, and only the xref lets consumers translate those into the canonical golden key and back. It also lets analysts audit which source records fed each golden record without access to the hub. The golden record answers "what is true"; the xref answers "which inputs produced it."

Q: How does MDM reduce the operational risk of a NAV restatement?
A: By ensuring positions and prices reference one correctly-matched, correctly-survived instrument record, MDM removes the double-counting and wrong-corporate-action errors that are common root causes of NAV errors. Fewer master defects mean fewer NAVs struck on bad reference data, and thus fewer restatements. The golden record is, in effect, a control on the most reused input to the NAV.

Q: What makes a corporate action particularly dangerous to apply to the wrong golden record?
A: A corporate action (split, merger, redenomination) transforms the instrument's economics, so applying it to the wrong golden record changes quantities or prices for securities it never touched, and every position and NAV referencing that record is corrupted. Because the master fans out platform-wide, the error is not local — it propagates to all downstream reports. This is why corporate-action survivorship and correct matching are treated as high-risk controls.

Q: In coexistence MDM, what new failure mode appears that consolidation avoids?
A: Coexistence pushes cleansed values back to source systems, introducing bidirectional synchronisation that can conflict — a source edits a value at the same time the hub writes a corrected one, raising a reconciliation/looping risk. Consolidation, being read-only downstream, never writes back and so avoids this. The price of converging the sources is managing that two-way consistency.

Q: Why might a fund administrator ultimately prefer coexistence over pure consolidation despite the extra complexity?
A: Because consolidation leaves the operational TA and accounting systems diverging forever (corrections live only in the hub), whereas coexistence feeds corrections back so the books-of-record actually improve. For a regulated administrator, having the operational systems carry clean data — not just the analytics hub — reduces downstream errors at source. The synchronisation cost buys genuinely better operational data.

Q: What is the danger of letting each downstream system keep its own "local master"?
A: Local masters drift apart, so the same fund or instrument is described differently in each system, and reports that should agree do not — two "AUM" figures, three "domiciles." Reconciling them after the fact is expensive and never finishes. The whole point of MDM is to replace many local masters with one published authoritative source.

Q: How do match weights help calibrate, not just apply, your thresholds?
A: Because the distribution of total match weights across candidate pairs reveals where true matches and non-matches separate, you set thresholds where that separation is cleanest rather than picking a number blindly. Labelled examples let you measure precision and recall at candidate cut-offs. The weights turn threshold-setting into evidence-based calibration instead of guesswork.

Q: What is the consequence of mis-estimating `u` for a high-cardinality identifier field?
A: If `u` is set too high for a near-unique identifier, agreement on it is under-credited and true matches score too low (missed merges); if `u` is too low, coincidental agreement is over-credited and you over-merge. Because identifier fields carry most of the matching signal, a `u` error there dominates the outcome. Random-sampling estimation with a large `max_pairs` is meant to get this right automatically.

Q: Why does the lesson ask you to "defend registry-vs-centralized in writing" rather than just pick one?
A: Because the right MDM style depends on context — existing systems, regulatory constraints, budget, and appetite for change — and the architect's job is to reason about that trade-off explicitly, not apply a default. Writing the defence forces you to weigh cost, intrusiveness, and golden-record strength for this specific administrator. It is the design-judgement deliverable the tool can never produce for you.

Q: How does effective dating interact with corporate-action survivorship?
A: Effective dating lets the master apply each corporate action at its correct as-of date and keep the pre- and post-action states, so a NAV struck for a past date uses the instrument as it was then. Corporate-action survivorship decides which vendor's version of the action (and its effective date) to apply. Together they ensure the right action is applied at the right time from the right source.

Q: What is a "comparison level" in Splink, and give an example for a name field?
A: A comparison level is one of the mutually exclusive buckets a field comparison is graded into, each with its own `m` and `u`. For a name you might have levels: exact match, close fuzzy match (e.g. Jaro-Winkler above a cutoff), partial match, and all-other (no match). Graded levels capture that "nearly the same" is stronger evidence than "completely different" but weaker than exact.

Q: Why are several comparison levels better than a binary agree/disagree comparison?
A: Because real data has degrees of similarity — a one-character typo is much more match-like than two unrelated strings — and a binary comparison throws that information away. Graded levels assign intermediate weights, so a near-miss contributes appropriately rather than being scored as a full disagreement. This materially improves matching on noisy fields like names and addresses.

Q: When two feeds disagree on an instrument's currency, why is this especially high-risk?
A: Because currency drives valuation: a position priced in the wrong currency mis-values the holding and therefore the sub-fund's NAV, and currency is also a reference-data code (ISO 4217) with platform-wide reach. The survivorship rule for currency must be conservative and ideally cross-checked, because the error is both subtle and large. It is a textbook case of small reference data with outsized blast radius.

Q: How does publishing MDM as events (rather than a nightly table dump) help consumers?
A: Event publishing pushes each golden-record change to subscribers as it happens, so consumers stay near-current and can react to a corrected instrument before the next NAV run instead of waiting for a batch. It also makes the master the active source of truth rather than a thing systems remember to re-pull. The trade-off is the added infrastructure to publish and consume events reliably.

Q: What is the minimal evidence set proving "the code/master is consumed everywhere from one place"?
A: You show the published golden-record and xref tables in the lakehouse, the lineage from each consuming report back to those tables, and the absence of competing local copies feeding the same reports. Ideally a query or catalog lineage confirms each consumer reads the published master. The proof is that no report derives the entity independently of the published source.

Q: Why is a check-digit validation worth running on every ISIN before it enters the master?
A: Because the ISIN's trailing check digit (modulus-10 over the preceding characters) catches most single-character typos and digit transpositions locally, before a malformed identifier can become a wrong matching key. Letting an invalid ISIN in risks either failing to match a real instrument or matching the wrong one. It is a cheap, deterministic gate that prevents a class of silent master errors.

Q: How would you treat instruments that legitimately have no ISIN (e.g. some OTC derivatives) in the master?
A: You fall through the identifier cascade: try FIGI or another available public identifier, and if none exists, assign and key on the internal surrogate identifier so the record is still uniquely tracked. Matching for these relies more on attribute-based probabilistic logic than on a shared public key. The master must not assume ISIN presence, or these instruments become unmanageable.

Q: Why is it risky to use a vendor identifier (rather than an internal key) as the master's primary key?
A: Because vendor identifiers can change, be re-licensed, or be discontinued, and keying the master on one couples your entire platform to that vendor's decisions and licensing. If the vendor reassigns or retires a code, your join keys break across every downstream system. The internal surrogate insulates the platform from external identifier churn and vendor lock-in.

Q: What is survivorship's relationship to data quality scoring of sources?
A: Quality scores can directly feed survivorship: instead of fixed source precedence, you let the highest-quality (most complete, most recently validated, conformance-checked) value win per attribute. This makes survivorship adaptive to actual feed quality rather than a static assumption that one vendor is always best. The risk is needing reliable, current quality metrics, or the scoring itself becomes the weak link.

Q: Why does the architect document survivorship rules as first-class artefacts, not just code?
A: Because survivorship encodes contested business decisions (which vendor wins, why) that stewards, auditors, and future engineers must understand and challenge, and buried code is neither reviewable nor governable. Documenting the rules makes them subject to approval and change control like any other policy. It also lets you explain a golden value without reverse-engineering SQL.

Q: Summarise how the seven MDM concerns — styles, match/merge, survivorship, hierarchy, stewardship, integration, and security-master specifics — fit together for a fund administrator.
A: You pick an MDM style sized to the administrator's appetite for change (often registry or coexistence), use match/merge (deterministic on ISIN/LEI, probabilistic on names) to link vendor feeds, apply per-attribute survivorship with explicit vendor precedence and corporate-action handling to assemble the golden record, and model the fund and issuer hierarchies so aggregations are correct. Stewardship resolves the ambiguous matches with an audit trail, and the integration pattern publishes the golden record plus cross-reference to the lakehouse so the master is authoritative everywhere. Together they ensure the most reused data behind every NAV is single, correct, and explainable.
