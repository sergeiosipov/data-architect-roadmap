# Phase 6 · 9.8.1 Synthetic & test data — 100 self-test questions

Q: What is synthetic data in the data-engineering sense?
A: Synthetic data is artificially generated data that imitates the structure, types, and statistical properties of real data without being a direct copy of it. It is used so developers and testers can work on realistic-looking datasets without exposing the real personal or confidential records underneath. In a fund-administration context, a synthetic investor table lets you build and test pipelines without putting genuine KYC data in a dev environment.

Q: Why does GDPR make synthetic data an engineering concern rather than just a legal one?
A: GDPR's data-minimization principle (Article 5(1)(c)) says you should process only the personal data you actually need, and dev/test rarely needs real personal data. Synthetic data is the engineering answer to that requirement: it lets you populate non-production environments with data that carries no real personal information. The architect owns this because the choice of how to generate it directly determines whether the privacy obligation is actually met.

Q: What are the two broad families of synthesis covered in this lesson?
A: The two families are rule-based synthesis and fitted (statistical) synthesis. Rule-based synthesis produces values from rules or generators you specify (for example Faker producing fake names and addresses), while fitted synthesis trains a statistical model on real data and samples new records from it (for example SDV's copula or CTGAN models). Rule-based is simpler and carries no leakage from real data; fitted preserves real correlations but must be checked for privacy leakage.

Q: What is Faker and what category of synthesis does it represent?
A: Faker is a Python library that generates fake but realistically formatted values such as names, addresses, emails, phone numbers, and dates. It represents rule-based synthesis: it does not learn from your real data, it just fabricates values from built-in providers and locales. Because it never sees the real data, Faker output cannot leak real records, but it also cannot reproduce the real statistical relationships between columns.

Q: What is SDV (the Synthetic Data Vault)?
A: SDV is an open-source Python library for fitted synthesis of tabular data, covering single-table, multi-table, and sequential data. You give it your real data plus metadata, it trains a statistical synthesizer, and then samples new rows that mimic the real distributions and relationships. It is the primary fitted-synthesis tool used in this lesson, paired with SDMetrics for evaluating the result.

Q: What is the core difference between rule-based and fitted synthesis?
A: Rule-based synthesis generates values from rules or generators you define, with no dependence on the real data's actual distribution, while fitted synthesis trains a model on the real data and then samples from the learned distribution. Rule-based gives you plausible-looking but statistically arbitrary data; fitted gives you data that reproduces the real correlations and marginals. The tradeoff is leakage: fitted synthesis can memorize real records, rule-based cannot.

Q: When is Faker the right tool rather than SDV?
A: Faker is right when you only need realistically formatted placeholder values and do not care about reproducing real statistical relationships — for example seeding a schema with names, emails, and IBANs so the UI renders and joins work. It is also the safest choice when you must guarantee zero leakage, because Faker never touches the real data. Reach for SDV only when downstream tests genuinely depend on the data's distributions and inter-column correlations.

Q: What does "fitted synthesis" mean mechanically?
A: Fitted synthesis means a generative model's parameters are estimated ("fitted") from your real data, after which you sample new records from that fitted model. The model learns the marginal distribution of each column and the joint relationships between columns, so sampled rows look statistically like the originals. Examples in SDV include Gaussian copula models and deep models like CTGAN and TVAE.

Q: What is a copula, in the SDV `GaussianCopulaSynthesizer` sense?
A: A copula is a statistical construction that separates each column's individual (marginal) distribution from the dependency structure linking columns together. The `GaussianCopulaSynthesizer` models each column's marginal, transforms it to a standard normal space, fits a multivariate Gaussian to capture correlations, and then inverts the process when sampling. This lets it reproduce both the per-column shapes and the pairwise correlations of the real data.

Q: What is CTGAN and how does it differ from a copula model?
A: CTGAN (Conditional Tabular GAN) is a deep generative adversarial network designed specifically for tabular data with mixed continuous and categorical columns. Unlike a Gaussian copula, which assumes a tractable parametric dependency structure, CTGAN learns the distribution through adversarial training between a generator and a discriminator. It can capture more complex, non-linear, multi-modal relationships but is slower to train and more prone to overfitting on small datasets.

Q: Which SDV synthesizer would you typically try first, and why?
A: You typically try `GaussianCopulaSynthesizer` first because it is fast, deterministic enough to reason about, and produces good fidelity for many tabular datasets. It is a sensible baseline before reaching for heavier deep models like `CTGANSynthesizer` or `TVAESynthesizer`. You escalate to a deep synthesizer only when the copula's fidelity, measured with SDMetrics, proves inadequate for your use case.

Q: Name the main single-table synthesizer classes in current SDV.
A: The main single-table synthesizers are `GaussianCopulaSynthesizer`, `CTGANSynthesizer`, `TVAESynthesizer`, and `CopulaGANSynthesizer`. The copula one is statistical and fast; CTGAN and TVAE are deep neural models; CopulaGAN combines copula preprocessing with a GAN. All share the same `fit()` and `sample()` interface, so you can swap them and re-evaluate quality without rewriting your pipeline.

Q: What is the `TVAESynthesizer`?
A: `TVAESynthesizer` is SDV's deep synthesizer based on a Variational Autoencoder adapted for tabular data (the "T" is for tabular). Like CTGAN it learns complex distributions through a neural network, but it uses an encoder/decoder reconstruction objective rather than adversarial training. It is an alternative to CTGAN when GAN training is unstable, and you choose between them empirically by comparing SDMetrics scores.

Q: What is the standard SDV workflow for single-table synthesis?
A: The standard workflow is: build or detect `Metadata`, instantiate a synthesizer (e.g. `GaussianCopulaSynthesizer(metadata)`), call `synthesizer.fit(real_data)`, then `synthesizer.sample(num_rows=...)` to produce synthetic rows. You then evaluate the result with SDMetrics before trusting it. The metadata-fit-sample-evaluate loop is the same regardless of which synthesizer you pick.

Q: What does `Metadata.detect_from_dataframe(data)` do?
A: `Metadata.detect_from_dataframe(data)` inspects a pandas DataFrame and automatically infers a metadata object describing each column's data type (numerical, categorical, datetime, id, etc.). It is a convenience that saves you writing the metadata by hand, but you should always review and correct the detected types because the inference can mislabel columns. For example it may detect an `ISIN` string column as categorical when you want it treated as an id.

Q: Why must you usually review auto-detected metadata before fitting?
A: Auto-detection guesses sdtypes from values, and it frequently mislabels columns — treating an id as categorical, a categorical code as numerical, or missing a primary key. If the metadata is wrong, the synthesizer models the wrong thing and produces low-fidelity or structurally invalid output. In a fund dataset you would correct things like marking `investor_id` as an id, `ISIN` as an id or text, and `nav_per_share` as numerical with the right bounds.

Q: What does `synthesizer.sample(num_rows=1000)` return?
A: It returns a new DataFrame of 1000 synthetic rows drawn from the fitted model, with the same columns and types as the training data but new, generated values. The number of rows is independent of the training set size, so you can generate more or fewer rows than the original. Each call draws a fresh sample, so two calls produce different synthetic rows unless you fix the random state.

Q: What is the fidelity-privacy tradeoff?
A: The fidelity-privacy tradeoff is the tension that the more closely synthetic data reproduces the real data's statistics (high fidelity), the higher the risk it leaks information about real individuals (low privacy). Pushing a model to perfectly match the real distribution eventually means it starts reproducing real records. The architect's job is to choose a point on that curve that is faithful enough to be useful for dev/test yet private enough to be safe to use outside production.

Q: Why can't you simply maximize fidelity?
A: Because maximizing fidelity drives the model toward memorizing and reproducing the real records, which defeats the privacy purpose of synthesizing in the first place. A perfectly faithful synthesizer is, in the limit, just a copy of the real data and would re-expose the personal information you were trying to protect. You therefore target "useful enough" fidelity, not maximal fidelity, and verify privacy explicitly.

Q: What is overfitting in the context of a synthesizer?
A: Overfitting is when a synthesizer learns the training data too specifically, so instead of capturing general patterns it captures the actual individual records. An overfitted model tends to reproduce real rows or near-duplicates when sampled, which makes the synthetic data both less general and a privacy hazard. Small training datasets and high-capacity deep models like CTGAN are the most prone to it.

Q: What is a membership-inference attack?
A: A membership-inference attack is when an adversary, given a candidate record, tries to determine whether that exact record was part of the data used to train the synthesizer. If they can tell membership reliably, the synthetic dataset has leaked the fact that a specific real person's record existed in the source — a privacy breach. Over-fitted synthesizers are especially vulnerable because they "remember" their training rows.

Q: Why is membership inference a particular risk in over-fitted synthesis?
A: An over-fitted synthesizer reproduces its training records (or near-duplicates) when sampled, so records that were in training are statistically more likely to appear in the synthetic output than records that were not. That difference in likelihood is exactly the signal a membership-inference attacker exploits. The fix is to avoid memorization — adequate training data, appropriate model capacity, and checking novelty metrics like `NewRowSynthesis`.

Q: In a fund context, why is membership inference more than a theoretical worry?
A: If an attacker can confirm that a specific investor's record was in the training data, they learn that this individual is an investor in a particular Luxembourg fund — itself sensitive personal and financial information. Confirming membership can also reveal relationships, holdings, or KYC attributes if the model leaks correlated fields. That makes membership-inference resistance a real regulatory and reputational concern, not just an academic metric.

Q: How does training-set size affect the fidelity-privacy tradeoff?
A: With a larger, more diverse training set, the model can learn general patterns without leaning on any single record, so it is easier to get high fidelity and good privacy at the same time. With a small training set, the model has little to generalize from and tends to memorize individual rows, worsening leakage risk. This is why synthesizing from a tiny table is dangerous: it is hard to be both faithful and private.

Q: What is referential integrity, and why does it matter for synthetic data?
A: Referential integrity means that every foreign-key value in a child table points to a primary-key value that actually exists in the parent table. It matters for synthetic data because if you synthesize each table independently, the foreign keys in the child table will reference parent keys that were never generated, breaking joins. A dev dataset where `orders.investor_id` does not match any row in `investors` is useless for testing pipelines that join those tables.

Q: Why does synthesizing each table independently break a multi-table dataset?
A: Independent synthesis generates new primary keys for the parent and new, unrelated foreign keys for the child, so the keys no longer line up across tables. Joins return empty or wrong results, cardinality patterns are lost, and any logic that depends on the parent-child relationship fails. Multi-table synthesis exists precisely to generate the tables together so the key relationships and cardinalities are preserved.

Q: What is the SDV synthesizer for multi-table data?
A: The multi-table synthesizer in SDV is `HMASynthesizer` (Hierarchical Modeling Algorithm). It models a set of related tables together using the relationships declared in the metadata, so that generated foreign keys reference generated primary keys and parent-child cardinalities are preserved. You fit it on a dictionary of DataFrames and it samples a dictionary of synthetic DataFrames.

Q: How do you declare a parent-child relationship in SDV metadata?
A: You add a relationship to the `Metadata` object specifying the parent table, the child table, the parent's primary key, and the child's foreign key — conceptually `add_relationship(parent_table_name=..., child_table_name=..., parent_primary_key=..., child_foreign_key=...)`. This tells the multi-table synthesizer how the tables connect so it can preserve the key references. Without declared relationships, the synthesizer cannot maintain referential integrity across tables.

Q: How does `HMASynthesizer` preserve foreign keys when sampling?
A: It models the tables hierarchically using the declared relationships, generating parent rows with their primary keys first and then generating child rows whose foreign keys point to those generated parents, with cardinalities drawn from the patterns learned in the real data. Because the keys are produced together rather than independently, the synthetic `orders` rows reference synthetic `investors` rows that actually exist. The result is a dataset where joins behave like the real one.

Q: What is a known practical limitation of `HMASynthesizer`?
A: `HMASynthesizer` does not scale well to very large or very wide schemas — its hierarchical modeling becomes slow and memory-heavy as the number of tables and columns grows. For big estates you may need to limit the number of columns per table, restrict the relationship depth, or use SDV's modeling tools to estimate feasibility before fitting. Always check current SDV docs because scale guidance and recommended approaches evolve between versions.

Q: What is data masking?
A: Data masking is transforming real data so that sensitive values are obscured while the rest of the record and its structure are preserved — for example replacing real names with pseudonyms, redacting digits of an account number, or shuffling a column. Unlike synthesis, masking starts from the real rows and alters specific fields rather than generating wholly new records. It is often the simpler and safer choice when you only need to hide a few identifying columns.

Q: When is masking the safer or simpler choice than synthesis?
A: Masking is preferable when you need to preserve the real records' structure and exact non-sensitive values (e.g. for reproducing a specific production bug) and only a handful of columns are sensitive. It avoids the statistical-leakage risks of fitted synthesis because you are not training a model that could memorize the data. The lesson notes this ties to topic 11.3, where masking and pseudonymization patterns are treated in depth.

Q: When is synthesis preferable to masking?
A: Synthesis is preferable when masking alone is insufficient — for instance when even the masked records remain re-identifiable through quasi-identifiers, or when you need many more rows than exist, or when you cannot use any real records at all in the target environment. Synthesis breaks the one-to-one link to real individuals entirely, whereas masking still operates on real rows. The decision hinges on residual re-identification risk and how much real structure you must keep.

Q: Why doesn't masking always satisfy data minimization?
A: Masking a few obvious identifiers can still leave quasi-identifiers (date of birth, postcode, fund + amount + date) that uniquely re-identify someone when combined. If a masked dataset is still effectively personal data, GDPR still applies to it, so you have not minimized. Synthesis, when done and verified well, severs the link to real individuals and can move the dataset out of the personal-data category entirely.

Q: What does it mean that masked data may still be personal data?
A: It means that if individuals can be singled out or re-identified from the masked dataset — directly or by combining it with other data — the records are still "personal data" under GDPR and the full obligations apply. Pseudonymization explicitly is treated as personal data because the link can be reversed with the key. True anonymization or well-evaluated synthesis aims to remove that re-identifiability so the data falls outside GDPR's scope.

Q: What is SDMetrics?
A: SDMetrics is the companion open-source library to SDV for evaluating the quality of synthetic data. It is model-agnostic, so it works on synthetic data from any source, and it provides reports and individual metrics covering validity, fidelity, and privacy. You use it to turn "the synthetic data looks fine" into measurable scores you can put in a privacy and quality justification.

Q: What two headline reports does SDMetrics provide?
A: SDMetrics provides the `DiagnosticReport` and the `QualityReport`. The diagnostic report checks that the synthetic data is structurally valid and that key references are correct, while the quality report measures how well the synthetic data reproduces the real data's statistical properties (fidelity). You typically run the diagnostic first to confirm validity, then the quality report to assess fidelity.

Q: What three properties does the SDMetrics `DiagnosticReport` check?
A: The `DiagnosticReport` checks Data Validity, Data Structure, and — for multi-table data — Relationship Validity. Data Validity confirms each column's values are in-range and of the right category; Data Structure confirms the synthetic table has the same columns; Relationship Validity confirms foreign keys reference existing primary keys and child counts are sensible. It returns scores as percentages and is meant to catch basic correctness problems before you look at fidelity.

Q: What does the Data Validity property in the diagnostic report measure?
A: Data Validity checks that each column contains valid values for its type — for example primary keys are unique (KeyUniqueness), numerical and datetime columns stay within the real data's boundaries (BoundaryAdherence), and categorical/boolean columns only contain seen categories (CategoryAdherence). The property score is the average validity across columns. A low score flags out-of-range numbers or invented categories that would break downstream constraints.

Q: What does the Data Structure property check?
A: Data Structure checks that the synthetic table has the same overall structure as the real table, principally the same set of column names, using the TableStructure metric. It catches cases where the synthetic output is missing columns, has extra columns, or has renamed columns. A perfect structure score means a pipeline expecting the real schema can ingest the synthetic data without schema errors.

Q: What does the Relationship Validity property check in multi-table diagnostics?
A: Relationship Validity, which only applies to multi-table datasets, verifies that the parent-child relationships are intact. It uses ReferentialIntegrity to confirm every foreign key references an existing primary key, and CardinalityBoundaryAdherence to confirm the number of child rows per parent stays within the bounds seen in the real data. This is the metric that proves referential integrity actually holds in your synthesized dataset.

Q: Which metric proves that foreign keys point to existing parents?
A: The ReferentialIntegrity metric, reported under the diagnostic report's Relationship Validity property, proves it. It checks that every foreign-key value in a child table corresponds to a primary-key value present in the parent table. A score of 100% means no orphaned foreign keys, which is exactly the "show referential integrity holds" requirement of this lesson's Done-when.

Q: What does CardinalityBoundaryAdherence check?
A: CardinalityBoundaryAdherence checks that the number of child rows linked to each parent in the synthetic data stays within the minimum and maximum child counts observed in the real data. For example if real investors each had between 1 and 50 orders, synthetic investors should not suddenly have 5000 orders. It ensures the synthesized relationship cardinality is realistic, not just non-orphaned.

Q: What three properties does the SDMetrics `QualityReport` measure for a single table?
A: For a single table the `QualityReport` measures Column Shapes and Column Pair Trends (the multi-table version adds Cardinality and Intertable Trends). Column Shapes asks whether each column's distribution matches; Column Pair Trends asks whether the relationships between pairs of columns match. Together they give an overall fidelity score as a percentage.

Q: What does the Column Shapes property measure, and with what metrics?
A: Column Shapes measures whether each synthetic column reproduces the shape of the corresponding real column's distribution. It uses KSComplement (based on the Kolmogorov-Smirnov statistic) for numerical and datetime columns and TVComplement (based on Total Variation distance) for categorical and boolean columns. A high Column Shapes score means the per-column marginals match the real data well.

Q: What does the Column Pair Trends property measure?
A: Column Pair Trends measures whether the synthetic data preserves the relationships between pairs of columns. It uses CorrelationSimilarity for numerical-to-numerical pairs and ContingencySimilarity for categorical-to-categorical pairs, discretizing numerical columns first for mixed pairs. A high score means correlations like "higher NAV tends to go with larger order sizes" are preserved, not just the individual columns.

Q: What is KSComplement and what does a score of 1 mean?
A: KSComplement is one minus the Kolmogorov-Smirnov statistic between the real and synthetic distributions of a numerical column, scaled so 1 is best. The KS statistic measures the maximum gap between the two cumulative distribution functions, so a small gap gives a score near 1. A KSComplement of 1 means the synthetic column's distribution is indistinguishable from the real column's under the KS test.

Q: What is TVComplement used for?
A: TVComplement is the categorical analogue of KSComplement; it is one minus the Total Variation distance between the real and synthetic category frequency distributions. It tells you whether each category appears in the synthetic data at roughly the right proportion. A TVComplement near 1 means, for example, that the mix of fund share classes in the synthetic data matches the real proportions.

Q: What is the `NewRowSynthesis` metric and why is it a privacy metric?
A: `NewRowSynthesis` measures whether each synthetic row is novel or is an exact match of a real row, scoring from 0 to 1 where 1 means every synthetic row is new and 0 means every synthetic row is a copy of a real row. It is a privacy metric because exact copies re-expose real records, so a high score is evidence the model did not simply memorize the data. It is computed as `1 - (matching synthetic rows / total synthetic rows)`.

Q: A `NewRowSynthesis` score of 1.0 means what?
A: A score of 1.0 means none of the synthetic rows exactly match any real row — every generated record is novel. That is the best outcome for this metric because it indicates the synthesizer is generalizing rather than copying its training data. It is a key piece of evidence in the written privacy argument that the dev/test dataset is safe to use.

Q: Can a few exact matches actually be acceptable for `NewRowSynthesis`?
A: Yes — the SDV documentation notes that a few matches can even be beneficial, because if an adversary cannot tell which combinations are real, the presence of some matching rows gives them no reliable signal. The concern is systematic copying (a low score), not the occasional coincidental match, especially for columns with few possible values. You interpret the score in context rather than demanding a hard 1.0.

Q: Why is SDMetrics described as model-agnostic, and why does that matter?
A: SDMetrics is model-agnostic because it compares real and synthetic datasets directly and does not need to know which model produced the synthetic data. That matters because you can evaluate output from SDV, Faker-augmented data, a vendor like MOSTLY AI, or any other generator with the same metrics. It lets you compare candidate approaches on a level playing field before committing.

Q: How do you generate a quality report in code with SDMetrics?
A: You import `QualityReport` from the appropriate module (`sdmetrics.reports.single_table` or `sdmetrics.reports.multi_table`), instantiate it, and call `report.generate(real_data, synthetic_data, metadata)`. For multi-table you pass dictionaries of DataFrames keyed by table name. The report then exposes the overall score and per-property breakdowns you can drill into.

Q: How do you generate a diagnostic report in code?
A: You import `DiagnosticReport` from `sdmetrics.reports.single_table` or `sdmetrics.reports.multi_table`, instantiate it, and call `report.generate(real_data, synthetic_data, metadata)`. For multi-table data you pass dictionaries of DataFrames and the metadata describing the relationships. The diagnostic report returns the validity/structure (and relationship) scores as percentages.

Q: In what order should you run the diagnostic and quality reports, and why?
A: Run the `DiagnosticReport` first because it answers the basic question "is this data valid and structurally correct" — if it fails, fidelity scores are meaningless. Once the diagnostic confirms the data is in-range, has the right columns, and preserves key references, run the `QualityReport` to assess how faithfully it reproduces the real statistics. Diagnose validity, then measure fidelity.

Q: What is the difference between the diagnostic report's job and the quality report's job?
A: The diagnostic report answers "is the synthetic data structurally valid and internally consistent" — correct columns, in-range values, intact keys — and is largely pass/fail correctness. The quality report answers "how statistically similar is it to the real data" — matching distributions and correlations — which is a graded fidelity measure. One is about correctness, the other about realism.

Q: For the lesson's "Do" task, what does step 1 ("fit an SDV multi-table synthesizer to the masked investor + orders tables") involve?
A: It involves building metadata for the two related tables with the investor table as parent and orders as child, declaring the relationship between `investor_id` and the order's foreign key, then creating and fitting an `HMASynthesizer` on the dictionary of (already masked) DataFrames. Masking first reduces the residual leakage risk before the model ever sees the data. Fitting learns both per-table distributions and the parent-child relationship.

Q: Why does the lesson say to mask the investor and orders tables before fitting the synthesizer?
A: Masking first is defense in depth: it strips direct identifiers before the model ever sees them, so even if the synthesizer over-fits and leaks, what leaks is already masked rather than raw PII. It reduces the worst-case impact of memorization and membership inference. Combining masking with synthesis is generally stronger than relying on either alone.

Q: What is the goal of "compare aggregate fidelity against the real data with SDMetrics"?
A: The goal is to quantify how well the synthetic dev dataset reproduces the real data's aggregate statistics — column distributions, pairwise correlations, and (multi-table) cardinality — using the SDMetrics quality report. This turns a subjective impression into numeric scores you can record and defend. If aggregate fidelity is too low, the dataset won't be useful for realistic testing and you iterate on the synthesizer.

Q: What must the "privacy argument" deliverable in this lesson establish?
A: It must establish a written justification of why the synthetic dataset is safe to use in dev/test — typically that the source was masked, that the synthesizer did not memorize records (evidenced by a high `NewRowSynthesis` and absence of exact matches), that membership-inference risk is acceptable, and that the data is no longer personal data under GDPR. It is the document a DPO or auditor would read to approve using the data outside production.

Q: What evidence would you cite in a privacy argument that synthesis is safe?
A: You would cite the masking applied before fitting, the `NewRowSynthesis` score showing few or no exact copies, a check for near-duplicates of real rows, a membership-inference assessment, and the absence of preserved direct identifiers. You would also note that referential integrity and fidelity are achieved without retaining real records. Together these support the claim that no real individual can be re-identified from the dataset.

Q: What are the lesson's two "Done when" criteria?
A: The two criteria are: (1) you can run dev/test environments on the data with a written justification of why it is safe, and (2) you can show that referential integrity holds across the synthesized tables. The first is the privacy/fidelity argument backed by SDMetrics; the second is demonstrated by the diagnostic report's ReferentialIntegrity metric scoring 100% (no orphaned foreign keys). Both must be evidenced, not just asserted.

Q: How would you concretely demonstrate that referential integrity holds across synthesized tables?
A: You run the multi-table `DiagnosticReport` and show the Relationship Validity property, specifically a ReferentialIntegrity score of 100%, meaning every synthetic order's foreign key references an existing synthetic investor. You can also independently verify with a SQL anti-join (`SELECT ... FROM orders LEFT JOIN investors ... WHERE investors.investor_id IS NULL`) returning zero rows. Both confirm no orphaned child rows exist.

Q: A SQL anti-join between synthetic orders and investors returns rows — what does that indicate?
A: It indicates broken referential integrity: those returned order rows have foreign keys that reference investor ids which do not exist in the synthetic investors table. This usually means the tables were synthesized independently rather than with a multi-table synthesizer, or the relationship was not declared in the metadata. The fix is to model them together with `HMASynthesizer` and a declared parent-child relationship.

Q: Your `GaussianCopulaSynthesizer.fit()` raises an error about an unrecognized column type — first thing to check?
A: First check the metadata: a column's sdtype is probably wrong or missing, because the synthesizer relies on the declared types to choose how to model each column. Re-examine the output of `Metadata.detect_from_dataframe()` and correct any mislabeled columns (for example an id detected as numerical). Refitting with corrected metadata typically resolves it.

Q: Sampled synthetic numerical values fall outside the real data's range — which metric catches this and why might it happen?
A: BoundaryAdherence (under the diagnostic report's Data Validity) catches it by flagging values outside the real min/max. It can happen because some synthesizers model a continuous distribution that extends past the observed range, or because the column's metadata or bounds were not set. You can address it by enforcing constraints or bounds in the synthesizer configuration so generated values stay in-range.

Q: Your synthetic data has a high quality score but a `NewRowSynthesis` score near 0 — what does that tell you and what do you do?
A: A high fidelity score with a near-zero `NewRowSynthesis` means the synthesizer is achieving realism by copying real rows — classic over-fitting and a serious privacy leak. You should not use this dataset as-is because it effectively re-exposes real records. Remedies include using more or more diverse training data, reducing model capacity, switching synthesizer, or applying constraints, then re-checking the metric.

Q: How do you make an SDV sampling run reproducible?
A: You set the synthesizer's random seed/state before sampling (current SDV exposes this via a method such as `reset_sampling()` or a seed parameter — confirm the exact API in the version's docs). With a fixed seed, repeated `sample()` calls produce the same synthetic rows, which is useful for deterministic test fixtures. Without it, each sample draws fresh random rows.

Q: Why might CTGAN produce worse results than GaussianCopula on a small fund table?
A: CTGAN is a high-capacity neural network that needs a reasonable amount of data and training to converge; on a small table it can both underfit (noisy, low-fidelity output) and overfit (memorizing rows), and it is slower. GaussianCopula's simpler parametric assumptions often generalize better from limited data. The empirical rule is to compare both with SDMetrics rather than assuming the deep model is better.

Q: What is a constraint in SDV, and when would you use one?
A: A constraint is a rule you attach to a synthesizer to enforce business logic that the statistical model alone won't guarantee — for example that `redemption_date >= subscription_date`, that an amount is positive, or that a value lies in a fixed set. You use constraints when valid synthetic data must satisfy relationships the model can otherwise violate. SDV applies them during sampling so generated rows respect the rule.

Q: Why might you need a constraint that NAV per share is strictly positive?
A: Because a purely statistical synthesizer fitting a numerical column can sample a negative or zero value if the distribution's tail extends there, but a NAV per share of zero or below is nonsensical and would break downstream calculations and validations. A positive-value constraint forces every sampled `nav_per_share` to stay valid. This keeps the synthetic data realistic against the business rule, not just the distribution.

Q: How would you synthesize realistic-looking but fake ISIN values?
A: ISINs are not free-form, so pure statistical synthesis won't produce valid ones; you would generate them rule-based with a generator that builds a 2-letter country prefix, a 9-character NSIN body, and the correct check digit, or use a constraint/transformer that preserves the format. Treating `ISIN` as an id and letting the synthesizer pass through or regenerate format-valid codes avoids leaking real ISINs while keeping joins working. The key is that the value's structure (and check digit) must be respected, which fitted synthesis alone won't guarantee.

Q: Why treat columns like `investor_id` and `ISIN` as ids rather than categorical in metadata?
A: Marking them as ids tells SDV they are identifiers, not values to model statistically, so it handles uniqueness and (for foreign keys) referential relationships correctly rather than trying to fit a distribution over them. If you leave them as categorical, the synthesizer may try to reproduce the exact set of real values — which both fails for high-cardinality keys and risks leaking real identifiers. Correct id typing is essential for multi-table integrity.

Q: For an EMT/EPT file pipeline, why use synthetic rather than real fund data in CI?
A: EMT (European MiFID Template) and EPT (European PRIIPs Template) files contain real product and cost data tied to specific funds and possibly investors, which you should not place in a CI/test environment under data minimization. Synthetic EMT/EPT rows let you exercise the parsing, validation, and transformation logic against realistic structures without exposing real product data. You generate them format-correct so the schema and field constraints are tested, while the values are fake.

Q: How do you keep a synthetic LEI both format-valid and non-real?
A: An LEI is a 20-character ISO 17442 code with a specific structure and a final check-digit pair, so you generate it rule-based to satisfy the format and checksum while ensuring the value does not correspond to a registered entity. Fitted synthesis over real LEIs would risk reproducing real ones and would not respect the checksum. So you treat LEI as a structured id and use a format-aware generator rather than a statistical model.

Q: Why is SWIFT/BIC data a good candidate for rule-based rather than fitted synthesis?
A: A BIC has a fixed structure (4-letter bank code, 2-letter country, 2-character location, optional 3-character branch) that statistical synthesis can't reliably reproduce, and the real codes map to real institutions you may not want to expose. Rule-based generation can emit structurally valid, fake BICs that exercise message-parsing logic. So you use a format generator (or constraint) instead of fitting a distribution over real BICs.

Q: How does synthetic data support testing a NAV calculation pipeline safely?
A: You synthesize the input tables (holdings, prices, share classes, orders) with referential integrity preserved, so the NAV pipeline can run end to end on realistic but non-real data. This lets you validate the calculation logic, edge cases, and joins in dev/test without exposing real portfolio positions. You add constraints (positive prices, consistent share counts) so the synthetic inputs produce sensible NAV outputs.

Q: Why is preserved cardinality (not just non-orphaned keys) important for fund test data?
A: Many fund processes depend on realistic relationship volumes — an investor with the right rough number of orders, a fund with a plausible number of investors — so a synthetic dataset where one parent has millions of children would distort performance tests and aggregation logic. CardinalityBoundaryAdherence keeps those counts within real bounds. Non-orphaned keys make joins valid; realistic cardinality makes the test results meaningful.

Q: What is MOSTLY AI in the context of this lesson?
A: MOSTLY AI is an EU-based commercial synthetic-data vendor that the lesson lists as a "Corp (evaluate)" option to assess at the build-vs-buy level, alongside FOSS tools like SDV and Faker. It is mentioned so the architect knows the commercial landscape (also Tonic and Gretel) when deciding whether to build synthesis in-house or buy. The hands-on work in the lesson is done with SDV plus Faker.

Q: Why might a regulated EU fund administrator prefer an EU synthetic-data vendor like MOSTLY AI when buying?
A: An EU vendor keeps processing and any residual data within the EU/EEA, which simplifies GDPR compliance and avoids the complications of international data transfers and third-country adequacy. For a regulated fund administrator subject to EU oversight, data residency and a vendor familiar with EU regulatory expectations reduce legal risk. That said, the decision is still a build-vs-buy evaluation, not automatic.

Q: How does synthetic data relate to DORA and operational resilience testing?
A: DORA requires resilience and continuity testing of ICT systems, and you often cannot run that testing on real production personal/financial data. Synthetic datasets that preserve realistic structure and referential integrity let you exercise failover, recovery, and load scenarios safely. This connects synthesis to the operational-resilience obligations as much as to privacy.

Q: What is the risk of using production data directly in a UAT environment, and how does synthesis address it?
A: Copying production data into UAT spreads real personal and financial data into a less-controlled environment with broader access, violating data minimization and increasing breach exposure. Synthesis addresses it by providing realistic-but-fake data so UAT testers can validate behavior without ever touching real records. The synthetic dataset must still be quality-checked so UAT results remain meaningful.

Q: Why is "the synthetic data looks realistic" not an acceptable privacy justification?
A: Looking realistic is about fidelity, not privacy, and a dataset can look realistic precisely because it memorized real rows — which is the privacy failure. A defensible justification needs measured evidence (high `NewRowSynthesis`, no exact/near matches, membership-inference assessment, masking applied), not a visual impression. The whole point of SDMetrics privacy metrics is to replace "looks fine" with proof.

Q: What is the difference between anonymization and synthesis?
A: Anonymization transforms real records so individuals can no longer be identified, but it still starts from and operates on the real rows; synthesis generates new records from a learned (or rule-based) model, so there is no one-to-one link to any real individual. Done well, synthesis can be stronger because there is no real record to re-identify. But synthesis can still leak if it memorizes, so it is not automatically anonymous.

Q: How can quasi-identifiers undermine a masked dataset that synthesis would handle better?
A: Even after masking direct identifiers, a combination of quasi-identifiers — say fund, subscription date, and amount — can uniquely single out one investor, leaving the data effectively personal. Synthesis breaks that because the combinations in synthetic rows do not correspond to real individuals (when novelty is verified). So when quasi-identifier re-identification is the risk, synthesis is the stronger control.

Q: What is the role of metadata sdtypes (numerical, categorical, datetime, id) in fitted synthesis?
A: The sdtype tells SDV how to model and transform each column — a numerical column gets a continuous distribution, categorical gets category frequencies, datetime is handled as time, and id is treated as an identifier rather than modeled. Correct sdtypes are the foundation of good fidelity and valid output. Wrong sdtypes lead to nonsensical synthesis (e.g. averaging id numbers) or validity failures.

Q: Why would you discretize a numerical column when evaluating a numerical-categorical column pair?
A: Column Pair Trends needs a way to compare the relationship between a numerical and a categorical column, and ContingencySimilarity works on categories. SDMetrics discretizes (bins) the numerical column into categories so it can build a contingency table and compute the similarity. This lets it score mixed-type trends consistently rather than skipping them.

Q: What does a low Column Pair Trends score with a high Column Shapes score tell you?
A: It tells you that each column's individual distribution is reproduced well, but the relationships between columns are not — the synthesizer captured marginals but missed correlations. For example NAV and order size might each be distributed correctly yet be uncorrelated in the synthetic data when they should move together. You would consider a synthesizer that better captures dependencies (a copula or deep model) and re-evaluate.

Q: Why does the lesson pair Faker with SDV rather than treating them as alternatives?
A: They solve different parts of the problem: SDV gives you statistically faithful, relationship-preserving records from fitted models, while Faker supplies realistically formatted values for columns that should not be statistically modeled (names, emails, format-bound codes). In practice you fit SDV for the structure and distributions and use Faker (or constraints) to fill or post-process columns that need format-valid fakes. They are complementary, not competing.

Q: After fitting `HMASynthesizer`, how do you generate the synthetic dataset?
A: You call `sample()` on the fitted multi-table synthesizer (optionally with a scale factor), and it returns a dictionary of synthetic DataFrames keyed by table name, with foreign keys consistent across them. The output preserves the parent-child relationships you declared in the metadata. You then feed that dictionary into the multi-table SDMetrics reports for validation.

Q: What does it mean to "scale" a multi-table sample, and why is it tricky?
A: Scaling means generating proportionally more (or fewer) rows than the real dataset across the related tables. It is tricky because you must grow parent and child tables consistently so cardinalities stay realistic — you cannot just multiply each table independently or you break the relationship ratios. SDV's multi-table sampling handles this via a scale parameter; check the version's docs for the exact argument.

Q: Why must deletion/retention concerns still apply to synthetic datasets?
A: If a synthetic dataset is poorly made and leaks real information, it may still count as personal data and fall under retention and deletion rules; even when it is truly non-personal, you still manage it as an artifact. The safe default is to treat synthetic datasets as governed assets until you have proven they are non-personal. This connects synthesis to the broader retention/lifecycle topic in the phase.

Q: What is a "near-duplicate" leak and why isn't `NewRowSynthesis` alone enough?
A: A near-duplicate leak is when a synthetic row isn't an exact copy but is so close to a real row that the real individual is effectively re-identifiable. `NewRowSynthesis` checks exact matches, so it can miss near-duplicates that differ in one trivial field. A thorough privacy argument therefore also checks distance-based similarity to real rows, not just exact-match novelty.

Q: How would you justify using synthetic data to a DPO who worries about re-identification?
A: You present the layered controls: the source was masked before fitting, direct identifiers were removed, the synthesizer did not memorize (`NewRowSynthesis` high, no exact or near matches), a membership-inference test showed no reliable signal, and quasi-identifier combinations don't map to real individuals. You back each claim with an SDMetrics report or test rather than assertion. The DPO needs evidence that re-identification risk is acceptably low.

Q: What is the danger of synthesizing from a table that has very few rows?
A: With very few rows the model has almost nothing to generalize from, so it tends to memorize and reproduce those individual records, giving high apparent fidelity but severe leakage and membership-inference risk. The synthetic output may also be statistically unstable. The mitigation is to gather more training data, prefer simpler models, or fall back to rule-based generation/masking for tiny tables.

Q: Why might you choose `CopulaGANSynthesizer` over `GaussianCopulaSynthesizer`?
A: `CopulaGANSynthesizer` applies copula-based preprocessing to the columns and then uses a GAN, aiming to combine the copula's handling of marginals with a GAN's ability to capture complex dependencies. You would try it when the plain GaussianCopula misses non-linear relationships but you still want the stabilizing benefit of copula transforms. As always, you confirm the choice empirically with SDMetrics rather than by reputation.

Q: How do constraints interact with referential integrity in multi-table synthesis?
A: Constraints enforce within-row or within-column business rules, while referential integrity is a cross-table property handled by the multi-table synthesizer's relationship modeling. They are complementary: the synthesizer keeps foreign keys valid, and constraints keep individual field values sensible. You typically need both for a usable synthetic fund dataset — valid keys and valid values.

Q: What does a 100% Data Structure score but a 60% Column Shapes score mean?
A: It means the synthetic table has exactly the right columns (structure is correct) but the values within several columns do not reproduce the real distributions well (fidelity is mediocre). Pipelines expecting the schema will run, but tests that depend on realistic distributions may behave differently than on real data. You would iterate on the synthesizer or its configuration to raise Column Shapes.

A: Because the architect's decision isn't whether synthetic data is useful — it is — but whether to build the capability with FOSS (SDV/Faker) or buy a managed platform (MOSTLY AI, Tonic, Gretel). That depends on team capacity, scale, support, residency, and governance needs. The lesson keeps the hands-on learning in FOSS while making you aware of the commercial alternatives for the evaluation.

Q: Summarize the architect's core responsibility in synthetic data per this lesson's "Why".
A: The architect owns the fidelity-privacy tradeoff: choosing and configuring synthesis so the data is faithful enough to be useful in dev/test yet private enough to be safe, and proving both with metrics. They must guard against membership-inference leakage from over-fitting and against broken referential integrity that makes a multi-table dev dataset useless. The deliverables — a working dev environment with a written privacy justification and demonstrated cross-table integrity — encode exactly that responsibility.
