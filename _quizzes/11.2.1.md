# Phase 6 · 11.2.1 Encryption & key management — 100 self-test questions

Q: What is "encryption at rest" and why is it the baseline expectation for any fund-data store?
A: Encryption at rest means data is stored on disk in ciphertext so that a stolen disk, leaked backup, or compromised storage account yields unreadable bytes rather than plaintext NAVs and investor records. It is the baseline because almost every regulatory and audit framework a fund administrator answers to (GDPR, DORA, UCITS depositary reviews) treats unencrypted persisted PII as a deficiency. The key point for a data architect is that "at rest" alone is not enough — you must also control *who holds the key*, which is the question the rest of this lesson answers.

Q: What is envelope encryption in one sentence?
A: Envelope encryption is the practice of encrypting your data with a data encryption key (DEK) and then encrypting (wrapping) that DEK with a separate key encryption key (KEK), so the protected payload travels with its own wrapped key rather than relying on a single master key for everything. The "envelope" metaphor is that the wrapped DEK is the sealed envelope and the KEK is what opens it. This two-tier structure is the foundation of essentially every cloud KMS design.

Q: What does the acronym DEK stand for and what does a DEK actually encrypt?
A: DEK stands for data encryption key, and it is the symmetric key (typically AES-256) that directly encrypts the bulk data — the blob, the database page, the file. It is the workhorse key that does the heavy bytes-level encryption locally and at high speed. Crucially, a DEK is never stored in plaintext alongside the data; it is stored only in its wrapped (encrypted) form.

Q: What does the acronym KEK stand for and what is its job?
A: KEK stands for key encryption key, and its only job is to encrypt (wrap) and decrypt (unwrap) DEKs — it never touches the bulk data itself. Because the KEK is used only on tiny inputs (a 256-bit DEK), it can live in a hardened, low-throughput store like a KMS or HSM without becoming a performance bottleneck. Compromising the KEK is what would let an attacker unwrap DEKs, so the KEK is the high-value secret you protect most heavily.

Q: In envelope encryption, why is the DEK never stored in plaintext?
A: If the plaintext DEK sat next to the ciphertext, anyone who could read the storage could also decrypt the data, defeating the whole scheme. Instead the DEK is stored only as a wrapped (KEK-encrypted) blob, so an attacker with the ciphertext and the wrapped DEK still cannot decrypt anything without access to the KEK. This is the property that lets you store ciphertext and wrapped DEKs together cheaply while keeping the trust boundary at the KEK.

Q: Walk through the steps of encrypting a payload with envelope encryption.
A: Generate a fresh DEK locally, encrypt the data with the DEK, then send the DEK to the KMS to be wrapped by the KEK, and finally store the ciphertext together with the wrapped DEK while discarding the plaintext DEK from memory. Only the small DEK crosses the network to the KMS — the bulk data stays local. A common implementation detail is that the KMS may return both the plaintext DEK (for immediate use) and the wrapped DEK (for storage) in a single `GenerateDataKey`-style call.

Q: Walk through the steps of decrypting a payload protected by envelope encryption.
A: Retrieve the ciphertext and its wrapped DEK, identify which KEK wrapped that DEK, ask the KMS to unwrap the DEK using the KEK, then use the unwrapped plaintext DEK locally to decrypt the data. The KEK never leaves the KMS/HSM during this; only the unwrap operation happens inside the boundary. After decryption the plaintext DEK should be wiped from memory.

Q: Why does envelope encryption send only the DEK to the KMS rather than the whole payload?
A: Because the KMS is a hardened, rate-limited, relatively low-throughput service, sending gigabytes of NAV history or a full investor table through it would be slow and expensive. By sending only the 256-bit DEK to be wrapped/unwrapped, the expensive bulk encryption happens locally at line speed while the KMS does tiny cryptographic operations. This is the single biggest reason envelope encryption scales to data-lake volumes.

Q: What is the "blast radius" advantage of using a unique DEK per object?
A: If each blob, file, or partition has its own DEK, then a single leaked DEK exposes only that one object rather than the entire store. This limits the blast radius of a key compromise to the smallest possible scope. It also makes key rotation and crypto-shredding granular — you can re-wrap or destroy keys per object without touching everything else.

Q: Why can a single KEK protect millions of objects without itself becoming a bottleneck?
A: Because the KEK only ever operates on tiny DEKs, not on the data, so its workload per object is constant and minuscule regardless of object size. One KEK can wrap millions of DEKs while the actual data encryption is spread across millions of local DEK operations. This separation of "many fast DEKs, one protected KEK" is what makes the hierarchy both secure and scalable.

Q: In an Azure-style hierarchy, name the three tiers from data up to the root of trust.
A: From the data upward: the DEK encrypts the data, the KEK (a customer-managed key in Key Vault) wraps the DEK, and a vault/HSM root key protects the KEK material inside the hardware boundary. Each tier is protected by the tier above it, and the topmost key never leaves the secured module. This is the canonical key vault → KEK → DEK chain.

Q: Where does each layer of the key hierarchy physically live?
A: The DEK lives next to (or within) the data store, stored in wrapped form; the KEK lives inside the cloud KMS / key vault and never leaves it in plaintext; and the vault's own root/master key lives inside the HSM hardware boundary. The design principle is that as you move up the hierarchy, the key moves into progressively stronger isolation. The bulk data and wrapped DEK can sit in cheap object storage precisely because the keys that matter are isolated above them.

Q: What is a KMS (key management service) responsible for?
A: A KMS is the managed service that generates, stores, protects, wraps/unwraps, rotates, and audits cryptographic keys, exposing operations like encrypt, decrypt, wrap, unwrap, and sign while keeping the actual key material inside its boundary. It centralizes key policy and access control so that applications request cryptographic operations rather than handling raw key bytes. Azure Key Vault, AWS KMS, and Google Cloud KMS are the major cloud examples.

Q: Why is centralizing keys in a KMS better than each application managing its own keys?
A: A KMS gives you one place to enforce access policy, one audit trail of every key use, consistent rotation, and a hardened boundary the keys never leave — none of which you get when keys are scattered in app config files or environment variables. It also separates duties: the people who can use a key for crypto operations need not be able to export it. For a regulated fund administrator, that central audit log is often the single artifact an auditor asks to see.

Q: What does it mean that a KEK "never leaves" the key vault?
A: It means cryptographic operations using the KEK are performed *inside* the vault/HSM boundary — you send the vault a DEK to wrap or unwrap and it returns the result, but the raw KEK bytes are never exported to your application or the network. This is what makes the vault the trust boundary: even a fully compromised application server cannot exfiltrate the KEK. It is also why client-side compromise exposes only the data the app could already decrypt, not the master key.

Q: What is a customer-managed key (CMK) in Azure Key Vault?
A: A CMK is a key you create and control in your own Key Vault (or Managed HSM) that Azure services then use as the KEK to wrap their service-generated DEKs. Because you own the CMK, you control its lifecycle — you can rotate it, audit its use, and revoke access to it. It is the opposite of letting the platform silently manage the key for you.

Q: What are platform-managed (Microsoft-managed) keys?
A: Platform-managed keys are encryption keys that Azure creates, stores, rotates, and manages entirely on your behalf, invisible to you, so that data is encrypted at rest by default with zero configuration. You get encryption at rest but no direct control over the key — you cannot rotate it on demand, revoke it, or see its audit trail. They are the sensible default for data classes that do not require customer-held key control.

Q: What do customer-managed keys actually buy you that platform-managed keys do not?
A: Three concrete things: the ability to revoke access (disable the key and render the store undecryptable on your schedule), a dedicated audit trail of key operations under your control, and control over rotation cadence and key lifecycle. They let you answer "we hold the key, and here is the log to prove it." What they do *not* magically buy is stronger cryptography — the AES is identical; only the control plane changes.

Q: What do customer-managed keys NOT give you, despite common assumptions?
A: They do not make the underlying encryption mathematically stronger, they do not stop Microsoft from operating the storage service, and they do not by themselves give you sovereignty over where compute or metadata live. They shift *control of the key* to you, not control of the entire platform. A common misconception in compliance reviews is treating "we use CMK" as a complete answer to data-sovereignty concerns, which it is not.

Q: In a Schrems II / EU data-sovereignty review, why is "who controls the keys" the central question?
A: Because the legal concern is whether a third party (including a cloud provider subject to foreign legal compulsion) can access plaintext fund and investor data. If you hold and can revoke the CMK, you can argue the provider cannot unilaterally produce plaintext; if the platform holds the key, that argument is much weaker. This is why EU fund firms push toward CMK plus tight key-access policy as part of their sovereignty posture — though CMK alone is not a complete defence.

Q: For a Luxembourg fund administrator, what is a sensible rule of thumb for which stores get CMK versus platform-managed keys?
A: Stores holding investor PII, beneficial-ownership/LEI registries, transfer-agency records, and any data subject to sovereignty commitments are good CMK candidates because you may need to prove control or revoke; low-sensitivity operational stores (build artifacts, ephemeral logs without PII) can stay platform-managed to reduce operational burden. The decision should be driven by data classification, not by defaulting everything to CMK. Every CMK choice must be justifiable in writing, because each adds a revocation/availability risk you now own.

Q: What is the operational cost of choosing CMK that teams routinely underestimate?
A: You now own a new availability dependency: if the key vault is unreachable, the key is accidentally disabled, the access policy is misconfigured, or the key is purged, the store can become undecryptable. You also own rotation, monitoring, and the revocation drill. The cryptography is free; the operational responsibility is the real cost, which is why "justify each CMK decision in writing" is part of the done-when.

Q: What does "encryption in transit" mean and what protocol provides it?
A: Encryption in transit means data is protected while moving across a network so it cannot be read or tampered with by anyone observing the wire, and TLS (Transport Layer Security) is the protocol that provides it. It complements encryption at rest: at-rest protects the disk, in-transit protects the connection. A complete posture needs both, because data is vulnerable both where it sits and where it moves.

Q: Why is "TLS everywhere" described as table stakes rather than an advanced control?
A: Because it is so universally expected and inexpensive that its *absence* is a finding, not its presence a credit — every modern data service, API, and database connection is expected to use TLS by default. An auditor will assume TLS and only flag the exceptions (an internal hop on plaintext, a legacy SWIFT/file-transfer link without TLS). It is the floor, not the ceiling, of a transport-security posture.

Q: What TLS version should you target today and which should you disable?
A: Target TLS 1.2 as the minimum and prefer TLS 1.3 where supported; disable SSL 3.0, TLS 1.0, and TLS 1.1, which are deprecated and have known weaknesses. Most cloud data services now let you enforce a minimum TLS version at the service level. Pinning a minimum version is a concrete, auditable control you can point to in a review.

Q: A pipeline pushes an EMT file to a counterparty over an internal hop in plaintext — why is that still a problem even "inside the network"?
A: Because the assumption that the internal network is trusted is exactly the assumption modern (zero-trust) security rejects: lateral movement, misconfigured taps, and shared infrastructure mean internal traffic can be observed. An EMT file carries fund and cost data that should not be readable on the wire. The fix is to enforce TLS on every hop, internal and external, so there is no "trusted but plaintext" segment.

Q: How does encryption in transit relate to encryption at rest — are they substitutes?
A: They are complementary, not substitutes: in-transit protects data on the network, at-rest protects data on disk, and a gap in either leaves a real exposure window. Data decrypted in transit is re-encrypted at rest at the destination, and vice versa, so the two together give continuous protection. Claiming "we encrypt at rest" while running plaintext connections, or vice versa, is an incomplete posture.

Q: What is key rotation and why do you do it?
A: Key rotation is replacing a key with a new version (or new key) on a schedule or on demand, so that any single key version is used for a bounded amount of data and time. You do it to limit the blast radius of a key compromise, to satisfy compliance mandates that require periodic rotation, and to retire keys whose exposure risk has grown. The principle is that the longer a key lives and the more it protects, the more damaging its compromise.

Q: In an envelope-encryption design, rotating the KEK does NOT require re-encrypting all the data — why?
A: Because the bulk data is encrypted with DEKs, not the KEK, rotating the KEK only requires *re-wrapping* the DEKs (or, with versioned keys, leaving old DEKs wrapped by the old KEK version that remains available for unwrap). You do not have to decrypt and re-encrypt terabytes of data. This is one of the biggest operational wins of the DEK/KEK split — KEK rotation is cheap.

Q: What does "automated rotation" in Azure Key Vault do?
A: Automated rotation lets you set a rotation policy on a key so the vault generates a new key version automatically at a defined cadence (for example every 90 or 365 days), creating a new current version while retaining older versions for unwrap. It removes the manual, error-prone step of rotating keys by hand. The policy can also fire near-expiry notifications via Event Grid so you can act before a key lapses.

Q: What is the "blast radius" of a key rotation, and why must you think about it before rotating?
A: The blast radius is the set of services, stores, and pipelines that depend on the key being rotated — anything that must now pick up the new version or that breaks if it is still pinned to the old one. If a consuming service references a specific key *version* rather than the versionless key, rotation can silently leave it on the old key or break it. You map this dependency set first so rotation does not brick a downstream NAV pipeline.

Q: In Azure, what is the difference between a versioned key identifier and a versionless one, and why does it matter for rotation?
A: A versioned key ID points at one specific version of the key, while a versionless ID always resolves to the current version. Services that reference a versioned ID will not auto-rotate — they stay pinned to that version — whereas services given the versionless ID pick up the new version automatically after rotation. Getting this wrong is a classic cause of "we rotated but nothing changed" or "rotation broke decryption."

Q: After rotating a KEK, why must the previous key version usually remain available for some time?
A: Because data and DEKs wrapped under the old key version can only be unwrapped by that version, so destroying it immediately would render that data undecryptable. You keep old versions in a usable (unwrap-capable) state until everything has been re-wrapped or aged out. This is why rotation enables new writes under the new key while old key versions still service reads.

Q: What is the safe sequencing of a key rotation to avoid an availability incident?
A: Create the new key version, point new encryption operations at it (or let the versionless reference pick it up), keep old versions enabled for unwrap, re-wrap or migrate existing DEKs as needed, and only then retire/disable old versions once nothing depends on them. Rotation is roll-forward then drain, never a hard cutover. Skipping the "keep old version available" step is the most common way rotation causes an outage.

Q: What is the difference between rotating a key and revoking a key?
A: Rotation replaces a key with a new version while keeping old versions available so decryption continues seamlessly; revocation deliberately removes access to a key so that the protected data becomes undecryptable. Rotation is a routine hygiene operation designed to be invisible; revocation is a deliberate, disruptive action you take to cut off access. Confusing the two leads to either ineffective rotation or accidental data lockout.

Q: The "Done when" asks you to answer "if we revoke the key, what actually happens and how fast?" — how do you actually answer it for a store?
A: You disable or remove access to the KEK and observe that new wrap/unwrap operations fail, which means the service can no longer obtain DEKs and so cannot decrypt the affected store; the speed depends on caching — already-cached DEKs may keep working until caches expire or the service restarts. So the honest answer is "decryption of new reads fails immediately at the vault, but in-flight/cached operations may continue for the cache lifetime." You should test this in a non-production store to get the real timing rather than assuming "instant."

Q: Why might revoking a CMK NOT take effect instantly for an Azure storage service?
A: Because services often cache the unwrapped DEK or the wrap key for performance, so already-decrypted/cached data and active sessions can continue until the cache TTL elapses or the service re-validates against the vault. Revocation reliably stops *new* unwrap operations at the vault, but the practical lockout time is governed by caching behaviour. This is exactly why the revocation drill must be measured, not assumed.

Q: What is a "revocation drill" and why does the lesson require documenting one?
A: A revocation drill is a rehearsed procedure for disabling/revoking a key and verifying what becomes inaccessible and how quickly, ideally tested in a safe environment. It is required because in a real Schrems-style or breach scenario you may need to prove you *can* cut off access on demand — and you do not want to discover the mechanics during the incident. Documenting it turns "we have CMK" into "we have tested that revocation works in N minutes per store."

Q: A store you thought was independent breaks when you revoke a key — what does this reveal?
A: It reveals an undocumented key dependency: some service or data path you did not inventory was relying on that key (directly or via a chained CMK), so revoking it had a wider blast radius than expected. This is precisely the "a revoked key bricks a store you didn't realise depended on it" failure the lesson warns about. The cure is a complete inventory mapping each store to the key(s) it depends on before you ever revoke.

Q: What is an HSM and how does it differ from a software-based key store?
A: An HSM (hardware security module) is a tamper-resistant physical device that generates and stores key material and performs cryptographic operations inside hardware, so the key bytes never exist in extractable form in general-purpose memory. A software key store protects keys with software controls and the host's memory, which is weaker against a host compromise. HSMs provide higher assurance and are often required for the most sensitive keys or by certain regulations.

Q: What is the difference between Azure Key Vault (standard/Premium vault) and Azure Managed HSM?
A: A standard Key Vault is a low-cost, multi-tenant service that supports software-protected keys (and HSM-backed keys in the Premium SKU); Managed HSM is a single-tenant, fully managed service that provides dedicated HSM instances and supports *only* HSM-protected keys. Managed HSM gives you a higher assurance level and sole control over a dedicated HSM pool. You pay more and accept more operational responsibility for that isolation.

Q: What FIPS 140 validation levels apply to Azure's vault versus Managed HSM?
A: A standard Azure Key Vault is validated at FIPS 140-2 Level 2, while the Premium vault's HSM-backed keys and Managed HSM reach FIPS 140-2 Level 3 (Microsoft has also moved the HSM fleet to FIPS 140-3 Level 3 validated firmware). Level 3 adds physical tamper-resistance/response requirements beyond Level 2. For the highest-assurance fund keys, Level 3 via Premium or Managed HSM is the relevant tier.

Q: What does FIPS 140-2/140-3 Level 3 add over Level 2 in practical terms?
A: Level 3 adds stronger physical security — tamper detection and response that can zeroize (destroy) keys if the device is physically attacked — and stricter identity-based authentication for crypto operations. Level 2 only requires tamper-evidence (you can tell it was opened), not active tamper-response. So Level 3 is the meaningful step up when you need hardware that actively defends keys against physical compromise.

Q: When is Managed HSM the right choice over a standard vault for a fund administrator?
A: Choose Managed HSM when you need single-tenant isolation, FIPS 140-2/140-3 Level 3 assurance, sole administrative control over a dedicated HSM pool, or when a regulator/depositary explicitly requires hardware-backed key custody for specific data classes. For routine encryption-at-rest of lower-sensitivity stores, the multi-tenant standard vault is more cost-effective. The driver is the assurance requirement, not a blanket preference.

Q: Managed HSM supports only HSM-protected keys — what is the operational implication?
A: It means you cannot store cheap software-protected keys there, so everything in a Managed HSM carries HSM cost and HSM operational handling; it is purpose-built for high-assurance material, not as a general secrets bucket. You would still use a standard vault for secrets, certificates, and software keys, reserving Managed HSM for the KEKs that truly need Level 3. Mixing the tiers by sensitivity keeps cost and assurance aligned.

Q: What is client-side encryption and how does it differ from service-side encryption?
A: Client-side encryption means your application encrypts the data *before* it is sent to and stored by the cloud service, so the service only ever sees ciphertext and never holds the plaintext or, ideally, the key. Service-side (server-side) encryption means the cloud service encrypts data after receiving it, holding the keys (platform-managed or your CMK). Client-side gives the strongest confidentiality from the provider but moves all key handling and its consequences to you.

Q: Why does client-side encryption break query pushdown for analytics?
A: Because the warehouse or query engine sees only ciphertext, it cannot filter, range-scan, join, aggregate, or index on the encrypted columns — those operations require plaintext semantics the engine no longer has. So predicates that would normally be "pushed down" to the storage/engine for efficiency cannot be evaluated there, and you must pull data back to the trusted client to decrypt and process. This can turn an efficient pushdown query into a full scan plus client-side compute.

Q: A client-side-encrypted column needs to be filtered in a `WHERE` clause — why can't the warehouse do it efficiently?
A: The warehouse only has ciphertext, and standard encryption is non-deterministic and non-order-preserving, so `WHERE encrypted_isin = 'LU...'` or a range filter has no meaningful predicate to evaluate against the ciphertext. It cannot use indexes or partition pruning on that column. The practical result is the engine must return rows to the client, which decrypts and filters them, losing the efficiency of pushdown.

Q: What are the main tradeoffs of client-side encryption you must document?
A: You gain the strongest confidentiality from the cloud provider (the service never sees plaintext) but you lose query pushdown, indexing, server-side joins/aggregation, and warehouse-native features on the encrypted columns; you also take on all key-management responsibility and complexity. The analytics cost can be severe for columns you need to query. The lesson explicitly asks you to document this analytics impact so the tradeoff is a deliberate decision, not a surprise.

Q: When is client-side encryption worth its analytics cost, and when is it not?
A: It is worth it for a small number of highly sensitive fields you store but rarely query analytically (for example a raw national ID you only ever retrieve by primary key), where confidentiality from the provider outweighs query flexibility. It is a poor fit for columns you need to filter, join, aggregate, or index — there, CMK-based service-side encryption plus column masking usually gives a far better balance. The decision should be column-by-column, driven by how the field is used.

Q: How can you keep strong confidentiality without fully losing query ability on an encrypted column?
A: You can mix techniques: use deterministic encryption or tokenization for columns you must equality-join or group by (accepting weaker confidentiality than randomized encryption), keep randomized client-side encryption only for fields you never query, and combine CMK service-side encryption with column masking for the rest. Each choice trades a precise amount of confidentiality for a precise amount of queryability. The architect's job is to pick the right point per column rather than applying one blanket scheme.

Q: What is the relationship between envelope encryption and the KMS hierarchy — are they the same thing?
A: Envelope encryption is the *technique* (wrap a DEK with a KEK), while the KMS hierarchy (vault root → KEK → DEK) is the *layered implementation* of that technique with each tier in a progressively stronger boundary. The hierarchy is envelope encryption applied recursively and operationalized inside a managed service. Understanding both lets you reason about where each key lives and what a compromise at each tier would expose.

Q: If an attacker steals a backup blob plus its wrapped DEK but has no vault access, what can they decrypt?
A: Nothing — the data is ciphertext and the DEK is wrapped, so without the KEK (which lives only in the vault) they cannot unwrap the DEK and therefore cannot decrypt the payload. This is the core security guarantee of envelope encryption: stealing storage is not enough. It is why the KEK access policy, not the storage, is the trust boundary you defend hardest.

Q: If an attacker compromises the KEK, what is exposed?
A: Every DEK wrapped by that KEK can be unwrapped, so all data protected under those DEKs is exposed — the KEK is the high-value single point whose compromise has the largest blast radius. This is exactly why the KEK lives in an HSM/vault, never leaves it, has tight access policy, and is audited. It also justifies KEK rotation and the option of a Level 3 HSM for the most sensitive KEKs.

Q: Why is it bad practice to use one KEK for every data class across the whole organisation?
A: Because a single KEK becomes a maximal blast radius — its compromise or accidental revocation affects everything, and you cannot revoke access to one data class without affecting all of them. Segmenting KEKs per data class or per tenant lets you scope revocation, rotation, and access policy independently. For a fund administrator this means, for example, separating the KEK protecting investor PII from the one protecting public reference data.

Q: How does the key hierarchy enable "crypto-shredding" (deleting data by destroying keys)?
A: Crypto-shredding means rendering data permanently unrecoverable by destroying the key needed to decrypt it rather than overwriting the data itself, which is invaluable when the data is replicated across backups and regions. If a given investor's records are protected by a dedicated DEK/KEK, destroying that key makes all copies of their data unreadable at once. This is a practical GDPR erasure mechanism, but it only works if your key granularity matches your deletion granularity.

Q: What is the inventory step the "Do" section asks for, and why is it first?
A: It asks you to inventory which Azure stores hold which data classes — mapping each store to the sensitivity of the data in it (investor PII, NAV/financial, public reference, operational). It is first because you cannot decide CMK-vs-platform-managed, key residency, or revocation impact without knowing what each store holds. Classification drives every subsequent key decision; skipping it means making key choices blind.

Q: Why must the CMK-versus-platform-managed decision be defensible "in writing"?
A: Because in a regulated fund context an auditor or depositary will ask you to justify why a given store holding investor data uses (or does not use) customer-managed keys, and "we just defaulted it" is not an acceptable answer. Writing the justification forces you to tie each choice to data classification and risk, and gives you an artifact to present in a review. It also captures the operational risk you accepted with each CMK.

Q: For a store holding transfer-agency investor records, would you choose CMK or platform-managed keys, and why?
A: CMK, because transfer-agency records contain investor PII and beneficial-ownership data where you may need to prove key control, produce an audit trail, or revoke access for a sovereignty or breach scenario. The control benefits (revocation, audit, rotation control) directly map to the regulatory questions you will be asked about that data. You then accept and document the added availability dependency on the key vault.

Q: For an ephemeral build-artifact store with no PII, would you choose CMK or platform-managed keys?
A: Platform-managed keys, because the data carries no PII and no sovereignty obligation, so the control benefits of CMK do not apply while the operational cost and availability risk would be pure overhead. Encryption at rest still happens by default; you simply do not take on key custody you do not need. This is the kind of "justify each decision" reasoning the lesson wants — defaulting low-sensitivity stores to platform-managed is the correct, defensible choice.

Q: What is the danger of accidentally purging a key in a vault, and how does Azure protect against it?
A: Purging permanently destroys the key, which would make any data still wrapped under it undecryptable — an irreversible data-loss event. Azure protects against this with soft-delete (a deleted key remains recoverable for a retention period of 7 to 90 days, default 90) and optional purge protection, which blocks permanent deletion until the retention period elapses. For any vault holding CMKs that protect real fund data, enabling purge protection is a standard safeguard.

Q: What is soft-delete in Azure Key Vault and what is its default retention period?
A: Soft-delete means a deleted key vault or key object is not immediately destroyed but moves to a recoverable state for a configurable retention period of 7 to 90 days, defaulting to 90 days. During that window you can recover an accidentally deleted key and avoid the data-loss that destroying a KEK would cause. The retention period is set at vault creation and cannot be changed afterward, so you choose it deliberately up front.

Q: What is purge protection in Azure Key Vault and when must you decide on it?
A: Purge protection enforces the soft-delete retention period so that even a privileged insider cannot permanently purge a deleted vault or key before the period elapses, defending against malicious deletion. It is optional and off by default and can only be enabled once soft-delete is on. For vaults holding CMKs that protect production fund data, you typically enable it early, because key destruction is one of the few truly irreversible operations.

Q: A NAV calculation job suddenly fails with a key-access error after a routine rotation — what is the first thing to check?
A: Check whether the job references a *versioned* key ID that is pinned to a now-non-current version (or whether its access to the vault/key was changed), versus the versionless ID that would have followed the rotation. Rotation breaking a consumer almost always traces to version pinning or an access-policy change. Confirm the new key version exists and the job's identity still has unwrap permission before suspecting the data itself.

Q: An Azure storage account configured with CMK suddenly returns errors and the data is inaccessible — what are the likely key-related causes?
A: The most likely causes are that the CMK was disabled, expired, rotated in a way the storage account did not pick up, deleted, or that the storage account's managed identity lost access permission to the vault — any of which stops the service from unwrapping its DEKs. Network/firewall changes blocking the vault are another cause. You verify the key is enabled and accessible and that the service identity still has the required key permissions.

Q: Why should the identity that uses a key for crypto operations generally not also be able to export or delete it?
A: Separation of duties: limiting an application's identity to wrap/unwrap (use) permissions while reserving key management/deletion to a separate administrative role means a compromised app cannot exfiltrate or destroy the key. This shrinks the blast radius of an application compromise to "can decrypt what it was already allowed to" rather than "can steal the master key." Azure RBAC/access policies on Key Vault are how you enforce this split.

Q: How does encryption at rest with CMK interact with backups and replicas?
A: Backups and replicas of a CMK-encrypted store remain ciphertext and still depend on the CMK to be readable, so a backup is only restorable while the key is available — and crypto-shredding by destroying the key affects backups too. This is powerful for guaranteed erasure but dangerous if you lose the key, because backups will not save you. You must therefore protect the key with at least the same care as the backups themselves.

Q: Why is "we encrypt everything at rest by default" not a complete answer to a compliance reviewer?
A: Because default encryption at rest uses platform-managed keys, which means the provider controls the key — so it does not answer the reviewer's real question of "who can produce plaintext and can you revoke that access?" A complete answer addresses key custody (CMK vs platform), where keys live, rotation, and revocation. Encryption-at-rest is necessary but the *key control story* is what the review actually probes.

Q: What does TLS protect that at-rest encryption does not, in the context of a SWIFT or file-transfer link?
A: TLS protects the data while it is in motion across the link — preventing eavesdropping and tampering on the wire — whereas at-rest encryption only protects it once written to disk at either end. A SWIFT message or fund file moving over an untrusted network is exposed precisely during transit, which is the gap TLS closes. Relying on at-rest encryption alone leaves the transmission itself unprotected.

Q: Is a key vault's audit log a control-plane or data-plane concern, and why does it matter for fund compliance?
A: It is primarily a data-plane and control-plane logging concern — recording who used a key for which crypto operation (data plane) and who changed key configuration or access (control plane). It matters because for regulated fund data the audit trail of key usage is often the evidence an auditor or depositary requests to prove key control. CMK gives you ownership of this log; platform-managed keys do not.

Q: How does the choice between deterministic and randomized encryption affect both security and analytics?
A: Deterministic encryption always maps the same plaintext to the same ciphertext, which preserves equality joins and grouping but leaks which values are equal (and is vulnerable to frequency analysis); randomized encryption hides equality and is more secure but destroys joinability on that column. So you trade confidentiality for queryability. For a column like a tokenized investor ID that must join across tables, deterministic is often necessary despite its weaker confidentiality.

Q: Why does the lesson tie key management so tightly to availability rather than just confidentiality?
A: Because in envelope-encryption systems the key is a hard dependency for *reading* the data, so a lost, disabled, purged, or revoked key is not just a security event — it is an availability/data-loss event. The same control that lets you revoke access can, if mishandled, lock you out of your own fund data. Treating key management as both a confidentiality and availability concern is what prevents a key mistake from becoming an outage.

Q: What is the difference between "key vault key" and a "secret" stored in a key vault?
A: A key vault *key* is cryptographic key material used for wrap/unwrap/sign/verify operations where the key never leaves the vault, whereas a *secret* is an arbitrary blob (a password, connection string, API token) that the vault stores and returns to you in plaintext on request. Keys are for crypto operations performed inside the boundary; secrets are values you retrieve and use elsewhere. Confusing the two leads to, for example, treating a KEK like a retrievable secret, which would break the "never leaves the vault" guarantee.

Q: Why should you not store a DEK as a plaintext secret in a key vault?
A: Because a secret is returned to callers in plaintext, so storing a DEK that way means anyone with secret-read permission can retrieve the raw DEK and decrypt the data, defeating the envelope model. The correct pattern is to store the DEK only in wrapped form (encrypted by the KEK) and have the vault unwrap it via a key operation, so the plaintext DEK exists only transiently in the client. The distinction between "key" and "secret" operations is exactly what enforces this.

Q: In a multi-region fund platform, why does key residency matter for sovereignty?
A: Because where the keys (and the HSM protecting them) physically reside can determine which jurisdiction's authorities could compel access, and EU fund firms often need keys to reside in the EU (or in their own custody) to support a sovereignty argument. Data residency without key residency is incomplete — the key is what unlocks the data. The lesson's "specify where keys live" step exists precisely to make residency a deliberate, documented choice.

Q: What does "specify where keys live" mean as a concrete deliverable?
A: It means documenting, per store/key, the exact location and custody of the KEK — which vault or Managed HSM, in which region, under whose control, and at what assurance tier (software, HSM-backed, Managed HSM) — so the residency and custody are explicit rather than implied. This feeds both the sovereignty story and the revocation/availability analysis. It turns "keys are in Azure somewhere" into an auditable map.

Q: Why is rotation cadence a per-key-class decision rather than one global number?
A: Because the right cadence depends on the key's exposure and sensitivity: a KEK protecting investor PII may warrant annual or more frequent rotation and a tested drill, while a low-sensitivity operational key can rotate rarely. Applying one global cadence either over-rotates low-risk keys (operational churn) or under-rotates high-risk ones (compliance gap). Tying cadence to data class keeps the effort proportionate to the risk.

Q: How would you map dependencies before rotating a KEK that protects several stores?
A: Enumerate every store, service, pipeline, and cached consumer that uses the key, identify whether each references the versioned or versionless ID, confirm each consumer's identity still has unwrap permission, and note any that pin to a specific version and would not auto-rotate. This dependency map *is* the blast radius. You rotate only after confirming the map, and you watch those consumers during and after rotation.

Q: A team proposes client-side encrypting the entire investor table to "be safe" — what is your architectural pushback?
A: Encrypting the entire table client-side would break warehouse-native filtering, joins, aggregation, and indexing on those columns, turning normal analytics into full scans plus client-side decryption and crippling reporting performance. The better design is CMK service-side encryption for the whole table plus targeted column masking, reserving client-side encryption for the one or two fields that are stored but essentially never queried. The pushback is to apply confidentiality per column based on how each is used, not as a blanket scheme.

Q: How do data masking and encryption differ in purpose, even though both protect sensitive fields?
A: Encryption makes data unreadable without a key and is reversible by anyone holding the key, protecting confidentiality of the stored bytes; masking transforms or hides values (often irreversibly or per-role) so that even authorized data readers see only what they need. Encryption answers "can the storage/provider read it?"; masking answers "should this particular user, who can read the table, see this value?" They operate at different layers and are used together, not interchangeably.

Q: Why does using your own CMK not stop Microsoft from operating the storage service or seeing metadata?
A: Because CMK only governs the encryption key for data at rest — Microsoft still runs the storage service, handles requests, and may process operational metadata; the CMK does not change the operating model, only key custody. So CMK strengthens your "we can revoke plaintext access" argument but is not a complete sovereignty or isolation control. Recognising this prevents overclaiming what CMK delivers in a compliance review.

Q: What is the practical meaning of "Bring Your Own Key" (BYOK) versus simply generating a CMK in the vault?
A: BYOK means you generate the key material in your own HSM and securely import it into the cloud KMS, so you can assert the key originated under your control rather than being generated by the provider; generating a CMK in the vault has the provider's HSM create it. BYOK can strengthen a custody/sovereignty argument but adds key-ceremony complexity and import risk. Both result in a customer-managed key; the difference is provenance of the key material.

Q: Why is "encryption" alone an insufficient answer to "is this fund data protected at rest?"
A: Because protection depends on the whole key-management story — who holds the KEK, where it lives, how it rotates, whether it can be revoked, and whether the data can be recovered if the key is lost — not merely on the fact that AES is applied. Two stores can both be "encrypted at rest" yet have wildly different protection postures based on key custody. The architect's job is to evaluate the key management, not check a box that says encryption is on.

Q: What is the risk of caching unwrapped DEKs in application memory, and how do you balance it?
A: Caching unwrapped DEKs improves performance by avoiding a vault round-trip per operation, but it widens the window in which a plaintext key sits in memory and delays the effect of revocation. You balance it with a short cache TTL, memory protection/zeroization, and awareness that revocation latency equals roughly the cache lifetime. The tradeoff is performance versus prompt revocability, and you should know which you chose.

Q: How does TLS protect the path to the key vault itself, and why is that important?
A: Calls to the key vault for wrap/unwrap and key retrieval travel over TLS, so the DEKs and operations crossing to the vault are encrypted in transit and cannot be observed or tampered with on the wire. This is important because the vault interaction is exactly where small but critical key material moves. If that path were plaintext, an attacker on the network could capture DEKs in transit even though the bulk data is protected.

Q: For an ISIN-keyed reference table that is public information, what encryption posture is appropriate?
A: Encryption at rest with platform-managed keys is appropriate, because public ISIN reference data carries no confidentiality or sovereignty obligation, so CMK control benefits do not apply and would be pure overhead. TLS in transit still applies as table stakes. The point is to align key effort with data sensitivity — not every store needs CMK, and a public reference table is a clear platform-managed case.

Q: What happens to data encrypted under a key if you migrate it to a new key but the old key is deleted prematurely?
A: If the migration (re-encryption or re-wrapping) is not fully complete and you delete the old key, any data or DEK still wrapped under the old key becomes permanently undecryptable. This is a classic migration foot-gun. The safe rule is to retire the old key only after verifying that nothing still depends on it, which is why soft-delete and purge protection exist as backstops.

Q: Why is it important that key rotation be "automated and its blast radius understood" rather than just automated?
A: Automation makes rotation reliable and timely, but if you do not understand which consumers depend on the key, an automatic rotation can silently break a downstream pipeline or leave a version-pinned consumer stranded on the old key. Automation without blast-radius understanding trades manual error for invisible breakage. You need both: the schedule fires automatically *and* the dependency map tells you what to watch.

Q: How does the assurance tier you choose (software vault vs HSM vs Managed HSM) map to data classes in a fund administrator?
A: Public/low-sensitivity data can use software-protected keys in a standard vault; investor PII and beneficial-ownership data warrant HSM-backed keys (Premium vault, FIPS 140-2/3 Level 3); and the most regulated or sovereignty-critical key material may warrant a single-tenant Managed HSM. The tier should rise with the sensitivity and the regulatory assurance demanded. Over-provisioning every store to Managed HSM wastes money; under-provisioning sensitive keys creates a compliance gap.

Q: What is the danger of a "key sprawl" where many teams create their own vaults and keys?
A: Key sprawl fragments your audit trail, makes it impossible to answer "where are all our keys and who can use them?", and increases the chance that some store depends on a forgotten, unmanaged key. It undermines centralized policy, rotation, and the very inventory the lesson requires. The remedy is a governed key-management practice with a known set of vaults, classification-driven CMK decisions, and a maintained store-to-key inventory.

Q: Why does the lesson frame revocation speed as something you must *measure*, not assume?
A: Because the real lockout time depends on caching, replication, and service behaviour, the only way to truthfully answer "how fast?" is to test it on a representative store and observe when reads actually start failing. Assuming "instant" can mislead an incident response or a compliance claim. Measuring it converts a guess into a defensible, documented number per store.

Q: How would you prove to an auditor that you can revoke access to a specific fund-data store's key on demand?
A: Show the documented revocation drill, the test results recording how the store became undecryptable and how quickly after disabling the CMK, the access policy demonstrating only authorized roles can manage the key, and the audit log capturing the revocation event. Together these demonstrate both capability and control. This is exactly what the "answer how fast revocation takes per store" done-when condition produces.

Q: What is the relationship between the KEK and a Managed HSM's own protection of that KEK?
A: When the KEK is held in a Managed HSM, the KEK material itself is generated and stored inside the FIPS 140-2/140-3 Level 3 hardware and never leaves it in plaintext, so the HSM is the hardware boundary protecting the KEK that in turn protects the DEKs. This nests the envelope model inside hardware: data ← DEK ← KEK ← HSM. The higher you push the KEK in assurance, the stronger the root of trust for everything beneath it.

Q: A client-side encryption scheme is proposed for a column that analysts frequently aggregate — what specific failure will they hit?
A: Aggregations like `SUM`, `AVG`, `GROUP BY`, and range filters on that column cannot run in the warehouse because it sees only randomized ciphertext with no arithmetic or ordering semantics, so the engine must return all rows to a trusted client to decrypt and compute. Performance collapses from a server-side aggregation to a full extract plus client-side compute. This is the concrete analytics impact the lesson asks you to document before choosing client-side encryption.

Q: Why might a regulator or depositary specifically ask about HSM backing rather than just "is it encrypted?"
A: Because HSM backing (especially FIPS 140-2/140-3 Level 3) provides hardware-assured key custody with tamper-response, which is a meaningfully higher protection level than software-protected keys for the most sensitive material. A sophisticated reviewer distinguishes "encrypted with a software key the provider could in principle access" from "key bound in tamper-resistant hardware under your control." The question probes the *assurance tier* of your key custody, not merely whether encryption exists.

Q: What is the correct mental model for "the key is the data" in a crypto-shredding context?
A: Once data is encrypted, the data is only as accessible as its key, so controlling and ultimately destroying the key is functionally equivalent to controlling and destroying the data — even across copies you cannot reach. This makes the key a powerful lever for guaranteed erasure (GDPR) but also a single point of catastrophic loss. The discipline is to treat the key with the same lifecycle rigor — backup, custody, deletion controls — that you would the most sensitive data itself.

Q: Why is AES-256 the typical algorithm for the DEK, and does the KEK have to use the same algorithm?
A: AES-256 is a fast, well-vetted symmetric cipher suited to encrypting bulk data at line speed, which is exactly the DEK's job. The KEK does not have to match — it can be a separate symmetric key or an asymmetric (RSA/EC) key in the KMS, because it only wraps tiny DEKs rather than bulk data, so throughput is irrelevant and an asymmetric KEK can even allow public-key wrapping where the unwrap private key never leaves the HSM. The two tiers are chosen independently: speed for the DEK, isolation and policy for the KEK.

Q: A vendor claims their managed data service is "encrypted, fully compliant" but cannot tell you who holds the key — how do you assess that claim?
A: Treat the claim as incomplete until you know the key custody model, because "encrypted" with a provider-held key does not answer whether you can revoke access, audit key use, or assert sovereignty. Ask whether CMK/BYOK is supported, where the KEK resides, what assurance tier (software vs HSM) protects it, and how rotation and revocation work. For regulated fund data, a service that offers no customer key control may be unacceptable for the most sensitive classes regardless of the "compliant" label.

Q: During a DORA-style operational-resilience review, why is the key vault itself a critical dependency you must include in continuity planning?
A: Because in an envelope-encryption design the vault holding the KEK is on the critical path for reading the data — if the vault is unavailable, services cannot unwrap DEKs and the dependent fund-data stores effectively go offline. DORA's focus on ICT resilience means you must plan for vault availability, region failover/replication of keys, and recovery (soft-delete, backups) just as you would for the data stores themselves. An unmanaged single-region vault is a concentration risk that a resilience review will flag.

Q: Summarize the end-to-end key-management story you must be able to tell for each sensitive fund-data store.
A: For each store you should be able to state: what data class it holds, whether it uses CMK or platform-managed keys and why, where the KEK lives (which vault/Managed HSM, region, assurance tier), the rotation cadence and whether consumers use versionless references, exactly what happens and how fast if you revoke the key, and the analytics impact of any client-side encryption. That is the inventory, the justified CMK decisions, the residency and rotation specification, and the tested revocation drill the lesson's done-when conditions require. Being able to tell that story per store — in writing — is the deliverable of this lesson.
