# Phase 5 · 8.1.1 Azure reference architecture (WAF + CAF landing zones) — 100 self-test questions

Q: What is the Azure Well-Architected Framework (WAF), and what is its purpose for a data platform?
A: The Azure Well-Architected Framework is Microsoft's set of design principles and best practices, organised into five pillars, for building high-quality cloud workloads. It exists to give architects a shared vocabulary and a structured way to assess trade-offs — for a fund-administration data platform it is the language used in architecture reviews, RFPs, and CSSF-aligned governance gates. You run a design against it to surface concrete findings before they become production incidents.

Q: Name the five pillars of the Azure Well-Architected Framework in their current Microsoft naming.
A: The five pillars are Reliability, Security, Cost Optimization, Operational Excellence, and Performance Efficiency. Each pillar has its own design principles, a tradeoffs page, and per-service guidance on Microsoft Learn. A common mnemonic is that they cover keeping the workload up, safe, affordable, well-run, and fast.

Q: What workload concern does the Reliability pillar address?
A: Reliability addresses resiliency, availability, and recovery — the workload's ability to meet its uptime commitments and recover from failures. Its design principles ask you to design for business requirements, build in resilience and recovery, simplify, and operate the workload deliberately. For a NAV-calculation platform, reliability is what lets you defend an RTO/RPO commitment to clients.

Q: What does the Security pillar of WAF focus on?
A: The Security pillar focuses on protecting the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of the workload and its data — data protection, threat detection, and mitigation. It pushes you toward defence in depth, least privilege, and assuming breach. In a regulated fund estate this is where data-residency, encryption, and segregation-of-duties arguments live.

Q: What is the central question the Cost Optimization pillar asks?
A: Cost Optimization asks whether you are getting the most value for every euro spent — through cost modelling, budgets, and eliminating waste. It balances usage optimisation (turning off or right-sizing what you do not need) against rate optimisation (reservations, savings plans). The pillar wants a cost-efficient mindset baked into design decisions, not bolted on after the bill arrives.

Q: What does the Operational Excellence pillar cover?
A: Operational Excellence covers how you run the workload — holistic observability, DevOps practices, standards, comprehensive monitoring, and safe deployment practices. It is about reducing operational toil and making change safe and repeatable. For an audited platform, this pillar maps directly to your IaC, CI/CD gates, and the four-eyes review on infrastructure change.

Q: What is the focus of the Performance Efficiency pillar?
A: Performance Efficiency focuses on a workload's ability to scale to meet demand — scalability, load testing, and monitoring solution health. Its principles favour horizontal scale, testing early and often, and right-sizing to actual load. For a data platform it governs how a NAV batch or a query workload behaves as fund volumes grow.

Q: What is the WAF "data-workload lens" and why does it matter?
A: The data-workload lens is the practice of reading each generic pillar through the specific demands of a data platform rather than a generic app. Reliability becomes "can I recover the data and re-run the pipeline", Security becomes "is the data classified, encrypted, and access-controlled", Performance becomes "do queries and batch loads scale". It matters because a one-size-fits-all WAF review misses data-specific risks like lineage, late-arriving data, and reprocessing.

Q: Which Microsoft tool lets you formally assess a workload against the five pillars?
A: The Azure Well-Architected Review is the free self-assessment tool at the Microsoft assessments site; it walks you through pillar-aligned questions and produces prioritised recommendations. It is the canonical artefact an architecture review board expects to see. You can re-run it as the design evolves to track posture over time.

Q: How does Azure Advisor relate to the Well-Architected Framework?
A: Azure Advisor analyses your live resource configuration and usage telemetry and emits recommendations categorised against the WAF pillars. The Advisor score aggregates those recommendations into a single actionable number you can break down per pillar. It is the runtime, telemetry-driven complement to the design-time Well-Architected Review.

Q: In a WAF review, what makes a "finding" useful rather than just an observation?
A: A useful finding names a specific gap tied to a pillar plus a concrete remediation and the Azure control that closes it — for example "data at rest is not zone-resilient (Reliability); remediate by moving storage to ZRS". An observation without a remediation and an owner is not actionable. The Phase 5 Do-step explicitly asks for one finding plus a remediation per pillar, five minimum.

Q: For your Capstone-4 architecture, give an example of a Security-pillar finding and its Azure remediation.
A: A typical finding is "service-to-service calls use connection strings stored in app config, exposing secrets". The remediation is to switch to managed identities so no secret exists, and to put any remaining secrets in Key Vault referenced at runtime. Naming the control (managed identity / Key Vault) is what turns the finding into a closeable item.

Q: What is a Cost Optimization finding you might raise on an over-provisioned NAV batch cluster?
A: A finding could be "the batch compute runs 24/7 but NAV is struck once per business day, so 90% of compute hours are idle". The remediation is to schedule the cluster to scale to zero outside the batch window, or move to a serverless/consumption tier. The mechanism is matching rate and usage to the actual once-a-day demand curve.

Q: What is the Cloud Adoption Framework (CAF) and how does it differ from WAF?
A: The Cloud Adoption Framework is Microsoft's guidance for adopting and governing Azure at organisation scale — strategy, planning, landing zones, governance, and operations. WAF is about the quality of an individual workload; CAF is about the environment that workloads land in. You use CAF to build the landing zone and WAF to build well inside it.

Q: Define an Azure landing zone in CAF terms.
A: An Azure landing zone is the standardised, repeatable environment — subscriptions with consistent identity, networking, security, governance, and monitoring already in place — into which you deploy workloads. It is the well-architected foundation aligned to CAF's design principles across eight design areas. The point is that every workload inherits the same controls instead of each team reinventing them.

Q: What are the two categories of landing zone, and what does each hold?
A: There are platform landing zones and application landing zones. A platform landing zone hosts shared services — identity, connectivity, and management — operated by central teams; application landing zones host individual workloads, one per environment as needed. An Azure landing zone consists of one platform landing zone plus one or more application landing zones.

Q: What three shared services typically live in the platform landing zone?
A: Identity, connectivity, and management are the canonical platform landing zone services, usually split into an Identity subscription, a Connectivity subscription, and a Management subscription. Consolidating them under central teams improves operational efficiency and enforces consistency. Workloads then consume these shared services rather than standing up their own.

Q: What is the management-group hierarchy and what is it for?
A: Management groups are containers above subscriptions that let you apply Azure Policy and RBAC to many subscriptions at once, in an inheritable tree. The CAF reference hierarchy nests platform subscriptions under a Platform management group and workload subscriptions under landing-zone groups such as `Online`, `Corp`, or `Local`. It is how governance scales without touching each subscription by hand.

Q: Why is the subscription treated as the "unit of management" in a landing zone?
A: The subscription is the natural boundary for billing, policy assignment, RBAC scope, and service quotas/limits, so it is the unit you provision, govern, and scale by. Splitting workloads or environments into separate subscriptions gives clean cost attribution and blast-radius isolation. In CAF, application landing zones are pre-provisioned subscriptions handed to workload teams via a subscription-vending process.

Q: What role does Azure Policy play in landing-zone governance?
A: Azure Policy enforces rules — allowed regions, required tags, mandated encryption, denied SKUs — declaratively across whatever scope it is assigned to, and management-group assignment makes it inherit down the tree. It is the mechanism that turns governance intent into automatically-enforced guardrails rather than documentation. For a CSSF-aligned estate, a policy that denies resource creation outside EU regions is a data-residency control you can demonstrate to auditors.

Q: List the eight CAF design areas of an Azure landing zone.
A: The eight design areas are Azure billing and Microsoft Entra tenant, identity and access management, management group and subscription organisation, network topology and connectivity, security, management, governance, and platform automation and DevOps. They are the dimensions you must decide on to stand up a landing zone. The reference architecture illustrates how they relate to one another.

Q: What is "subscription vending" in CAF?
A: Subscription vending is the codified, repeatable process by which the platform team pre-provisions application landing zone subscriptions — already wired into management groups, policy, networking, and identity — and hands them to workload teams. It avoids ad-hoc subscription creation that bypasses governance. It is typically implemented in IaC so every new subscription is born compliant.

Q: A workload team asks for a new subscription outside the landing-zone process. What is the architectural risk?
A: An out-of-process subscription does not inherit the management-group policies, so it can deploy resources in non-EU regions, skip required encryption or tags, and sit outside central monitoring — breaking your data-residency and segregation arguments. The fix is to route it through subscription vending so it lands under the correct management group with policy inherited. This is exactly the "designs leak across subscription/identity boundaries" failure the lesson warns about.

Q: What is the hub-and-spoke network topology?
A: Hub-and-spoke is a topology where a central hub VNet holds shared connectivity and security services (firewall, gateways, DNS, ExpressRoute/VPN) and workload spoke VNets peer to it. Spokes reach on-prem and each other through the hub, centralising inspection and shared services. It is one of the two reference topologies CAF documents for the connectivity landing zone.

Q: What is Azure Virtual WAN and when is it preferred over classic hub-and-spoke?
A: Azure Virtual WAN is a Microsoft-managed networking service that provides a global transit hub with automated any-to-any connectivity for branches, VNets, and users. It is preferred over hand-built hub-and-spoke when you have many regions or many on-prem sites and want Microsoft to manage the transit routing and scaling. CAF documents both Virtual WAN and hub-and-spoke as reference topologies; the choice is operational-effort vs control.

Q: Why do shared services like identity and connectivity live in the platform landing zone rather than in each workload?
A: Centralising identity and connectivity means every workload inherits one consistent, audited control plane instead of each team configuring its own firewall, DNS, and identity — which would drift and leave gaps. It also lets a small central team operate connectivity and identity at scale and enforce policy uniformly. The trade-off is a dependency on the platform team, which is acceptable because these services are genuinely cross-cutting.

Q: In hub-and-spoke, why is VNet peering preferred over routing all spoke traffic through a single VNet?
A: VNet peering gives low-latency, high-bandwidth private connectivity between the hub and each spoke without traversing a gateway, and traffic stays on the Microsoft backbone. Spokes do not peer directly to each other by default, so the hub remains the inspection and routing chokepoint. This keeps the security boundary in one place while still giving spokes fast paths to shared services.

Q: A transfer-agency feed must reach an on-prem mainframe over a private link. Where does that connectivity belong in the landing zone?
A: It belongs in the connectivity/platform landing zone hub, terminating the ExpressRoute or VPN gateway there, with the workload spoke peering to the hub to reach on-prem. Putting the gateway in the shared hub means one audited, monitored ingress for all on-prem traffic rather than per-workload tunnels. The transfer-agency workload spoke then routes through the hub, inheriting the central firewall and DNS.

Q: What is Microsoft Entra ID and what is its role in the landing-zone identity baseline?
A: Microsoft Entra ID (formerly Azure Active Directory) is Azure's cloud identity and access management service — it issues tokens, authenticates users and workloads, and backs RBAC. In the landing zone it is the single identity plane that every subscription and workload trusts, anchored in the Entra tenant design area. It is what makes managed identities and conditional access possible across the estate.

Q: What is a managed identity in Azure?
A: A managed identity is an Entra ID identity automatically assigned to an Azure resource so the resource can obtain Entra tokens and call other services without any stored secret. It eliminates the developer burden of managing credentials, keys, and certificates, which are a known source of outages and breaches. Managed identities are available at no extra cost.

Q: Why are managed identities called the "no-secret default" for service-to-service auth?
A: Because the credential is never created, stored, or seen by anyone — Azure injects and rotates it for the resource, and code retrieves a token via the Azure.Identity SDK or MSAL with no secret in config, code, or state. Removing the secret removes the largest class of credential-leak and rotation-failure incidents. This is why landing-zone designs default to managed identity for service-to-service calls.

Q: What is the difference between a system-assigned and a user-assigned managed identity?
A: A system-assigned identity is created on, and tied to the lifecycle of, a single resource — delete the resource and the identity is deleted, and it cannot be shared. A user-assigned identity is a standalone Azure resource with an independent lifecycle that can be attached to many resources at once. Microsoft recommends user-assigned identities for most services because they survive resource recycling and can be pre-authorised.

Q: When should you prefer a user-assigned managed identity?
A: Prefer user-assigned when several resources should share one identity and one set of role assignments, when resources are recycled frequently but permissions must stay stable, or when you need to pre-authorise access before the consuming resource exists (provisioning flows). For example, three NAV-worker VMs that all read the same fund-data storage account can share one user-assigned identity. This avoids re-granting RBAC every time a VM is replaced.

Q: When is a system-assigned managed identity the better fit?
A: A system-assigned identity fits a workload contained in a single resource that needs its own independent identity and where automatic cleanup on deletion is desirable. Because its service principal is deleted with the resource, you cannot leave orphaned identities behind. The trade-off is that it cannot be shared and is re-created (with a new principal) if the resource is rebuilt.

Q: What underlying Entra object is created when you enable a managed identity?
A: A service principal of a special managed-identity type is created in Microsoft Entra ID to represent the identity; for system-assigned it shares the resource's name and lifecycle, for user-assigned it is independent. You then grant that service principal RBAC roles on target resources. Token requests and sign-in activity for the identity appear in the Entra sign-in logs, and CRUD operations appear in the Azure Activity log.

Q: How does code actually obtain a token from a managed identity, and what library is used?
A: Service code uses the Azure.Identity SDK (via `DefaultAzureCredential` or `ManagedIdentityCredential`) or MSAL to request an Entra token; the runtime resolves the identity from the environment with no secret supplied. As long as the identity holds the right RBAC role, the SDK returns a token to call the downstream service. The absence of any secret in the call is the whole point.

Q: What is a Federated Identity Credential (FIC) using a managed identity, and what limit applies?
A: Workload Identity Federation lets a managed identity act as a credential on an Entra ID application, replacing a certificate or password so the app stays credential-free. The workload uses its managed identity to get a token, then exchanges it for an Entra application token via federation. There is a limit of 20 federated identity credentials when using managed identities as FIC on a single Entra app.

Q: Do managed identities cost extra, and what is the practical implication?
A: No — managed identities can be used at no extra cost, so there is no financial reason to fall back on stored secrets. The practical implication is that "secrets are cheaper" is never a valid argument against adopting them. The only cost is the small design effort to assign RBAC correctly.

Q: A pipeline reading fund-data blobs fails with a 403 even though the managed identity exists. What is the first thing to check?
A: First check the RBAC role assignment — the identity existing is not enough; it must hold a data-plane role such as `Storage Blob Data Reader` on the storage account or container, and the assignment may take a few minutes to propagate. A 403 (`AuthorizationPermissionMismatch`) almost always means missing or wrong data-plane role rather than a token problem. Confirm you granted a data role, not just `Reader`, which only grants control-plane access.

Q: What is an Azure availability zone?
A: An availability zone is a logical grouping of one or more physically separate datacentres within a region, each with independent power, cooling, and networking. If one zone has an outage, services in the other zones keep running, so zones protect against datacentre-scoped failures up to a whole-zone loss. Zones are typically a few kilometres apart and usually within 100 km, giving low-latency links.

Q: What is the typical inter-zone latency target, and why does it matter?
A: Microsoft strives for round-trip inter-zone latency under approximately 2 milliseconds. That low latency is what makes synchronous replication across zones feasible — for example ZRS storage writing to three zones before acknowledging. It means most workloads can spread across zones with no noticeable performance hit, though latency-sensitive components should be tested.

Q: What is the difference between a zonal and a zone-redundant resource?
A: A zonal resource is deployed into a single availability zone that you pick; it is isolated from other zones' faults but you must build your own multi-zone redundancy and handle failover. A zone-redundant resource is automatically replicated or distributed across multiple zones by the service, and Microsoft manages the spread and failover. Zone-redundant is the default choice for resilience; zonal is for latency-sensitive co-location.

Q: When is a resource considered "zone-resilient"?
A: A resource is zone-resilient when it is configured as zone-redundant, or when you run multiple instances of a zonal resource spread across two or more zones. Zone resilience means it survives the loss of a single availability zone. A single zonal instance is not zone-resilient — losing that zone takes it down.

Q: What is a "nonzonal" or "regional" deployment, and what is its risk?
A: A nonzonal deployment is one not configured for zones — Azure may place it in any zone of the region, and you do not control which. If the zone it happens to land in fails, the resource can go down, because it has no cross-zone redundancy. It is the weakest option in a zone-capable region and should be avoided for production.

Q: What are physical versus logical availability zones, and why can the mapping differ between subscriptions?
A: Each datacentre belongs to a physical zone, but those physical zones are mapped to logical zone numbers (1, 2, 3) independently per subscription, assigned at subscription creation. So "zone 1" in subscription A may be a different datacentre than "zone 1" in subscription B. To co-locate resources across subscriptions you must resolve the mapping using `az account list-locations` with the `availabilityZoneMappings` query or the Check Zone Peers API.

Q: What is an Azure region pair?
A: A region pair is an association Microsoft defines between two Azure regions, usually within the same geography, used by some services for geo-replication and geo-redundancy and to support certain disaster-recovery behaviours. Pairs provide a prioritised recovery sequence and staggered platform updates. Only a subset of services actually use pairs; many use availability zones as their primary redundancy instead.

Q: Why are region pairs relevant to EU data residency?
A: Almost all region pairs sit within the same geography, so geo-replicating to your region's pair keeps data inside the same residency boundary — for example a West Europe primary pairs with North Europe, both in the EU. This lets you survive a region loss without exporting fund data out of the EU. The documented exceptions are listed in the Azure regions list and must be checked.

Q: What are the two benefits, beyond data residency, of deploying across a region pair?
A: First, region recovery sequence — in a geography-wide outage, one region in each pair is prioritised for recovery, so paired components recover faster. Second, sequential updating — Azure staggers planned platform updates across the pair, reducing the chance a faulty update hits both regions at once. Both are reasons to align a multi-region design to the documented pair.

Q: Is deploying to a region pair enough to give you high availability and automatic failover?
A: No. Microsoft is explicit that deploying to a paired region does not automatically provide HA, DR, or failover — you must design and implement your own HA/DR plan. Even service features that use pairs (like GRS) only failover in catastrophic situations after repeated recovery attempts. You should never rely on Microsoft-managed pair failover as your primary DR strategy.

Q: What are "nonpaired" regions and how do they achieve resiliency?
A: Nonpaired regions are newer Azure regions that have no designated pair and instead rely on availability zones as their primary redundancy mechanism. Many Azure services support geo-replication between arbitrary regions regardless of pairing, so you can still build multi-region DR. The design shift is away from a fixed pair toward zone-resilient single regions plus self-chosen secondary regions.

Q: How does the existence of nonpaired regions change your DR design thinking?
A: It means you can no longer assume a "free" paired secondary; for nonpaired regions you must explicitly choose a secondary region and implement geo-replication yourself, while zones cover in-region resilience. For data-residency-constrained fund data, zone-resilience inside one EU region may be the primary play, with a self-selected EU secondary for region loss. Always check the service's reliability guide to see whether it uses pairs or supports arbitrary-region geo-replication.

Q: Name the two single-region (primary-region) Azure Storage redundancy options.
A: Locally redundant storage (LRS) and zone-redundant storage (ZRS) both keep all copies within the primary region. LRS keeps three copies in a single datacentre; ZRS spreads copies synchronously across three or more availability zones in the region. Both satisfy data-governance rules that require data to stay within one region.

Q: What does LRS protect against and what does it not protect against?
A: LRS replicates data within a single physical datacentre, protecting against drive, server, and rack failures, with at least 11 nines (99.999999999%) annual durability. It does not protect against the loss of that datacentre — a fire or flood can destroy all replicas. It is the cheapest and least durable option, suitable only for easily reconstructable data or strict single-datacentre governance.

Q: How does ZRS work and what is its durability?
A: ZRS writes data synchronously across three or more availability zones in the primary region, so a write succeeds only after all available zones acknowledge, and data stays readable and writable if one zone fails. It offers at least 12 nines (99.9999999999%) annual durability and keeps all data inside the single region. Microsoft recommends ZRS in the primary region for high-availability and for Data Lake Storage and Azure Files workloads.

Q: What is the difference between GRS and GZRS?
A: Both copy data asynchronously to a secondary region for region-loss protection; the difference is how data is stored in the primary region. GRS uses LRS in the primary (single datacentre), while GZRS uses ZRS in the primary (three zones). Within the secondary region both always use LRS. GZRS therefore survives a zone loss in the primary that would take down a GRS account.

Q: What durability do GRS and GZRS provide, and what is the catch?
A: Both GRS and GZRS provide at least 16 nines (99.99999999999999%) annual durability by replicating to a secondary region hundreds of miles away. The catch is that replication to the secondary is asynchronous, so a primary-region disaster can lose the writes not yet replicated — this gap is the RPO. Microsoft's Geo priority replication can hold block-blob RPO at 15 minutes or less, but you must still plan for some data loss.

Q: What do RA-GRS and RA-GZRS add over GRS/GZRS?
A: The "RA" prefix means read access to the secondary region — with GRS/GZRS alone the secondary data is inaccessible unless you fail over, but RA-GRS/RA-GZRS let you read the secondary at any time. You read it via the `-secondary` endpoint suffix, for example `myaccount-secondary.blob.core.windows.net`, using the same account keys. This lets applications keep reading during a primary outage and lets you test secondary reads in advance.

Q: For EU fund data that must never leave the EU but must survive a zone failure, which storage redundancy do you choose and why?
A: Choose ZRS, because it replicates synchronously across three availability zones entirely within the primary EU region, surviving a zone loss without copying data outside the region. GRS/GZRS would replicate to the paired region, which is acceptable only if that pair is also in the EU and residency rules permit it. ZRS is the cleanest single-region answer for a hard in-EU constraint.

Q: For EU fund data that must survive a full region loss while staying in the EU, what storage option fits?
A: GZRS (or RA-GZRS for read access) replicating to an EU paired region fits — ZRS in the primary for zone resilience plus async geo-replication to a secondary region in the same EU geography. You must confirm the primary region's pair is itself in the EU, since almost all pairs share a geography. This gives both zone and region resilience without breaching residency.

Q: A storage account is GRS, the primary region fails, and the app still cannot read data. Why?
A: GRS does not allow reads from the secondary unless you perform an account failover; the secondary is dormant for reads by default. To read during a primary outage without failover you need RA-GRS, which exposes the `-secondary` endpoint. The fix is either to enable read access (RA-GRS) or to initiate the documented account-failover process.

Q: What does "RPO" mean and how does it appear in Azure Storage geo-replication?
A: RPO (Recovery Point Objective) is the maximum acceptable amount of data loss measured in time — how far back the last consistent recoverable point is. In GRS/GZRS the async replication lag to the secondary is the RPO: writes after the last replicated point are lost if the primary is unrecoverable. Azure Storage Geo priority replication targets a block-blob RPO of 15 minutes or less.

Q: What does "RTO" mean and how does it differ from RPO?
A: RTO (Recovery Time Objective) is how long you can tolerate the service being down before it must be restored, whereas RPO is how much data you can tolerate losing. RTO is about time-to-recover; RPO is about data-loss-at-recovery. The Phase 5 exit criteria require you to state both for each platform tier and name the topology that delivers them.

Q: A region pair is described as "asymmetrical". What does that mean and give an example.
A: An asymmetrical pair is one where the pairing is not bidirectional — region A names B as its pair but B names a different region. For example, Brazil South is paired with South Central US, but South Central US is not paired back with Brazil South; West US 3 is paired one-way with East US. You must check direction when relying on pair-based behaviours, because failover and recovery sequencing follow the documented direction.

Q: Why is the management-group hierarchy the right place to enforce EU-only region deployment?
A: Because a single Azure Policy assigned at a top management group inherits to every subscription beneath it, so an "allowed locations = EU regions" policy enforced there blocks non-EU deployments estate-wide without per-subscription work. This is durable, auditable governance rather than a manual checklist. Demonstrating that inherited policy is a strong data-residency control in a CSSF-aligned review.

Q: Sketch the four levels of a CSSF-friendly landing zone the lesson asks you to draw.
A: Management groups at the top (carrying inherited policy and the EU-residency guardrail), then subscriptions as the unit of isolation and billing (platform subs for identity/connectivity/management, application subs per workload/environment), then VNets/networks (hub for shared connectivity, spokes per workload), then identities (Entra tenant, managed identities as the no-secret default). The boundary at each level is policy at the management group, blast-radius and cost at the subscription, network segmentation at the VNet, and least-privilege at the identity. You should be able to name the boundary each level enforces.

Q: What boundary does the management-group level enforce in that sketch?
A: The management group enforces governance and policy inheritance — allowed regions, required encryption, mandatory tags, RBAC — across all subscriptions beneath it. It is the level at which you guarantee data residency and segregation rules apply uniformly. Nothing deployed under it can escape the inherited policy without an explicit exemption.

Q: For each WAF pillar, the lesson asks you to name "the Azure control that closes it". Give the canonical examples.
A: Governance findings close with Azure Policy (and management groups); security findings with Private Link, managed identities, and Key Vault; reliability findings with availability zones / ZRS and multi-region topology; cost findings with budgets, reservations, and scale-to-zero; operational findings with IaC, Monitor, and gated deployment. The discipline is to pair every finding with the specific control rather than a vague intention. That mapping is what an architecture review board expects.

Q: Why is "architecture that isn't in code" considered just a slide in this context?
A: Because a diagram cannot be provisioned, reviewed line-by-line, torn down, or rebuilt identically, and it leaves no audit trail — a regulated estate needs the exact resources, policies, and network rules as version-controlled IaC. Without code, environments drift and DR rebuilds become guesswork. The landing zone itself is expected to be deployed via IaC accelerators, not the portal.

Q: What does CAF recommend for deploying landing zones — portal or IaC — and why?
A: CAF strongly recommends Infrastructure-as-Code (the Azure landing zone IaC Accelerator using Azure Verified Modules in Bicep or Terraform) over the portal accelerator, because IaC gives flexibility, repeatability, and scalability. The portal accelerator is offered only as a fallback for organisations lacking IaC expertise. For an audited platform, IaC is also the reproducibility and four-eyes-review mechanism.

Q: What are Azure Verified Modules (AVM) in the landing-zone context?
A: Azure Verified Modules are Microsoft's curated, supported library of reusable Bicep and Terraform modules, including modules for deploying the platform landing zone via the IaC accelerator. They give you Microsoft-blessed building blocks instead of hand-writing every resource. Using AVM for the landing zone means inheriting Microsoft's well-architected defaults.

Q: Why is a written WAF review of your own design valuable even before anything runs in Azure?
A: Because the pillars surface design-level findings — missing zone resilience, secrets in config, no cost model, no observability — while they are cheap to fix on paper rather than after deployment. The Phase 5 Do-step makes you run exactly this written review against your Capstone-4 design as if on Azure. It also rehearses the review-board conversation you will have for real.

Q: How does the WAF Reliability pillar connect to your RTO/RPO statements?
A: The Reliability pillar's recovery and resilience principles are what force you to define RTO and RPO per tier and choose a topology (zones, region pairs, storage redundancy) that meets them. Stating "NAV store is GZRS, RPO ≤ 15 min, RTO 1 h via documented failover" is a Reliability-pillar answer. The pillar gives the structure; the numbers and topology are your design decisions.

Q: What is the data-residency note the Phase 5 Do-step asks you to write?
A: It is a one-paragraph justification naming the region pair you chose and explaining why it keeps fund data in-boundary — for example "primary West Europe, paired North Europe, both in the EU geography, GZRS replication, EU-only policy at the management group". It connects the technical choice to the regulatory requirement. It is the artefact that wins the data-residency argument in review.

Q: A reviewer asks why you used West Europe rather than an arbitrary cheaper region. How do you answer using residency and pairing?
A: West Europe is an EU region whose documented pair (North Europe) is also in the EU geography, so GZRS geo-replication stays inside the residency boundary while surviving a region loss; a cheaper non-EU region would breach residency. You also confirm West Europe supports availability zones for in-region resilience. The answer ties cost, residency, and reliability into one defensible choice.

Q: How would you keep EU fund data inside a residency boundary while still surviving a zone or region loss?
A: Pick an EU region that supports availability zones, use ZRS/GZRS so data is zone-redundant in-region, and if region-loss survival is needed, geo-replicate (GZRS) only to the EU-geography paired region. Enforce an Azure Policy at the management group that denies any non-EU region. This delivers zone and region resilience without ever moving data out of the EU.

Q: Why can you not rely on a single Azure region for a regulated NAV platform, even with zones?
A: Availability zones protect against zone-scoped failures but explicitly do not protect against a full-region outage. For a regulated platform with a region-loss DR requirement you must add a secondary region (paired or self-chosen) plus geo-replication and backup/restore. If residency forbids a secondary region, you must document in-region redundancy plus backup and set an RTO that reflects region-rebuild time.

Q: What is the relationship between WAF and CAF in one sentence?
A: CAF builds the well-governed environment (landing zones, policy, identity, connectivity) that workloads land in, and WAF ensures each workload built inside that environment is high quality across the five pillars. They are complementary: CAF is environment-level adoption and governance, WAF is workload-level design quality. You use both — CAF to set up the estate, WAF to review every design within it.

Q: Why is mapping a design to landing-zone boundaries a prerequisite for passing an architecture review board?
A: Because the review board reasons in landing-zone and pillar language — it wants to see which management group, subscription, VNet, and identity each component sits in and how that enforces residency and segregation. A design that cannot be mapped to those boundaries cannot be governed or audited, so it fails the gate. The lesson frames this as the difference between getting a design approved and losing the residency argument before you start.

Q: What does "policy-driven governance" buy you over manual review of each subscription?
A: Policy-driven governance enforces rules automatically and continuously at scale through inheritance, so non-compliant resources are denied or flagged the moment they are created, with no human in the loop. Manual review is slow, inconsistent, and misses drift between reviews. For audit, an enforced inherited policy is far stronger evidence than a reviewer's signature.

Q: Compare the AWS Well-Architected Framework to Azure's at a high level. Why learn it?
A: AWS Well-Architected is the same idea — a pillar-based design-quality framework — but with six pillars (Operational Excellence, Security, Reliability, Performance Efficiency, Cost Optimization, and Sustainability) rather than Azure's five. You learn it as comparison vocabulary so you can read multi-cloud RFPs and translate findings between clouds. The pillars largely overlap, which makes cross-cloud architecture conversations tractable.

Q: Which pillar appears in AWS Well-Architected but not (as a named pillar) in Azure's five?
A: Sustainability is a named pillar in the AWS Well-Architected Framework but is not one of Azure's five named pillars. Azure addresses sustainability through separate guidance rather than a dedicated WAF pillar. Knowing this difference helps when reconciling a multi-cloud review where one side reports against six pillars and the other against five.

Q: What does "assume breach" mean as a Security-pillar mindset, and how does the landing zone embody it?
A: Assume breach means designing as though an attacker is already inside, so you minimise blast radius with segmentation, least privilege, and monitoring rather than relying on a hard perimeter. The landing zone embodies it through subscription isolation, hub-spoke network segmentation, managed identities scoped by RBAC, and Private Link to keep traffic off the public internet. If one workload identity is compromised, its RBAC scope limits what it can reach.

Q: What is Azure Private Link and which pillar finding does it typically close?
A: Azure Private Link exposes a PaaS service (like a storage account or SQL database) over a private endpoint inside your VNet, so traffic never traverses the public internet. It typically closes a Security-pillar finding about data-plane exposure — for example a fund-data storage account reachable over a public endpoint. The remediation is "add a private endpoint and disable public network access".

Q: Why is managed identity preferable to a service principal with a client secret for a pipeline?
A: A service principal with a client secret still requires you to store, rotate, and protect that secret, and an expired or leaked secret causes outages or breaches; a managed identity has no secret to store or rotate at all. For workloads running on Azure compute, the managed identity is auto-provisioned and tokens are fetched secretlessly. You only fall back to a service principal secret when the workload runs somewhere a managed identity cannot be assigned.

Q: How would you give a NAV-calculation VM access to read EMT/EPT files from a storage account with no secrets?
A: Enable a managed identity on the VM (ideally a user-assigned one if multiple VMs share access), then assign that identity the `Storage Blob Data Reader` role on the storage account or container holding the EMT/EPT files. The VM's code uses `DefaultAzureCredential` to fetch a token and read the blobs with no secret in config. RBAC scope keeps it read-only and limited to that container.

Q: A user-assigned managed identity is attached to a VM but token acquisition is ambiguous. What is a common gotcha?
A: When more than one user-assigned identity is attached, `DefaultAzureCredential`/`ManagedIdentityCredential` may not know which to use, so you must specify the client ID of the intended identity explicitly. Forgetting this leads to the wrong identity (or none) being selected and a confusing auth failure. The fix is to pass the managed identity's client ID to the credential.

Q: Why does the lesson stress that designs "leak across subscription/identity boundaries" without landing-zone discipline?
A: Without enforced management-group policy and a vetted subscription/identity model, workloads can spin up resources in the wrong region, share over-broad identities, or peer networks that should be isolated — eroding residency and segregation. The boundaries only hold if they are designed and policy-enforced, not assumed. Leakage at these boundaries is precisely what loses the data-residency and segregation arguments in a regulated review.

Q: What is a "geography" in Azure and why does it matter for residency?
A: A geography is a data-residency and compliance boundary that contains one or more Azure regions, typically aligning to a country or area (for example, Europe). It matters because region pairs and many compliance commitments are scoped to stay within a geography, so geo-replication inside a geography keeps data within its residency boundary. For EU fund data, staying inside the Europe geography is the residency goal.

Q: How do availability zones help you meet residency without single-region risk?
A: Zones let you achieve resilience entirely inside one region — spreading replicas across separate datacentres in the same region — so you survive a datacentre/zone failure without exporting data to another region. For residency-constrained data this is the primary recommended way to maximise availability without leaving the region. The remaining gap, full-region loss, is handled by in-region backup or an in-geography secondary.

Q: When residency forbids any secondary region, how do you still address region-wide outage risk?
A: You combine in-region zone redundancy (ZRS) with a robust in-region backup-and-restore plan (for example Azure Backup) and set an RTO that honestly reflects region-rebuild time, since you cannot fail over to another region. You document that the design accepts longer recovery in exchange for strict residency. This is an explicit, defensible trade-off rather than a gap.

Q: What does the Operational Excellence pillar imply for how landing zones should be changed?
A: It implies infrastructure changes go through code, version control, automated testing, and gated deployment with monitoring — not portal clicks — so changes are repeatable, reviewable, and reversible. This is why landing zones are deployed via IaC and why a `plan` is the review artefact. It also implies comprehensive observability so you can detect and respond to issues quickly.

Q: How does subscription-per-environment support the Cost Optimization pillar?
A: Separate subscriptions per environment give clean cost attribution — you can see exactly what dev, test, and prod each cost and apply budgets and alerts at the subscription scope. This makes waste visible and chargeback possible, which is the heart of cost modelling. It also lets you shut down or scale down a non-prod subscription's resources independently.

Q: What is the risk of putting all environments in one subscription from a governance standpoint?
A: A single shared subscription blurs cost attribution, widens blast radius (a prod and a dev resource share the same RBAC and quota scope), and makes per-environment policy and budgets awkward. A misconfiguration or a runaway dev workload can affect prod-adjacent resources. The landing-zone answer is to separate environments into their own subscriptions under the right management groups.

Q: In review, how do you justify the choice between hub-and-spoke and Virtual WAN for a Luxembourg fund estate?
A: Choose hub-and-spoke when you want maximum control over routing and a small, mostly single-region footprint, accepting that you operate the transit yourself; choose Virtual WAN when you have many sites or regions and want Microsoft to manage global transit and scaling. For a compact EU estate with a few on-prem transfer-agency links, hand-built hub-and-spoke is often sufficient and cheaper to reason about. The justification is operational effort and scale, not capability gaps.

Q: What is the difference between control-plane and data-plane RBAC, and why does it trip people up?
A: Control-plane RBAC (roles like `Owner`, `Contributor`, `Reader`) governs managing the Azure resource itself; data-plane RBAC (roles like `Storage Blob Data Reader`) governs accessing the data inside it. People grant `Reader` and are surprised they still get 403s on blob reads, because `Reader` is control-plane only. For a managed identity to read fund-data blobs it needs the data-plane role, not just `Reader`.

Q: Why is `Contributor` a poor choice to grant a NAV pipeline's managed identity on a storage account?
A: `Contributor` is a broad control-plane role that lets the identity manage the storage account (including rotating keys and deleting it) but, by itself, does not grant data-plane blob access — and it violates least privilege by granting far more than reading data. The correct grant is the narrow data-plane role such as `Storage Blob Data Reader`. Over-granting here is a Security-pillar finding waiting to happen.

Q: How does the landing zone make a DR rebuild auditable rather than guesswork?
A: Because the entire environment — subscriptions, policies, networks, identities — is expressed in version-controlled IaC, a DR rebuild re-applies that exact code to produce an identical estate, and the code plus its history is the audit trail. Reviewers can see precisely what would be rebuilt, not a stale diagram. This is the reproducibility guarantee a regulated estate requires.

Q: What does "tailoring the architecture" mean in the CAF landing-zone reference?
A: CAF presents the landing-zone reference architecture as an opinionated starting point that you adapt to your organisation's size, compliance needs, and topology — for example collapsing or adding management groups, choosing hub-spoke vs Virtual WAN, or adjusting policy sets. You are not meant to deploy it verbatim. Tailoring keeps the design principles while fitting your specific CSSF and residency constraints.

Q: What are the three management approaches for application landing zones?
A: Central team management (a central IT team fully operates the landing zone), application team management (the platform team delegates the landing zone to the app team, but management-group policy still governs it), and shared management (for platforms like AKS where a central team runs the underlying service and app teams own what runs on top). The choice depends on how much autonomy workload teams need versus central control. Policy inheritance keeps governance intact regardless of approach.

Q: Pull it together: state, for a Luxembourg NAV platform, the region/zone/storage topology that meets EU residency without single-region risk.
A: Run in an EU region that supports availability zones (e.g. West Europe), make compute and data zone-redundant so a zone loss is survived in-region, and use GZRS (or RA-GZRS for read) to geo-replicate fund data only to the EU-geography paired region (North Europe) for region-loss survival. Enforce EU-only deployment with Azure Policy at the management group, and use managed identities for secretless access throughout. State RTO/RPO per tier (e.g. NAV store RPO ≤ 15 min via geo priority replication) and you have a topology you can defend in a CSSF-aligned review.
