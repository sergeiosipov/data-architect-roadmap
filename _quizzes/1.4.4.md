# Phase 8 · 1.4.4 Data mesh (and 13.2.1 enabling platforms) — 100 self-test questions

Q: What is data mesh in one sentence?
A: Data mesh is a decentralized sociotechnical approach to analytical data, proposed by Zhamak Dehghani, that distributes ownership of data to the business domains that know it best while a shared self-serve platform and federated governance keep the whole interoperable. It is primarily an organizational and operating-model shift, not a specific technology — the technology merely enables the operating model, which is why "buying a mesh product" is a category error.

Q: Who originated the concept of data mesh and in what primary text?
A: Zhamak Dehghani originated the term in 2019 articles on martinfowler.com and developed it fully in the O'Reilly book `Data Mesh: Delivering Data-Driven Value at Scale` (2022). The book's parts I–II lay out the four principles and the target operating model, which are the syllabus's primary reading; part III covers execution and is skim-only here.

Q: What are the four principles of data mesh?
A: They are domain ownership, data as a product, self-serve data platform, and federated computational governance. The first two decentralize responsibility to domains, the third gives those domains the tooling to act autonomously without each reinventing infrastructure, and the fourth keeps the decentralized estate coherent through globally-agreed, automatically-enforced policy.

Q: State the principle of domain ownership.
A: Domain ownership means the business domain that produces or best understands a dataset also owns its analytical data end-to-end — modelling, quality, serving, and lifecycle — rather than handing raw data to a central team to interpret. The premise is that domain teams have the context a central data team lacks, so pushing ownership to them shortens the feedback loop and removes the central translation bottleneck.

Q: State the principle of data as a product.
A: Data as a product means each domain treats its served data as a product with named consumers, an owner, a service-level agreement, discoverability, addressability, and documented quality — not as a byproduct dumped into a lake. The mindset shift is from "I produced some data" to "I am accountable to consumers for a usable, trustworthy, supported product," which is what stops decentralization from producing unowned fragments.

Q: What are Dehghani's "baseline usability characteristics" of a data product?
A: Dehghani lists characteristics such as discoverable, addressable, understandable, trustworthy/truthful (with stated SLOs), natively accessible, interoperable, valuable on its own, and secure. The shorthand acronym sometimes used is DATSIS or similar; the point is that a data product is only a product if a consumer can find it, reach it, trust it, and combine it without phoning the owner.

Q: State the principle of self-serve data platform.
A: The self-serve data platform is shared infrastructure and tooling that lets domain teams build, deploy, and operate their own data products without deep platform expertise or central gatekeeping. It exists so that decentralization does not multiply cost: instead of every domain standing up its own ingestion, storage, catalog, and governance, the platform team provides those as paved-road capabilities the domains consume.

Q: State the principle of federated computational governance.
A: Federated computational governance is a model where a federation of domain and platform representatives agrees global policies, and those policies are executed automatically by the platform rather than enforced by manual review. "Federated" means decisions are shared across domains, and "computational" means they are encoded as code/configuration the platform runs — for example a policy-as-code rule that blocks publishing a data product without an owner and a documented SLO.

Q: Why is data mesh described as a "sociotechnical" approach rather than a technical one?
A: Because its hardest problems are organizational — team boundaries, ownership, accountability, incentives — and technology alone cannot solve those. You can deploy Trino, OpenMetadata, and Unity Catalog perfectly and still have no mesh if no domain actually owns a product or is accountable for its SLOs; the "socio" half is what most failed adoptions skip.

Q: What core problem is data mesh trying to solve?
A: It targets the bottleneck of a central data team that must understand, model, and serve data for every domain in a large organization, which does not scale: the team becomes a backlog, lacks domain context, and slows everyone down. Mesh distributes that work to domains so the organization can scale analytical data delivery with the number of domains rather than the size of one central team.

Q: According to the lesson's framing, which of your already-built platform capabilities map to which mesh principles?
A: The platform inventory from Phases 6–7 already realizes principles 2–4 in a single-domain form: your data-product contracts and SLAs serve "data as a product," your catalog/lakehouse/orchestration serve the self-serve platform, and your policy-as-code and catalog serve federated governance. The principle you have not exercised is domain ownership at scale — principle 1 — because today there is effectively one domain, so nothing forces multi-domain ownership and federated execution.

Q: Which mesh principle is the one you have NOT yet exercised in a single-domain build, and why?
A: Domain ownership (principle 1) is the unexercised one, because with a single domain there are no boundaries to negotiate, no cross-domain ownership disputes, and no need to federate governance across independent teams. The single-domain version of mesh is essentially "a well-run data platform"; mesh proper only begins to bite when domains multiply and you must decide who owns what at the seams.

Q: What changes about "data as a product" when domains multiply from one to many?
A: With one domain, a product contract is an internal discipline; with many, the same contract becomes the interoperability boundary between independently-owned teams, so versioning, deprecation policy, and SLO enforcement stop being optional. The output port of one domain becomes the input dependency of another, so a silent breaking change now propagates across organizational lines rather than within one team.

Q: What changes about the self-serve platform when domains multiply?
A: It must shift from "tooling that helped my one team" to a true product serving internal customers with onboarding, golden paths, and abstractions that non-experts can use, because you can no longer hand-hold every domain. If the platform stays artisanal, multiplying domains either re-centralizes work onto the platform team or forces each domain to build its own stack — both of which defeat the mesh.

Q: What changes about governance when domains multiply?
A: Governance moves from a single team applying rules consistently by hand to a federation agreeing global policies that the platform executes automatically across all domains, because manual enforcement does not scale to many autonomous teams. This is exactly why the "computational" half matters: a human cannot review every data product in twenty domains, but a policy-as-code rule in the publishing pipeline can.

Q: Define federated computational governance in one sentence.
A: Federated computational governance is global, cross-domain policy decided by a federation of domain and platform owners and then enforced automatically as code by the self-serve platform, balancing organization-wide interoperability against local domain autonomy. The one-sentence test from the lesson's "done when" is exactly this: federation for who decides, computational for how it is enforced.

Q: Give a concrete federated-governance policy example drawn from your own build.
A: A computational policy could require that every published data product carries a registered owner, a classification tag, and a freshness SLO, with the catalog/CI pipeline rejecting publication otherwise — enforced by your policy-as-code engine, not a reviewer. Another is auto-applying PII masking on any column tagged `pii` across all domains, so the global rule is uniform while each domain still decides its own schema and modelling.

Q: In federated governance, what is decided globally versus left to local domain autonomy?
A: Global decisions are the things that must be consistent for the mesh to interoperate and stay compliant — identity, classification taxonomy, access-policy semantics, interoperability standards, encryption and retention rules. Local autonomy covers everything internal to a domain — its data models, technology choices within the paved road, transformation logic, and how it shapes its own products — so domains move fast without a central queue.

Q: How does policy-as-code make governance "computational" rather than documentary?
A: Policy-as-code expresses a rule as an executable artifact (for example an Open Policy Agent / Rego rule or a CI check) that the platform runs at publish or query time, so the rule is enforced automatically and identically everywhere. A documented policy in a wiki is "definition documented"; a policy-as-code check that blocks a non-compliant deploy is "definition executed" — and only the latter actually prevents drift.

Q: Why is automated enforcement essential at mesh scale, with a concrete failure if it is missing?
A: Manual review cannot keep pace with many autonomous domains, so without computational enforcement, policies become aspirational and compliance silently rots. Concretely, if "every product must mask PII" is only a guideline, one of twenty domains will eventually publish an unmasked investor-identity column, and you will discover it during an audit rather than at the pipeline gate that should have blocked it.

Q: What is the relationship between a data catalog and federated computational governance?
A: The catalog is the substrate that makes computational governance possible: it holds the ownership, classification, lineage, and policy metadata that the rules read and write, and it is where products become discoverable and addressable. In OpenMetadata terms, domains, data products, glossary terms, and tags live in the catalog, and governance automation acts on that metadata — without it, there is nothing for the policy engine to evaluate.

Q: What is "mesh-washing"?
A: Mesh-washing is rebranding an existing centralized platform or a plain set of data warehouses as a "data mesh" without actually decentralizing ownership or implementing the four principles. It is the failure mode where the org chart, accountability, and operating model are unchanged but the slides say "mesh"; the tell is that you cannot name a single domain that genuinely owns its products end-to-end.

Q: What is platform-team starvation as a mesh failure mode?
A: Platform-team starvation is decentralizing domain work before the self-serve platform is mature enough to support it, so the under-resourced platform team cannot deliver the paved roads domains need. Domains then either stall waiting for capabilities or build their own divergent stacks, and the promised autonomy turns into fragmentation — the lesson warns that adopting mesh too early "starves the platform team."

Q: What is premature decentralization as a mesh failure mode?
A: Premature decentralization is splitting a small or immature data estate into domain-owned fragments before there are enough domains, enough scale, or enough platform maturity to justify the overhead. The result is unowned or under-owned fragments, duplicated effort, and lost economies of scale — for a small firm this shatters a manageable central estate into pieces nobody is staffed to maintain.

Q: List three mesh failure modes and one early-warning signal for each.
A: Mesh-washing — warning signal: the team cannot name a domain that truly owns a product end-to-end, only renamed central pipelines. Platform-team starvation — warning signal: domains repeatedly wait on or work around the platform team, or each builds bespoke infrastructure. Premature decentralization — warning signal: there are very few domains, products are going unowned, and the same work is being duplicated across newly-split teams.

Q: Which book is the syllabus's "pragmatic counterweight" to Dehghani, and what is its stance?
A: It is `Data Management at Scale, 2nd edition` by Piethein Strengholt, positioned as "domains without dogma." It accepts domain-oriented decentralization as valuable but is more pragmatic about hybrid and centralized elements, warning against treating mesh as an all-or-nothing ideology and emphasizing where a strong platform and selective centralization still make sense.

Q: Why pair a "for" text (Dehghani) with a "counterweight" text (Strengholt) in this lesson?
A: Because the architect's job here is judgement about when mesh's costs are justified, and you need both the strongest articulation of the ideal and a sober view of where it over-applies. Reading only Dehghani risks dogmatic over-adoption; reading only the counterweight risks dismissing a pattern a 3,000-person group genuinely needs — the "done when" requires arguing both sides for the same firm.

Q: What is the free, condensed reference for the four principles?
A: It is the `Data Mesh Principles and Logical Architecture` article on martinfowler.com, which states the four principles and their logical architecture without the full book. It is the reference-tier resource for quickly re-checking the principle definitions; the book parts I–II remain the primary source for the operating model.

Q: What is the "sizing test" the lesson asks you to internalize?
A: The sizing test is the judgement of whether an organization is large and complex enough that decentralizing to domains pays off — the syllabus's worked case is why a 50-person fund administrator should not mesh. Below the threshold the coordination overhead, platform investment, and ownership fragmentation cost more than the central-team bottleneck they remove.

Q: Why should a 50-person fund administrator NOT adopt data mesh?
A: At 50 people there are too few genuine domains and too little scale for the central data function to be a real bottleneck, while the platform investment, federated-governance ceremony, and per-domain ownership overhead are pure cost. A small, well-run central platform serves a 50-person admin better; mesh would shatter a manageable estate into fragments nobody is staffed to own.

Q: What threshold signals suggest an organization may be ready for mesh?
A: Signals include many distinct business domains with real autonomy, a central data team that is a chronic delivery bottleneck, enough headcount to staff both domain data teams and a dedicated platform team, a mature self-serve platform, and genuine demand to combine data across independently-owned domains. The decision is driven by organizational scale and domain multiplicity, not by data volume alone.

Q: What is the single organizational precondition that most decides whether mesh fits?
A: The decisive precondition is whether the organization actually has multiple autonomous business domains capable of owning data products, backed by enough scale to staff a real platform team. Without genuine domain autonomy and platform capacity, you get mesh's costs (coordination, federation, fragmentation) without its benefit (removing the central bottleneck), so that precondition is the hinge for both the for- and against-argument.

Q: Why is data volume a poor trigger for adopting mesh?
A: Mesh solves an organizational scaling problem — the central-team bottleneck across many domains — not a data-size problem, which is addressed by storage and compute architecture. A single-domain firm with petabytes still has no ownership-distribution problem to solve, so it needs better infrastructure, not a mesh; conversely a moderate-data firm with many autonomous domains may genuinely benefit.

Q: What are "enabling platforms" in the context of this lesson?
A: Enabling platforms are the technical substrate that makes the self-serve platform and federated governance executable — query federation, governed catalogs with domains, and domain-scoped governance. The lesson names Trino/Starburst federation, Databricks Unity Catalog domains, and OpenMetadata domains; they are necessary enablers but not sufficient, because they implement the platform principle without supplying the missing socio half.

Q: What is Trino and why is it relevant to data mesh?
A: Trino is an open-source distributed SQL query engine that federates queries across many heterogeneous sources (object stores, databases, lakehouses) through connectors, returning a single result without copying data into one store. For mesh it provides cross-domain access so a consumer can query products owned by different domains in different systems, supporting addressability and interoperability without forcing centralization of the data itself.

Q: What is Starburst and how does it relate to Trino?
A: Starburst is the commercial enterprise distribution built on Trino (the project that grew out of Presto), adding security, governance, connectors, management tooling, and support. The lesson lists Trino as the FOSS substrate you already run and Starburst as the corporate package of the same federation capability to evaluate — same engine lineage, enterprise wrapping.

Q: How does query federation support the data-as-a-product principle?
A: Federation lets each domain serve its product from the store and format that suits it while consumers still query across products through one engine, so domains keep autonomy over technology without breaking addressability. It avoids the anti-pattern of forcing every product into one central warehouse just to make it queryable, which would re-centralize the very thing mesh decentralizes.

Q: What is a Databricks Unity Catalog domain in mesh terms, and what is the catalog hierarchy?
A: Unity Catalog governs data across a Databricks account through a three-level namespace `catalog.schema.table` under a metastore (one per region), where catalogs are the primary unit of data isolation and typically map to organizational units or data domains. Mapping a catalog to a domain gives each domain an isolated, independently-governed namespace with inherited privileges, which is how UC packages domain-scoped ownership and governance.

Q: In Unity Catalog, what is the metastore and how does it relate to catalogs and schemas?
A: The metastore is the top-level metadata container for a region and exposes the three-level namespace; inside it, catalogs are the first organizational layer and the primary isolation unit, and schemas (databases) sit inside catalogs and hold tables, views, volumes, and functions. Privileges are inherited downward through this hierarchy, so granting on a catalog cascades to its schemas and tables unless overridden.

Q: How do OpenMetadata domains support a data mesh operating model?
A: OpenMetadata provides first-class Domains and Data Products: domains group data assets, glossaries, teams, and other entities under a unified, independently-managed structure, and data products group related assets within a domain with ownership, documentation, and lifecycle. This makes domains and products discoverable and governable in the catalog, which is the substrate federated computational governance acts upon.

Q: What lifecycle stages does an OpenMetadata Data Product support?
A: An OpenMetadata Data Product moves through lifecycle stages including `IDEATION`, `DESIGN`, `DEVELOPMENT`, `TESTING`, `PRODUCTION`, `DEPRECATED`, and `RETIRED`. Tracking the stage in the catalog lets consumers see whether a product is production-grade or still maturing, and lets governance rules treat a `PRODUCTION` product differently from one in `DEVELOPMENT`.

Q: What is the difference between an "aggregate" domain and a "consumer-aligned" domain in OpenMetadata?
A: An aggregate domain collects and curates data drawn from multiple source-aligned domains, while a consumer-aligned domain assembles data from various domains into an end product shaped for business users. The distinction mirrors Dehghani's source-aligned, aggregate, and consumer-aligned data product taxonomy, helping you reason about where a product sits in the value chain.

Q: What is the "domain-only view" feature in OpenMetadata and why does it matter for mesh?
A: The domain-only view scopes a user's navigation and discovery to a specific domain, surfacing only the assets and products relevant to that domain's work. For mesh it reinforces domain ownership and reduces noise, so a domain team operates within its boundary while cross-domain discovery remains available when explicitly needed.

Q: Why are these enabling platforms "necessary but not sufficient" for a real mesh?
A: They implement the self-serve-platform and governance-substrate principles, but they cannot supply domain ownership or accountability, which are organizational decisions. You can have UC catalogs per domain, Trino federation, and OpenMetadata domains and still have no mesh if no domain team is genuinely accountable for its products' SLOs — the tooling enables, the operating model decides.

Q: Map the four mesh principles onto your existing FOSS stack (Trino, OpenMetadata, policy-as-code).
A: Domain ownership maps to OpenMetadata domains plus the team/accountability model you assign; data as a product maps to OpenMetadata data products with contracts and SLOs; self-serve platform maps to your shared ingestion/lakehouse/orchestration plus Trino federation; federated computational governance maps to your policy-as-code engine acting on catalog metadata. The gap is consistently principle 1 — assigning real, autonomous ownership — which no tool grants for you.

Q: What is a source-aligned (native) data product?
A: A source-aligned data product serves data that closely mirrors the operational reality of the system or domain that generates it, with minimal cross-domain modelling — for example a trade-capture domain serving raw-ish executed orders. It is the rawest mesh product type and exists so downstream aggregate and consumer-aligned products can build on a trustworthy, owned source.

Q: What is a consumer-aligned (fit-for-purpose) data product?
A: A consumer-aligned data product is shaped for a specific downstream use or consumer, typically by combining and transforming source-aligned and aggregate products into the exact form a use case needs. In fund-admin terms, a regulatory-reporting product assembling NAV, holdings, and investor data for a specific filing is consumer-aligned — opinionated and purpose-built, not a neutral source.

Q: What is an "output port" of a data product?
A: An output port is the interface through which a data product exposes its data to consumers — for example a SQL endpoint, a set of files, a table in a governed catalog, or an event stream. Defining explicit output ports is what makes a product addressable and interoperable; consumers depend on the port's contract, not on the product's internal storage, which the domain is free to change.

Q: What is an "input port" of a data product?
A: An input port is how a data product ingests the upstream data it depends on — typically the output ports of other products or operational sources. Treating dependencies as input ports consuming named output ports is what turns informal data hand-offs between domains into explicit, contracted, version-managed connections across the mesh.

Q: How does a data contract relate to a data product?
A: A data contract is the explicit, versioned agreement an output port honours — schema, semantics, quality/SLO guarantees, and change policy — so consumers can depend on it without coordinating with the producer on every change. It is the mechanism that lets data-as-a-product scale across domains: the contract is the stable interface, and breaking it requires versioning and deprecation rather than a silent edit.

Q: How is a data mesh different from a data lake?
A: A data lake is centralized storage of raw data, usually owned and curated by one central team, whereas a data mesh is a decentralized operating model where many domains own and serve products, possibly on top of lake or lakehouse storage. Mesh is about ownership distribution and product thinking; a lake is about where bytes sit — you can build mesh-style products on a lakehouse, or dump an unowned swamp into one.

Q: How is data mesh different from a data warehouse?
A: A data warehouse centralizes modelled, integrated analytical data under one team and schema, while data mesh decentralizes ownership to domains that each serve their own products under federated standards. They are not strictly mutually exclusive — a domain might serve a warehouse-style product — but the organizing principle differs: one central model versus many owned, interoperating products.

Q: How does data mesh differ from data fabric?
A: Data fabric is primarily a technical/architectural approach using metadata, integration, and automation to connect and access data across an estate, often with strong central tooling, whereas data mesh is an organizational ownership model. They can coexist — a fabric-style integration layer can be part of a mesh's self-serve platform — but fabric does not, by itself, decentralize ownership the way mesh requires.

Q: Why does the lesson insist mesh is "the organizational scaling answer" specifically?
A: Because mesh scales the organization's ability to deliver analytical data with the number of autonomous domains rather than the size of one central team, which is the constraint that bites large firms. It does not make queries faster or storage cheaper; it removes the human bottleneck of one team having to understand and serve everyone, which is why it surfaces in every large-firm interview.

Q: What happens to a 3,000-person group that refuses mesh entirely?
A: It typically keeps a central-data-team bottleneck forever: the central team cannot acquire deep context for every domain, becomes a perpetual backlog, and slows delivery across the whole group. The lesson's framing is that dismissing mesh outright at that scale leaves the organization permanently rate-limited by one team, even as the number of domains and demands grows.

Q: What is the strongest one-paragraph counter-argument to recommending mesh for a 300-person fund administrator?
A: The strongest counter is that 300 people in a regulated, tightly-integrated domain (NAV, TA, accounting all interdependent) may have too few truly independent domains and too little platform headcount to staff both domain teams and a mature platform, so decentralizing risks platform-team starvation and fragmented compliance. Regulatory accountability (DORA, UCITS/AIFMD) favours clear, auditable central control, and splitting ownership could blur who is answerable for a wrong NAV.

Q: How would you answer that counter-argument when recommending "yes-partially / not-yet"?
A: Answer that mesh is not all-or-nothing: you can decentralize ownership only where domains are genuinely independent (e.g. distribution analytics) while keeping the NAV/accounting core centrally governed, and stage the move behind explicit triggers — a mature self-serve platform and a staffed platform team. Federated computational governance actually strengthens auditability by enforcing compliance as code uniformly, so partial, trigger-gated adoption captures benefit without abandoning regulatory accountability.

Q: Why is "argue both for and against mesh for the same firm" a required competency here?
A: Because the architect's value is judgement, not advocacy: the same 300-person firm has a credible yes (multiple domains, central bottleneck emerging) and a credible no (insufficient platform maturity, regulatory accountability), and a good architect can articulate both and name the precondition that tips it. Being able to argue only one side signals dogma, which the lesson explicitly warns against in both the over-adopt and dismiss-outright directions.

Q: In the "Do" exercise, what does marking each Phase-6/7 component against a mesh principle reveal?
A: It reveals which principles your build already satisfies single-domain (data-as-a-product, self-serve platform, federated governance) and which it does not (multi-domain ownership and federated policy execution across independent teams). The exercise surfaces the concrete gaps — for example, you have policy-as-code but it is enforced for one domain, not federated across many — which is precisely what scaling to a mesh would require.

Q: Why is the DDD context map from 8.4.1 used as the candidate domain decomposition?
A: Because domain-driven design's bounded contexts and context map already express where the meaningful business boundaries and inter-domain relationships are, which is exactly the question mesh's domain ownership asks. Reusing the context map means your mesh domains are grounded in real business semantics and integration patterns rather than drawn arbitrarily, so ownership lines follow the seams that already exist.

Q: How does domain-driven design relate to data mesh's domain ownership?
A: Mesh borrows DDD's notion of bounded contexts to define domains: a domain in mesh is the analytical counterpart of a DDD bounded context, owned by the team responsible for that part of the business. DDD gives you the principled way to draw domain boundaries and identify relationships (upstream/downstream, conformist, anti-corruption layer), which mesh then uses to assign data-product ownership.

Q: For a fund administrator, what might the candidate mesh domains be?
A: Plausible domains follow the operational seams: fund accounting / NAV, transfer agency (investor register and dealing), portfolio/holdings and reference data, distribution and reporting, and possibly compliance/regulatory. Each is a candidate owner of its data products, with the NAV and accounting domains being the most tightly-coupled and therefore the riskiest to decentralize first.

Q: Which fund-admin domain is the riskiest to decentralize first, and why?
A: The NAV / fund-accounting domain is the riskiest, because NAV is the firm's most consequential, regulated, and downstream-critical figure, and many other products (investor statements, regulatory files, EMT/EPT) depend on it. Decentralizing it prematurely risks ambiguous accountability for a wrong NAV and breaks the strongest argument for clear, auditable ownership — so it is usually the last thing to move, if ever.

Q: How does federated computational governance help meet regulatory obligations like DORA in a mesh?
A: It encodes compliance requirements as platform-executed policies applied uniformly across every domain — for example mandatory lineage, access controls, retention, and incident-relevant logging — so accountability does not fragment as ownership decentralizes. DORA's emphasis on operational resilience and auditability is actually easier to evidence when controls are computational and consistent than when each domain enforces its own interpretation by hand.

Q: How would a mesh handle a cross-domain product that needs NAV (from accounting) and investor data (from transfer agency)?
A: It would be built as an aggregate or consumer-aligned data product whose input ports consume the NAV domain's and the TA domain's output ports through their contracts, combining them without owning either source. The owning team is accountable for the combined product's SLOs, while each source domain remains accountable for its own contract — and federation across domain boundaries replaces an ad-hoc join through a central team.

Q: In a mesh, who owns the ISIN-to-instrument reference data, and why does it matter?
A: Reference data like ISIN-keyed instrument master is naturally a source-aligned product owned by a reference-data/securities domain, because it is shared across portfolio, accounting, and reporting domains. Clear ownership matters because a wrong or stale ISIN mapping propagates into NAV, holdings, and regulatory files; making it an owned product with an SLO is exactly the mesh remedy for shared-data quality.

Q: How does an EMT/EPT file fit the data-as-a-product framing?
A: An EMT (European MiFID Template) or EPT (European PRIIPs Template) file is a consumer-aligned data product: a fit-for-purpose deliverable for distributors and regulators, assembled from upstream NAV, cost, and instrument products. Treating it as a product with an owner, a schema contract, quality checks, and an SLA is precisely the discipline that prevents the recurring industry problem of late or malformed EMT/EPT files.

Q: Why might LEI and entity reference data be a shared source-aligned product in a fund-admin mesh?
A: Because the Legal Entity Identifier and counterparty/entity reference data are consumed across reporting, risk, and onboarding domains, so a single owned source-aligned product avoids each domain maintaining a divergent copy. Centralized ownership of a shared product (not centralized ownership of everything) is the mesh way to keep entity identity consistent for regulatory reporting where wrong LEIs cause rejected filings.

Q: How do SWIFT-driven flows (e.g. settlement messaging) interact with a mesh's domain boundaries?
A: SWIFT message flows belong to the operational settlement/custody domain, and the analytical product derived from them (e.g. a settled-transactions product) is owned by that domain and served via an output port. Other domains consume that product's contract rather than parsing raw SWIFT themselves, which keeps the messaging complexity encapsulated where the expertise lives — a direct application of domain ownership.

Q: Why is a regulated, integrated fund-admin context an unusually hard case for mesh?
A: Because its core domains (NAV, accounting, transfer agency) are tightly interdependent and the outputs are heavily regulated, so the clean domain autonomy mesh assumes is partly illusory and the cost of ambiguous accountability is high. The tight coupling weakens principle 1 (true independent ownership), and regulators reward clear, auditable control — both push toward selective, trigger-gated adoption rather than wholesale meshing.

Q: What is the difference between "centralizing a shared product" and "centralizing the platform" in a mesh?
A: Centralizing a shared product means one domain owns a widely-consumed product (e.g. instrument reference data) and serves it to others — fully mesh-compatible. Centralizing the platform means the self-serve platform itself is a central capability all domains use — also mesh-compatible and in fact required. What mesh decentralizes is domain data ownership, not necessarily every product or the platform.

Q: Is having a central platform team compatible with data mesh?
A: Yes — a central platform team is essential, because the self-serve platform principle requires shared, well-maintained infrastructure that domains consume rather than each rebuilding. What mesh removes is the central team's role as the bottleneck that models and serves everyone's data; the platform team instead builds paved roads and treats the platform itself as a product for internal domain customers.

Q: What does "the platform as a product" mean and why does it matter?
A: It means the platform team treats domain teams as customers, with onboarding, documentation, golden paths, SLOs, and feedback loops, rather than as ticket-raisers. It matters because self-serve only works if the platform is genuinely usable by non-experts; a platform that is not productized re-creates the bottleneck by forcing domains to depend on platform specialists for routine work.

Q: How does mesh change the role of the former central data team?
A: The central team typically splits: some members move into domains to seed domain data ownership and product thinking, and others form the platform team that builds self-serve capabilities. Its identity shifts from "the team that produces all data" to "the team that enables domains to produce their own," which is also the organizational change mesh-washing conspicuously skips.

Q: Why can decentralization increase, not decrease, total platform cost if done wrong?
A: If the self-serve platform is immature, each domain rebuilds ingestion, storage, catalog, and governance, multiplying infrastructure and effort instead of sharing it — the duplication is the cost. This is the mechanism behind platform-team starvation and premature decentralization: decentralizing ownership without first centralizing reusable platform capability turns one shared cost into many redundant ones.

Q: What is the correct sequencing of investment when adopting mesh?
A: Generally, mature the self-serve platform and federated computational governance first, then decentralize domain ownership onto that paved road, rather than the reverse. Decentralizing ownership before the platform can support it causes starvation and fragmentation; building the platform first means that when a domain takes ownership, it inherits working tooling and enforced policy rather than a blank slate.

Q: What is "domain-agnostic" versus "domain-specific" capability in a mesh platform?
A: Domain-agnostic capabilities are the reusable, generic services the platform provides to all domains — storage, compute, catalog, pipelines, policy enforcement — while domain-specific logic is the modelling, transformations, and products each domain builds on top. The platform must stay domain-agnostic; the moment it starts encoding domain-specific business logic, it becomes the bottleneck mesh was supposed to remove.

Q: How does a governed catalog enable both discoverability and addressability of data products?
A: A governed catalog (e.g. OpenMetadata) registers each product with metadata, ownership, documentation, and a stable reference, so consumers can discover it by browsing/searching and address it by a consistent identifier or endpoint. Without such a catalog, decentralized products become undiscoverable silos, which would defeat data-as-a-product — the catalog is what turns scattered domain outputs into a navigable mesh.

Q: What is the relationship between a glossary term (documented) and a federated policy (executed)?
A: A glossary term documents the agreed business definition (definition documented), while a federated computational policy executes a rule that enforces consistency (definition executed); linking them keeps both from rotting. For example a glossary defines "NAV" and a policy enforces that any product tagged with the NAV term carries the required quality checks — documentation alone never prevents drift, execution does.

Q: Why does the lesson say principle 4 builds directly on "your policy-as-code and catalog work" from Phase 6?
A: Because federated computational governance is operationally the same machinery you already built — policies expressed as code and metadata managed in a catalog — now applied across multiple federated domains rather than one. The Phase 6 work proves you can encode and enforce a policy; the mesh step is governing who agrees the global policies and ensuring they execute uniformly across autonomous domains.

Q: What does it mean that mesh distributes ownership but federates governance?
A: It means autonomy and consistency are deliberately split: domains independently own and build their products (distributed ownership), while a cross-domain federation sets global interoperability and compliance rules the platform enforces everywhere (federated governance). This is the core balance — too little federation and the mesh fragments into incompatible silos; too much and you have just re-centralized control under a new name.

Q: How would you recognize that an organization has actually achieved mesh rather than mesh-washing?
A: You can name specific business domains that own their data products end-to-end with accountable owners and published SLOs, a productized self-serve platform those domains genuinely use, and global policies enforced automatically across domains. If instead you find renamed central pipelines, no real domain accountability, and governance by manual review, it is mesh-washing regardless of the labels in the catalog.

Q: What is the danger of adopting mesh "too early" specifically named in the lesson's Why?
A: The lesson warns that adopting too early "starves the platform team while shattering a small firm's estate into unowned fragments" — i.e. platform-team starvation plus premature decentralization simultaneously. The platform team is asked to support decentralization it is not resourced for, while the estate is split before there are enough domains to own the pieces, leaving fragments nobody maintains.

Q: What is the danger of dismissing mesh outright, per the lesson's Why?
A: Dismissing it outright means "a 3,000-person group keeps its central-team bottleneck forever" — the large organization stays permanently rate-limited by a single data team that cannot scale its context across all domains. The lesson frames both errors as failures of judgement: the architect's job is to know when the costs are justified, not to be reflexively for or against.

Q: How do Trino/Starburst and Unity Catalog play different roles in the same mesh?
A: Trino/Starburst provide cross-source query federation so consumers can read products that live in different domains' stores, while Unity Catalog provides governed, domain-isolated namespaces and access control within the Databricks estate. One is primarily about reaching across heterogeneous sources, the other about governing and isolating domains; a real platform may use both, plus a catalog like OpenMetadata for discovery and product metadata.

Q: Could you implement a mesh substrate entirely on FOSS, and with what components?
A: Yes — the lesson's FOSS path is Trino for federation plus OpenMetadata for domains, data products, glossary, and governance metadata, layered on your existing lakehouse and policy-as-code. This gives self-serve access, discoverability, domain/product modelling, and computational governance without commercial licensing; the corporate alternatives (Starburst, Unity Catalog) package similar capabilities with vendor support.

Q: Why is "self-serve" the principle most under-estimated in effort?
A: Because building a platform usable by non-expert domain teams — with onboarding, abstractions, golden paths, and reliability — is a sustained product investment, not a one-off tooling install. Organizations consistently underestimate it, then either starve the platform team or watch domains build divergent stacks; the self-serve principle is where most of the real engineering cost of mesh actually lands.

Q: What is the difference between logical and physical isolation of domains in Unity Catalog?
A: Unity Catalog supports one metastore per region but provides mechanisms for both logical isolation (separate catalogs/schemas with distinct permissions) and physical isolation (separate managed storage at metastore, catalog, or schema level). Mapping a domain to its own catalog with its own storage gives a domain both governance and data-location independence, which supports domain ownership boundaries.

Q: How does privilege inheritance in Unity Catalog interact with domain autonomy?
A: Privileges are inherited downward through `metastore → catalog → schema → table`, so granting at the catalog level cascades to everything inside that domain's catalog unless overridden. This lets a domain owner be granted broad rights over their own catalog (autonomy) while federated rules still set the global access semantics — inheritance is the mechanism that makes per-domain delegation manageable.

Q: Why might a firm run OpenMetadata for discovery even when its data lives in Databricks Unity Catalog?
A: Because OpenMetadata adds cross-platform discovery, business glossary, domains/data-products modelling, lineage across many sources, and richer collaboration that complements UC's in-platform governance. UC governs and isolates within Databricks; an organization-wide catalog gives the mesh a single discovery and product-metadata layer spanning Databricks and non-Databricks sources alike.

Q: What is the "thin" versus "thick" platform debate in mesh adoption?
A: A thin platform provides minimal shared capability and pushes most work to domains (risking duplication and starvation if domains lack skill), while a thick platform centralizes more capability (risking re-creating a bottleneck if it absorbs domain logic). The architect tunes the boundary so the platform owns domain-agnostic capability richly but stops at domain-specific business logic — getting this line right is central to avoiding both failure modes.

Q: How does mesh change incident accountability when a downstream report is wrong?
A: Accountability follows ownership: if a consumer-aligned report is wrong, you trace through input ports to the owning domain whose product breached its contract or SLO, and that domain is accountable for the fix. This is cleaner than a central team being blamed for everything, but it only works if ownership and contracts are real — which is again why principle 1 plus enforced contracts are non-negotiable.

Q: Why is versioning and deprecation policy more important in a mesh than in a monolith?
A: Because output ports are consumed by independently-owned domains, so a producer cannot coordinate every change with every consumer the way a single team can internally. Explicit versioning and a deprecation policy let a domain evolve its product without silently breaking cross-domain consumers — without them, principle 2 collapses into the same fragile point-to-point coupling mesh aimed to replace.

Q: What is the relationship between data products and SLOs/SLAs in a mesh?
A: Each data product publishes service-level objectives (freshness, completeness, availability, correctness) that its consumers can rely on, turning quality from an implicit hope into a stated, monitorable promise. SLOs are part of the trustworthiness characteristic of a product; federated governance can require that every product declares them, which is how the mesh keeps quality accountable across autonomous owners.

Q: How does Strengholt's "domains without dogma" differ from a strict Dehghani reading?
A: Strengholt embraces domain orientation and decentralization but is willing to retain centralized or hybrid elements where they pay off, rather than treating all four principles as mandatory everywhere at once. The pragmatic stance accepts partial adoption, strong platforms, and selective centralization as legitimate, which is exactly the nuance the lesson wants you to hold against an all-or-nothing interpretation.

Q: What does "premature decentralization" specifically cost a small firm's estate?
A: It costs the firm its economies of scale and clear ownership: a previously manageable central estate is split into fragments, each needing an owner and tooling the small firm cannot staff, so quality and maintenance degrade. The lesson's phrase is "shattering a small firm's estate into unowned fragments" — the fragments are unowned precisely because there were never enough teams to own them.

Q: When is partial / hybrid mesh adoption the right answer rather than full or none?
A: Partial adoption fits when an organization has a few genuinely independent domains and an emerging platform, but also tightly-coupled, regulated cores best kept centrally governed. You decentralize ownership where autonomy is real (e.g. distribution analytics) and keep central control where accountability and coupling demand it (e.g. NAV), gated behind explicit triggers — which is the honest "not-yet / yes-partially" recommendation the memo exercise targets.

Q: What trigger conditions would convert a "not yet" mesh recommendation into a "yes"?
A: Triggers include the central data team becoming a demonstrable, persistent bottleneck; the emergence of several genuinely autonomous domains; sufficient headcount to staff both domain data teams and a dedicated platform team; and a self-serve platform mature enough that a domain can own a product without bespoke infrastructure. When these hold, the cost-benefit flips and the precondition of real domain autonomy is satisfied.

Q: Why is the memo asked to include "platform prerequisites" explicitly?
A: Because mesh's benefits depend entirely on the self-serve platform existing and being usable, so any recommendation that ignores platform readiness is unsound and invites platform-team starvation. Naming the prerequisites — catalog with domains/products, federated policy execution, paved-road pipelines, onboarding — forces the recommendation to be honest about whether the organization can actually support decentralization yet.

Q: Summarize the architect's core judgement for this lesson in one statement.
A: The architect must decide whether an organization's scale and domain multiplicity justify decentralizing ownership to domains backed by a mature self-serve platform and federated computational governance — adopting too early starves the platform and fragments a small estate, dismissing it outright traps a large group in a permanent central bottleneck. The decision hinges on genuine domain autonomy and platform capacity, is often partial and trigger-gated, and is organizational before it is technical.
