# Phase 8 · 1.8.11 Strangler fig (legacy replacement strategy) — 100 self-test questions

Q: What is the strangler fig pattern in one sentence?
A: It is an incremental legacy-replacement strategy where you place a facade in front of a legacy system and gradually route functionality to a new system until the legacy core is hollow, then switch it off. The new system grows around the old one piece by piece rather than replacing it in a single big-bang event. The name comes from the strangler fig vine that germinates in a host tree, grows around it, and eventually replaces it.

Q: Why is the pattern named after the strangler fig plant?
A: Martin Fowler took the metaphor from strangler fig vines he saw in Queensland rainforests, which germinate in a tree's branches, send roots down, and gradually envelop the host until they become self-sustaining and the original tree is gone. He later renamed it "Strangler Fig Application" to stress the metaphorical growth rather than the violent "strangling" connotation. The biological image captures the key idea: the new thing grows on and around the old until the old is no longer needed.

Q: What is the alternative to a strangler fig migration that the pattern exists to avoid?
A: The alternative is the big-bang rewrite, where you build the entire replacement in parallel and cut over everything at once on a target date. Big-bang rewrites frequently fail because they take too long, users need features in the meantime, and hidden legacy behaviours are hard to rediscover in the new system. The strangler fig spreads investment, risk, and returns gradually and visibly instead of betting everything on one switchover.

Q: In a Luxembourg fund-administration context, what kind of legacy system is a typical strangler-fig target?
A: A legacy core such as the transfer-agency (TA) system or a legacy reporting engine that can only be replaced incrementally because too many feeds and consumers depend on it. These estates accumulate decades of undocumented business rules, so a clean rewrite is risky. The strangler fig lets you peel off one report or one capability at a time while the rest of the legacy core keeps running NAV and shareholder processing.

Q: What is the "facade" in the strangler fig pattern?
A: The facade is a proxy or interception layer placed between the consumers and the back-end systems that routes each request either to the legacy system or to the new replacement. Consumers keep talking to the same stable interface and are unaware a migration is underway. As migration progresses you flip routing rules so the facade sends more traffic to the new system and less to the legacy one.

Q: Where does facade placement live for a data system, as opposed to a request-response application?
A: For data systems the facade is typically placed at the consumption layer using database views, a routing/serving layer, or CDC-fed parallel tables, rather than an HTTP proxy in front of an app. You point consumers at a view or a serving endpoint whose underlying source you can switch from legacy table to new gold model without the consumer noticing. The "interception" happens where queries are resolved, not where HTTP requests arrive.

Q: How can a database view act as a strangler facade?
A: A view gives consumers a stable name and shape while you change what it selects from underneath. You first point the view at the legacy mart, then once the new gold model is validated you repoint the same view at the new model, so consumers migrate with zero query changes. The view is the seam that lets you swap the system of record invisibly.

Q: What are the four phases shown in the Azure Architecture Center diagram of the pattern?
A: (1) Introduce the facade routing most requests to the legacy system; (2) incrementally shift functionality so the facade routes more requests to the new system; (3) decommission the legacy system once nothing depends on it, with the facade routing everything to the new system; (4) remove the facade so clients talk directly to the new system. Each phase is a stable, shippable state rather than a single cutover moment.

Q: Why does the strangler fig pattern reduce migration risk compared with a rewrite?
A: Because each increment is small, reversible, and independently shippable, so a problem affects one slice of functionality instead of the whole system. You learn the legacy system's hidden behaviour incrementally and validate each migrated piece against the original before moving on. Investment and returns arrive gradually and visibly, which also keeps stakeholders funded and engaged throughout.

Q: What does Fowler mean by finding "seams" in the legacy system?
A: A seam is a natural boundary where you can isolate a replaceable component without rewriting everything around it. Identifying seams lets you carve off one capability — say a single report or one feed — and route just that slice through the new system. Good seams usually follow domain or bounded-context lines, which is why DDD context mapping feeds directly into a strangler plan.

Q: What is "transitional architecture" in the strangler fig context?
A: It is the temporary scaffolding — the facade, parallel tables, CDC pipelines, reconciliation jobs — that exists only to enable the migration and is meant to be removed once it completes. Fowler stresses you should consciously accept it as throwaway, not let it calcify into permanent infrastructure. The discipline is to budget for tearing it down, not just standing it up.

Q: What is dual-write and why does the syllabus forbid it in a strangler design?
A: Dual-write means the application writes the same change to both the legacy and the new data store synchronously in the same transaction. It is forbidden because there is no atomic transaction spanning two systems, so a partial failure leaves the stores inconsistent, and the application is now coupled to both systems' availability. The pattern instead uses asynchronous CDC or an outbox to propagate one authoritative write to the other store.

Q: What replaces dual-write in a correctly designed strangler migration?
A: A single authoritative write to one store, with change-data-capture (CDC) or the transactional outbox pattern propagating those changes to the parallel store asynchronously. This keeps one system of record, avoids the two-phase-commit problem, and tolerates one store being briefly behind. Reconciliation then verifies the follower matches the leader before any cutover.

Q: What is the transactional outbox pattern and how does it support a strangler migration?
A: The outbox pattern writes business changes and an "event to publish" row into the same database transaction in one store, then a relay reads the outbox table and propagates events to the other system. Because the write and the event are atomic in one database, you never lose or duplicate the propagation, avoiding dual-write inconsistency. In a strangler migration it feeds the parallel new store from the legacy source of record reliably.

Q: What role does CDC play in feeding the parallel new system?
A: Change data capture streams inserts, updates, and deletes from the legacy database (often by reading its transaction log) into the new store, keeping the parallel system continuously in sync without the application writing twice. The Azure example uses an initial ETL load followed by CDC to sync the new domain database from the monolith. CDC is what makes the parallel run live rather than a stale snapshot.

Q: In the Azure database example, what sequence loads and syncs the new domain database?
A: An initial extract-transform-load (ETL) process bulk-loads the domain's historical data into the new domain database, after which a CDC process continuously syncs ongoing changes from the monolithic database. During this phase the legacy system still reads and writes the monolith while the new system writes the new database. You validate consistency between the two before cutover.

Q: What is a parallel run and what is its purpose before cutover?
A: A parallel run is a period where the legacy and new systems both process the same inputs so you can compare their outputs and build confidence the new system is correct. It is the trust-builder: you do not cut over until the new system has reproduced legacy results within agreed tolerances over enough time. It reuses the Phase-4 reconciliation pattern as the gating evidence.

Q: Why is reconciliation, not a calendar date, the gate for cutover in a strangler plan?
A: Because the goal is "the new system provably matches the old," which is a measurable condition, whereas a date is just hope. Tying cutover to reconciliation drift thresholds means you only switch when the data agrees, protecting consumers from a premature or wrong cutover. Dates-and-hope migrations cut over to meet a deadline and discover the discrepancies in production.

Q: Name three concrete reconciliation checks you would define for a NAV reporting mart migration.
A: Row counts per fund and share class between legacy and new marts, NAV-per-share compared within a defined numeric tolerance, and aggregate AUM reconciled at fund and total level. You would also check that every required template field is populated and that period-over-period values are plausible. Each check produces a measurable drift number that the cutover gate can test against a threshold.

Q: What is a "drift threshold" and how is it used?
A: A drift threshold is the maximum acceptable discrepancy between legacy and new outputs that still permits cutover — for example NAV-per-share differing by no more than a defined number of basis points, or zero row-count mismatch on active share classes. If reconciliation drift exceeds the threshold, the gate blocks cutover and the migration stays in parallel run. It turns "looks close enough" into a hard, auditable pass/fail.

Q: Why might NAV-per-share reconciliation use a tolerance rather than demanding exact equality?
A: Because legitimate differences in rounding rules, valuation timing, or floating-point precision can produce tiny deviations that are not real errors. A tolerance expressed in basis points or a fixed decimal place distinguishes acceptable numerical noise from a genuine logic discrepancy. The tolerance must still be tight enough that a real pricing or methodology bug breaches it.

Q: What should a strangler migration ADR contain?
A: The facade design (views/routing), the CDC-fed parallel-run period, the reconciliation gates and drift thresholds, the cutover criteria, the rollback plan, and a sunset date with a named decommission deliverable. It records why dual-write is excluded and what replaces it. Capturing it as an ADR makes the migration a reviewable, defensible decision rather than tribal knowledge.

Q: What is the "strangler that never finishes" anti-pattern?
A: It is a migration that gets stuck mid-flight: the new system carries most traffic but the legacy core is never fully decommissioned, so the organization permanently runs two systems plus the facade and CDC scaffolding. The legacy system "never dies — it just gains a twin," doubling cost and complexity indefinitely. The counter is sunset discipline: a funded, dated decommission milestone with an accountable owner.

Q: What organizational forces produce never-finishing stranglers, and what counters them?
A: Forces include the last few low-value legacy features being unattractive to migrate, budget evaporating once the headline benefits are captured, and no one owning decommissioning. The counters are a funded sunset milestone, a named owner who signs off the switch-off, and making decommissioning an explicit deliverable rather than an afterthought. Without these, the "90% done" migration stalls forever.

Q: What is "sunset discipline"?
A: Sunset discipline is treating the decommissioning of the legacy system as a planned, funded, owned deliverable with a date — not as something that happens automatically once the new system is live. It means budgeting the work to switch off legacy components, removing their data and feeds, and getting a sign-off. It is the antidote to the never-finishing strangler.

Q: Why does the facade itself need monitoring during the migration?
A: Because the facade carries all consumer traffic and routes between two systems, so it can become a single point of failure or a performance bottleneck. If it degrades, every consumer is affected regardless of which back end serves them. You monitor its latency, error rate, and routing correctness, and you must make sure it keeps up with the migration rather than lagging behind the cutovers.

Q: What does it mean that "the facade must keep up with the migration"?
A: The routing rules in the facade must be updated in lockstep as each capability moves to the new system, so traffic actually follows the migration. If the facade still points at the legacy system after the new one is validated, you gain none of the benefit and risk divergence. Stale routing config is a common reason a migration stalls invisibly.

Q: After migration completes, what are your two options for the facade?
A: You can remove the facade entirely and reconfigure clients to talk directly to the new system, completing the migration. Alternatively you can keep the facade as an adapter so legacy clients keep their old interface while you modernize the core for newer clients. Either way you weigh the facade's ongoing infrastructure cost against the compatibility it preserves.

Q: When is the strangler fig pattern NOT suitable?
A: When requests to the back-end system cannot be intercepted, when you lack access to the legacy source code needed to disable migrated features and redirect internal calls, when the system is small enough that a whole replacement is simple, or when you must fully decommission the original quickly. In those cases the facade-and-increment overhead is not justified. The pattern assumes the legacy system can coexist for an extended period.

Q: Why does the strangler fig pattern require the legacy system to coexist for an extended period?
A: Because migration proceeds one increment at a time, both systems must run simultaneously for the whole transition while functionality and data move across. If business or regulatory pressure forces a fast full shutdown, there is no time for the gradual coexistence the pattern depends on. The extended coexistence is the price of lower risk.

Q: How does the strangler fig pattern operationalize A.10 roadmapping and migration planning?
A: It turns the abstract roadmap into concrete, measurable migration increments with reconciliation gates, cutover criteria, and a dated sunset, each a milestone you can track. Roadmapping says "we will replace the legacy reporting engine"; the strangler plan says how, in what order, with what gates and rollback. It is the executable form of the migration roadmap.

Q: What is the first practical step when planning a strangler migration of a legacy reporting mart?
A: Pick the target and inventory it: list its consumers (every downstream report, feed, and team) and its inbound feeds (every source that populates it). You cannot route around a system safely until you know who depends on it and what feeds it. That inventory drives the order of increments and reveals which seams to cut first.

Q: Why must you inventory consumers before strangling a legacy mart?
A: Because every consumer must be migrated to the new source or routed through the facade, and an unknown consumer reading the legacy mart directly will break or silently diverge at cutover. The consumer count is also a cutover criterion — you do not switch off the legacy mart until all known consumers are migrated. Hidden consumers are the classic reason a "completed" migration leaves the legacy system alive.

Q: How can "consumer migration count" serve as a measurable cutover gate?
A: You track how many of the inventoried consumers have moved from the legacy mart to the new source; cutover and decommission are blocked until that count reaches 100% of known consumers (or an explicitly risk-accepted subset). This makes "are we ready to switch off?" an objective number rather than a judgement call. Combined with drift thresholds, it forms a complete, measurable gate.

Q: What is a rollback plan in a strangler migration and why is it required?
A: It is the documented procedure to revert routing to the legacy system if the new system fails after a cutover increment, including the conditions that trigger rollback. It is required because incremental cutovers can still surface problems, and consumers must not be stranded. The rollback window typically lasts only while the legacy data and sync still exist.

Q: When does the rollback window close in the Azure database example, and why does that matter?
A: Rollback is possible during the parallel-run phase and at the start of cutover, while the legacy domain tables, stored procedures, and CDC sync still exist. Once you remove those legacy objects, rolling back requires restoring them and replaying data changes — a high-effort, high-risk operation. So removing legacy objects should be a deliberate final step taken only after the new system is fully validated.

Q: Why should removing legacy objects be treated as a deliberate final step?
A: Because their removal closes the cheap rollback path: until then you can revert by repointing to the still-present legacy data, but afterwards recovery means restoring objects and replaying changes. Treating removal as the last, separate step preserves a safety net through validation. You only pull the net away once you are confident the new system is the system of record.

Q: How does the anti-corruption layer (ACL) pattern relate to the strangler fig pattern?
A: During migration both systems must coexist and call each other, and an ACL acts as an adapter that translates requests between them so the new system is not forced to adopt legacy semantics. Without it, cross-system calls leak legacy conventions into the new design or break components. The ACL protects the clean new model while still letting the legacy system reach new services.

Q: Give a concrete cross-system dependency that an ACL handles during a strangler migration.
A: The new NAV-calculation service might still need a security master record that only the legacy system holds, and the legacy system might need a result computed only by the new service. An ACL translates between the legacy record format and the new model so neither side adopts the other's conventions. This keeps the partially migrated estate working without corrupting the new design.

Q: What is "system of record" and why is establishing it central to a strangler migration?
A: The system of record is the authoritative source for a given piece of data — the one that owns writes and against which everything reconciles. A strangler migration is fundamentally about moving the system of record for each domain from legacy to new, one domain at a time. Cutover is precisely the moment the new store becomes the system of record and the legacy store stops being authoritative.

Q: In the Azure database decomposition example, what makes the new domain database the system of record?
A: After validation, routing responsibility shifts so the new system performs all reads and writes against the new domain database and the legacy monolith no longer serves that domain. At that point the corresponding domain tables, stored procedures, and dependencies are removed from the monolith. The new database is then the single source of truth for that domain.

Q: Why is a shared data store a complication in strangler migrations?
A: Because if both the legacy and new systems must read and write the same store during migration, you have to guarantee both can access it consistently and avoid one corrupting the other's assumptions. Shared state resists clean seams, so part of the work is decomposing the shared store into domain-owned stores. The Azure example explicitly extracts domain tables out of a shared monolithic database to break this coupling.

Q: How does DDD bounded-context decomposition feed a strangler migration plan?
A: Bounded contexts give you the natural seams to migrate one domain at a time — the context map identifies which capability and data can be carved off independently. Each context becomes a candidate increment with its own facade route, parallel run, and cutover. This is why a fund-administration context map (TA, fund accounting, custody, regulatory reporting, client reporting) is the natural decomposition for a phased replacement.

Q: For a fund administrator replacing a legacy TA system, which DDD artifact orders the migration increments?
A: The bounded-context map: you migrate context by context, choosing seams along context boundaries so each increment is self-contained and reconcilable. You might strangle the registry/holdings context first, then dealing, then reporting, each with its own gate. Cutting along context lines avoids tangled cross-domain dependencies that would otherwise force a near-big-bang.

Q: How does a strangler migration enable high-ROI replacements first?
A: Because the pattern decomposes the system into independently replaceable pieces, you can sequence increments by value and migrate the high-return components before the low-return ones. You capture benefit early instead of waiting for a complete rewrite. The trade-off is that the last low-value pieces are the ones most likely to stall, which is why sunset discipline matters.

Q: Why does the strangler fig pattern align with the Cost Optimization pillar of the Azure Well-Architected Framework?
A: It maximizes the return on the existing system by keeping it running and productive while you modernize incrementally, rather than writing off the investment in a big-bang cutover. You spend modernization budget where ROI is highest first and defer or skip low-value rewrites. The cost is the temporary transitional architecture you must fund and later remove.

Q: Why does the strangler fig pattern support the Reliability pillar?
A: Its incremental approach contains the blast radius: a fault during migration affects one slice of functionality, not the whole system, and you validate each increment with testing before moving on. Large systemic changes all at once are far more likely to cause widespread outages. Reversibility within the rollback window adds further resilience.

Q: A team proposes writing every NAV record to both the legacy mart and the new gold model in the same job. What is wrong and what do you propose instead?
A: This is dual-write: there is no atomic transaction across the two stores, so a crash between the writes leaves them inconsistent and couples the job to both stores' availability. Propose a single authoritative write to one store with CDC or an outbox relay propagating changes to the other, then reconcile. This keeps one system of record and removes the partial-failure window.

Q: A strangler migration has been "90% complete" for eighteen months. What is happening and what is your intervention?
A: It is the never-finishing-strangler anti-pattern: the remaining low-value legacy features were never migrated, so the legacy system, the new system, and the facade all run permanently. The intervention is to make decommissioning a funded, dated deliverable with a named owner who signs the switch-off, and to either migrate or explicitly risk-accept the stragglers. Without a sunset gate the migration never ends.

Q: During parallel run, AUM reconciles within tolerance but row counts differ by a handful of share classes. What do you do?
A: You do not cut over, because a row-count mismatch on active share classes means the new system is missing or duplicating records even if aggregate AUM happens to net out. Investigate the missing or extra share classes — often a filter, a join, or a late-arriving feed — fix it, and re-run reconciliation until both row counts and AUM pass. The drift threshold on row counts should be zero for active share classes.

Q: Why can aggregate figures reconcile while underlying records do not?
A: Because offsetting errors can cancel at the aggregate level — a missing positive and a missing negative, or a duplicated and a dropped record, can net to the same total. Reconciling only the aggregate masks per-record defects that will surface later for individual consumers. This is why a strangler plan reconciles at multiple grains: aggregate AUM, per-fund, and per-share-class row counts.

Q: How do you demonstrate that a strangler plan has "measurable gates instead of dates-and-hope"?
A: Show that cutover is conditioned on quantified reconciliation results — defined drift thresholds on row counts, NAV-per-share tolerance, AUM agreement — plus a consumer-migration count, all evaluated automatically. There is no step that says "cut over on the 1st regardless." Each gate produces a pass/fail number that a reviewer can audit.

Q: Why is "dates-and-hope" a failure mode rather than a plan?
A: Because it commits to a cutover date and assumes the new system will be correct by then, discovering discrepancies only after consumers are already on the new system. It optimizes for a deadline rather than for correctness, and there is no objective stop condition. Regulated fund data makes this especially dangerous because a wrong cutover can corrupt filings.

Q: How might a strangler-driven cutover of a NAV mart go wrong if a feed is missed in the inventory?
A: An un-inventoried inbound feed would keep populating the legacy mart while consumers move to the new model, so the new model would silently miss those records, producing incomplete NAV or AUM. Reconciliation may catch the divergence if the legacy mart is still compared, but if the legacy mart is already retired the gap goes unnoticed. This is why inventorying inbound feeds is step one.

Q: What is the relationship between strangler fig migration and your Phase-4 reconciliation work?
A: The Phase-4 reconciliation pattern is reused as the parallel-run trust-builder: the same row-count, tolerance, and aggregate checks that validate data quality become the gates that decide cutover readiness. Reconciliation that previously proved a load was correct now proves the new system matches the legacy one. The migration leans entirely on that existing capability.

Q: How do Dagster schedules and sensors support a strangler migration's gates?
A: You can implement reconciliation as scheduled Dagster jobs that compute drift each period and a sensor that holds cutover until drift is within threshold, mirroring the four-eyes and completeness gates used in regulatory reporting. The migration's gates become orchestrated, auditable steps rather than manual checks. This ties the strangler plan into the same orchestration plane as the rest of the platform.

Q: Why is a legacy TA or reporting engine particularly hard to replace big-bang in a Luxembourg estate?
A: Because it encodes years of undocumented business rules, regulatory edge cases, and bespoke client logic that no one fully remembers, and any gap surfaces as a wrong NAV or a failed CSSF filing. A rewrite cannot rediscover all that hidden behaviour at once, so a big-bang cutover almost guarantees missed cases. Incremental strangling lets you reconcile each behaviour against the live legacy system before retiring it.

Q: How does a strangler migration interact with regulatory reporting obligations?
A: Reports such as AIFMD Annex IV or PRIIPs EPT must remain correct and on time throughout the migration, so you keep the legacy engine authoritative for a report until the new pipeline reconciles within tolerance and a four-eyes approval signs the cutover. You never move a regulatory output to an unvalidated system. The reconciliation gate is effectively a submission-grade completeness check applied to the migration.

Q: What measurable evidence would you require before strangling the Annex-IV production path?
A: Field-by-field comparison of the new XML output against the legacy output for several periods, schema validation against the published ESMA XSD, completeness checks on every required template field, and plausibility checks (AUM within tolerance of last period). Only when these pass across enough periods, with a documented approval, do you cut the regulatory feed to the new system. Until then the legacy path remains the system of record.

Q: Why is "the facade is a single point of failure" a serious concern for a NAV data product?
A: Because every consumer — board packs, distributors, the API, the regulatory pipeline — flows through the facade during migration, so a facade outage takes down access to NAV for all of them at once. That couples availability of the whole product to the migration scaffolding. You mitigate with redundancy, monitoring, and keeping the facade thin so it adds minimal failure surface.

Q: What does "route at the consumption layer" mean concretely for a data warehouse migration?
A: It means consumers query a stable view, a semantic-layer metric, or a serving endpoint whose backing source you control, so you switch from legacy table to new gold model by changing the routing/view definition rather than touching consumers. The interception point is where queries are resolved. This is the data-warehouse analogue of an HTTP facade in an application migration.

Q: How does a semantic layer help as a strangler facade for metrics?
A: If consumers get AUM and NAV only through the semantic layer, you can repoint the metric's underlying model from legacy to new without any dashboard or notebook changing its query. The semantic layer is a single, governed interception point for metric definitions. Swapping the source behind one metric migrates every consumer of that metric at once.

Q: What is the difference between the facade and the anti-corruption layer in a migration?
A: The facade is the routing/interception layer that decides whether a request goes to legacy or new; the anti-corruption layer is an adapter that translates data and semantics between the two systems so neither corrupts the other's model. The facade is about traffic direction; the ACL is about model isolation. A migration often uses both: the facade routes, the ACL translates cross-system calls.

Q: Why does the syllabus say a never-finishing strangler means the legacy system "gains a twin"?
A: Because if cutover and decommission never complete, you end up permanently operating the legacy system, the new system, and the facade plus sync scaffolding — two estates instead of one, with dual-running cost and drift risk forever. The replacement was supposed to retire the legacy core, but instead it just added a parallel copy. The twin is the symptom; missing sunset discipline is the cause.

Q: What is "dual-write drift" and how does it relate to multi-year parallel estates?
A: Dual-write drift is the gradual inconsistency that accumulates when two stores are written independently and there is no single authoritative source reconciled between them. Over a long parallel estate the two systems quietly disagree, and no one trusts either. Avoiding dual-write (using CDC/outbox from one system of record) and enforcing a sunset prevents the estate from drifting indefinitely.

Q: How do you decide the order in which to strangle capabilities out of a legacy system?
A: Order by a mix of value (high-ROI first), risk (least-coupled seams first to build confidence), and dependency (you cannot migrate something that depends on an un-migrated capability without an ACL). Bounded-context boundaries usually provide the cleanest cut points. The sequence is part of the migration ADR, not improvised mid-flight.

Q: What is the "facade as adapter for legacy clients" option, and when would you keep it?
A: After the core is migrated you can retain the facade so older clients keep their legacy interface while newer clients use the modern system directly, deferring the cost of migrating those last clients. You keep it when migrating the remaining legacy clients is expensive or low value and the facade's running cost is acceptable. The trade-off is permanent transitional infrastructure, which conflicts with full sunset.

Q: Why is access to the legacy source code often a precondition for the pattern?
A: Because to redirect traffic cleanly you usually need to disable already-migrated features inside the legacy system and reroute its internal calls, which requires modifying its code. If you cannot change the legacy system at all, you may be unable to prevent it from continuing to serve migrated functionality. The Azure guidance lists "can't access the legacy source code" as a reason the pattern may not fit.

Q: When is replacing the whole system at once actually the better choice over a strangler fig?
A: When the system is small and simple enough that a full rewrite is low risk, or when you must decommission the original quickly (for example a hard regulatory or licensing deadline) and cannot afford an extended coexistence. The strangler's facade-and-increment overhead is only justified for large, complex, long-lived systems. For a trivial legacy tool, the scaffolding costs more than it saves.

Q: How does the strangler fig pattern support the Operational Excellence pillar?
A: It provides a continuous-improvement, small-batch approach where incremental replacements are safer to deploy than large systemic changes, fitting standardized, repeatable processes and safe-deployment practices. Each increment is a controlled change with its own validation. This reduces the operational shock that big-bang cutovers impose on teams.

Q: What is the role of "validate consistency before cutover" in the Azure database example?
A: Before the new domain database becomes the system of record, you compare it against the monolith to confirm the migrated data matches, catching ETL or CDC errors while rollback is still cheap. Only after this validation does routing shift fully to the new database and legacy objects get removed. It is the reconciliation gate expressed in the Azure walkthrough.

Q: Why should the sunset milestone name a specific decommission deliverable and signer?
A: Because "decommission the legacy system" without a concrete artifact and an accountable owner tends never to happen — the work is unglamorous and easy to defer. Naming what gets switched off (specific feeds, tables, jobs) and who signs makes it a tracked, owned task. This is the structural counter to the never-finishing strangler.

Q: What concrete things "get switched off" at the sunset of a legacy reporting mart?
A: The legacy mart's inbound feeds, its scheduled refresh jobs, the facade routes still pointing at it, the CDC/sync pipelines, and finally the mart's tables and stored procedures themselves, with access revoked. Each is an explicit deliverable in the decommission plan. Until all are off, the legacy system is still alive and consuming cost.

Q: How would you prove to a review board that dual-write is excluded from your design?
A: Show the architecture has a single authoritative write per domain to one store, with CDC or an outbox relay propagating to the parallel store, and point to the absence of any code path that writes the same record to both stores synchronously. Show the reconciliation job that verifies the follower matches the leader. The evidence is the data-flow diagram plus the ADR rationale, not an assertion.

Q: What is the danger of letting transitional architecture become permanent?
A: The facade, parallel tables, and sync pipelines were sized as temporary scaffolding, so leaving them in place adds permanent cost, complexity, and failure surface that nobody budgeted to operate long-term. They also tend to ossify into load-bearing infrastructure that is then hard to remove. Fowler's discipline is to plan their removal as part of the migration, not to discover them years later.

Q: How does the strangler fig pattern relate to a 1.9.7-style schema/data evolution policy?
A: A strangler migration is a large, planned evolution where the new schema replaces the old; the same versioning, deprecation-notice, and compatibility discipline applies at the level of the whole mart or system. The migration ADR is effectively an evolution decision with reconciliation gates. The two are the same governance applied at different scales.

Q: Why does the syllabus frame the strangler plan as part of the architect's portfolio?
A: Because a defensible migration (strangler) plan demonstrates the architect can retire legacy estates incrementally with measurable gates and rollback, which is a core enterprise-architecture competency. It pairs with the ADR log, context map, and roadmap as evidence of disciplined change. A reviewer should be able to trace the legacy-replacement decision in the portfolio.

Q: What question should you be able to answer to defend a strangler plan, per the "Done when" criteria?
A: You should be able to verify the plan has measurable gates (reconciliation drift thresholds) instead of dates-and-hope, explain why dual-write is forbidden and what replaces it, and name the organizational forces that produce never-finishing stranglers with a counter for each. These three checks confirm the plan is rigorous, not aspirational. Each maps to a specific design artifact.

Q: How does CDC differ from a nightly batch copy when feeding the parallel new store?
A: CDC streams individual changes continuously (often from the database log), keeping the new store near-real-time and enabling live reconciliation, whereas a nightly batch copy leaves the new store up to a day stale and only proves correctness for the snapshot moment. For a strangler parallel run you want CDC so the new system genuinely tracks production. Batch copies are fine for the one-off initial historical load.

Q: Why combine an initial ETL load with ongoing CDC rather than using only one?
A: ETL efficiently moves the large historical backlog in bulk, which CDC alone would be slow to replay, while CDC then keeps the new store in sync with ongoing changes that ETL cannot capture in real time. Together they give you a complete and current parallel store. The Azure example uses exactly this load-then-sync pattern.

Q: A consumer reports their numbers changed after a strangler cutover even though reconciliation passed. First thing to check?
A: Check whether that consumer was actually routed through the facade/view or was reading the legacy mart directly outside the inventory, because a hidden direct dependency would have been migrated unexpectedly or missed entirely. Also verify the reconciliation grain covered that consumer's specific slice (e.g. their share classes). Reconciliation passing at aggregate does not guarantee every per-consumer figure matched.

Q: Why is consumer inventory completeness a recurring theme in strangler failures?
A: Because the pattern's safety depends on routing every consumer through the facade and migrating each one before sunset; any consumer you did not know about either breaks at cutover or keeps the legacy system alive. Hidden consumers are the most common reason a "finished" migration cannot actually decommission the legacy core. Discovery (lineage, query logs, catalog) is therefore part of the plan.

Q: How can lineage and catalog metadata reduce strangler migration risk?
A: They reveal which downstream tables, reports, and consumers depend on the legacy mart, making the consumer inventory more complete and the cutover gate trustworthy. Lineage also lets you trace whether a migrated figure still ties back to source. Good metadata turns "we think these are the consumers" into evidence.

Q: A legacy pricing engine exposes no interception point — consumers call its stored procedures directly with no view or proxy layer. What does this imply for a strangler approach?
A: With no place to intercept requests, you cannot insert a facade to route traffic between legacy and new, which is one of the conditions under which the Azure guidance says the pattern may not fit. Your first job is to create a seam — wrap the procedures behind a view or a thin service that consumers are migrated onto — before any strangling can begin. Until an interception point exists, the pattern's routing mechanism has nowhere to live.

Q: What is the difference between "incremental migration" and "running two systems forever"?
A: Incremental migration is purposeful: each increment moves the system of record and is followed by decommissioning the corresponding legacy part, so the legacy footprint shrinks to zero. Running two systems forever is the failure where increments stop happening and nothing is decommissioned. The presence of a shrinking legacy footprint and a sunset date is what distinguishes the two.

Q: Why does Fowler stress organizational/cultural change alongside the technical pattern?
A: Because without revised development practices and a culture that owns the migration through to completion, the new system will accumulate the same rot as the old, and the migration may never finish. The pattern is as much about how teams work — funding decommissioning, owning seams, sustaining momentum — as about routing and CDC. Technical scaffolding alone does not retire a legacy estate.

Q: What does it mean to "switch off" the legacy core at the end of a strangler migration?
A: It means the legacy system no longer serves any traffic, its feeds and jobs are stopped, its data is archived or removed, and its infrastructure is decommissioned, with a sign-off confirming nothing depends on it. The facade either routes everything to the new system or is itself removed. This final switch-off is the whole point of the pattern, and skipping it is the anti-pattern.

Q: How do you set a cutover criterion that combines data correctness and consumer readiness?
A: Require both that reconciliation drift is within threshold across all grains (row counts, NAV-per-share, AUM) for a sustained period and that the consumer-migration count has reached all known consumers. Cutover proceeds only when both conditions are met. This guards against switching when the data is right but consumers are not ready, or vice versa.

Q: Why might you migrate the lowest-risk capability first even if it is not the highest value?
A: To build confidence and prove the migration machinery — facade, CDC, reconciliation, rollback — on a low-stakes increment before tackling high-value, high-risk capabilities. Early success also sustains stakeholder funding and momentum. The sequencing balances proving the approach against capturing ROI, and the chosen order is recorded in the ADR.

Q: What is a "pivotal" capability in a fund-admin strangler and why migrate it carefully?
A: A pivotal capability is one many others depend on — for instance the security master or the NAV-striking process — so migrating it affects most downstream consumers at once. You migrate it carefully, often late and with extensive reconciliation, because errors propagate widely. Until it moves, an ACL bridges the new and legacy versions.

Q: How does the strangler fig pattern keep clients "unaware that a migration is in progress"?
A: By preserving a stable interface at the facade, so consumers send the same requests or run the same queries while routing silently shifts behind it. The contract the consumer sees does not change even as the back end is replaced increment by increment. Invisibility to consumers is a design goal, not an accident.

Q: Why is preserving the consumer contract important during a fund-data migration?
A: Because distributors, board reporting, the API, and regulatory pipelines have their own schedules and validations, and a surprise interface change would break loads or filings. Keeping the contract stable (same view shape, same EMT field semantics) lets you migrate the source without renegotiating every downstream integration. The facade is what holds the contract steady.

Q: What ODCS/data-contract concern interacts with a strangler migration?
A: If the data product is under a contract, the migration must not break the guaranteed schema, semantics, freshness, or SLAs as you swap the backing system — the contract is the invariant the facade must preserve. A breaking change during migration should still go through the deprecation/versioning process. The contract gives consumers assurance the source switch will not silently change their data.

Q: How would you handle a required schema change that coincides with a strangler cutover?
A: Separate the concerns: complete the source migration while preserving the existing contract, then version and deprecate the schema change through the normal evolution policy with a notice period and migration path. Bundling a breaking schema change into the invisible cutover defeats the point of the facade. If they must coincide, treat it as a contracted breaking change, not a silent one.

Q: What is the trust-building purpose of running the new system in "shadow" alongside legacy before any traffic shifts?
A: Shadow running feeds the new system the same inputs and compares its outputs to legacy without serving any consumer, so you accumulate reconciliation evidence with zero consumer risk. It lets you catch divergences before the facade routes any real traffic to the new system. It is the safest stage of the parallel run.

Q: Why is "rollback is possible until the legacy database is fully decommissioned" an important design property?
A: It means the migration is reversible through most of its life, so a defect found after cutover can be remediated by repointing to the still-present legacy data rather than a forced forward fix. This dramatically lowers the risk of each increment. The property only holds while legacy objects and sync remain, which is why their removal is the deliberate final step.

Q: How do you distinguish a healthy strangler migration from mesh-washing or folder-renaming?
A: A healthy strangler shows the legacy footprint genuinely shrinking — capabilities and the system of record moving across, legacy objects being removed, consumers re-routed — with measurable gates and a sunset. Cosmetic exercises rename or wrap legacy systems without ever moving the system of record or decommissioning anything. Evidence of decommissioned legacy parts is the tell.

Q: What metric would you put on a dashboard to track strangler progress honestly?
A: The shrinking legacy footprint: percentage of capabilities (or consumers, or data domains) cut over to the new system and the count of legacy objects/feeds decommissioned, trending toward zero remaining. A flat "90% for months" line exposes a stall. Tracking decommission count, not just migration count, prevents the never-finishing failure from hiding.

Q: Why is it dangerous to report only "functionality migrated" without "legacy decommissioned"?
A: Because functionality can be migrated while the legacy parts stay running indefinitely, so a migration looks complete while the estate is still doubled. Reporting decommissioned legacy components alongside migrated functionality reveals whether the strangler is actually finishing. The gap between the two numbers is exactly the never-finishing risk.

Q: In summary, what three things make a strangler plan defensible to a review board?
A: Measurable cutover gates (reconciliation drift thresholds plus consumer-migration count) rather than dates; an explicit no-dual-write design with CDC/outbox from a single system of record and reconciliation; and a funded, dated, owned sunset milestone that counters the never-finishing anti-pattern. Each is backed by an artifact — the ADR, the data-flow diagram, the decommission deliverable. Together they show the migration will both start safely and actually finish.
