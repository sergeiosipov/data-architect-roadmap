# Phase 5 · 12.2.2 Disaster-recovery patterns — 150 self-test questions

Q: What does RPO (Recovery Point Objective) measure, and in what units?
A: RPO is the maximum duration of acceptable data loss in a disaster, expressed in units of time (e.g. "15 minutes of data" or "4 hours of data"), not in bytes or rows. It effectively answers "how far back in time can we afford to lose writes?" and is driven by how frequently data is replicated or backed up. For a fund administrator a 4-hour RPO on a NAV mart means up to 4 hours of valuation updates could vanish, which the business must explicitly accept.

Q: What does RTO (Recovery Time Objective) measure?
A: RTO is the maximum duration of acceptable downtime in a disaster, also measured in units of time such as "8 hours of downtime", where the precise definition of "downtime" is part of your specification. It bounds how long the service or pipeline may be unavailable before objectives are breached. RTO and the failover detect-plus-switch time must be aligned: if failover takes 30 minutes to detect and promote, you cannot claim a 10-minute RTO.

Q: How do RTO and RPO differ conceptually?
A: RPO is about data loss (how much recent data you can lose), while RTO is about downtime (how long you can be offline). They are independent dials: you might tolerate hours of downtime but zero data loss (high RTO, low RPO) or the reverse. Designing DR means setting both per workload flow because the topology and cost that satisfy one do not automatically satisfy the other.

Q: What is RCO and why might an architect track it alongside RTO/RPO?
A: RCO (Recovery Consistency Objective) bounds the acceptable degree of inconsistency across related data after recovery — i.e. how out-of-sync interdependent stores may be when service resumes. It matters when a NAV depends on positions, prices and FX that replicate independently: each store may individually meet its RPO yet still be mutually inconsistent at a point in time. Azure's core BC/HA/DR article centres on RTO and RPO; RCO is a complementary objective you add when cross-store consistency is itself a recovery requirement.

Q: How does Azure's reliability guidance define business continuity?
A: Business continuity is the state in which a business can continue operations during failures, outages, or disasters, achieved through proactive planning, preparation, and resilient systems and processes. It is the umbrella goal; high availability and disaster recovery are the two strategies you combine to deliver it. A business continuity plan must include people and manual processes, not just platform resiliency features.

Q: How does Azure distinguish high availability from disaster recovery?
A: High availability designs the solution to be resilient to common, day-to-day issues (transient faults, reboots, single-node failures) so it meets routine uptime needs. Disaster recovery plans for uncommon, catastrophic events (region loss, ransomware, mass data deletion) that HA cannot absorb. The split is driven by risk classification: common/expected risks go to HA, rare/severe risks go to DR.

Q: In Azure's risk-classification model, what separates a "common" risk from an "uncommon" one?
A: Common risks are planned and expected — transient network blips, VM reboots for patching, timeouts — and a high-availability strategy must control for each of them. Uncommon risks are unforeseeable catastrophic events such as natural disasters or major attacks, and these are handled by disaster recovery processes. The same physical event can be classified differently per workload, which is the next escalation of this idea.

Q: Why can a full Azure region outage be an HA risk for one workload but a DR risk for another?
A: For a single-region workload, losing the region is a catastrophic, uncommon event handled by DR. But for an active-active workload spanning multiple regions with full replication and automatic region failover, the same outage is absorbed routinely and is therefore classified as an HA risk. Risk classification depends on the architecture and business requirements, not just the event.

Q: What is the relationship between "criticality tier" classification and DR design?
A: Azure's business-continuity planning starts by classifying workloads into criticality tiers based on importance to the business, each with different availability requirements. Higher-tier workloads justify more expensive DR topologies and tighter RTO/RPO; lower-tier ones accept cheaper, slower recovery. This is the architect's lever for spending DR budget where it matters — gold NAV marts versus bronze raw landing.

Q: List the four rungs of the DR topology ladder in increasing capability and cost.
A: The ladder runs backup-and-restore → pilot light → warm standby → active-active (also called hot standby or multi-site active/active). Each rung lowers RTO/RPO but raises steady-state cost, because you keep progressively more of the secondary environment provisioned and in sync. The architect's job is to place each data tier on the cheapest rung that still meets its objectives.

Q: In a backup-and-restore DR topology, what is provisioned in the secondary location before a disaster?
A: Almost nothing is running in the secondary region — you store backups (often in another region) and provision/restore the environment only when disaster strikes, typically using infrastructure-as-code. This is the cheapest rung but has the longest RTO because you must spin up infrastructure and restore data on demand. It is appropriate for re-derivable or low-criticality stores like bronze raw data.

Q: What characterises a "pilot light" DR topology?
A: In pilot light, a minimal core of the secondary environment is kept running and continuously fed with data (for example a replicated database), while the rest of the compute is dormant and scaled up only on failover. It costs more than backup-restore because the data layer is live, but less than warm standby because most compute is off. RTO is shorter than backup-restore since the critical data is already present and current.

Q: How does warm standby differ from pilot light?
A: Warm standby keeps a scaled-down but fully functional copy of the entire workload running in the secondary region, so failover is mostly a matter of scaling up and redirecting traffic. Pilot light only keeps the data/core alive and must provision much of the application on failover. Warm standby therefore gives a lower RTO at higher steady-state cost, which is why gold marts often sit here.

Q: What defines an active-active (multi-site) DR topology and its cost trade-off?
A: Active-active runs full production in two or more regions simultaneously, serving live traffic from each with synchronised data, so a region loss is absorbed with near-zero RTO/RPO and is treated as an HA event. It is the most expensive rung because you fully provision and operate every region and must solve cross-region data consistency and conflict resolution. Reserve it for the highest-criticality flows where downtime is intolerable.

Q: Why does the topology ladder follow a cost curve rather than being free to climb?
A: Each rung up keeps more of the secondary environment provisioned and more data in sync in real time, so you pay continuously for idle-but-ready capacity, replication egress, and operational complexity. The architect must cost the delta of the chosen topology versus single-region to justify it. The discipline is choosing the lowest rung that still meets the tier's RTO/RPO, not climbing for its own sake.

Q: What is failover, and why must it be aligned with RTO?
A: Failover is the process of switching traffic and operations from a failed primary deployment to a secondary site, typically involving detection, decision, and redirection. It takes time to detect the primary has failed and to switch, so that elapsed time must fit inside the workload's RTO. Azure Front Door and Traffic Manager can automate traffic failover; Azure Site Recovery automates VM/on-prem failover.

Q: What is failback, and why is it often harder than failover?
A: Failback is restoring operations to the primary region after it recovers. It is complex because data may have been written to the secondary after failover began, so you must reconcile or migrate those writes back without loss or conflict. Planning failback explicitly — including how to handle the divergent data — is part of a complete DR plan, not an afterthought.

Q: Is DR an automatic feature of Azure?
A: No — Azure explicitly states DR is not an automatic feature; the platform provides capabilities and services you assemble into a DR strategy, but the plan, runbooks, and testing are your responsibility. Many DR plans rely on process controls and manual intervention. This is why "the cloud is resilient" is not a substitute for an owned, tested DR design.

Q: What three documented elements should every DR plan include per Azure's guidance?
A: A strong DR plan should include a clear runbook (step-by-step recovery actions), a well-defined communication plan, and a structured escalation path. These turn strategy into decisive action under pressure, when ad-hoc thinking fails. The plan should also be testable, because an untested plan is likely to fail in a real disaster.

Q: For Azure Storage, what does LRS (locally redundant storage) replicate and where?
A: LRS replicates your data three times within a single physical datacenter in the primary region, providing at least 99.999999999% (11 nines) durability over a year. It protects against drive, server, and rack failures but not against a datacenter-wide fire or flood, where all replicas can be lost. It is the cheapest option and suits easily reconstructable data.

Q: What does ZRS (zone-redundant storage) protect against that LRS does not?
A: ZRS replicates data synchronously across three or more availability zones within the primary region, each with independent power, cooling, and networking, giving at least 99.9999999999% (12 nines) durability. It keeps data readable and writable even if a whole zone goes down, which LRS cannot survive. It does not by itself protect against a region-wide disaster affecting multiple zones.

Q: How does GRS (geo-redundant storage) replicate data, and what is its durability?
A: GRS first copies data synchronously using LRS within the primary region, then copies it asynchronously to a secondary region hundreds of miles away, where it is again stored with LRS, giving at least 99.99999999999999% (16 nines) durability. The asynchronous cross-region hop is what creates an RPO and a potential data-loss window. By default the secondary copy is not readable until a failover occurs.

Q: What does GZRS combine, and when is it recommended?
A: GZRS combines ZRS in the primary region (synchronous across three-plus zones) with asynchronous geo-replication to a secondary region stored as LRS, also at 16 nines durability. It is Microsoft's recommendation for applications needing maximum availability, durability, and regional-disaster protection at once. It requires a primary region that both supports availability zones and has a paired region.

Q: Why does the cross-region copy in GRS/GZRS create an RPO greater than zero?
A: Because the copy to the secondary region is asynchronous — writes commit in the primary and replicate later — the secondary lags behind by some interval, and a sudden loss of the primary can lose everything written after the last replicated point. That interval is precisely the RPO for the storage account. Azure's Geo Priority Replication can constrain block-blob RPO to 15 minutes or less, but it is never instantaneously zero.

Q: What do the "RA-" variants RA-GRS and RA-GZRS add?
A: The read-access variants make the secondary-region copy available for read operations at all times, even before any failover, via a `-secondary` endpoint suffix (e.g. `myaccount-secondary.blob.core.windows.net`). Plain GRS/GZRS keep the secondary inaccessible until a failover repoints DNS. RA variants let you test secondary reads in advance and serve reads during a primary outage; note Azure Files does not support RA-GRS/RA-GZRS.

Q: For a GRS account, is failover to the secondary region automatic or customer-initiated?
A: For an unplanned regional outage, customer-managed account failover is required to restore write availability — it is not automatic. The failover repoints the storage DNS so the secondary endpoints become the new primary, after which you can read and write there. Because replication was asynchronous, you must anticipate data loss and inconsistencies across containers when you fail over.

Q: A fund's bronze raw landing zone stores re-ingestible EMT/EPT source files. Which storage redundancy and DR rung fits, and why?
A: Because the bronze data can be re-fetched or re-ingested from upstream providers, it is re-derivable, so LRS or ZRS plus backup-and-restore is defensible — you accept a longer RTO and rebuild rather than pay for live cross-region copies. Spending warm-standby money on re-derivable data wastes budget that gold marts need. The architect documents that recovery means re-ingestion, with the cost delta noted.

Q: For a gold NAV mart that downstream reporting depends on, why might GZRS plus warm standby be justified despite the cost?
A: The gold NAV mart is source-of-truth, not re-derivable cheaply, and downstream regulatory and client reporting depend on it, so a short RTO/RPO is worth the steady-state spend. GZRS gives zone plus region durability and a bounded RPO, and a warm standby keeps a ready-to-promote copy for fast recovery. The architect defends this with numbers: the cost of an extra region versus the cost of a missed NAV deadline and regulatory exposure.

Q: What distinguishes "re-derivable" data tiers from "source-of-truth" tiers in DR planning?
A: Re-derivable tiers (bronze raw, intermediate transforms, cached aggregates) can be regenerated from upstream sources or by re-running pipelines, so their DR can lean on cheap backup-restore and re-computation. Source-of-truth tiers hold the only authoritative copy (the system-of-record positions, the published NAV) and demand replication and tested restore because loss is permanent. Classifying every store into one of these two buckets is the first step of tiering a platform.

Q: In PostgreSQL, what is the difference between file-based log shipping and streaming replication?
A: File-based log shipping ships completed 16 MB WAL segments to the standby one file at a time via `restore_command`/`archive_command`, so the data-loss window is at least a full segment (tunable down with `archive_timeout`). Streaming replication has the standby connect to the primary via `primary_conninfo` and receive WAL records continuously as they are generated, shrinking the lag to typically under a second. Streaming is the basis of a low-RPO warm/hot standby.

Q: What is the difference between a PostgreSQL warm standby and a hot standby?
A: A warm standby continuously replays WAL and is ready to be promoted but does not accept client queries. A hot standby does the same replication but additionally serves read-only queries when `hot_standby = on`, adding read capacity. Both give comparable failover speed; the difference is whether the standby is usable for reads before promotion.

Q: What role does the WAL play in PostgreSQL replication, and from which three sources can a standby obtain WAL?
A: The Write-Ahead Log records every change before it is applied, and the standby reaches the primary's state by replaying that WAL. A standby obtains WAL, in order, from the archive via `restore_command`, then from its local `pg_wal` directory, then via streaming replication from the primary if `primary_conninfo` is set. This layered sourcing lets a standby catch up after being offline and then stream live.

Q: What is the minimum `wal_level` setting on the primary to support streaming replication?
A: `wal_level = replica` is the minimum required to support streaming replication and continuous archiving; the default already supports this. `minimal` strips out the information standbys need and so cannot feed replication, while `logical` adds still more for logical replication. You also need `max_wal_senders` set high enough for your standby connections.

Q: What do physical replication slots do, and what is their main risk?
A: A replication slot ensures the primary does not recycle WAL segments until every consuming standby has received them, created with `pg_create_physical_replication_slot('name')` and referenced via `primary_slot_name` on the standby. The risk is that if a standby disconnects, the primary retains WAL indefinitely and can fill `pg_wal`, so you bound it with `max_slot_wal_keep_size`. Slots are more precise than the blunt `wal_keep_size` retention setting.

Q: How do you promote a PostgreSQL standby to primary during failover?
A: You promote it with the `pg_ctl promote` command or the `SELECT pg_promote();` SQL function; the standby restores any immediately available WAL from archive or `pg_wal`, exits recovery, and begins accepting writes. Crucially it does not try to contact the old primary during promotion, so it works even when the primary is gone. Set `recovery_target_timeline = 'latest'` so a future standby can follow the new timeline.

Q: What is the practical difference between asynchronous and synchronous PostgreSQL replication for RPO?
A: Asynchronous replication (the default) commits on the primary immediately and ships WAL afterward, so a primary crash can lose the un-replicated transactions — a small but non-zero RPO. Synchronous replication, enabled by setting `synchronous_standby_names` non-empty, makes commit wait for standby confirmation, driving RPO toward zero at the cost of commit latency. For a NAV system, the choice trades data-loss risk against throughput.

Q: What do `synchronous_commit = remote_write` versus `remote_apply` control?
A: With `remote_write` the primary's commit waits until the standby's operating system has received and written the WAL (but not necessarily flushed to disk), giving lower latency but slightly weaker durability. With `remote_apply` the commit waits until the standby has actually replayed the transaction so it is visible to reads there, which is strongest but slowest. The default `on` waits for a standby disk flush, sitting between the two.

Q: What does `synchronous_standby_names = 'ANY 2 (s1, s2, s3)'` express?
A: It is quorum-based synchronous replication: a commit is acknowledged once any 2 of the listed standbys s1, s2, s3 confirm, with the rest treated as asynchronous. This balances durability and availability — you survive losing any single synchronous standby without blocking commits. The `FIRST n (...)` form instead picks a priority-ordered fixed set rather than any-quorum.

Q: What is Kafka MirrorMaker 2 and what is it used for?
A: MirrorMaker 2 (MM2) is Kafka's cross-cluster replication tool, built on Kafka Connect, that consumes records from a source cluster and produces them to a target cluster for disaster recovery, geo-distribution, or migration. It runs continuously, propagating new messages, and is the standard answer to "how do I get my topics into a second region?". It replaces the original MirrorMaker with a far richer, connector-based design.

Q: What are the three main connectors that make up MirrorMaker 2?
A: MM2 comprises the MirrorSourceConnector (does the core topic data replication source-to-target), the MirrorCheckpointConnector (synchronises consumer-group offsets and emits checkpoints for failover), and the MirrorHeartbeatConnector (emits periodic heartbeats to monitor replication health and lag). Together they let you both move the data and let consumers resume correctly after failover. Heartbeats are how you tell whether the mirror is caught up.

Q: Why is offset translation necessary in MirrorMaker 2 cross-cluster DR?
A: Because each Kafka cluster assigns its own independent offsets, a consumer that fails over to the target cluster cannot reuse the source-cluster offsets directly. The MirrorCheckpointConnector tracks how source offsets map to target offsets and writes checkpoints, so a failed-over consumer group can resume at the correct position rather than re-reading or skipping messages. Without it, failover risks duplicate processing or data gaps.

Q: Why does MirrorMaker 2 prefix replicated topic names with the source cluster name?
A: MM2 renames replicated topics with the source cluster's alias as a prefix (e.g. `us-east.nav-events`) so that replicated topics are clearly distinguishable from topics natively created on the target. This prevents naming collisions and confusion, and is what makes bidirectional active-active replication possible without infinite loops. You must account for the prefix in consumer configuration after failover.

Q: Why is Kafka cross-cluster replication described as asynchronous, and what does that mean for RPO?
A: MM2 consumes committed records from the source and produces them to the target after the fact, so the target always trails the source by some replication lag — replication is asynchronous and typically active-passive. That lag is the effective RPO: messages produced but not yet mirrored are at risk if the source region is lost. Monitoring heartbeat lag tells you how large that window currently is.

Q: For a lakehouse, what does "re-derivable vs source-of-truth tiers" imply for DR effort?
A: In a lakehouse, gold and silver layers are usually re-derivable by re-running transformations from bronze, so their DR can rely on replicating bronze plus re-running pipelines rather than independently protecting every derived table. The true source-of-truth — bronze raw plus the table catalog/metastore — needs first-class backup and replication because losing it is unrecoverable. This insight lets you spend DR budget on the irreplaceable layer, not on copies you can recompute.

Q: Why are Iceberg/Delta snapshots not a complete backup when the catalog is lost?
A: Table-format snapshots are pointers and metadata maintained by a catalog/metastore; if the catalog that resolves table names to current metadata is destroyed, the data files in object storage become orphaned and the tables are effectively unrecoverable even though the bytes still exist. So lakehouse DR must explicitly protect the catalog (e.g. its backing database) as a source-of-truth tier. A snapshot is a time-travel feature, not a substitute for backing up the metadata layer.

Q: What is a "game day" in DR testing?
A: A game day is a scheduled exercise that simulates a disaster — for instance failing over a region or killing a database — to validate that the DR plan, tooling, and people actually work under realistic conditions. It is how you discover broken runbooks, missing permissions, or RTO/RPO gaps before a real outage. Azure's guidance stresses including the human processes in drills, not just the technical steps.

Q: What is a failover drill and what should it record?
A: A failover drill executes the actual failover procedure (promote standby, repoint traffic, validate the recovered service) and measures the outcome. It should record the achieved RTO and achieved RPO and compare them against the targets, so gaps are quantified rather than assumed. The lesson's "done when" requires exactly this: a tabletop drill writeup of achieved versus target objectives.

Q: Why does Azure insist DR drills include human processes, not just technical ones?
A: Because real DR plans rely heavily on process controls, manual intervention, runbooks, communication, and escalation — and these fail far more often than the infrastructure when untested. Drilling only the technical failover leaves the slowest, most error-prone parts unvalidated, so the real RTO is discovered during the incident. Including people surfaces unclear ownership, stale contact lists, and missing decision authority.

Q: What is the danger of never testing your DR plan, framed by Azure?
A: Azure states bluntly that if you have not tested your recovery processes in a disaster simulation, you are more likely to face major problems during an actual disaster — and you cannot validate the feasibility of your stated RTO. In other words, an untested RTO is a guess. The organisation otherwise discovers its real RTO during the outage and then explains the gap to a regulator.

Q: What is an Azure region pair, and how does it relate to residency-bound DR?
A: A region pair is a predefined coupling of two regions (often within the same geography) used by some services for replication and staged updates. For residency-bound DR, the key point is that most Azure services now let you replicate to a region of your choice inside the same data-residency boundary, so you are not strictly limited to the Azure-defined pair. This flexibility lets EU fund data stay within EU borders during cross-region DR.

Q: For a Luxembourg fund administrator, how do EU residency constraints shape the choice of secondary DR region?
A: The secondary region must sit inside the permitted data-residency boundary — typically another EU region — so that NAV-relevant and investor data never leaves the legal jurisdiction even during failover. Azure's residency-bound DR pattern is to select a secondary in the same compliance boundary when cross-region failover is allowed. If cross-region replication is forbidden, you fall back to in-region zones plus backup-restore and accept a longer RTO.

Q: What is the "in-region-only resilience" DR pattern and when is it used?
A: When residency or sovereignty rules forbid any cross-region replication or failover, you rely on availability zones, in-region redundancy, and backup-and-restore only, and set recovery objectives that account for longer recovery during a region-wide event. You then document formal acceptance of the residual risk that a whole-region disaster cannot be quickly recovered. This is the compliant-but-slower posture for the most tightly regulated data.

Q: Under the residency-bound "restore after regional recovery" pattern, when do you actually restore?
A: For workloads where cross-region data movement is not allowed, you plan to restore from backup after the primary region itself recovers, rather than failing over to another region. You only restore when the existing resources did not survive the outage. This trades a longer RTO for strict residency compliance, and the trade must be documented and accepted.

Q: Why is DORA relevant to this lesson's DR design work?
A: DORA (the EU Digital Operational Resilience Act) gives RTO/RPO design and DR testing regulatory teeth for EU financial entities, requiring documented, tested operational-resilience capabilities including recovery objectives and scenario testing. A fund administrator must be able to recover NAV-relevant data within stated objectives and evidence that it has tested doing so. This turns "what happens if the region dies?" from an engineering nicety into a compliance obligation.

Q: How should an architect elicit honest RTO/RPO numbers from the business?
A: Frame the conversation around concrete consequences and cost rather than asking "how much downtime is OK?", because everyone answers "zero". Present the cost curve — each tighter objective costs progressively more — and force trade-offs against budget, so the business reveals what it will actually pay for. Discuss per-flow, because different flows (publish NAV vs back-office reporting) genuinely have different tolerances.

Q: Why is asking the business for RTO/RPO without showing cost a trap?
A: Without the cost dimension, stakeholders default to demanding near-zero RTO and RPO everywhere, which is technically possible but prohibitively expensive and usually unjustified. Azure explicitly warns that aiming for zero/zero is difficult and costly, and that technical and business stakeholders must decide realistic requirements together. The architect's job is to make the price of each objective visible so the business sets honest, fundable targets.

Q: What is the first step the lesson requires when designing platform DR?
A: Tier every store in the platform by RPO/RTO class — for example gold marts versus bronze raw — so each store's recovery requirements are explicit before any topology is chosen. You cannot pick a topology or cost it without knowing the objective it must meet. This classification is what later justifies why gold gets warm standby while bronze gets backup-restore.

Q: After tiering stores, what are the next three steps the lesson prescribes?
A: Choose a topology per tier from the ladder (backup-restore, pilot light, warm standby, active-active) and justify it; cost the delta of each chosen topology versus single-region; then script and run one tabletop failover drill recording the achieved RTO/RPO. The sequence forces every design choice to be both justified and priced, then validated by an actual drill. The deliverable is a defensible per-tier DR design with measured outcomes.

Q: A NAV pipeline must publish by a fixed cut-off each day. How does that deadline translate into RTO?
A: The RTO for the NAV-producing stores must be short enough that, after a disaster, you can still recover and complete the valuation before the regulatory/client cut-off — the deadline effectively caps the tolerable downtime. If the cut-off is 18:00 and a failure at 14:00 needs the pipeline back in two hours to make it, the RTO is roughly two hours, not eight. Missing the cut-off has client and regulatory consequences, which is what justifies the topology spend.

Q: "Failover succeeds but the secondary NAV mart is missing the last 20 minutes of price updates." What objective failed?
A: The RPO failed: asynchronous replication left a 20-minute data-loss window that exceeded the tier's target, so recovered data is stale. RTO may have been met (the service came back quickly) yet data correctness was not. The fix is tightening RPO — synchronous or near-synchronous replication, or Geo Priority Replication — accepting the added latency/cost, or formally widening the accepted RPO.

Q: You promote a PostgreSQL standby, but applications still try to write to the old primary's address. What is the first thing to check?
A: Check the connection-routing layer — the DNS name, virtual IP, connection pooler, or service endpoint that applications use — because promotion makes the standby writable but does not automatically redirect clients. Failover must repoint that endpoint to the new primary; otherwise apps keep hitting the dead or read-only node. This is why failover design always includes a traffic-redirection mechanism, not just database promotion.

Q: A GRS storage account loses its primary region but the app cannot read the data. What is the likely cause?
A: The account is plain GRS, not RA-GRS, so the secondary copy is not readable until a customer-initiated account failover repoints DNS — until then the secondary is inaccessible by design. The fix during an outage is to perform the account failover; the design fix is to use RA-GRS/RA-GZRS so the secondary serves reads at any time. Note the asynchronous lag means some recent writes may still be lost after failover.

Q: Your PostgreSQL standby falls behind and the primary's `pg_wal` is filling the disk. What is the likely cause and remedy?
A: A replication slot is retaining WAL because a standby disconnected or is lagging, and the primary will not recycle those segments until the slot's consumer catches up. The immediate remedy is to fix or remove the stalled standby/slot; the preventive fix is `max_slot_wal_keep_size` to cap how much WAL a slot may pin. Without that cap, a dead standby can take the primary down by exhausting disk.

Q: Why might LRS be a deliberate, compliant choice rather than just the cheapest option?
A: If governance requires data to be replicated only within a single region, LRS (or ZRS) is the correct choice precisely because it does not copy data across regions, keeping it inside the residency boundary. Here the limitation is a feature: geo-redundancy would violate the constraint. The trade-off is accepting that a region-wide disaster needs in-region recovery and a longer RTO.

Q: What does it mean that ZRS writes are synchronous across zones, and why does it matter for RPO?
A: A ZRS write returns success only after the data is committed to replicas in all three availability zones, so there is no inter-zone lag and effectively zero RPO for a single-zone failure. This is why ZRS keeps data both readable and writable through a zone outage. The synchronous guarantee is what distinguishes intra-region zone redundancy from asynchronous cross-region geo-replication.

Q: Why is GRS's RPO non-deterministic without features like Geo Priority Replication?
A: Standard asynchronous geo-replication does not guarantee how far the secondary lags — it depends on write volume, network, and queue depth — so the data-loss window on a sudden primary loss is variable. Azure's Geo Priority Replication adds a guarantee that block-blob RPO stays at or under 15 minutes, turning an unbounded lag into a bounded one. For a tier with a strict RPO, you need such a guarantee or a synchronous mechanism.

Q: How should the catalog/metastore of a lakehouse be treated in a backup strategy?
A: The catalog (e.g. the metastore database that maps table names to current snapshot metadata) is a source-of-truth tier and must be backed up and replicated with the same rigor as a transactional database, because losing it orphans all the data files. Snapshotting the data files alone is insufficient. In practice this means PITR and geo-replication of the catalog's backing store, tested by restore.

Q: What is the role of infrastructure-as-code in a backup-and-restore DR topology?
A: IaC (Bicep, ARM, Terraform) lets you recreate the secondary environment quickly and reproducibly during a disaster, which is essential when almost nothing is pre-provisioned. It reduces recovery time and human error versus manual rebuilding, directly improving achievable RTO. For the cheapest DR rung, IaC is what keeps RTO from becoming unbounded.

Q: Why does Azure recommend storing backups in a different region from the primary data?
A: A backup co-located with the data it protects can be destroyed by the same regional disaster, defeating its purpose, so backups should be stored separately, typically in another region within the compliance boundary. This separation is what lets a backup survive a primary-region loss. For residency-bound data, that other region must still be inside the permitted boundary.

Q: Why does Azure say backup restoration should be a "last resort" within a DR plan?
A: Because backups are taken infrequently, restoring from them usually involves more data loss (a larger RPO) and longer recovery (a larger RTO) than failing over to a live replica. The DR plan should specify the sequence of recovery attempts — replica failover first, backup restore only if those fail or data is corrupted. Backups remain essential for corruption/ransomware where replicas are also compromised.

Q: When is restoring from backup the only option, even if a replica exists?
A: When the data itself is corrupted or maliciously altered (e.g. ransomware or a bad migration), failing over to a synchronously replicated copy just replicates the corruption — so you need a clean point-in-time backup instead. Replicas protect against infrastructure loss, not against bad data being faithfully copied. This is why immutable backups complement replication rather than being replaced by it.

Q: How does point-in-time restore (PITR) relate to RPO for a database tier?
A: PITR lets you restore a database to any moment within a retention window, so the achievable RPO for a corruption or deletion event is bounded by the granularity of the log/backup chain, not by a fixed snapshot interval. It is the mechanism behind tight RPOs for "undo the bad change" scenarios. Its limit is the retention period: you cannot restore to a point older than the window allows.

Q: Why must the RTO budget explicitly include restore time, not just detection and decision time?
A: Restoring large datasets takes real time, and Azure stresses that the workload's RTO must account for how long restoration actually takes — which you only know by testing it. If you budget RTO assuming instant restore, the real recovery blows the objective. This is why restore drills time the operation, turning an assumption into a measured number.

Q: What is the difference between durability and availability in Azure Storage redundancy terms?
A: Durability (the "nines" like 11 or 16) is the probability your data is not lost over a year, driven by how many copies exist and where. Availability is whether you can read/write the data right now, which depends on whether a surviving copy is reachable during an outage. LRS has high durability but zero cross-region availability during a region loss, illustrating that the two are distinct.

Q: Why is "we have backups" an incomplete answer to a DR auditor?
A: Backups only count if you can demonstrate a recent, successful restore within objective time — the auditor's real question is "when did we last restore successfully, with a date?". Untested backups routinely fail to restore (corruption, missing dependencies, wrong retention), so possession is not recovery. This lesson's done-when criteria require answering with a date, not a shrug.

Q: How does choosing a topology per tier let you optimise total DR cost?
A: Because most platforms have a few critical source-of-truth tiers and many re-derivable ones, putting only the critical tiers on expensive rungs (warm standby, active-active) and the rest on cheap rungs (backup-restore) concentrates spend where downtime actually hurts. Costing each delta versus single-region makes the savings explicit. The result is a defensible "€ per protected tier" rather than blanket over-provisioning.

Q: Why is active-active the only topology where a region loss can be a near-zero-RTO HA event?
A: Active-active already serves live traffic from multiple regions with synchronised data, so losing one region simply removes one of several active sites without a provisioning or promotion step — there is nothing to spin up. The remaining regions keep serving, so detection-and-switch time approaches zero. The price is running and synchronising full production everywhere, plus solving multi-master consistency.

Q: What consistency challenge does active-active introduce that warm standby does not?
A: Active-active means multiple regions accept writes concurrently, so you must resolve write conflicts and maintain cross-region consistency (last-writer-wins, CRDTs, partitioned ownership, or quorum). Warm standby has a single active writer, so it sidesteps conflict resolution entirely. This complexity is a major reason active-active is reserved for the highest-value flows.

Q: For Kafka DR, why is monitoring MirrorMaker heartbeat lag operationally important?
A: Heartbeat lag tells you how far the target cluster trails the source, which is your live RPO for that data stream — a growing lag means a widening data-loss window if the source fails now. If you fail over without watching lag, you cannot know how much in-flight data you lost. Heartbeats are the signal that the mirror is "caught up" enough to make failover safe.

Q: In an active-passive Kafka DR setup, what must consumers do after failing over to the secondary cluster?
A: Consumers must point at the target cluster's brokers, account for the source-cluster topic-name prefix, and resume from the translated offsets supplied by the MirrorCheckpointConnector so they neither re-process nor skip messages. Simply reconnecting without offset translation risks duplicate or lost processing. This is why the checkpoint connector is essential, not optional, for clean failover.

Q: Why might a fund administrator prefer active-passive over active-active Kafka for SWIFT/transaction event streams?
A: Active-passive keeps a single source of truth for ordering and offsets, avoiding the conflict-resolution and duplicate-message complexity that active-active introduces — important when message ordering and exactly-once semantics matter for financial messaging. The trade-off is a non-zero RTO during failover and the offset-translation step. For strictly ordered SWIFT-style flows, simpler and correct usually beats faster and ambiguous.

Q: What is a tabletop drill, as opposed to a live failover?
A: A tabletop drill walks the team through the DR scenario and runbook on paper or in a controlled simulation — validating decisions, communication, and the sequence of steps — without necessarily taking real production traffic down. It is cheaper and lower-risk than a live cutover and surfaces process gaps. The lesson asks you to script and run one, recording achieved versus target RTO/RPO.

Q: Why should each flow, not just each whole application, get its own RTO/RPO?
A: Azure notes that different flows within one workload have different importance — in a fund platform, publishing the official NAV is more critical than back-office reconciliation reporting — so blanket objectives either over-protect cheap flows or under-protect critical ones. Per-flow objectives let you place each flow's stores on the right topology rung. This granularity is how DR budget is spent precisely.

Q: How does the "achieved vs target" framing prevent DR self-deception?
A: By measuring the actual RTO and RPO during a drill and writing them next to the targets, you replace optimistic assumptions with evidence and expose gaps that must be closed or formally accepted. A plan that only states targets has never been proven to meet them. Azure stresses that drills validate the feasibility of your RTO — the achieved number is that validation.

Q: Why is "the cloud is already redundant, so we don't need DR" a flawed argument?
A: Built-in redundancy (LRS three copies, zone redundancy) protects against hardware and zone failures, but Azure is explicit that DR is not automatic and that region loss, ransomware, and human error still require an owned, tested plan. Redundancy is HA-level protection against common faults, not DR-level protection against catastrophes. Conflating the two is exactly how an org discovers its real RTO mid-outage.

Q: Why is `recovery_target_timeline = 'latest'` important on a PostgreSQL standby intended for HA?
A: After a promotion, the cluster's WAL history forks onto a new timeline; setting `recovery_target_timeline = 'latest'` lets a following standby detect and follow that new timeline rather than getting stuck on the old one. Without it, a standby may fail to track the newly promoted primary. It is the default and the correct choice when any standby might itself become primary.

Q: What is `archive_timeout` and how does it bound RPO in file-based log shipping?
A: `archive_timeout` forces the primary to switch and archive the current WAL segment after a set interval even if it is not full, so the standby receives WAL at least that often. Without it, a low-traffic primary might not fill a 16 MB segment for a long time, leaving a large data-loss window. It is the lever that caps RPO in file-based shipping, at the cost of more frequent (partly empty) segment archiving.

Q: A regulator asks a fund administrator to evidence its NAV-data RTO. What artifact best satisfies this?
A: A tabletop or live failover-drill writeup that records the achieved RTO and RPO against the documented targets, dated and signed off, plus the per-tier DR design that states the objective and the topology meeting it. This turns the claimed RTO into tested evidence rather than an aspiration. Under DORA-style scrutiny, a stated-but-untested RTO is a finding waiting to happen.

Q: Why does the secondary region for GRS being "hundreds of miles away" matter for DR?
A: Sufficient geographic separation ensures a single natural disaster (earthquake, flood, regional power loss) cannot take out both primary and secondary, which is the whole point of geo-redundancy. If the regions were adjacent, a wide-area event could destroy both copies. The distance is a deliberate design property that protects against region-scale catastrophes.

Q: What is the trade-off introduced by synchronous cross-region replication for a strict-RPO database?
A: Synchronous cross-region replication can drive RPO to zero because commits wait for the remote copy, but the inter-region network latency is added to every commit, hurting write throughput and user-facing latency. Over long distances this latency can be prohibitive. Architects therefore often choose synchronous within a region (zones) and asynchronous across regions, accepting a small cross-region RPO.

Q: How does soft delete / immutability on backups defend against ransomware in a DR context?
A: Immutable, soft-deletable backups cannot be altered or deleted within their locked retention window even by an attacker with admin rights, so a ransomware actor cannot destroy your recovery point. This makes the backup a trustworthy last resort precisely when replicas are compromised. It addresses the "rogue administrator" and ransomware risks that plain replication cannot.

Q: Why is the LEI of a fund irrelevant to RPO calculation but relevant to a DR runbook?
A: RPO is a time-based data-loss objective derived from replication/backup cadence, so the Legal Entity Identifier — a static reference identifier — does not affect it. However, a DR runbook and communication plan may need the LEI to correctly notify counterparties, regulators, or messaging systems about the affected entity during recovery. It is reference data the plan references, not an input to the objective math.

Q: What does it mean to "cost the delta versus single-region", and why is it the persuasive number?
A: It is the incremental annual cost of the chosen DR topology above running the platform in one region only — the extra storage, compute, egress, and operations a given rung adds. Expressing DR spend as a delta per tier (rather than a total) directly answers the CFO's "what does this protection cost us?" and lets build-vs-buy and tier-by-tier decisions be argued with numbers. The architect who can produce this delta wins the design argument.

Q: Why is read-access to the secondary (RA-GRS) useful even before any disaster?
A: It lets you test that your application reads correctly from the secondary endpoint in advance, so you have confidence the failover read path works rather than discovering bugs during an outage. It also enables serving read traffic from the secondary to offload the primary. Validating the secondary read path beforehand is itself a DR-testing discipline.

Q: In Azure terms, why might a region pair be used for "staged" platform updates as well as DR?
A: Region pairs are designed so that platform updates and recovery can be coordinated across the two regions — for example sequencing maintenance so both halves of a pair are not updated simultaneously — which protects availability during planned changes. This staged behaviour is a reliability feature distinct from your own DR replication choice. For DR you may still pick a non-paired secondary within your residency boundary.

Q: How would you justify warm standby for gold marts but backup-restore for bronze, "with numbers"?
A: You state each tier's RTO/RPO target, show that gold's deadline-driven RTO (say two hours, near-zero RPO) cannot be met by rebuild-from-backup, and that warm standby's incremental cost is small against the regulatory and client cost of a missed NAV. For bronze, you show it is re-ingestible so backup-restore's longer RTO is acceptable and cheaper, with the avoided warm-standby cost quantified. The defence pairs objectives, achievable topology, and the cost delta for each tier.

Q: Why must a DR plan specify the order of recovery attempts before restoring from backup?
A: Because backup restore is the slowest, highest-data-loss option, the plan should attempt faster, lower-loss recoveries first — replica failover, then partial recovery — and only fall to backup restore when those are impossible or the data is corrupted. An explicit sequence prevents responders from reaching for the worst option under pressure. It also keeps the achieved RTO/RPO as good as the situation allows.

Q: How does failure mode analysis (FMA) feed DR design?
A: FMA systematically identifies how a workload or its components could fail and how the system behaves under each failure, which surfaces the specific disaster scenarios your DR plan must cover. It turns vague "what if the region dies?" worry into an enumerated set of failure modes with required responses. Each significant mode then maps to an HA control or a DR procedure with its own RTO/RPO.

Q: Why is it dangerous to assume your DR secondary has the same performance capacity as primary?
A: In pilot light and warm standby, the secondary is deliberately scaled down to save cost, so on failover it may not handle full production load until it scales up — and that scale-up time is part of RTO. If you assume parity, you under-budget RTO and the recovered service is overwhelmed. The drill must therefore test recovery under realistic load, not just connectivity.

Q: What single sentence captures why an architect owns the DR topology rather than the ops team alone?
A: The architect owns it because topology choice is a design-and-cost decision — which tier gets which rung at what incremental spend to meet which regulatory objective — and only that ownership ties RTO/RPO, the cost delta, and residency constraints into one defensible design. Ops executes the runbook, but the tier-by-tier topology that determines whether objectives are even achievable is an architectural deliverable. Without that ownership, DR is chosen on aesthetics and discovered in production.

Q: After failing over, you want to rejoin the old PostgreSQL primary as a standby without a full base backup — which tool and prerequisite do you need?
A: Use `pg_rewind`, which synchronises a diverged data directory by copying only the blocks that changed since the timelines split, far cheaper than a fresh `pg_basebackup`. It requires either `wal_log_hints = on` or data checksums enabled on the cluster so it can identify changed blocks, plus enough WAL (via archive or slot) covering the divergence point. Without one of those hint sources, `pg_rewind` refuses to run and you fall back to a full re-sync.

Q: Why does Azure's customer-managed unplanned storage failover leave you without geo-redundancy afterwards?
A: An unplanned failover converts the account to `LRS` in the new primary region and deletes the copy in the original region, so you are protected only locally until you act. To regain geo-redundancy you must reconfigure the account back to `GRS`/`GZRS`, after which Azure begins copying to a fresh secondary. This means a region failover quietly degrades your durability posture, and the runbook must include the re-enable step.

Q: What is the `Last Sync Time` property and how do you use it before an unplanned storage failover?
A: `Last Sync Time` (LST) is the most recent moment at which all primary-region writes were confirmed replicated to the secondary; anything written before it survives failover, anything after it may be lost. You read it before failing over to estimate the exact data-loss window — your realised RPO for that incident — rather than guessing. It is also checked before failback to compare against writes made on the temporary primary.

Q: How does a planned storage-account failover differ from an unplanned one in terms of data loss?
A: A planned failover first synchronises all remaining data to the secondary and only then switches, so there is no expected data loss and the redundancy configuration is preserved (no need to re-enable geo-redundancy). An unplanned failover is for when the primary is already unreachable, accepts the LST data-loss window, and downgrades the account to `LRS`. Use planned failover for drills and migrations, unplanned only in a genuine primary outage.

Q: Why can you not initiate an Azure storage account failover on an `LRS` or `ZRS` account?
A: Account failover repoints DNS from the primary endpoints to a secondary-region copy, but `LRS` and `ZRS` keep all copies inside the primary region and have no secondary region to fail over to. Only geo-redundant tiers (`GRS`, `GZRS`, and their `RA-` variants) expose a secondary that failover can promote. So if a tier needs cross-region failover capability, it must be provisioned geo-redundant from the start.

Q: A standby keeps requesting WAL files the archive no longer has, logging "requested WAL segment has already been removed". First thing to check?
A: The archive retention or the primary's WAL recycling has outpaced the standby — the segment was pruned before the standby fetched it, common after a long disconnect. First confirm whether a physical replication slot exists (`primary_slot_name`) to pin WAL, and check `archive_command` is actually retaining segments long enough. The durable fix is a slot bounded by `max_slot_wal_keep_size`, or a longer archive retention, so segments survive standby downtime.

Q: How does `restore_command` interact with streaming replication when a standby is catching up?
A: A standby drains WAL from the archive via `restore_command` first, then its local `pg_wal`, and only switches to live streaming once it exhausts the archived segments and reaches the primary's current position. This lets a long-offline standby replay cheaply from archive before opening a streaming connection rather than overloading the primary with a huge backlog. If `restore_command` is missing or wrong, the standby can only stream and may fail to catch up past the primary's retained WAL.

Q: What is `synchronous_commit = off` and why is it dangerous for a NAV system of record?
A: `synchronous_commit = off` lets the primary acknowledge a commit before the WAL record is flushed to its own disk, trading durability for throughput — a crash can lose recently "committed" transactions even on a single node. For a NAV system of record this means published valuations could silently vanish on a power loss, which is unacceptable. It is a per-session-tunable performance knob, never appropriate for the authoritative valuation path.

Q: Distinguish `synchronous_commit` from `synchronous_standby_names` — they sound related but do different jobs.
A: `synchronous_standby_names` decides which standbys (and how many, via `ANY`/`FIRST`) must acknowledge before a commit returns, effectively turning on synchronous replication. `synchronous_commit` then decides what level of acknowledgement counts — `remote_write`, `on` (remote flush), or `remote_apply` — and also governs local-disk durability when no sync standby is named. You need the first to choose the standbys and the second to choose how strong their confirmation must be.

Q: A consumer fails over to the Kafka target cluster but re-reads thousands of already-processed messages. What configuration was likely missing?
A: Automated offset sync was off — `sync.group.offsets.enabled` and `emit.checkpoints.enabled` must both be `true` on the checkpoint connector for translated offsets to be written into the target cluster's `__consumer_offsets`. Without them the failed-over group starts from its configured `auto.offset.reset` (often `earliest`), replaying everything. The fix is enabling both, accepting that time-based syncing still allows a small duplicate window at the cutover point.

Q: What is the default interval for MirrorMaker 2 offset and checkpoint syncing, and why does it bound failover duplication?
A: Both `sync.group.offsets.interval.seconds` and `emit.checkpoints.interval.seconds` default to 60 seconds, so the target's stored offsets can trail the source by up to that interval. On failover a consumer resumes from the last synced offset, meaning up to roughly a minute of already-processed messages may be re-consumed. Tightening the intervals shrinks the duplicate window at the cost of more checkpoint traffic, but never reaches exactly-once across clusters.

Q: Why does MirrorMaker 2 not sync offsets for idle consumer groups by default, and why does that matter for DR?
A: MM2's offset translation tracks active groups whose source offsets it observes; a group that has stopped committing (idle) produces no new checkpoints, so its translated offset on the target can be stale or absent. In DR this means a rarely-running batch consumer might fail over to a wrong position, re-reading or skipping. You mitigate by keeping critical groups committing or by explicitly seeding their target offsets before relying on failover.

Q: In MirrorMaker 2's `IdentityReplicationPolicy` versus the default, what changes about topic naming, and what is the trade-off?
A: The default `DefaultReplicationPolicy` prefixes replicated topics with the source alias (e.g. `us-east.nav-events`), preventing collisions and enabling loop-free bidirectional replication. `IdentityReplicationPolicy` keeps the original name on the target, which eases one-way migration and lets consumers reconnect without renaming — but it cannot safely support active-active because it loses the loop-prevention prefix. Choose identity only for unidirectional DR/migration, never for bidirectional mirroring.

Q: How do you size `max_wal_senders` and `max_replication_slots` for a primary feeding two streaming standbys plus pg_basebackup?
A: Each streaming standby consumes one WAL sender, and an online `pg_basebackup --wal-method=stream` consumes an additional sender, so you need `max_wal_senders` at least 3 with headroom for reconnects. `max_replication_slots` must cover one slot per standby you give a persistent slot (here 2) plus any temporary slots base backup uses. Under-sizing either causes "all replication slots are in use" or "number of requested standby connections exceeds max_wal_senders" errors at the worst time.

Q: What does `pg_basebackup --wal-method=stream` give you over `fetch`, and why prefer it when seeding a standby?
A: With `stream`, `pg_basebackup` opens a second connection and streams WAL generated during the backup alongside copying the data files, guaranteeing the backup is self-consistent and immediately startable. With `fetch`, WAL is only collected at the end, risking missing segments if the primary recycled them mid-backup. `stream` is the safer default for seeding a warm standby, at the cost of needing an extra WAL sender slot.

Q: Your warm standby shows replication lag climbing during the nightly NAV batch but recovers by morning. Is this an RPO problem?
A: It is a transient RPO widening: during the heavy write burst the async standby trails further, so a primary loss mid-batch would lose more than the steady-state window. Whether it is a problem depends on whether that peak lag still fits the tier's RPO target during the critical NAV window, not just on average. The fix, if it breaches, is faster replication (sync or quorum), more standby I/O, or scheduling the batch outside the protected window.

Q: How would you measure achieved RPO during a PostgreSQL failover drill, in concrete terms?
A: Compare the last transaction visible on the promoted standby against the last transaction the application believed it committed on the old primary — e.g. via a monotonic sequence, an `xact_commit` watermark, or the timestamp of the last replicated row. The gap, in time or transactions, is the achieved RPO for that drill. You record it next to the target so a breach is quantified rather than assumed, satisfying the lesson's "achieved vs target" requirement.

Q: How would you measure achieved RTO during the same drill?
A: Start the clock at the simulated failure (or at detection, per your defined "downtime") and stop it when the service is fully writable and serving correct traffic through the redirected endpoint — not merely when the standby was promoted. Logging timestamps for detect, decide, promote, repoint, and validate breaks the RTO into stages so you see where time goes. The total against target is the achieved RTO recorded in the writeup.

Q: A pilot-light secondary takes 40 minutes to scale compute on failover, blowing a 30-minute RTO. What are your options?
A: Either move that tier up a rung to warm standby so the compute is already running and only needs scaling, pre-provision the dormant compute as reserved/stopped capacity that starts faster, or formally widen the RTO to accommodate the scale-up. The root cause is that pilot light keeps only data live, so cold-compute startup is inside RTO. The architect prices the warm-standby delta against the cost of a 40-minute breach to decide.

Q: Why is detection time, not just switch time, a critical and often-underestimated part of RTO?
A: Failover cannot begin until the system or operators conclude the primary has actually failed, and conservative health checks (to avoid flapping) deliberately wait through several missed probes, adding minutes. If your monitoring needs three failed 30-second checks before declaring an outage, that 90 seconds-plus is RTO you spent before any recovery action. Drills must time detection explicitly, because an optimistic RTO that ignores it is unachievable in practice.

Q: How does a connection pooler like PgBouncer or a virtual IP simplify PostgreSQL failover for clients?
A: It gives applications a single stable endpoint, so on failover you repoint the pooler/VIP at the newly promoted primary and clients reconnect transparently without each app changing its connection string. This decouples client configuration from the physical primary's address, which is the routing layer that promotion alone does not update. Tools like Patroni automate exactly this — promote a standby and update the endpoint atomically.

Q: What does Patroni add on top of raw PostgreSQL streaming replication for HA/DR?
A: Patroni is a cluster manager that uses a distributed consensus store (etcd, Consul, or ZooKeeper) to elect a leader, automate failover/promotion, and prevent split-brain by fencing the old primary. It turns the manual `pg_promote` plus endpoint-repoint dance into an automated, quorum-driven process, lowering RTO. It does not change the underlying replication or RPO — it orchestrates the people-and-process steps that drills otherwise expose as slow.

Q: What is split-brain in a database failover context, and why is it catastrophic for NAV data?
A: Split-brain is when two nodes both believe they are primary and accept writes — typically after a network partition where the standby promotes itself while the old primary is still alive. For NAV data this means two divergent sources of truth with conflicting valuations that cannot be cleanly merged, corrupting the authoritative record. Fencing (STONITH, slot/lock-based, or consensus quorum) prevents it by ensuring the old primary is demoted or killed before promotion.

Q: How does quorum-based fencing prevent split-brain during automated failover?
A: A consensus layer (etcd/Consul) only grants the leader lease to a node that can reach a majority of members, so a minority-side node — including a partitioned old primary — cannot hold or regain leadership and must demote itself. This guarantees at most one writable primary at any time. It is why automated HA stacks pair replication with a quorum store rather than trusting two nodes to negotiate alone.

Q: Why might an architect deliberately choose asynchronous cross-region replication even when synchronous is technically available?
A: Synchronous cross-region commit adds the round-trip network latency to every write, which over hundreds of miles can be tens of milliseconds per commit, crippling throughput for a high-write NAV pipeline. Async keeps commits fast and accepts a small, bounded RPO instead. The common compromise is synchronous within a region across zones (sub-millisecond) and asynchronous across regions, getting zero intra-region RPO without the cross-region latency tax.

Q: A regulator under DORA asks for evidence that your DR plan was tested in the last year. What two artifacts do you present?
A: A dated drill writeup recording achieved versus target RTO/RPO for the in-scope critical flows, and the per-tier DR design document mapping each store to its objective and topology. Together they show both that recovery was exercised and that the design is deliberate rather than incidental. DORA's scenario-testing expectation makes an untested RTO a finding, so the date on the drill is the load-bearing detail.

Q: Under DORA, why is testing the recovery of "critical or important functions" specifically, not just any system, the relevant bar?
A: DORA scopes operational-resilience obligations to functions whose disruption would materially impair the financial entity, so the NAV-publishing and investor-record flows must be tested, while a non-critical internal dashboard need not meet the same bar. This lets you concentrate DR rigour and drill effort where the regulation actually bites. Misclassifying a critical flow as non-critical is itself the compliance risk.

Q: How does the requirement to keep EU fund data in-region interact with the unplanned-failover-to-LRS behaviour of Azure storage?
A: Since unplanned failover moves the active copy to the paired secondary region, that secondary must already be inside the permitted EU residency boundary or the failover itself breaches residency. Choosing a geo-redundant pairing within the boundary is therefore a compliance decision made at provisioning, not at incident time. If no compliant secondary exists, you must use in-region zones plus backup-restore and accept the longer RTO.

Q: When residency forbids any cross-region copy, how do you still defend against a full-region outage?
A: You rely on `ZRS`/`GZRS`-in-primary zone redundancy for zone failures and on backup-and-restore (with backups themselves kept in-region or in a compliant location) for region-scale events, restoring only after the region recovers. You then document formal business acceptance that a whole-region disaster carries a long RTO. This "in-region-only resilience" posture trades recoverability speed for strict sovereignty compliance.

Q: Why does the choice of secondary region for a Luxembourg fund administrator usually exclude US or non-EU Azure regions outright?
A: Investor and NAV-relevant data is subject to EU/Luxembourg residency and GDPR transfer constraints, so replicating or failing over to a non-EU region would move regulated personal and fund data outside the legal boundary even transiently. The secondary must sit in an EU region within the compliance perimeter. This narrows the pairing options before any RTO/RPO or cost discussion begins.

Q: How does an ISIN's role as instrument identifier matter (or not) to RPO math for a positions store?
A: It does not affect RPO, which is a time-based replication/backup cadence number; the ISIN is static reference data on each position row. It matters to recovery validation, though — after restore you reconcile that positions still resolve to the correct ISINs and none were orphaned. So ISIN integrity is a post-recovery consistency check, not an input to the objective calculation.

Q: After a failover, your recovered NAV references prices that replicated fine but positions that lagged. Which objective does this expose?
A: This is an RCO (Recovery Consistency Objective) failure: prices and positions each met their individual RPO but are mutually inconsistent at the recovery point, so the NAV computed from them is wrong. RTO and per-store RPO can both pass while cross-store consistency fails. The remedy is to recover interdependent stores to a common consistent point — coordinated snapshots or a consistency group — not to tighten each store independently.

Q: What is a "consistency group" and why does it help meet an RCO across several stores?
A: A consistency group ties multiple volumes or databases together so they are snapshotted or replicated to the same point in time, guaranteeing they recover mutually consistent rather than each at its own lag. This directly serves an RCO where, say, positions, prices, and FX must align for a valid NAV. Without it, independently-replicated stores can each be fresh yet collectively incoherent after recovery.

Q: Why is restoring a lakehouse from bronze plus re-running pipelines sometimes slower than the RTO allows, despite being cheap?
A: Re-deriving silver and gold means re-executing potentially hours of transformation and aggregation jobs, so the compute-bound replay time can exceed a tight NAV-deadline RTO even though storage cost is minimal. The cheap-DR assumption only holds if recomputation fits inside the objective. For deadline-critical gold tables you may instead replicate the gold output directly, accepting the extra storage to buy back RTO.

Q: A lakehouse catalog (e.g. a Postgres-backed metastore) is the source-of-truth tier — what DR does it specifically need?
A: It needs first-class database DR: PITR for "undo the bad migration", geo-replication or geo-redundant backup for region loss, and a tested restore, exactly as a transactional database would. Losing it orphans every data file in object storage regardless of how well the files themselves are replicated. So the metastore's DR rung is typically as high as the most critical table it indexes, not as cheap as the bronze files.

Q: Why are Delta/Iceberg time-travel snapshots not protection against accidental table drop in the catalog?
A: Time travel restores prior table states only while the catalog still knows the table and retains its snapshot metadata; dropping the table from the catalog (or expiring snapshots/vacuuming files) removes the pointers so there is nothing to travel back to. The data files may linger as orphans but are unreferenced and eventually garbage-collected. Real protection is catalog backup plus retention policies that outlast your restore window.

Q: How does Iceberg snapshot expiration or Delta `VACUUM` interact dangerously with using snapshots as a backup?
A: `VACUUM` (Delta) and snapshot expiration (Iceberg) physically delete data files no longer referenced by retained snapshots, so once your retention threshold passes, older "backup" points become unrecoverable as their files are purged. If you leaned on time travel as backup, a short retention silently shrinks your real RPO. Genuine backup must copy data out independently of the table's own retention/compaction lifecycle.

Q: Why does Azure call backup restore a "last resort" even though it is the most robust against corruption?
A: Backups are taken on a cadence, so restoring usually means a larger RPO (more lost data) and a longer RTO (slower rebuild) than failing over to a live replica. The plan should try replica failover first and reach for backup only when replicas are unavailable or themselves corrupted. The exception is logical corruption/ransomware, where replicas faithfully copy the bad data and only a clean point-in-time backup helps.

Q: How does immutability (WORM) on a backup vault change the ransomware threat model versus geo-replicated replicas?
A: Immutable, time-locked backups cannot be altered or deleted within their retention window even by an attacker holding admin credentials, so they remain a trustworthy recovery point precisely when replicas have been encrypted or deleted. Geo-replication only protects against infrastructure loss — it dutifully replicates ransomware. So immutability complements replication by defending against malicious data destruction, which replication cannot.

Q: Why must restore time be empirically measured rather than estimated from backup size?
A: Real restore throughput depends on the storage tier, rehydration of cool/archive blobs, index rebuilds, and the target's I/O, so a naive size-divided-by-bandwidth estimate routinely understates the true duration. Azure stresses that you only know restore time by testing it, which is why restore drills time the operation. An RTO budgeted on an estimate, not a measured restore, is likely to be breached.

Q: A backup lives in the Azure archive access tier to save cost — what hidden cost does this add to RTO?
A: Archive-tier blobs are offline and must be rehydrated to an online tier before restore, which can take hours, and that rehydration latency is added directly to RTO. So a cheap storage choice for backups can silently make the recovery objective unachievable. For tiers with a tight RTO, backups must sit in hot/cool tiers or be rehydrated proactively, trading storage cost for recovery speed.

Q: Why is "we replicate everything synchronously, so RPO is zero everywhere" usually a wrong design?
A: Synchronous replication makes every commit wait for the remote acknowledgement, so applying it to all tiers — including re-derivable bronze that does not need zero RPO — adds latency and cost without business value. RPO should be set per tier, with sync reserved for the few source-of-truth flows that justify the latency. Blanket synchronous replication over-spends and slows the whole platform to protect data that did not need it.

Q: How do you decide between `ANY 1 (...)` and `FIRST 1 (...)` in `synchronous_standby_names` for a two-region setup?
A: `FIRST 1 (regionA, regionB)` always waits for the first-listed (local) standby, giving predictable low-latency sync to a same-region replica while the remote stays async. `ANY 1 (regionA, regionB)` acknowledges on whichever responds first, which can be the distant one and adds variable latency. For a local-sync, remote-async design you want `FIRST` to pin the synchronous partner to the low-latency standby.

Q: What happens to commits if all named synchronous standbys are down and `synchronous_standby_names` is set?
A: The primary blocks new commits, waiting indefinitely for a synchronous acknowledgement that cannot come, so writes hang — availability is sacrificed to guarantee zero data loss. This is the durability-versus-availability trade-off made explicit: synchronous replication can halt the primary to avoid losing data. Operators must decide in the runbook whether to drop the sync requirement (accepting RPO risk) to restore write availability, a deliberate, logged choice.

Q: Why is active-active particularly hard to justify for a single authoritative NAV publication?
A: A NAV is a single official figure per fund per valuation point, so having two regions independently accept writes invites conflicting or duplicate publications that violate the "one source of truth" requirement and complicate audit. Active-passive keeps one writer and a clear lineage, which regulators and reconciliation prefer. The multi-master consistency cost of active-active buys speed the NAV flow rarely needs, so warm standby is usually the right ceiling.

Q: How does failure mode analysis change the secondary you choose, not just the topology?
A: FMA enumerates concrete failure modes — single-zone power loss, full-region outage, fibre cut, ransomware — and each implies a different recovery surface: zone failures need ZRS, region failures need a geo-secondary, ransomware needs immutable backups. So FMA can reveal you need both a geo-secondary and immutable backups, not one topology. It turns "pick a rung" into "cover this enumerated set of modes", which may combine controls.

Q: Why should a DR runbook name the LEI and ISINs of affected funds even though they do not affect RTO/RPO?
A: During recovery the communication plan must notify regulators, counterparties, and messaging systems about precisely which legal entity (LEI) and instruments (ISINs) are impacted, and SWIFT/EMT-style messages key on these identifiers. Having them in the runbook avoids scrambling for reference data mid-incident. They are recovery-execution metadata the plan references, distinct from the objective math.

Q: What is the difference between a tabletop drill and a live failover, and why might a regulated fund start with tabletop?
A: A tabletop walks the team through the scenario and runbook in a controlled simulation without taking production down, surfacing decision, communication, and sequencing gaps cheaply and safely. A live failover actually cuts traffic to the secondary, proving the technical path but risking real disruption. A regulated fund often runs tabletop first to validate process and de-risk before scheduling a live drill in a maintenance window, satisfying the lesson's "script and run one tabletop drill" deliverable.

Q: For a tier with a hard daily NAV cut-off, how do you turn "failure at time T" into a concrete RTO target across the day?
A: The usable RTO is the cut-off minus the time of failure minus the residual processing time still needed to complete the valuation, so a late-afternoon failure leaves a far tighter window than a morning one. You therefore size the topology to the worst credible failure time (just before cut-off), not the average. This is why deadline-driven gold tiers land on warm standby — only a near-ready replica can recover fast enough when failure strikes close to the cut-off.

Q: Why does Azure Site Recovery suit VM/on-prem tiers but not natively solve database-engine-level RPO?
A: Azure Site Recovery orchestrates crash-consistent replication and failover of whole VMs (and physical/on-prem servers), giving block-level recovery and automated runbooks, which is ideal for lift-and-shift application servers. It does not understand a database's transaction log, so for application-consistent, low-RPO database recovery you still use engine-native replication (PostgreSQL streaming, failover groups) underneath. The architect layers ASR for the infrastructure tier and engine replication for the data tier rather than expecting ASR to deliver a transactional RPO.
