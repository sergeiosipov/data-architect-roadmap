# Phase 6 · 10.4.2 Managed data observability platforms — 100 self-test questions

Q: What is a "managed data observability platform" in one sentence, and what does Monte Carlo represent in the build-vs-buy decision?
A: It is a vendor-hosted SaaS that automatically monitors your data estate for reliability problems — freshness, volume, schema, distribution drift — using machine learning over warehouse metadata instead of hand-written rules. Monte Carlo is the canonical "buy" side of the data-quality build-vs-buy choice, the alternative to hand-rolling monitors with FOSS tools like Soda and Elementary. An architect must evaluate it on coverage and economics, not on a sales demo.

Q: Why does the syllabus warn that Monte Carlo's "ML-anomaly pitch needs an architect's informed evaluation, not a sales demo's"?
A: Because the failure mode is mispricing the decision in either direction: over-paying for ML anomaly detection on metrics you could trivially encode as a rule, or hand-rolling freshness and volume monitors that a managed tool would catch for far less effort. A demo shows the tool catching impressive anomalies but hides where rules are cheaper and where the per-table cost compounds. The architect's job is to map coverage to failure classes and to estate-scale economics.

Q: What does "anomaly detection on metadata" mean, and why is operating on metadata significant?
A: It means flagging problems by analysing the warehouse's own metadata — row counts, last-update timestamps, byte sizes, schema definitions, query logs — rather than scanning the full data. This is cheap and fast because it never reads the rows themselves, so a freshness or volume anomaly can be detected without a costly table scan. The trade-off is that metadata anomalies catch "the table changed shape" but not necessarily "this specific value is business-wrong".

Q: Name the three core "shape" signals Monte Carlo derives anomaly monitors from automatically.
A: Freshness (when the table was last updated versus its learned cadence), volume (row count or byte size versus its learned baseline), and schema (columns, types, and nullability changing). These three are derivable purely from metadata, so they can be turned on across an entire estate with near-zero configuration. Distribution and lineage round out the broader five-pillar model.

Q: What is the key advantage of anomaly detection over hand-written rules for freshness monitoring?
A: A rule requires you to know and encode the expected SLA — "this table must update by 06:00 UTC daily" — for every table, which does not scale to thousands of tables and goes stale as cadences change. Anomaly detection learns each table's historical update pattern and flags deviations automatically, including tables nobody thought to write a rule for. The gotcha is that learned baselines need a warm-up period and can be fooled by tables whose cadence is genuinely irregular.

Q: What is a "freshness" anomaly, and give a fund-data example of why it matters.
A: A freshness anomaly fires when a table has not updated within its learned expected window — the data is stale. In fund administration this is critical because a stalled price feed means today's NAV could be struck on yesterday's prices: the gold table looks populated but its `as_of` date is wrong. Freshness monitoring catches the silent failure where a connector dies and the table simply stops refreshing rather than erroring loudly.

Q: What is a "volume" anomaly, and how would it catch a partial load?
A: A volume anomaly fires when the row count or size of a load deviates from the learned baseline — too few rows, or too many. It catches a partial load where, say, a transfer-agency feed normally delivers 50,000 subscription/redemption records but today delivers 5,000 because an upstream file was truncated. The table is fresh and schema-valid, so only volume monitoring catches that the load silently shrank.

Q: What is a "schema" anomaly, and why is it often an early warning rather than a data error?
A: A schema anomaly fires when columns are added, dropped, renamed, or change type. It is an early warning because a renamed or dropped upstream column will break downstream transformations and joins before any wrong value appears — you learn about the breakage at the source change, not three hops downstream. For example, a vendor renaming `isin` to `instrument_isin` would surface as a schema change immediately.

Q: What is a "distribution" anomaly, and how does it differ from a volume anomaly?
A: A distribution anomaly fires when the statistical shape of a column's values shifts — the mean, null rate, cardinality, or the frequency of categories changes beyond the learned range — even though the row count is normal. It differs from volume in that the count of rows is fine; it is the content that drifted. Example: a `currency` column that is suddenly 30% null, or a `price` column whose distribution jumps, signalling a units or feed problem.

Q: What is the "lineage" pillar's role in a data observability platform?
A: Lineage maps the dependency graph of tables, columns, and downstream BI assets, so the platform knows what feeds what. It turns an isolated alert into context: this stale table feeds these twelve gold tables and these three dashboards. Lineage is what enables blast-radius assessment and lineage-aware incident grouping rather than a flood of disconnected alerts.

Q: List the five pillars of data observability as the syllabus frames them.
A: Freshness, volume, schema, distribution, and lineage. The first four are properties you monitor on individual tables/columns, and the fifth — lineage — is the connective tissue that relates them across the estate. Together they describe "is the data arriving on time, in the right amount, in the right shape, with the right values, and where does it flow".

Q: What does "incident workflow" mean in Monte Carlo, and what is its purpose?
A: It is the lifecycle a detected anomaly travels through: detection, alerting, triage, ownership assignment, investigation, resolution, and post-mortem. Monte Carlo packages this as Incident IQ so that an alert is not just a notification but a tracked incident with status, owner, and history. The purpose is to move teams from "we got pinged" to "this is owned and being resolved" with an audit trail.

Q: What is "blast radius" in the context of a data incident?
A: Blast radius is the set of downstream assets — tables, models, dashboards, reports, ML features — affected by an upstream data problem, computed from lineage. It answers "if this table is stale, what breaks?" and lets a team prioritise: an incident with twenty downstream dashboards including a client-facing NAV report is urgent; one feeding only a sandbox is not. Without lineage you cannot compute blast radius and every incident looks equally important.

Q: What is "lineage-aware incident grouping" and what problem does it solve?
A: It is the practice of collapsing many correlated alerts that share a common upstream root cause into a single incident, using lineage to relate them. It solves alert-storm fatigue: one stale source table that feeds forty downstream tables would otherwise generate forty separate freshness alerts, but lineage groups them into one incident pointed at the root. This makes the signal actionable instead of a wall of noise.

Q: How does lineage help with root-cause analysis specifically?
A: Lineage lets the platform trace an anomaly upstream to the earliest table or job where the problem originated, rather than just reporting the symptom downstream. With column-level lineage and log correlation it can point to the specific upstream change — a schema edit, a failed job — that triggered the cascade. This shortens the investigation from "search every table" to "start at the root the graph identifies".

Q: What is the difference between table-level and column-level (field-level) lineage, and why does column-level matter for impact analysis?
A: Table-level lineage records that table A feeds table B; column-level lineage records that column `nav_per_share` in A specifically populates `nav` in B. Column-level matters because it narrows blast radius precisely: a change to a column nobody downstream uses has no real impact, whereas table-level lineage would over-report it. For a security master, column-level lineage tells you exactly which downstream reports consume the `isin` field versus the `figi` field.

Q: Describe Monte Carlo's pricing model dimensions as the syllabus frames them.
A: The economics are driven primarily by per-table and per-monitor counts — you broadly pay in proportion to the number of tables observed and/or monitors configured across the estate. This means cost scales with estate size, so a 200-pipeline estate with thousands of tables costs very differently from a 20-table one. The architect must model this against the labour cost of building and maintaining equivalent FOSS coverage.

Q: Why does "per-table" pricing change the build-vs-buy calculus as an estate grows?
A: Because the buy cost grows roughly linearly with the number of monitored tables, while a FOSS build has a large fixed engineering cost up front but a flatter marginal cost per table once the framework exists. At small scale the managed tool's low setup wins; at very large scale the per-table fees can exceed the amortised cost of maintaining your own framework. The crossover point is exactly what the architect must estimate.

Q: For a 200-pipeline estate, what is the core build-vs-buy question an architect must answer?
A: Whether the labour to build, run, and maintain freshness/volume/schema/distribution coverage across ~200 pipelines (plus on-call for the monitoring system itself) costs more or less than the managed tool's per-table fees plus integration effort. The honest answer is usually hybrid: buy the automatic metadata anomaly detection that is tedious to build, and keep rule-based DQ for the business-specific checks the tool cannot infer. Estate size is the deciding variable.

Q: What is "complementarity" between rule-based and anomaly-based DQ?
A: It is the recognition that the two approaches catch different, partially non-overlapping failure classes, so the best estates use both rather than choosing one. Rules encode known, business-specific expectations; anomaly detection finds unknown statistical deviations across far more tables than you could write rules for. Treating them as competitors is the mistake — they are layers.

Q: Give one failure class anomaly detection catches that hand-written rules typically never will.
A: An unexpected volume or distribution drift on a table nobody thought to monitor — because writing a rule presupposes you anticipated the problem, whereas anomaly detection blankets every table by default. For example, a slow upstream change that gradually halves the row count of an obscure dimension table would be caught by a learned volume baseline but would have no rule because nobody predicted it. This "unknown unknowns" coverage is anomaly detection's signature strength.

Q: Give one failure class hand-written rules catch that anomaly detection typically will not.
A: A precise business-rule violation that is statistically unremarkable — for example "every `isin` must be 12 characters and pass the check digit" or "`nav_per_share` must be positive and within tolerance of the prior day". These values look perfectly normal to a distribution model because the anomaly is semantic, not statistical. Only an explicit rule encodes the domain truth.

Q: A NAV is published with a valid-looking but wrong value because two prices were swapped. Would Monte Carlo's anomaly detection catch it? Explain.
A: Probably not on its own, because two swapped prices may leave row counts, freshness, schema, and even the column distribution within normal bounds — the anomaly is a business-logic error, not a statistical one. This is the textbook case where rule-based DQ (a reconciliation or cross-check rule) wins and anomaly detection loses. It illustrates why you keep both layers.

Q: A previously daily-updating table silently stops refreshing for three days. Which approach catches it faster and why?
A: Anomaly-based freshness monitoring catches it automatically because it learned the daily cadence and flags the missed updates without anyone having configured an SLA for that table. A rule-based system catches it only if someone had written an explicit freshness rule for that specific table, which is unlikely across a large estate. This is anomaly detection's strongest, cheapest win.

Q: Why is "freshness and volume monitoring" specifically called out as the thing you should not hand-roll if a managed tool covers it?
A: Because freshness and volume are derivable from metadata uniformly across every table, so a managed tool delivers near-complete coverage with no per-table configuration, whereas hand-rolling means writing and maintaining a monitor and baseline for each of thousands of tables. The labour-to-value ratio is terrible for a build here. That is precisely the work managed tools automate best and the easiest case for "buy".

Q: Conversely, why might it be wasteful to "buy" anomaly detection for a metric you could rule-encode?
A: If a metric has a known, fixed, business-defined threshold — "this count must equal exactly N" — a one-line rule expresses it perfectly and deterministically, with no false positives and no learning period. Paying per-monitor for ML to approximate a constraint you already know exactly is over-paying for a worse tool. Anomaly detection earns its cost on unknowns, not on known constants.

Q: What is the difference between "data observability" and "data quality" as concepts?
A: Data quality is about whether specific values meet defined correctness criteria (accuracy, completeness, validity), usually via rules and tests. Data observability is the broader operational discipline of knowing the health and behaviour of your data systems over time — freshness, volume, schema, distribution, lineage — so you detect that something changed even when you have not defined what "correct" is. Observability tells you the data moved; quality tells you whether it is right.

Q: How does data observability differ from infrastructure observability (the Phase 5 kind)?
A: Infrastructure observability watches CPU, memory, latency, error rates, and traces of the compute and services — is the machine healthy. Data observability watches the data itself moving through those systems — is the table fresh, the load complete, the schema stable. A pipeline can be infra-green (jobs succeed, no errors) while producing silently wrong data, which only data observability catches.

Q: A Debezium CDC connector stalls but reports no error. Which observability layer surfaces this, and via which pillar?
A: Data observability surfaces it, primarily via the freshness pillar — the downstream table stops updating on its learned cadence even though the infra shows the job "running". Volume may also flag it as the load shrinks to zero. Infra observability would show the connector process as alive, so it would miss the silent data stall entirely.

Q: What is meant by a "silent failure" in data pipelines, and why are managed observability tools pitched at it?
A: A silent failure is one where the pipeline does not error or alert — jobs report success — but the data is stale, shrunken, or wrong. These are dangerous because they surface only when a human notices a wrong number, often after it reached a client or regulator. Managed observability tools are pitched at exactly this gap: they monitor the data's behaviour rather than the job's exit code.

Q: Why does the syllabus call Monte Carlo a vendor source to "read critically"?
A: Because vendor documentation naturally frames the product favourably, emphasising what ML anomaly detection catches and downplaying its false-positive cost, its blind spot for business-logic errors, and the per-table price at scale. An architect reads it for the mechanism (how the anomaly and incident model works) while consulting a vendor-neutral counterweight — Data Quality Fundamentals — for the honest trade-offs. The skill is extracting architecture facts without absorbing the sales framing.

Q: What is the "Monte Carlo-class" category, and name two competitors in it.
A: It is the category of managed, ML-driven, metadata-based data observability platforms that auto-monitor freshness/volume/schema/distribution and provide lineage-aware incident management. Competitors in the same class include Bigeye and Databand (and others such as Anomalo and Sifflet). The syllabus uses "Monte Carlo-class" so the evaluation is about the category, not one vendor.

Q: What is the role of "baselines" in metadata anomaly detection, and what is the cold-start problem?
A: A baseline is the learned normal range for a table's freshness cadence, volume, and distribution, built from historical observations. The cold-start problem is that a brand-new table or pipeline has no history, so the tool cannot yet distinguish normal from anomalous until it accumulates enough observations. During warm-up you get either no alerts or noisy ones, so critical new tables often still need an explicit rule.

Q: What causes false positives in anomaly detection, and why do they threaten adoption?
A: False positives arise when legitimate but unusual events — a holiday with no load, a planned backfill, a genuine seasonal spike — deviate from the learned baseline and trip an alert. They threaten adoption because alert fatigue trains teams to ignore the tool, so a real incident gets dismissed with the noise. Tuning sensitivity and feeding back "expected" annotations is essential to keep the signal trustworthy.

Q: How can seasonality fool a naive volume anomaly detector, and how is it mitigated?
A: A naive detector that learns a single flat baseline flags the month-end or quarter-end spike in transfer-agency activity as an anomaly every period, even though it is expected. Mitigation is a model that learns weekly/monthly seasonality, or explicit annotation of known periodic events so the baseline accounts for them. In fund data, month-end NAV cycles and quarter-end reporting are the classic seasonal patterns that must be modelled.

Q: What is the practical first step in the syllabus's "Do" — outlining your FOSS stack coverage by failure class — and why start there?
A: You enumerate your current Soda + Elementary coverage and map each check to the failure class it catches (freshness, volume, schema, distribution, business-rule). You start there because build-vs-buy is meaningless in the abstract: you can only judge what a managed tool adds once you know which failure classes you already cover and which are gaps. The map exposes the real decision surface.

Q: How would you map Monte Carlo's coverage against your existing failure classes?
A: For each failure class, mark whether your FOSS rules cover it, whether Monte Carlo's anomaly detection covers it, and the relative cost/effort of each. You will typically find Monte Carlo blankets freshness/volume/schema/distribution automatically while your rules cover the business-specific checks it cannot infer. The overlap and the gaps drive the build/buy/hybrid recommendation.

Q: What should a "half-page build-vs-buy comparison for a 200-pipeline estate" contain at minimum?
A: Coverage by failure class for each option, a rough cost sketch (FOSS engineering and maintenance labour plus on-call versus the managed tool's per-table/per-monitor fees and integration cost), and a recommendation tied to estate size. It should name which failure classes only anomaly detection catches and which only rules catch. The conclusion is build, buy, or hybrid with a one-line justification.

Q: What is a defensible "Done when" recommendation for a small estate, and why?
A: For a small estate (tens of tables) a defensible recommendation is "build", because the per-table managed fees buy little beyond what a handful of Soda/Elementary checks already cover, and the FOSS maintenance burden is low at that scale. The one-line justification ties directly to estate size: too few tables to amortise a vendor contract. The economics flip as table count grows.

Q: What is a defensible recommendation for a large, fast-growing estate, and why?
A: "Buy" or "hybrid", because at hundreds of pipelines and thousands of tables the labour to build and maintain blanket freshness/volume/schema coverage — plus on-call for your own monitoring stack — typically exceeds the managed tool's value, and the tool's automatic coverage of new tables matters as the estate grows. The justification is that automatic, zero-config coverage scales where hand-rolled monitors do not. You still keep rules for business logic, hence often hybrid.

Q: Why is "hybrid" usually the honest answer to data observability build-vs-buy?
A: Because the two approaches are complementary, not substitutes: anomaly detection wins on broad, automatic, unknown-unknown coverage, and rule-based DQ wins on precise, business-specific, known constraints. A pure-buy estate misses semantic errors the tool cannot infer; a pure-build estate drowns in the labour of blanket metadata monitoring. Hybrid buys the tedious blanket coverage and builds the high-value domain rules.

Q: In Monte Carlo, what is "Incident IQ" and what does it add over a raw alert?
A: Incident IQ is Monte Carlo's incident-management capability that turns detected anomalies into managed incidents with status, ownership, enriched context, and workflow rather than just firing a notification. Over a raw alert it adds blast radius, probable root cause, past occurrences, and routing, so teams triage by impact instead of reacting to each ping. It is the difference between "alerting" and "incident management".

Q: What context does Monte Carlo surface to help triage an incident, beyond the fact that it fired?
A: It surfaces the affected downstream assets (blast radius from lineage), probable upstream root cause, history of past occurrences of the same anomaly, and the suggested owner/route. This context lets a responder judge urgency and start at the likely root rather than searching blindly. The goal is to compress mean-time-to-resolution by front-loading the investigation context.

Q: Why is "assigning an owner to each detector/incident" part of the operational discipline?
A: Because a detector with no owner produces alerts nobody is accountable for, which decay into ignored noise. Assigning an owner means every freshness/volume/schema detector and every incident has a named human or team responsible for triage and resolution. This is the organisational half of observability — the tool detects, but ownership turns detection into resolution.

Q: What is a "data incident triage runbook", and what should it specify?
A: It is a documented procedure for what happens when a data incident fires: who is paged, what they check first, how they confirm versus dismiss, escalation path, and how it is resolved and recorded. It should specify the owner, the diagnostic steps per pillar (e.g. for freshness: check the upstream job and connector), and the communication/escalation rules. It converts an alert into a repeatable response rather than ad-hoc firefighting.

Q: A freshness alert fires on a gold NAV table. What is the first thing to check?
A: Check the upstream load — whether the source feed or job that populates the table actually ran and completed, because freshness almost always traces to a stalled or failed upstream step. Use lineage to walk up to the earliest stale table, which is likely the true root, rather than firefighting the gold table itself. Confirm whether it is a real stall or an expected gap (holiday, schedule change) before paging.

Q: A volume alert says a transfer-agency load shrank 80%. What are the first checks?
A: Confirm whether the source file itself was truncated or only partially delivered (file size, record count at the boundary), and whether an upstream filter or join changed and dropped rows. Check lineage to see if the shrinkage originated upstream or was introduced in your own transformation. Distinguish a genuine business drop (a real quiet day) from a pipeline defect before declaring an incident.

Q: A schema-change alert fires after a vendor renames a column. What is the impact path and the fix?
A: The impact path is that downstream models referencing the old column name will fail or silently null out, propagating through the lineage graph to dependent tables and reports. The fix is to update the ingestion mapping to the new column name and, ideally, add a contract or alert so the next rename is caught at the boundary. Column-level lineage tells you exactly which downstream assets must be checked.

Q: How does a managed observability tool integrate with the wider alerting stack, and why is "unified alerting" desirable?
A: It routes data incidents through the same channels as the rest of operations — Slack/Teams, PagerDuty/Opsgenie, email, webhooks — so data alerts land where the on-call team already works. Unified alerting is desirable because a separate, siloed data-alert channel gets ignored; routing data incidents alongside infra incidents means one consistent triage and escalation path. It also prevents two competing sources of truth for "is something broken".

Q: Why might an architect deliberately keep some rule-based checks even after buying Monte Carlo?
A: Because business-logic constraints — ISIN check digits, NAV sign and tolerance, reconciliation equalities, regulatory completeness — are semantic and deterministic, which rules express better and cheaper than statistical anomaly detection. These checks have known thresholds and zero tolerance for the false positives anomaly models produce. The bought tool covers the statistical layer; the rules cover the domain-truth layer.

Q: What is the risk of relying solely on anomaly detection for regulatory data (e.g. an EMT file)?
A: Regulatory data has exact, mandated correctness criteria — required fields populated, valid enumerations, correct identifiers — that anomaly detection does not encode; a missing-but-not-statistically-unusual field passes unnoticed. An EMT (European MiFID Template) file with a blank mandatory field looks statistically normal but is regulatorily invalid. Such requirements demand explicit validation rules, not learned baselines.

Q: How could anomaly detection still help with an EMT/EPT generation pipeline despite needing rules?
A: It catches the operational failures around the rules — the EMT extract that arrived late (freshness), shrank because an upstream join dropped funds (volume), or gained an unexpected column (schema). These are exactly the silent failures that produce a valid-but-incomplete deliverable. So anomaly detection guards the pipeline's health while rules guard the file's field-level regulatory correctness.

Q: A LEI (Legal Entity Identifier) reference table normally has a stable row count, then quietly grows 5x overnight. Which pillar flags it and what might it mean?
A: The volume pillar flags it as a row-count anomaly versus the learned baseline. A 5x jump could mean a duplicate load (the same file ingested twice), a join fan-out introducing duplicate LEI rows, or a genuine bulk vendor update. The volume alert prompts investigation before the inflated table corrupts downstream counterparty counts or reconciliations.

Q: How does lineage-aware blast radius help prioritise an incident on a shared `isin` security-master table?
A: Because the security master feeds nearly everything, blast radius shows the incident touches many downstream tables and client-facing reports, which elevates it to top priority immediately. Lineage lets you enumerate exactly which NAV, position, and reporting tables consume the affected `isin` records so you can communicate impact precisely. Without lineage, a security-master incident's true reach is invisible until reports break.

Q: Why is per-monitor pricing a reason to be selective rather than enabling every possible monitor?
A: Because each monitor adds cost and potential noise, so blanketing every table and column with every monitor inflates the bill and the false-positive rate without proportional value. Selectivity means monitoring high-value and high-blast-radius assets (gold, security master, NAV) intensively and leaving low-stakes staging tables lightly covered. The architect tunes monitor placement to where incidents actually hurt.

Q: What is the difference between a "monitor" and an "incident" in this model?
A: A monitor is a configured detector that continuously watches a metric (a table's freshness, a column's distribution) and evaluates it against a baseline or threshold. An incident is the instance created when a monitor fires — a tracked event with status, owner, blast radius, and resolution history. One monitor can produce many incidents over time; one incident may be linked to many monitors via lineage grouping.

Q: How does a managed tool's automatic table discovery work, and what is its scaling benefit?
A: It connects to the warehouse metadata/information-schema and automatically enumerates tables, then starts learning baselines without per-table setup. The scaling benefit is that new tables get monitored as soon as they appear, so coverage keeps pace with a growing estate instead of waiting for an engineer to add a monitor. This zero-config-on-new-tables property is a major reason the buy case strengthens with estate growth.

Q: What is the downside of automatic discovery monitoring every table indiscriminately?
A: It can balloon per-table cost and generate noise from low-value or transient tables (scratch, staging, dev), drowning real signals. Many platforms therefore let you scope or tier coverage so important tables get full monitoring and trivial ones get little or none. Leaving discovery unbounded is a common way estates overspend and erode trust in alerts.

Q: Why is "Data Quality Fundamentals" used as the "vendor-neutral counterweight" to Monte Carlo docs?
A: Because it explains the observability pillars and the rule-versus-anomaly trade-offs without a product to sell, so it gives an honest framing of where anomaly detection helps and where it does not. Reading it alongside vendor docs lets the architect separate the genuine mechanism from the marketing. The book provides the conceptual five-pillar model that the vendor instantiates.

Q: What does it mean that anomaly detection finds "unknown unknowns" while rules find "known knowns"?
A: Rules encode failures you already anticipated and described — known knowns. Anomaly detection surfaces deviations you never thought to look for — unknown unknowns — because it learns normality and flags anything outside it. The two cover different parts of the risk surface, which is the mechanical basis of their complementarity.

Q: Why can anomaly detection produce false negatives, and give an example.
A: It produces false negatives when a real problem stays within learned statistical bounds, so nothing looks unusual. For example, a price feed that returns slightly wrong-but-plausible numbers, or a record where two valid values were swapped, leaves volume, freshness, schema, and distribution all normal. The error is semantic, so only a business rule or reconciliation catches it.

Q: How does the choice of monitoring layer (warehouse metadata vs query results) affect what gets caught?
A: Metadata monitoring (counts, timestamps, sizes, schema) is cheap and catches structural/operational anomalies but is blind to value-level correctness. Query-result or field-health monitoring samples actual values to catch null-rate spikes, distribution shifts, and out-of-range values but costs more compute. Managed tools layer both, using cheap metadata broadly and deeper value checks selectively on important tables.

Q: What is "data downtime" and how do observability tools quantify it?
A: Data downtime is the period during which data is missing, stale, erroneous, or otherwise not trustworthy. Tools quantify it roughly as number of incidents × (time-to-detection + time-to-resolution), so the goal of observability is to drive both detection and resolution time down. It reframes data quality as an operational SLA metric the business can track.

Q: How does reducing time-to-detection differ in value from reducing time-to-resolution?
A: Reducing time-to-detection limits how long bad data circulates undetected — the silent-failure window — which is exactly what anomaly detection shrinks by flagging problems automatically. Reducing time-to-resolution depends on incident workflow, lineage root-cause, and ownership, which is what Incident IQ-style features target. Both reduce data downtime, but detection is the part managed anomaly tools most directly improve.

Q: Why is "schema drift" sometimes a positive change that should not be alerted as an incident?
A: Because adding a new optional column or a planned migration is intentional, not a fault, yet a naive schema monitor flags any change. Good tooling lets you acknowledge or whitelist expected changes so they do not become recurring noise. The architect must distinguish breaking changes (drops/renames/type changes on used columns) from additive, non-breaking ones.

Q: What is the relationship between data observability and data contracts?
A: Data contracts declare the expected schema/semantics a producer promises a consumer; observability detects when reality violates that promise. A schema anomaly is effectively a contract breach surfaced operationally. Together, contracts express the expectation up front and observability enforces and monitors it at runtime.

Q: In the build-vs-buy cost sketch, what FOSS costs are commonly under-counted?
A: The ongoing maintenance and on-call cost of the monitoring stack itself — keeping Soda/Elementary, baselines, alert routing, and dashboards healthy — plus the engineering time to add coverage for every new table. Teams price the initial build but forget that someone must run the monitor of the monitors. Under-counting this makes "build" look cheaper than it is at scale.

Q: In the same sketch, what managed-tool costs are commonly under-counted?
A: The integration and tuning effort (connecting sources, scoping coverage, suppressing false positives), the per-table fees as the estate grows, and the cost of still building business-logic rules the tool cannot infer. Buyers price the licence but forget the tuning labour and the residual rule layer. Under-counting these makes "buy" look like a complete solution when it is not.

Q: What metric would you track to prove a managed observability tool is earning its cost?
A: Reduction in data downtime — specifically incidents caught before they reached a consumer, and the drop in time-to-detection and time-to-resolution versus the pre-tool baseline. You also track false-positive rate to confirm the alerts stay trustworthy. If detection time and downtime do not improve, the spend is not justified regardless of feature count.

Q: Why is it dangerous to evaluate Monte Carlo only on "how many anomalies it found" in a demo?
A: Because raw anomaly count conflates true incidents with false positives, and a demo is tuned to look impressive on cherry-picked data. A tool that fires constantly looks "powerful" but produces alert fatigue in production. The right evaluation metrics are precision (are the alerts real) and downtime reduction, not anomaly volume.

Q: How does lineage that includes BI assets (dashboards/reports) sharpen incident prioritisation?
A: Because the blast radius then reaches all the way to what humans actually consume, so you can say "this stale table breaks the client NAV dashboard", which is a business-impact statement, not a technical one. That lets you prioritise incidents by who is affected, not just by how many tables. For a fund administrator, knowing a client-facing report is in the blast radius escalates urgency immediately.

Q: What is the architect's risk of "over-paying for anomaly detection you could rule-encode" in concrete terms?
A: You pay per-monitor for ML to watch metrics whose correct value you already know exactly and could assert with a deterministic rule that never false-positives. The money buys a fuzzier, noisier version of a constraint you could express in one line. The fix is to reserve anomaly detection for genuine unknowns and rule-encode the knowns.

Q: What is the opposite risk — "hand-rolling freshness/volume monitoring a managed tool would catch for less"?
A: You spend scarce engineering time building and maintaining per-table freshness and volume monitors and baselines across thousands of tables — work a managed tool automates with near-zero config. The labour, on-call, and opportunity cost can exceed the licence fee. The fix is to buy the blanket metadata coverage and spend engineering on the domain-specific rules only you can write.

Q: Why is freshness monitoring especially load-bearing in a NAV pipeline?
A: Because NAV correctness depends on pricing every instrument as of the right valuation point, and a single stale price feed silently produces a NAV on stale inputs without any job erroring. Freshness monitoring on each price/source table catches the stall before the NAV is struck and distributed. A wrong NAV that reaches investors is a regulatory and reputational event, so early freshness detection is high-value.

Q: How would distribution monitoring catch a units-of-measure error in a price feed?
A: If a vendor switches a price field from major to minor units (e.g. dollars to cents), the values jump by ~100x, shifting the column's distribution far outside its learned range even though row count and freshness are fine. Distribution monitoring flags the statistical shift, prompting investigation before the inflated prices corrupt NAV. Row-level rules might miss it if no explicit range was set, so the learned baseline adds value here.

Q: Why is "per-table economics at estate scale" the crux of the pricing-model learning bullet?
A: Because the headline price is meaningless without multiplying by the number of monitored tables, and at estate scale (thousands of tables) that multiplication dominates the total cost of ownership. The architect must model total spend as table count × unit price, not react to a per-table figure that looks small. Estate scale is exactly where buy can become more expensive than build.

Q: How should you scope monitoring tiers across a fund-administration estate to control cost?
A: Apply intensive monitoring (freshness, volume, distribution, plus rules) to gold/NAV/security-master/regulatory-output tables with high blast radius, lighter monitoring to silver/staging, and minimal or none to scratch and dev tables. This concentrates spend and attention where incidents cause real harm. Tiering by blast radius is how you get most of the value without paying to watch everything equally.

Q: What does "data observability is the data plane's heartbeat" mean in contrast to DQ rules?
A: It means observability continuously signals whether data is flowing on time and in the right shape — a liveness/health signal — whereas DQ rules assert correctness of specific values at specific points. The heartbeat catches "the data stopped or changed shape"; the rules catch "this value is wrong". You need the heartbeat to know the system is alive and the rules to know it is right.

Q: How does anomaly detection handle a brand-new pipeline with no history, and what should you do meanwhile?
A: It cannot establish a reliable baseline until it has observed enough cycles, so during the cold-start window it may under- or over-alert. Meanwhile you protect critical new pipelines with explicit rules (a hard freshness SLA, an expected row-count range) until the model warms up. This is a concrete case where build (rules) bridges a gap in the bought tool.

Q: Why might two different vendor records for the same instrument NOT be caught by anomaly detection, and what catches it?
A: Two valid-looking records for one instrument are statistically unremarkable — counts, freshness, and schema all look fine — so anomaly detection sees nothing wrong. What catches it is master-data match/merge logic and deduplication rules that recognise the records as the same entity. This is a domain-modelling problem, not a statistical one, illustrating anomaly detection's limit.

Q: What is the danger of routing data incidents to a channel separate from the team's main alerting?
A: A separate, low-traffic data-alert channel tends to be muted or unwatched, so real incidents sit unnoticed — defeating the point of monitoring. Unified alerting routes data incidents into the same on-call and escalation flow as everything else the team already responds to. The organisational integration matters as much as the detection technology.

Q: How do you decide which detectors deserve a human owner versus automated handling?
A: High-blast-radius detectors on critical assets (NAV, security master, regulatory outputs) need a named owner and a runbook because their incidents demand judgement and fast resolution. Low-stakes detectors can be batched, auto-suppressed, or owned at a team level. The principle is to align ownership intensity with business impact, not to assign every detector identically.

Q: What is "anomaly correlation" and how does it reduce noise?
A: Anomaly correlation groups multiple anomalies that occur together and share a lineage or temporal relationship, inferring they have one root cause. It reduces noise by presenting one correlated incident instead of dozens of independent alerts. This is the mechanism behind lineage-aware incident grouping and is essential for keeping a large estate's alerting actionable.

Q: When is rule-based DQ strictly better than anomaly detection, in one principle?
A: When the correctness criterion is known, exact, and zero-tolerance — a deterministic constraint you can state — a rule is strictly better because it is precise, explainable, and produces no false positives. Anomaly detection only approximates such constraints and adds noise. Known, exact, regulatory, or reconciliation checks are the rule's home turf.

Q: When is anomaly detection strictly better than rules, in one principle?
A: When you need broad coverage of failures you cannot enumerate in advance across more tables than you could ever write rules for, anomaly detection is strictly better because it learns normality automatically and finds the unknown unknowns. Rules would require foreseeing each failure and writing per-table code that does not scale. Blanket freshness/volume/schema coverage is the anomaly's home turf.

Q: How does a managed tool's "automatic runbooks/workflows" claim change your evaluation?
A: It is a workflow convenience — templated steps and routing — not a substitute for your domain knowledge of how to resolve a NAV or reconciliation incident. Evaluate whether it actually shortens resolution in your context or just adds a generic checklist you would write anyway. Read such claims critically, since runbook quality depends on your own encoded process, not the vendor's defaults.

Q: What is the relationship between OpenLineage events and a managed observability tool's lineage?
A: OpenLineage is an open standard for emitting lineage events from jobs, giving you portable, tool-agnostic lineage you control. A managed tool builds its own lineage (often by parsing query logs) to power blast radius and incident grouping. They can complement each other, but relying on a vendor's proprietary lineage is a lock-in consideration the architect should weigh against open standards.

Q: Why is vendor lock-in a real factor in the Monte Carlo-class buy decision?
A: Because the tool builds proprietary lineage, baselines, incident history, and integrations that do not export cleanly, so leaving later means rebuilding observability from scratch. The deeper the estate integrates, the higher the switching cost. An architect weighs this against the FOSS alternative's portability (Elementary in your dbt project, OpenLineage events you own).

Q: How does dbt-native FOSS observability (Elementary) differ in scope from a Monte Carlo-class platform?
A: Elementary runs inside your dbt project, adding freshness/volume/schema/anomaly tests and a report scoped to dbt-managed models, so its coverage is your transformation layer. A Monte Carlo-class platform observes the whole warehouse, including tables dbt does not manage, plus deeper lineage and incident workflow. The trade-off is dbt-native simplicity and ownership versus broader estate coverage and managed convenience.

Q: An architect inherits an estate with 200 pipelines and no observability. What is a pragmatic phased recommendation?
A: Start hybrid: buy or stand up automatic metadata anomaly detection (freshness/volume/schema) to get fast blanket coverage of silent failures, while building targeted business-logic rules on the highest-risk NAV/security-master/regulatory assets. Concentrate paid monitoring and ownership on high-blast-radius tables first, expanding as value is proven. Phasing controls cost and avoids both the under-coverage of pure-build and the over-spend of monitoring everything on day one.

Q: Why should "rough cost sketch" rather than a precise quote be enough for the architect's decision?
A: Because the decision hinges on order-of-magnitude crossover — whether maintained FOSS labour is roughly above or below per-table fees at your table count — not on a precise figure. A rough sketch (tables × unit price versus engineer-months of build and run) reveals the direction of the answer fast. Precision matters only once the rough sketch shows the decision is close.

Q: How do you tell whether your false-positive rate is acceptable for a managed observability tool?
A: Track the ratio of alerts that turned out to be real incidents versus dismissed noise over a period; if responders routinely dismiss alerts, the precision is too low and trust is eroding. Acceptable means alerts are believed and acted on without "ignore by default" behaviour. You tune sensitivity, scope, and seasonality handling until precision is high enough to sustain that trust.

Q: What is the single most important question to ask a Monte Carlo-class vendor in evaluation, and why?
A: "Show me, on my data, the precision of your alerts and the reduction in time-to-detection over a trial period" — because that grounds the decision in your estate's signal-to-noise rather than the vendor's curated demo. Coverage breadth is easy to claim; useful precision on your tables is what determines whether the tool reduces real data downtime. Insist on a proof-of-value trial measured on your metrics.

Q: How does DORA's operational-resilience expectation strengthen the case for data observability in an EU fund administrator?
A: DORA (the Digital Operational Resilience Act) requires regulated financial entities to detect, manage, and report ICT-related incidents quickly, and a silent data failure that corrupts a NAV is an operational-resilience event. Data observability provides the detection and incident-management evidence — what failed, blast radius, time-to-detection and resolution — that supports DORA incident reporting. It turns "we eventually noticed a wrong number" into a monitored, auditable incident process.

Q: A daily SWIFT-fed settlement table is fresh and full but a downstream reconciliation breaks. Why might no anomaly fire, and what catches it?
A: No freshness, volume, or schema anomaly fires because the table arrived on time, full, and correctly shaped — the values are simply mismatched against another source, which is statistically unremarkable. What catches it is a reconciliation rule comparing the two sources, not anomaly detection. This is a classic complementarity case: the bought tool guards the pipeline's health while a rule guards the cross-source equality.

Q: How can you tune a distribution monitor that keeps false-positiving on a legitimately spiky column?
A: Widen or seasonalise the learned baseline, exclude or annotate known event periods (month-end, corporate actions), reduce sensitivity, or move that column from anomaly detection to an explicit range rule if its valid behaviour is known. The goal is to stop the recurring noise without going blind to real shifts. Persistent false positives on one column usually mean the metric is a poor fit for unsupervised anomaly detection and belongs in a rule.

Q: What is "monitoring-as-code", and why does an architect prefer it even with a managed tool?
A: Monitoring-as-code means defining monitors and their configuration declaratively in version-controlled files rather than clicking them into a UI. An architect prefers it because it is reviewable, reproducible, auditable, and portable — it survives team turnover and eases migration or audit, which matters in a regulated context. Even a managed tool with a UI is better governed when its monitor definitions live in code under change control.

Q: What is the difference between "detection coverage" and "alert precision", and why must an evaluation report both?
A: Detection coverage is how many real failure classes and tables the tool watches; alert precision is what fraction of its alerts are genuine incidents rather than noise. You must report both because a tool can have high coverage but poor precision (it watches everything and cries wolf) or high precision but narrow coverage (trustworthy but missing whole failure classes). A good build-vs-buy verdict weighs both, not coverage alone.

Q: A managed observability tool reports a freshness incident, but the upstream job log shows success. What does this discrepancy most likely mean?
A: It most likely means the job ran and "succeeded" but produced no new or stale data — for example it processed an empty or already-consumed source, or wrote to the wrong partition — so the table did not actually refresh despite a green exit code. This is the textbook silent failure that infra success metrics miss and data freshness catches. The fix is to investigate the source the job read, not the job's status, using lineage to find where the staleness originates.

Q: Summarise the architect's decision rule for managed data observability in one principle.
A: Buy the blanket, zero-config metadata anomaly coverage (freshness/volume/schema/distribution and lineage) that is tedious and unscalable to build, build the precise business-logic and regulatory rules that anomaly detection cannot infer, and let estate size and blast radius decide how much paid coverage to apply where. The answer is almost always hybrid, justified by complementarity and per-table economics. Evaluate on precision and downtime reduction, never on demo anomaly counts.
