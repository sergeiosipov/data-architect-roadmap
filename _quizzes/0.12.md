# Phase 0 · 0.12 Code review basics — 100 self-test questions

Q: What is a code review?
A: A code review is a process where one or more people other than the author read a proposed code change and give feedback before it is merged, to catch defects and improve quality.

Q: What are the three main things reviewers typically look for?
A: Correctness (does it do the right thing without bugs), clarity (is it readable and maintainable), and scope (does the change stay focused on one concern without creep).

Q: What does "correctness" mean in the context of a review?
A: It means the code produces the right results, handles edge cases and errors, and behaves as the description and tests claim — not just that it compiles or runs.

Q: What does "clarity" mean when reviewing code?
A: Clarity means the code is easy to read and understand — good names, sensible structure, helpful comments where needed — so a future maintainer can follow it without the author present.

Q: What does "scope" mean as a review concern?
A: Scope is whether the change does only what it set out to do; unrelated refactors, drive-by changes, or feature creep make a change harder to review and riskier to merge.

Q: What does `PR` stand for?
A: `PR` stands for pull request — a proposed set of changes on a branch that you ask maintainers to review and merge into a target branch.

Q: Why are small PRs easier to review than large ones?
A: Reviewers can hold a small change fully in their head, spot bugs more reliably, and respond faster; review quality drops sharply as diff size grows.

Q: What is a rough rule of thumb for a reviewable PR size?
A: Keep it small enough to review in one sitting — often cited as a few hundred changed lines or fewer; beyond that, defect-finding effectiveness falls off.

Q: What does "one concern per PR" mean?
A: Each PR should address a single logical change — one bug fix, one feature, or one refactor — so reviewers and the git history each map to a clear, separable unit of work.

Q: Why should you self-review a PR before requesting review from others?
A: Self-review catches obvious mistakes, leftover debug code, and unclear bits, saving reviewers' time and signalling that you respect their attention.

Q: How do you self-review a PR effectively?
A: Read your own diff line by line as if you were the reviewer, in the PR's diff view rather than your editor, checking each change is intentional and explained.

Q: What belongs in a good PR description?
A: What the change does, why it is needed, how to test or verify it, and any context, trade-offs, or links to the issue or ticket it resolves.

Q: Why does the "why" matter more than the "what" in a PR description?
A: Reviewers can read the diff to see "what" changed, but only the author knows the motivation; the "why" lets reviewers judge whether the approach fits the goal.

Q: What is a "stacked" or "chained" PR?
A: A series of small dependent PRs where each builds on the previous one, letting you break a large feature into reviewable pieces that merge in order.

Q: Why is reviewing code, not the person, a core feedback principle?
A: Critiquing the code keeps feedback objective and non-defensive; attacking the author triggers ego responses and damages trust without improving the change.

Q: Rewrite "you always forget error handling" as code-focused feedback.
A: "This block doesn't handle the case where the file is missing — should we catch and log that?" — it points at the code and proposes a path forward.

Q: What are Conventional Comments?
A: A lightweight convention where each review comment starts with a labelled prefix (like `praise:`, `nitpick:`, `suggestion:`, `issue:`, `question:`) to signal its intent and severity.

Q: What does the `nitpick:` label communicate?
A: That the comment is minor and optional — a small preference the author can take or leave without it blocking the merge.

Q: What does the `suggestion:` label communicate?
A: That you are proposing a specific change the author should consider; it carries more weight than a nitpick but is usually still discussable.

Q: What does the `issue:` label communicate?
A: That you have found a real problem that should be addressed before merge — a bug, risk, or correctness concern, not just a preference.

Q: What does the `question:` label communicate?
A: That you genuinely do not understand something and want clarification, rather than asserting it is wrong — useful when you may be missing context.

Q: Why include `praise:` comments in a review?
A: Genuine praise reinforces good patterns, balances critical feedback, and keeps review a collaborative rather than purely fault-finding exercise.

Q: What is the value of marking comment severity explicitly?
A: It tells the author which comments are blocking versus optional, preventing them from over- or under-reacting and speeding up resolution.

Q: How should you respond to review feedback "without ego"?
A: Treat comments as being about the code, assume good intent, fix or discuss each point on its merits, and avoid taking critique as a personal attack.

Q: What should you do if you disagree with a reviewer's comment?
A: Respond respectfully with your reasoning, ask a clarifying question, or propose an alternative — discuss rather than silently ignore or comply resentfully.

Q: What should you do before resolving a review comment thread?
A: Make sure the comment is actually addressed — either by changing the code, or by replying with a rationale the reviewer accepts — not just clicking resolve to make it disappear.

Q: Who should resolve a review comment thread, the author or the reviewer?
A: Conventions vary, but a common rule is the author marks it resolved after addressing it, while the reviewer can re-open if not satisfied; agree on this as a team.

Q: Why is "review as design enforcement" important?
A: Review is the gate where the team checks that changes follow agreed architecture, patterns, and standards, preventing erosion of the design over time.

Q: How does code review serve as audit evidence?
A: A recorded review (who approved, when, what was discussed) provides a documented control showing changes were independently checked — valuable for regulated environments.

Q: Why does a regulated EU fund administrator care about code-review audit trails?
A: Regulators and auditors expect evidence of change control; a mandatory, recorded peer review of code touching NAV or investor data demonstrates segregation of duties and oversight.

Q: What is "segregation of duties" and how does review support it?
A: It means the person who makes a change is not the only one who approves it; requiring a reviewer's approval before merge enforces that separation.

Q: In Git terms, what does an approved PR usually gate?
A: It usually gates the merge into a protected branch like `main`, so unreviewed code cannot reach the branch that deploys to production.

Q: What is a "protected branch"?
A: A branch with rules preventing direct pushes and requiring conditions — like passing checks and at least one approval — before any change can merge in.

Q: What is a "required reviewer" or `CODEOWNERS` rule?
A: A rule that automatically requests review from designated owners of certain files or directories, ensuring the right experts approve changes to sensitive areas.

Q: What does a `CODEOWNERS` file do?
A: It maps file paths or patterns to owning users or teams so that PRs touching those paths automatically require review from the listed owners.

Q: Why is it bad to mix a refactor and a bug fix in one PR?
A: Reviewers cannot tell which diff lines fixed the bug versus merely moved code, making it harder to verify the fix and risky to revert later.

Q: What should you do if a PR is getting too big mid-development?
A: Split it — extract independent pieces into separate smaller PRs, or land prerequisite refactors first, so each review stays focused and manageable.

Q: What is a "draft" or "WIP" PR for?
A: To share work in progress for early feedback or visibility while signalling it is not ready to merge, so reviewers know not to do a final approval yet.

Q: What does it mean to "approve" a PR?
A: It records that you reviewed the change and believe it is ready to merge, accepting shared responsibility for the code going in.

Q: What does "request changes" mean on a PR?
A: It blocks the merge until the author addresses your concerns and you re-review, signalling that the change is not yet acceptable.

Q: What is the difference between "comment" and "approve" on a PR?
A: A plain comment leaves feedback without blocking or endorsing the merge, while approve actively signs off that it is ready to go in.

Q: Why should a reviewer pull and run the code in some cases rather than only reading the diff?
A: Reading catches logic and style issues, but running it reveals runtime behaviour, integration problems, and whether the change actually works as described.

Q: What is a "rubber stamp" review and why is it harmful?
A: Approving without really reading the change; it defeats the purpose of review, lets defects through, and creates false audit evidence of oversight.

Q: How does a reviewer avoid bikeshedding?
A: By focusing on substantive correctness and design issues and deferring trivial style debates to automated tools like linters and formatters.

Q: What is the role of automated linters and formatters in reducing review friction?
A: They handle objective style and formatting mechanically, so humans spend review time on logic and design instead of arguing over whitespace.

Q: What does CI mean in a PR context and why does it matter to reviewers?
A: CI (continuous integration) runs automated builds, tests, and checks on the PR; green CI lets the reviewer focus on judgement rather than re-verifying basics by hand.

Q: Why should you not review a PR whose CI is failing?
A: The failing checks may already reveal problems; reviewing prematurely wastes effort on code that will change once the author fixes the failures.

Q: What is a "blocking" comment versus a "non-blocking" one?
A: A blocking comment must be resolved before merge because it concerns correctness or risk; a non-blocking one is optional and can be deferred or ignored.

Q: How should you phrase a blocking comment so the author understands its weight?
A: State clearly that it must be addressed before merge and why, e.g. "issue (blocking): this will divide by zero when the fund has no holdings."

Q: Why keep review comments specific rather than vague?
A: Specific comments tell the author exactly what to change and where; vague ones like "this feels off" create back-and-forth and frustration.

Q: What is the benefit of suggesting code directly in a review comment?
A: A concrete suggestion (e.g. GitHub's suggestion blocks) removes ambiguity and lets the author apply the fix with one click, speeding resolution.

Q: When is it better to discuss a PR synchronously (call/pairing) instead of in comments?
A: When the thread is going in circles, the disagreement is deep, or the design needs rethinking — a quick conversation resolves it faster than many comment rounds.

Q: What is "scope creep" in a PR and why resist it?
A: Adding unrelated improvements beyond the stated goal; it bloats the diff, delays review, and entangles changes that should be independently reviewable and revertible.

Q: If you spot an unrelated bug while reviewing, what should you do?
A: Note it but keep it out of this PR — file an issue or a follow-up so the current change stays focused and the bug still gets tracked.

Q: Why is a small, single-concern PR easier to revert?
A: Because reverting it undoes exactly one logical change without dragging along unrelated work, keeping `git revert` clean and low-risk.

Q: What does `git revert` do?
A: It creates a new commit that undoes the changes of a previous commit, preserving history rather than rewriting it.

Q: Why is a clear PR title useful beyond review?
A: It becomes part of the merge commit and git history, so a descriptive title helps anyone later doing `git log` understand what each change did.

Q: What is a "self-review checklist" and why use one?
A: A short list of items to verify before requesting review (tests added, no debug prints, description complete); it standardises quality and reduces avoidable comments.

Q: Why should debug `print` statements or `console.log` calls be removed before review?
A: They clutter the diff, leak noise into production logs, and signal an unfinished self-review; reviewers shouldn't have to flag obvious leftovers.

Q: What does it mean that review feedback should be "actionable"?
A: Each comment should make clear what the author can do about it — a specific change, a question to answer, or a decision to make — not just an observation.

Q: How quickly should reviewers aim to respond to a PR, and why?
A: Quickly, often within a day, because a blocked PR stalls the author's work and large queues encourage rushed reviews; agree on a team turnaround norm.

Q: What is the risk of a PR sitting unreviewed for a long time?
A: It drifts out of date with `main`, accumulates merge conflicts, the author loses context, and the change becomes harder and riskier to land.

Q: How does keeping PRs small reduce merge conflicts?
A: Small PRs merge faster, so they spend less time diverging from `main`, reducing the window in which conflicting changes can land.

Q: What should the author do when a reviewer asks a clarifying question?
A: Answer it directly, and if the confusion stemmed from unclear code, improve the code or add a comment so the next reader doesn't have the same question.

Q: Why is "the reviewer was confused" itself useful signal?
A: If a competent reviewer misread the code, future maintainers likely will too; confusion often points to a real clarity problem worth fixing.

Q: Why should the author, not the reviewer, usually do the rework?
A: The author owns the change and has the most context; the reviewer's job is to identify issues, not to take over authorship.

Q: When might a reviewer push a commit directly to the author's branch?
A: Only for tiny, agreed fixes (like a typo) with the author's consent; otherwise it muddies ownership and skips the author's understanding of the change.

Q: What does "approve with comments" or "approve, non-blocking nits" signal?
A: That you trust the author to address minor suggestions at their discretion and you don't need to re-review before merge.

Q: Why is tone important in written review comments?
A: Text lacks vocal cues, so blunt phrasing reads harsher than intended; courteous, curious wording keeps the exchange collaborative.

Q: Rewrite "this is wrong" as a constructive review comment.
A: "I think this returns the previous day's NAV instead of today's — should the date filter use `>=` here?" — it explains the problem and invites discussion.

Q: Why should you assume good intent when reading review comments as an author?
A: Reviewers are trying to improve the change, not attack you; assuming good intent keeps you open to feedback and the discussion productive.

Q: What is "review fatigue" and how do small PRs help?
A: Review fatigue is declining attention over a long or large review; small PRs keep each review short enough to stay thorough.

Q: How should a reviewer prioritise their comments?
A: Lead with correctness and design issues that block merge, then clarity suggestions, then optional nitpicks — so the author addresses what matters first.

Q: What is the purpose of a PR template?
A: It prompts the author to fill in standard sections (summary, testing, risk) so every PR arrives with the context reviewers need.

Q: For a PR that parses EMT files, what correctness checks should a reviewer prioritise?
A: That mandatory EMT fields and the ISIN are validated, numeric and date formats parse correctly, and malformed or missing rows are handled rather than silently dropped.

Q: For a PR computing NAV, what should a reviewer scrutinise most?
A: Rounding and precision of monetary values, correct currency handling, the valuation date logic, and that totals reconcile — correctness errors here have real financial impact.

Q: Why use exact decimal types rather than floats for NAV in code under review?
A: Floating point introduces rounding errors unacceptable for money; a reviewer should flag `float` for monetary values and recommend a decimal type.

Q: What should a reviewer check about ISIN handling in a PR?
A: That ISINs are validated (12 characters, correct check digit) and treated as opaque identifiers, not parsed for meaning or silently truncated.

Q: For a transfer-agency code change touching investor records, what extra review rigour applies?
A: Higher scrutiny on data correctness, access controls, audit logging, and personal-data handling, plus likely required sign-off from a data owner via `CODEOWNERS`.

Q: Why might a fund-industry PR require two approvals instead of one?
A: For high-risk or regulated changes (NAV, investor money, reporting), a second reviewer strengthens the control and the audit evidence of independent oversight.

Q: How does linking a PR to a ticket help audit and review?
A: It ties the code change to an authorised business reason, creating a traceable chain from requirement to change to approval that auditors can follow.

Q: What is a "squash merge" and how does it relate to clean history?
A: It combines all of a PR's commits into one commit on merge, so the main branch shows one tidy entry per change rather than messy work-in-progress commits.

Q: What is the downside of many tiny "fix review comment" commits, and how is it handled?
A: They clutter history; squash-merging or interactive cleanup collapses them into a single meaningful commit on the target branch.

Q: Why should a PR's automated tests be part of what the reviewer evaluates?
A: Tests are the durable proof of correctness; the reviewer checks they exist, cover the change, and would actually fail if the behaviour broke.

Q: What is a red flag if a bug-fix PR has no test?
A: Without a regression test the bug can silently return; a reviewer should ask for a test that fails before the fix and passes after.

Q: How should a reviewer handle a PR that is correct but poorly named/structured?
A: Flag the clarity issues as suggestions or issues depending on severity; correctness is necessary but maintainability also matters for the long term.

Q: Why is "it works on my machine" not enough to satisfy a reviewer?
A: Correctness must hold across environments and inputs; reviewers look for tests, edge-case handling, and reproducibility, not just one passing local run.

Q: What is the author's responsibility regarding the PR diff's readability?
A: To present a clean, logically ordered diff — separate formatting from logic changes, avoid unrelated noise — so the reviewer can follow the intent easily.

Q: Why separate a pure-formatting commit from a logic commit?
A: So reformatting noise doesn't bury the real change; reviewers can skim the formatting commit and focus attention on the logic one.

Q: What should you do as a reviewer if a PR is simply too large to review well?
A: Say so and ask the author to split it; approving a too-large change you can't actually understand undermines the whole purpose of review.

Q: What is the danger of approving to "unblock a colleague" without real review?
A: It creates false audit evidence, lets defects through, and erodes the team's trust in approvals as a meaningful quality gate.

Q: How does code review spread knowledge across a team?
A: Reviewers learn the codebase and the change's context, and authors learn from feedback, reducing bus-factor risk and aligning the team on patterns.

Q: Why might a reviewer prefix a comment with "non-blocking:"?
A: To make explicit that, although they are raising a point, it should not hold up the merge and is at the author's discretion.

Q: What is the value of the author leaving inline self-comments on their own PR?
A: Pointing out tricky decisions, asking targeted questions, or flagging a workaround guides reviewers' attention and pre-empts confusion.

Q: How should disagreements that can't be resolved between author and reviewer be escalated?
A: To a tech lead, owning team, or agreed tie-breaker, so the change isn't deadlocked and the decision is recorded.

Q: What is a sensible first thing to check when a reviewer says "this doesn't do what the description claims"?
A: Re-read your own diff against the description — either the code has a bug or the description is inaccurate, and you fix whichever is wrong.

Q: Your PR has 40 review comments — what does that usually indicate and how do you respond?
A: It usually means the PR was too large or under-self-reviewed; address the comments calmly, and next time split the work and self-review first.

Q: A reviewer keeps re-requesting changes on style the linter doesn't catch — how do you resolve it sustainably?
A: Agree the rule as a team and encode it in the linter or a style guide, so the standard is enforced automatically rather than re-litigated per PR.

Q: Why is a merged, reviewed PR stronger audit evidence than an emailed approval?
A: It links the exact code diff, the reviewer's identity, timestamps, comments, and the passing checks in one immutable record tied to the change itself.

Q: What is the single best habit for getting fast, high-quality reviews?
A: Submit small, single-concern, well-described, self-reviewed PRs — they are quick to review, easy to approve, and least likely to hide defects.
