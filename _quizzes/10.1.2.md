# Phase 6 · 10.1.2 Enterprise catalogs (Purview, Collibra) — 100 self-test questions

Q: What two enterprise data catalogs does this lesson compare, and why these two specifically?
A: Microsoft Purview and Collibra. Purview is the de-facto default whenever an organisation already lives in Azure/M365, while Collibra is the long-standing incumbent in EU financial-services data-governance programmes. The lesson's premise is that as an architect you will most likely evaluate or inherit one of these, so you must be able to judge them past a polished demo rather than picking on a feature checklist.

Q: What is the single deciding factor this lesson says almost always determines catalog success, and what is the runner-up?
A: Connector coverage is the deciding factor: a catalog that cannot scan half your estate is worthless no matter how good its features look. The close runner-up is stewardship workflow — whether anybody actually maintains the catalog after go-live. Features themselves are rarely the differentiator because both products do roughly the same things on a slide.

Q: In current Microsoft Purview, what are the two primary data-governance solutions and how do they divide responsibility?
A: `Data Map` and `Unified Catalog`. `Data Map` scans your assets and multicloud sources to capture metadata, while `Unified Catalog` is the SaaS curation experience where you build governance domains, data products, glossary terms, manage data quality, and grant access. All content in both is metadata only — neither grants access to the underlying data itself.

Q: What does the Microsoft Purview Data Map actually store, and what does it explicitly not store?
A: The Data Map stores only metadata — schemas, asset names, classifications, lineage relationships, and scan results — not the underlying data rows themselves. The Microsoft docs stress this twice: none of the roles or permissions in Data Map or Unified Catalog grant access to the real data. This matters for fund data because you can catalog a NAV table's structure without the catalog ever holding investor PII or position values.

Q: What is a Data Map "scan" in Purview and what does it produce?
A: A scan connects to a registered data source, walks its schemas/objects, and ingests technical metadata into the Data Map, optionally applying classifications. You point a scan at a source, choose a scan rule set, and schedule it; the result is asset entries with detected types and classifications. Scans are the ingestion mechanism — without a successful scan an asset never appears in the catalog.

Q: Since 6 January 2025, are Purview Data Map scans billed, and what is now charged instead?
A: No — under the pricing model effective 6 January 2025, scanning and Data Map usage are free. Billing moved to two pay-as-you-go meters: governed assets (assets you actively curate by linking to a governance concept) and Data Governance Processing Units (DGPU) for compute such as running data-quality rules. This is a deliberate "scan everything for free, pay only when you govern" design.

Q: What are the three Data Governance Processing Unit (DGPU) tiers in Purview and their list prices?
A: The DGPU tiers are Basic at `$15` per DGPU, Standard at `$60` per DGPU, and Advanced at `$240` per DGPU. They represent escalating compute for data-quality rule execution and similar processing. You pick the tier appropriate to the complexity of the quality workload, so cost scales with how much active quality processing you do, not with how much you scan.

Q: In the new Purview pricing, what exactly counts as a "governed asset" that you pay for?
A: A governed asset is one you actively manage by associating it with a governance concept — for example linking it to a data product or a critical data element. Merely scanning an asset into the Data Map does not make it governed or billable. Charges are counted per unique governed asset per day and prorated across the monthly billing cycle, so a brief association costs little.

Q: What is a "governance domain" in the Purview Unified Catalog?
A: A governance domain is an organisational boundary that groups data products and business concepts and establishes accountability for an area of the data estate. It lets you mirror business areas (for example "Transfer Agency" or "Fund Accounting") and apply governance controls within that scope. Domains complement the technical collections that exist elsewhere, giving business-facing ownership rather than source-system grouping.

Q: What is a "data product" in the Purview Unified Catalog and who is it for?
A: A data product is a curated group of assets bundled around a use case that you can share with data consumers. Instead of forcing consumers to find raw tables, you publish a coherent product (for example "Daily NAV pack") with description, owner, and access workflow. It is the consumer-facing unit of discovery and access in the Unified Catalog.

Q: What is the role of glossary terms in Microsoft Purview, and how do they differ from purely descriptive labels?
A: Glossary terms provide business vocabulary so users can find data using familiar language rather than physical source names. In the modern Unified Catalog they are active: terms can carry context and connect to governance concepts that influence how data is managed and made discoverable. They bridge the gap between a physical column like `nav_per_share` and a business concept like "Net Asset Value per Share".

Q: Beyond glossary terms, what other business concepts can the Purview Unified Catalog link data to?
A: It can connect data to objectives and key results (OKRs) and to critical data elements, in addition to glossary terms. This lets a data product be tied to a measurable business objective and flag the columns that matter most for governance. Linking an asset to such a concept is also what turns it into a billable governed asset.

Q: Purview has "classifications" and "sensitivity labels" — what is the difference?
A: Classifications describe what kind of data a column contains (for example credit-card number, national ID, email), driven by detection logic during scans. Sensitivity labels categorise an asset for data security and privacy purposes and originate from the Microsoft Information Protection label taxonomy. Classifications answer "what is this data"; labels answer "how protected must it be".

Q: Roughly how many built-in classifications does Purview ship with, and can you add your own?
A: Purview ships with over 200 built-in system classifications covering common sensitive types. You can also create custom classifications, where the custom classification is the label and one or more custom classification rules supply the detection logic. The rules are then added to scan rule sets so the scan can detect and apply them.

Q: On what two detection mechanisms can a Purview custom classification rule be based?
A: A custom classification rule can be based on a regular-expression pattern or on a dictionary (a list of values to match). Regex suits structured identifiers like an ISIN or LEI pattern, while a dictionary suits enumerated code lists such as fund share-class codes. You attach the rule to a scan rule set so scans apply your custom classification automatically.

Q: What is a Purview "scan rule set" and why does it matter?
A: A scan rule set is a reusable bundle of file-type and classification settings applied to scans of a given source type. It controls which classification rules (built-in and custom) run during the scan, so it determines what gets detected and tagged. Defining a fund-specific scan rule set lets you consistently detect ISINs, LEIs, and investor identifiers across every source you scan.

Q: A fund admin wants Purview to flag every column containing an ISIN. How would you approach this?
A: Create a custom classification (the label, e.g. "ISIN"), define a custom classification rule with a regex matching the 12-character ISIN structure, add that rule to a scan rule set, then scan the relevant sources with that rule set. ISIN's check-digit means a pure regex catches format but not validity, so expect some false positives. After scanning, the classification appears on matching columns in the Data Map.

Q: What are Purview's standout integration strengths according to this lesson?
A: Its deep integration with the Microsoft 365 and Azure ecosystem — it natively understands and reuses Microsoft Information Protection sensitivity labels, integrates with Microsoft Fabric/OneLake, and ties into Azure identity and security tooling. If your estate is Azure-centric, this gives you classification, labelling, and discovery with minimal extra plumbing. That ecosystem fit is the strongest reason organisations adopt Purview.

Q: What are Purview's main weaknesses that this lesson highlights?
A: Lineage and cost gaps. Lineage coverage is strong inside the Microsoft estate but thin or manual outside it, so a mixed estate leaves gaps you must fill yourself. The pricing, while restructured to be cheaper for scanning, still introduces governed-asset and DGPU meters you must model carefully or costs surprise you at scale.

Q: Why is Purview lineage often described as "strong inside the Microsoft estate but weak outside it"?
A: Purview captures rich automatic lineage for Microsoft-native services (Fabric, ADF, Synapse, Power BI), but for third-party engines and bespoke pipelines lineage is limited or must be pushed in manually via the API. So a Luxembourg fund admin running mixed Informatica, dbt, and SQL Server pipelines will see lineage holes exactly where the non-Microsoft tools sit. This is why open lineage standards like OpenLineage are often layered alongside.

Q: What does "Purview Analytics in OneLake" let you do?
A: It lets you export curated metadata from the Unified Catalog into OneLake so business users can run analytics over governance data inside Microsoft Fabric. In effect your catalog's metadata becomes queryable like any other Fabric dataset. It is a concrete example of the M365/Fabric integration advantage Purview offers over neutral third-party catalogs.

Q: How does Security Copilot relate to discovery in the Purview Unified Catalog?
A: Security Copilot enables natural-language search over the catalog, so a user can ask for data by business context rather than guessing table names. It is part of Purview's "responsible innovation" pillar aimed at making discovery friendlier for non-technical consumers. This reflects the wider AI-assisted-discovery trend that neutral catalogs are also racing to match.

Q: What is the recommended Purview onboarding workflow at a glance?
A: Assign a user the Data Governance Administrator role, use Data Map to scan sources for metadata, then in Unified Catalog build governance domains and curate data products, connect data to business concepts like OKRs and glossary terms, and finally improve data quality to raise data health. The sequence is scan, then curate, then govern, then measure. The administrator role is the prerequisite because it grants the power to assign all other governance roles.

Q: What does the term "federated approach" mean in Purview's data-governance model?
A: It is the middle ground between fully centralised and fully decentralised governance: a central data office sets the rules and standards, while domain owners and stewards who understand each data area govern it day to day. This balances oversight with the local knowledge needed to govern data well. It maps cleanly onto a fund admin where central compliance sets policy but each business line stewards its own data.

Q: In Purview's role model, what does the "central data office" do versus a "data steward"?
A: The central data office establishes and enforces governance policies, maintains active metadata and compliance, and watches overall governance health. Data stewards focus on data quality, discoverability, glossary upkeep, consistency, and lineage for their area, working with the central office. The split mirrors policy-setting versus hands-on curation.

Q: What is Collibra's core architectural strength according to this lesson?
A: Its operating model — the ability to mirror an organisation's real governance structure of communities, domains, asset types, and steward roles inside the platform, driven by configurable workflows. Collibra excels at turning governance into auditable, repeatable processes rather than ad-hoc tagging. This makes it well suited to large enterprises with mature governance programmes.

Q: What is a Collibra "operating model" and what objects compose it?
A: The operating model is Collibra's metamodel: the configured set of communities, domains, asset types (such as Data Attribute, Data Entity, Data Model, Data Concept, Data Domain), and the relations between them. You shape it to mirror how your organisation is actually governed. Because it is configurable, two Collibra deployments can look very different depending on how the operating model was designed.

Q: In Collibra, what is the difference between a "community" and a "domain"?
A: A community is a top-level organisational grouping (often a business unit or function) that can contain sub-communities and domains, while a domain is a container of assets of related types within a community. Together they form the hierarchy that mirrors your org structure. For a fund admin you might model a "Fund Accounting" community containing a "NAV Glossary" domain and a "NAV Lineage" domain.

Q: What are Collibra workflows, and why are they considered a differentiator?
A: Collibra workflows are configurable, designer-built processes (BPMN-style) that automate stewardship tasks such as approvals, certifications, and issue management. They turn governance from manual chores into streamlined, auditable, enforceable processes. This workflow strength — not raw cataloging — is the main reason mature enterprises choose Collibra over lighter tools.

Q: What is Collibra "Guided Stewardship" and what does it provide out of the box?
A: Guided Stewardship is a Collibra offering that helps data stewards organise and govern data by supplying a ready-made operating model and related features on top of the Stewardship application. It reduces the heavy up-front modelling effort by giving you predefined asset types and layers. It is Collibra's answer to the criticism that standing up its operating model from scratch is slow.

Q: The Guided Stewardship operating model has multiple data layers — what are they conceptually?
A: It is organised around structural data layers: a physical layer representing how data actually exists in source systems, plus semantic and conceptual layers with asset types such as Data Attribute, Data Entity, Data Model, Data Concept, and Data Domain. The layers separate "what is physically stored" from "what it means to the business." This layering lets you govern business concepts independently of shifting physical schemas.

Q: What is Collibra's main drawback that this lesson emphasises?
A: Implementation weight. Because Collibra's power comes from a configurable operating model and workflows, it requires significant design, configuration, and ongoing administration effort to stand up and keep maintained. It can become shelfware if an organisation buys the licence but never invests in the operating-model design and stewardship discipline. The product is heavy precisely because it is flexible.

Q: Why can Collibra's flexibility be both its biggest strength and its biggest risk?
A: The configurable operating model lets it mirror almost any governance structure, which is why mature enterprises love it — but that same flexibility means nothing works until someone designs and maintains the model, so a half-configured deployment delivers little value. Flexibility shifts the burden onto your implementation team. Guided Stewardship exists specifically to blunt this risk with sensible defaults.

Q: What is Collibra Edge and what problem does it solve?
A: Collibra Edge is a deployable connectivity component (an "Edge site" running near your data) that lets the cloud platform reach on-premises and cloud sources to ingest metadata and lineage without exposing those sources directly. You register a source via Edge and add Edge capabilities (such as JDBC ingestion) to a site. It is the modern mechanism for getting metadata into Collibra securely across network boundaries.

Q: How does technical lineage flow through Collibra Edge at a high level?
A: An Edge capability harvests metadata from the configured sources, uploads it to Collibra Data Lineage's metadata-ingest pipeline, and triggers a sync pipeline that parses the metadata into lineage assets and relations, stitching partial lineages into a single graph. So Edge does the local extraction and the cloud service does the stitching. This split keeps source credentials local while centralising the lineage graph.

Q: What is the status of the Collibra CLI "lineage harvester," and what is the deadline you must know?
A: The standalone CLI lineage harvester is deprecated, with end-of-life on 31 July 2026, and Collibra recommends moving to technical lineage via Edge. If you inherit a Collibra deployment still using the CLI harvester, migrating to Edge is a near-term obligation, not an option. Knowing this date prevents you from building new pipelines on a dying mechanism.

Q: You inherit a Collibra estate whose lineage still runs on the deprecated CLI harvester. What is your first move?
A: Plan and schedule a migration to technical lineage via Edge before the 31 July 2026 end-of-life, since the CLI harvester will stop being supported. Audit which sources the CLI harvester covers, stand up Edge sites with equivalent capabilities, validate the lineage matches, then decommission the harvester. Doing this proactively avoids a lineage outage when the deprecated tool is removed.

Q: What does "catalog TCO realities — connector coverage decides everything" mean in practice?
A: Total cost of ownership is dominated not by licence price but by whether the catalog can actually connect to your sources; gaps force expensive custom integration or manual entry that quietly dwarfs the licence. A cheaper catalog that misses half your systems costs more in the end than a pricier one that connects to everything. So you evaluate connectors against your real estate before you ever look at the price sheet.

Q: Why is connector coverage, not feature count, the right axis for evaluating an enterprise catalog?
A: Because both Purview and Collibra offer broadly similar features, but a catalog only delivers value over assets it can actually see; an unreachable source contributes nothing regardless of how rich the feature set is. Missing connectors mean blind spots, manual maintenance, and stale metadata. The honest evaluation question is "what fraction of my estate can this catalog automatically reach and keep current?"

Q: How should you build a connector-coverage table for a catalog evaluation?
A: List every distinct source type in your estate (databases, warehouses, BI tools, file stores, message queues, ETL engines) down the rows, and OpenMetadata, Purview, and Collibra across the columns, marking native connector / partial / none for each cell. The exercise immediately exposes which catalog can see most of your estate without custom work. It turns a vague "they all integrate" claim into evidence a sponsor can act on.

Q: When sketching a TCO comparison for a 300-person fund administrator, what cost categories must you include beyond licence fees?
A: Include licence/subscription, implementation and operating-model design, connector build-out for unsupported sources, ongoing stewardship and administration headcount, infrastructure (for example Collibra Edge sites or Purview DGPU compute), and training. The hidden categories — implementation effort and stewardship headcount — usually dominate. A TCO that lists only licence cost is misleading and will not survive scrutiny.

Q: For a mid-size fund admin already standardised on Azure and M365, what TCO argument favours Purview?
A: Because the estate is Microsoft-centric, Purview reuses existing identity, sensitivity labels, and Fabric integration, so implementation and connector effort are lower and scanning is free under the new pricing. You mainly pay for governed assets and DGPU compute you actually use. The integration "comes for free" advantage shrinks the implementation cost category that usually dominates TCO.

Q: For that same fund admin, what TCO argument might still favour Collibra despite its weight?
A: If the organisation has a mature, audited governance programme with complex stewardship workflows and regulatory approval chains, Collibra's operating model and workflow engine can encode those processes in a way Purview's lighter model cannot. The heavier implementation cost buys auditable, enforceable governance that a regulator-facing fund admin may genuinely need. The decision turns on governance maturity, not technology preference.

Q: What is the "migration/coexistence" pattern this lesson asks you to understand?
A: Running an open catalog (such as OpenMetadata) alongside an enterprise catalog (Purview or Collibra) rather than ripping one out for the other. The open catalog can cover sources the enterprise catalog misses or serve engineering teams, while the enterprise catalog serves business stewardship and compliance. The goal is coexistence that fills coverage gaps instead of a risky big-bang migration.

Q: Why might a fund admin deliberately run OpenMetadata alongside Collibra or Purview rather than choosing only one?
A: Because no single catalog connects to everything, an open catalog can cover engineering-centric or niche sources the enterprise catalog lacks connectors for, while the enterprise catalog provides the audited stewardship and compliance workflows the regulator expects. Coexistence covers the estate fully and hedges against connector gaps. The trade-off is the cost of keeping two catalogs in sync.

Q: What is the chief risk of a two-catalog coexistence architecture, and how do you mitigate it?
A: The chief risk is metadata drift — the same asset described differently in each catalog, eroding trust. Mitigate it by designating one catalog as system of record per metadata type, syncing via APIs or a shared standard like OpenLineage, and avoiding duplicate manual curation. Without a clear ownership rule, coexistence degrades into two stale catalogs nobody trusts.

Q: When you migrate from an open catalog to an enterprise catalog, what should you migrate first and what last?
A: Migrate the technical metadata and lineage first because it can be regenerated by scanning, and migrate human-curated content — glossary terms, ownership, certifications, stewardship history — last and most carefully, because it is expensive and cannot be re-scanned. Curation is the irreplaceable asset. A coexistence period lets you validate the new catalog before retiring the old one.

Q: What goes into a vendor-selection memo a sponsor could actually act on?
A: A clear recommendation up front, the connector-coverage evidence for your real estate, a TCO comparison with all cost categories, the deciding factors named explicitly (connector coverage and stewardship workflow), key risks and mitigations, and a phased rollout plan. The sponsor needs a decision and the reasoning, not a feature catalogue. Leading with the recommendation and the deciding factor is what makes it actionable.

Q: A vendor demo of Purview or Collibra looks flawless. What should make you skeptical "past the demo"?
A: Demos use curated, fully-connected sample data, so they reveal nothing about whether the product connects to your specific messy estate or whether anyone will maintain it. Ask to connect it to two of your hardest real sources and to see the stewardship workflow run end to end. The demo tests the feature; your estate tests the connector coverage and stewardship reality that actually decide success.

Q: Why is "Purview is the Azure default" a buy-side fact an architect must weigh carefully?
A: Because being the default means it is often adopted by inertia when an organisation is already in Azure, sometimes before anyone checks whether it covers the non-Azure half of the estate. Default status lowers friction but does not guarantee fit. The architect's job is to validate connector coverage against the real estate rather than accepting the default uncritically.

Q: Why is Collibra described as the "EU-FS incumbent," and what does inheriting it imply?
A: Collibra has deep penetration in European financial-services governance programmes, so a Luxembourg or Dublin fund admin is statistically likely to already own it. Inheriting it means you must judge an existing, possibly partially-configured deployment rather than choosing from scratch. Your task often becomes "make the inherited Collibra actually work" rather than "should we buy Collibra."

Q: A regulator asks a Luxembourg fund admin to demonstrate end-to-end lineage for the daily NAV. Which catalog characteristic matters most?
A: Connector and lineage coverage across the full NAV production chain — order capture, pricing, fund accounting, and EMT/EPT output — regardless of which engines are involved. A catalog that only captures lineage for part of that chain cannot satisfy the regulator. This is exactly where Purview's outside-Microsoft lineage gaps or Collibra's harvester configuration determine whether you pass.

Q: How would an enterprise catalog help evidence DORA-style operational-resilience requirements for fund data?
A: By providing a maintained inventory of critical data assets, their owners, lineage, and quality status, the catalog supplies the evidence that you know where critical data lives and how it flows — central to demonstrating operational resilience. Lineage shows blast radius if a source fails. The catalog turns "we think we know our data" into auditable metadata a regulator can inspect.

Q: Why is stewardship workflow especially important in a regulated fund-administration context?
A: Because regulators expect not just a catalog but evidence that data is actively owned, certified, and corrected through a repeatable, auditable process. A catalog with no maintained stewardship is just a stale inventory that proves nothing. This is why Collibra's workflow engine is attractive to regulated firms and why an unmaintained Purview deployment is a compliance liability.

Q: How would you classify and govern investor KYC data in either catalog, and what extra care is needed?
A: Apply a classification/sensitive-info type that detects the personal identifiers, attach the appropriate sensitivity label, assign a steward and retention policy, and restrict the catalog's own metadata visibility. Extra care: even catalog metadata (column names, sample values if captured) can leak PII, so disable sample collection for KYC sources. The catalog should describe KYC data without becoming a new copy of it.

Q: How can an enterprise catalog support an LEI as a master reference across systems?
A: By recording the LEI as a critical data element / business term, classifying the columns that carry it, and using lineage to show every system and report that consumes it. The catalog then answers "where is the LEI mastered and who depends on it" in one place. That makes the LEI's authoritative source and downstream impact explicit, which matters when an LEI changes or lapses.

Q: A scan in Purview completes but a column you expected to be classified as "ISIN" is untagged. What do you check first?
A: First confirm the scan used a scan rule set that includes your ISIN classification rule — a missing or wrong rule set is the most common cause. Then verify the regex actually matches the column's values and that the column held data during the scan (empty columns aren't classified). Re-run the scan after fixing the rule set rather than expecting retroactive tagging.

Q: In Purview, you scanned a source but it never appears in the Unified Catalog for business users. What is the likely cause?
A: Scanning populates the Data Map, but an asset only becomes a curated, consumer-visible entity when it is added to a data product or governance domain in the Unified Catalog. A scanned-but-uncurated asset sits in the Data Map and is not surfaced as a governed catalog entry. Curate it into a data product to make it discoverable — and note that also makes it a billable governed asset.

Q: Your Purview bill is rising even though you have not scanned new sources. What changed and why?
A: Scanning is free, so the rising cost is from governed assets and DGPU compute, not scans. Someone likely associated more assets with data products or critical data elements (each becomes a billable governed asset) or increased data-quality rule execution on a higher DGPU tier. Audit the count of governed assets and the DGPU tier rather than the scan schedule.

Q: A Collibra lineage diagram is missing a source that you know is connected. What do you check first in an Edge-based setup?
A: Check that the relevant Edge site has the correct capability configured and ran successfully, since the harvest happens at the Edge capability and feeds the lineage pipeline. A failed or misconfigured Edge capability silently produces gaps. Confirm the source is in the Edge configuration, the capability completed, and the sync pipeline ran before suspecting the lineage parser.

Q: A Collibra deployment was bought two years ago but barely used. As the new architect, what is the most likely root cause?
A: The operating model and stewardship workflows were never properly designed and adopted — Collibra's value depends entirely on that investment, so an unconfigured deployment delivers little. The licence was bought but the implementation weight was underestimated. The fix is to design a fit-for-purpose operating model (Guided Stewardship can jump-start it) and embed real stewardship workflows, not to buy more product.

Q: How does Purview's reuse of Microsoft Information Protection sensitivity labels save effort in an M365 shop?
A: Because the same MIP sensitivity-label taxonomy used across Office documents and email can be applied to data assets, you avoid building a second, separate classification scheme. Labels and their protections carry across the estate consistently. This is a concrete instance of the M365 integration advantage that lowers Purview's implementation cost in Microsoft-centric organisations.

Q: What is a key limitation of Purview's auto-labelling based on classifications?
A: Built-in classifications can trigger automatic sensitivity-label application on the next scan, but advanced detectors — custom sensitive info types, named entities, trainable classifiers, credentials, and Exact Data Match — are not supported for that auto-labelling. So your most sophisticated custom detection may classify but not auto-label. You must plan labelling around what auto-labelling actually supports.

Q: Why does this lesson treat "glossary embedded in catalogs" (topic 7.3.1 delivered as a feature) as part of the same discipline?
A: Because maintaining a business glossary is the same governance discipline whether you run it standalone or as a Collibra/Purview feature — the value is in the curation and adoption, not the hosting product. Embedding it in the catalog just co-locates terms with the assets they describe. The lesson links term governance to the catalog so the glossary stays connected to real data.

Q: How does a business glossary in the catalog improve NAV data governance specifically?
A: It gives a single authoritative definition of "NAV" and related terms (NAV per share, swing pricing, cut-off) linked to the physical columns that implement them, so every consumer interprets the figure the same way. Linked terms also let you trace which assets implement a contested definition. This kills the ambiguity that causes reconciliation disputes between systems.

Q: Both products claim "policy" capabilities. What is the realistic state of Purview's policy ambitions?
A: Purview has data-policy ambitions — using the catalog to drive access and governance decisions — but in practice policy enforcement maturity lags the cataloging and classification features. You should treat policy as an evolving area and validate exactly what is enforced versus advisory before relying on it. Buying on the policy roadmap rather than current behaviour is a classic over-promise trap.

Q: What does "Collibra is well-suited to large, complex enterprises" actually imply about smaller organisations?
A: It implies the operating-model and workflow machinery that justifies Collibra in a large, mature enterprise is overkill for a smaller or less mature organisation, where the implementation weight outweighs the benefit. A small fund admin may get more value faster from a lighter catalog. Fit to organisational maturity, not raw capability, should drive the choice.

Q: In Collibra terms, what is an "asset type" and why does configuring it matter?
A: An asset type defines a category of governed object in the operating model — for example Data Attribute, Data Entity, Table, Report, or Business Term — each with its own attributes and allowed relations. Configuring asset types correctly is what lets the catalog mirror your domain precisely. Poorly chosen asset types produce a model that doesn't fit how the business thinks, undermining adoption.

Q: Why do this lesson's "Done when" criteria insist you can name the deciding factor in one sentence?
A: Because an architect who cannot articulate that connector coverage plus stewardship workflow — not features — decide catalog success will be seduced by demos and feature checklists. Naming it forces the right evaluation discipline. The single-sentence test is a check that you have internalised the buy-side lesson, not just the product trivia.

Q: How would you prove "connector coverage" objectively rather than trusting a vendor's connector list?
A: Take your real source inventory and attempt to connect each candidate catalog to a representative sample, recording native / partial / manual / impossible per source, rather than counting logos on a marketing page. A connector existing is not the same as it covering your version, auth method, or network path. The proof is a populated catalog over your estate, not a list.

Q: What is the danger of evaluating Purview and Collibra purely on their feature matrices?
A: Their feature matrices look nearly identical, so a matrix comparison hides the only things that actually differ for you — whether each connects to your estate and whether your organisation will maintain it. Matrices reward vendors for breadth of features nobody uses. The honest comparison weighs connector fit and stewardship fit, which a matrix systematically omits.

Q: A sponsor says "just buy Purview, we're a Microsoft shop." What is your measured response?
A: Agree that Azure fit lowers cost and risk, then insist on validating connector coverage for the non-Microsoft half of the estate and confirming the lineage gaps outside the Microsoft stack are acceptable. The default is a strong starting hypothesis, not a finished decision. You endorse it only after the connector-coverage and lineage evidence supports it for the actual estate.

Q: How does Collibra's workflow engine help with the certification of fund reference data?
A: You can model a certification workflow — submission, steward review, approval, periodic re-certification — that runs automatically and leaves an audit trail proving each reference dataset was reviewed and approved. This converts "trust us, it's correct" into auditable evidence. For regulated reference data like fund master records, that auditability is the point of the workflow engine.

Q: What does it mean that Purview's Unified Catalog is a "single-tenant SaaS" experience?
A: It means each organisation's Unified Catalog runs as a managed, isolated SaaS instance rather than infrastructure you host, with Microsoft operating it for your tenant alone. You configure and curate, but you don't run servers. This lowers operational burden compared with self-hosting but ties you to Microsoft's hosting and pricing model.

Q: How does the Data Map "free scanning" model change how aggressively you should scan?
A: Because scanning is free under the post-January-2025 model, you can scan broadly to build a complete metadata picture without per-scan cost, deferring cost until you actively govern selected assets. So a "scan wide, govern narrow" strategy is now economically rational. Just remember the moment you curate an asset into a product it becomes a billable governed asset.

Q: What is a "critical data element" in Purview and why tie data to it?
A: A critical data element is a governance concept representing data that matters most to the business, to which you can link assets in the Unified Catalog. Tying assets to it both flags their importance for stewardship and prioritises quality monitoring. For a fund admin, NAV inputs and investor identifiers are obvious critical data elements; linking them also makes them governed (billable) assets.

Q: How do Purview governance domains compare conceptually to Collibra communities/domains?
A: Both give a way to mirror business structure and assign accountability — Purview's governance domains group data products and concepts with owners, while Collibra's communities and domains form a configurable hierarchy of asset containers. Collibra's model is generally more configurable and heavier; Purview's is lighter and more opinionated. The conceptual goal — accountable business-aligned boundaries — is the same.

Q: Why does the lesson pair "stewardship workflow" with "connector coverage" as joint deciding factors?
A: Because connector coverage gets accurate metadata into the catalog, but stewardship workflow is what keeps it accurate and trusted over time; a catalog needs both or it fails. Coverage without stewardship gives you a complete but rotting inventory; stewardship without coverage gives you a well-maintained but partial one. Success requires the catalog to both see the estate and stay maintained.

Q: What practical question best summarises a catalog TCO evaluation?
A: "Over three to five years, what will it cost to connect this catalog to my real estate, keep its metadata current through stewardship, and operate it — not just to license it?" Framing TCO this way surfaces the implementation and stewardship costs that licence-only comparisons hide. It also forces you to price the connector gaps specific to your sources.

Q: How might EMT/EPT file production benefit from being cataloged with full lineage?
A: Cataloging the EMT/EPT outputs with lineage back to their fund-data and cost-data inputs lets you trace exactly which sources feed each regulated field, so when a field is wrong you can find the upstream cause fast. It also evidences to regulators that you control the EMT/EPT production chain. This is precisely the kind of end-to-end lineage where outside-Microsoft gaps would hurt.

Q: Why is "nobody maintains it" listed alongside "can't see half your estate" as the two ways a catalog fails?
A: Because these are the two distinct failure modes: incomplete coverage (a connector problem) and abandonment (a stewardship problem). Either alone makes the catalog untrustworthy. The lesson pairs them because evaluating only features blinds you to both, and both are people-and-process realities that demos never reveal.

Q: How does running OpenMetadata beside Purview help with Purview's lineage weakness?
A: OpenMetadata, with broad open connectors and OpenLineage support, can capture lineage for the non-Microsoft pipelines where Purview is thin, and that lineage can complement Purview's Microsoft-native coverage. The open catalog fills the exact gap Purview leaves. The coexistence cost is keeping the two views reconciled rather than divergent.

Q: When recommending coexistence, how do you avoid it becoming "two catalogs nobody trusts"?
A: Assign each catalog a clear scope and system-of-record role per metadata type, automate synchronisation where they overlap, and forbid duplicate manual curation so a fact is edited in one place only. Clear ownership and automated sync are what separate deliberate coexistence from accidental sprawl. Without them, coexistence is just two competing sources of truth.

Q: What is the right sequence of artefacts to produce for the catalog decision in this lesson's "Do" list?
A: First the connector-coverage table across OpenMetadata, Purview, and Collibra for your estate; second a TCO comparison sized for a 300-person fund administrator; third the vendor-selection memo with a clear recommendation. Evidence precedes recommendation. The memo's credibility rests on the coverage table and TCO that came before it.

Q: Why does the connector-coverage table include OpenMetadata alongside the two enterprise catalogs?
A: Because the open catalog is the realistic coexistence or fallback option, and including it shows whether an enterprise catalog's connector gaps could be filled by open connectors instead. It reframes the decision from "which one" to "which mix covers my estate at acceptable cost." OpenMetadata's broad open connectors often expose how narrow an enterprise catalog's real coverage is.

Q: How should you handle a source that no candidate catalog connects to natively?
A: Plan to push its metadata in via the catalog's API or a custom ingestion (for Purview, the Apache Atlas-compatible API; for Collibra, an Edge custom capability or import), and account for that build-and-maintain cost in TCO. A missing connector is not fatal but is never free. Pricing that custom work honestly is exactly what stops a connector gap from ambushing the project later.

Q: Why is the underlying metadata model (Apache Atlas heritage) relevant when extending Purview?
A: Purview's Data Map exposes an Apache Atlas-compatible API, so you can push custom asset and lineage metadata for sources lacking a native connector. Knowing this gives you a concrete escape hatch for coverage gaps. It also means Atlas-format lineage from other tools can be ingested rather than re-implemented.

Q: A demo shows automatic lineage in Purview for a Power BI report. Why might you not get the same for a Tableau report?
A: Automatic lineage is richest for Microsoft-native services like Power BI; non-Microsoft BI tools such as Tableau often need lineage pushed in manually via the API or a third-party connector. So the in-estate demo overstates what you'll get for the rest of the stack. This is the outside-Microsoft lineage gap in concrete form — verify it against your actual BI tools.

Q: What governance benefit does linking glossary terms to physical assets provide during a fund-data audit?
A: It lets an auditor trace a business term like "NAV cut-off" directly to the physical columns and pipelines that implement it, proving the firm knows where its key concepts live and how they are produced. The links convert abstract definitions into verifiable data lineage. Unlinked glossaries, by contrast, are just dictionaries that prove nothing about the real data.

Q: Why might a fund admin with mostly on-premises legacy systems lean toward Collibra over Purview?
A: Because Collibra Edge and its broader enterprise connector ecosystem are built to reach on-premises and mixed estates, whereas Purview's strongest automation favours the Azure/M365 stack. A heavily legacy, non-Azure estate is exactly where Purview's coverage and lineage thin out. The connector-coverage table would make this concrete before the decision.

Q: What is the trap in choosing a catalog because it has "the most connectors"?
A: A long connector count is meaningless if those connectors don't cover your specific sources, versions, and auth methods — coverage of your estate matters, not the catalog of available connectors. A product can advertise hundreds of connectors and still miss your one critical legacy system. Always map connectors to your actual sources rather than counting them.

Q: How does the lesson's guidance change if you are inheriting a catalog versus selecting one?
A: When selecting, you run the full coverage/TCO/memo exercise to choose; when inheriting, you instead assess the existing deployment's coverage gaps, stewardship health, and configuration debt and decide whether to fix, extend (via coexistence), or replace it. Inheritance shifts the question from "which product" to "make this one deliver." Many real engagements are inheritance, which is why the lesson stresses judging past the demo.

Q: Why is "produce a memo a sponsor could act on" an explicit done-when criterion?
A: Because the architect's deliverable is a decision a non-technical sponsor can fund and defend, not a technical comparison only an engineer understands. The memo must lead with a recommendation, justify it with coverage and TCO evidence, and name the risks. If the sponsor can't act on it, the analysis — however correct — has failed its purpose.

Q: How would Purview's data-quality capability appear in a fund-data governance programme, and what is the cost implication?
A: You can define data-quality rules in the Unified Catalog (for example completeness or validity checks on NAV inputs) that run on DGPU compute, surfacing quality scores against governed assets. The cost implication is that quality processing consumes DGPUs at the chosen tier, so widespread rules raise the bill. You scope quality rules to the assets that matter rather than running them everywhere.

Q: What does "data health" mean in Purview and why is it a useful governance metric?
A: Data health is an aggregate view of how well-governed and high-quality your data estate is — combining curation completeness, quality results, and issue resolution — so you can track governance progress over time. It turns governance into a measurable outcome rather than an activity. For a fund admin it gives management a dashboard answer to "is our critical data actually trustworthy?"

Q: Why does the lesson list lineage and cost as Purview's gaps but implementation weight as Collibra's gap?
A: Because the products fail differently: Purview's risk is incomplete coverage outside Microsoft plus an unfamiliar cost model, while Collibra's risk is that its power demands heavy, sustained implementation effort. Knowing each product's characteristic weakness lets you probe exactly the right thing in evaluation. You stress-test Purview on coverage/cost and Collibra on whether anyone will configure and maintain it.

Q: If a sponsor wants one catalog for both engineering and business governance, what tension must you flag?
A: That engineering teams want broad automated connectivity and lineage (favouring open tools), while business governance wants curated stewardship and audit (favouring an enterprise catalog), and few single products excel at both. Forcing one tool risks underserving one audience. This tension is exactly why coexistence — open catalog plus enterprise catalog — is often the pragmatic answer.

Q: What single litmus test reveals whether a catalog deployment is actually maintained rather than abandoned?
A: Check whether stewardship workflows are running and recent — recent certifications, resolved issues, fresh scans, and active owners — versus a catalog whose newest activity is months old. Maintenance shows up as activity, not as the existence of features. An abandoned catalog looks identical to a healthy one on a feature list but very different on its activity log.

Q: Why does the lesson frame Purview and Collibra as "the buy side of the catalog decision" rather than a tooling exercise?
A: Because as an architect you rarely build a catalog from scratch — you evaluate vendors or inherit an existing deployment, so the skill being taught is procurement and judgement, not implementation. The decision commits budget, headcount, and years of stewardship, which is a business choice. Framing it as a buy-side decision keeps the focus on connector coverage, stewardship, and TCO rather than on which product has the prettier UI.

Q: Pulling it together: state in one sentence the buy-side lesson an architect must take from comparing Purview and Collibra.
A: Choose and judge an enterprise catalog on whether it can actually connect to your real estate and whether your organisation will sustain stewardship over it — connector coverage plus stewardship workflow — because features are nearly identical and everything else, including cost and credibility, follows from those two.
