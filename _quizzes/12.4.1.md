# Phase 8 · 12.4.1 Data SLAs & SLO management — 100 self-test questions

Q: What is the difference between an SLI, an SLO, and an SLA in the SRE vocabulary?
A: An SLI (Service Level Indicator) is a measured quantity, such as the fraction of business days the NAV was fresh by 08:00 CET; an SLO (Service Level Objective) is the target you commit to for that SLI, such as `99%`; and an SLA (Service Level Agreement) is the contract with consequences if the SLO is missed. The SLI is the ruler, the SLO is the line on the ruler you promise to stay above, and the SLA is what happens when you fall below it. In a data product the SLO becomes contractual the moment you publish it on the product page and encode it in the ODCS contract.

Q: Why does the lesson say an SLO turns a contract's "fresh by 08:00 CET" from marketing copy into something operational?
A: A stated freshness like "fresh by 08:00 CET" is just a sentence until you measure it; without an SLI you cannot say whether you kept the promise. Measured SLOs with an error budget turn the promise into a number you can track day by day and alert on, so the first missed NAV deadline becomes a documented burn-rate alert with a response rather than finger-pointing. The mechanism is measurement: you cannot manage, defend, or breach what you do not instrument.

Q: Define an SLI in the context of a data product rather than a request-serving web service.
A: A data SLI is a measurable proxy for some dimension of data quality or availability that consumers care about, typically expressed as good events over total events. For data the events are often pipeline runs, partitions, or rows rather than HTTP requests, so a freshness SLI might be "share of business days the gold table landed by the deadline" rather than "share of requests served under 200ms." The key is that it must be objectively measurable from telemetry, not a subjective judgement.

Q: What are the four data SLI families this lesson asks you to learn?
A: The lesson names freshness, completeness, accuracy proxies, and serving-layer availability as the measurable indicators. Freshness asks whether the data arrived in time, completeness asks whether all expected records are present, accuracy proxies stand in for correctness you cannot directly measure, and serving-layer availability asks whether consumers can actually read the product when they query it. Together they cover "is it here, is all of it here, is it right, and can I get to it."

Q: How would you define a freshness SLI for a daily NAV feed?
A: Freshness measures the lag between when data is expected and when it actually lands, so the SLI is the fraction of business days the NAV gold table is fully populated by the contractual deadline, e.g. 08:00 CET. You compute it as good days (landed on time) divided by total business days over a window, which gives a percentage you can hold an SLO against. A common gotcha is measuring wall-clock arrival of the first row instead of completion of the full partition — freshness should fire on "complete and queryable," not "started loading."

Q: What is a completeness SLI and how does it differ from freshness?
A: Completeness measures whether all the records you expected are actually present, independent of when they arrived. For a fund product it is naturally "share of active share classes that have a NAV row for the valuation date" — freshness can be green while completeness is red if the table landed on time but three share classes are missing. They answer different questions: freshness is about timeliness, completeness is about coverage, and a strong contract instruments both.

Q: Why are "accuracy proxies" used as an SLI instead of measuring accuracy directly?
A: True accuracy requires a trusted source of ground truth (was this NAV the genuinely correct price?), which you usually do not have at serving time, so you measure proxies that correlate with correctness. Examples include the share of NAVs within an expected day-over-day move tolerance, the rate of rows passing validation rules, or reconciliation match rates against an independent calculation. The proxy is honest about its limits: it catches gross errors and drift, but a "passing" proxy is not a guarantee of correctness.

Q: Give two concrete accuracy-proxy SLIs you could compute for a NAV product.
A: One proxy is the percentage of share-class NAVs whose day-over-day percentage change falls inside an expected band (flagging a fat-fingered price or a corporate-action miss), and another is the reconciliation match rate between the published NAV and an independently recomputed NAV. A third would be the share of rows passing structural validation such as positive NAV, valid `ISIN`, and a populated valuation date. Each is a measurable stand-in for "the numbers are plausibly right" without claiming to prove correctness.

Q: What does "serving-layer availability" mean as a data SLI?
A: It measures whether consumers can actually reach and query the served product, treating the gold view, API, or warehouse endpoint like any service whose uptime matters. The SLI is typically the fraction of query attempts (or probe checks) that succeed within a latency bound over a window. It is distinct from freshness and completeness: the data can be perfectly fresh and complete yet useless if the serving endpoint is down when the board-pack analyst queries it at 08:15.

Q: How do you turn a raw SLI definition into a number you can store daily?
A: You fix a measurement window (often one business day), define which events count as "good" versus "valid total," and compute the ratio, then persist that day's value so attainment accumulates over time. For freshness that is good days over business days; for completeness it is present share classes over expected share classes for that valuation date. Storing the daily value is what lets you later compute rolling 28-day or quarterly attainment and chart a trend instead of a single snapshot.

Q: What is the danger of declaring "vanity nines" when setting an SLO?
A: A vanity SLO is a high target chosen because it sounds impressive (five nines, 99.999%) rather than because any consumer needs it, and it is both expensive to hit and dishonest. Every extra nine costs disproportionately more engineering and leaves almost no error budget, so you spend heavily protecting a margin nobody uses. The fix is to derive the target from a named consumer need so it is justified, achievable, and defensible.

Q: According to the lesson, how should you actually set the right SLO target?
A: You interview the consumers — using business-analyst skills — to learn what they genuinely need, instead of picking a round number from the air. The board-pack analyst, distributor ops, and the regulatory deadline owner each have a real tolerance for lateness or missing data, and the SLO should reflect the tightest genuine need, not the most flattering figure. The deliverable is an SLO whose every digit traces back to a stated requirement.

Q: Why is "interview the consumer" framed as a business-analyst skill in an SRE topic?
A: Because the hardest part of an SLO is not the maths but eliciting the real requirement: consumers will say "as fast as possible" until you ask what breaks if it is late. BA techniques — open questions, scenario walk-throughs, separating wants from needs — surface the actual decision deadline (e.g., "the board pack must be printed by 09:00, so NAV must be queryable by 08:00"). That elicited deadline becomes the SLO target, which is why the skill belongs here.

Q: The "Do" step asks you to interview yourself as three consumers — name them and what each cares about.
A: The three are the board-pack analyst, distributor ops, and the regulatory deadline owner. The board-pack analyst needs the NAV queryable in time to assemble the morning board pack; distributor ops needs complete share-class data to feed downstream platforms and EMT/EPT distribution; and the regulatory deadline owner cares about meeting a hard filing or reporting cut-off. Each implies a different SLI emphasis — timeliness, completeness, and a non-negotiable hard deadline respectively.

Q: How might the board-pack analyst's need translate into a specific freshness SLO?
A: If the board pack must be finalised by 09:00 CET and assembling it takes an hour, the analyst needs NAV queryable by 08:00 CET, so the freshness SLO becomes "99% of business days fresh by 08:00 CET." The target percentage reflects how often a one-off late day can be absorbed by manual workaround versus how often it genuinely breaks the board meeting. The point is the digits come from the analyst's downstream deadline, not from convention.

Q: How might distributor ops drive a completeness SLO rather than a freshness one?
A: Distributor ops feeds every active share class to downstream platforms, so a single missing share class is a broken feed regardless of timing, which pushes completeness toward a hard target like "100% of active share classes present." Here a 99% completeness target would be unacceptable because the missing 1% is always somebody's fund. This is a case where a justified SLO is genuinely 100%, and that is defensible precisely because the consumer need demands it.

Q: Why might a regulatory deadline owner justify a stricter SLO than the others?
A: A regulatory filing often has a legally fixed cut-off with penalties, so the owner cannot absorb lateness the way an analyst might with a manual workaround. That hard external consequence justifies a tighter freshness SLO and a smaller error budget for that consumer's slice, because the cost of a breach is regulatory exposure under regimes like UCITS, AIFMD, or DORA-driven operational-resilience expectations. The named regulatory consequence is the justification for the stricter number.

Q: What is an error budget and how is it derived from an SLO?
A: An error budget is the amount of failure you are allowed before you breach the SLO, computed as one minus the SLO over the measurement window. A 99% freshness SLO over a 20-business-day month gives a 1% budget, which is 0.2 of a day — effectively zero late days that month — whereas 99% over a quarter of ~65 business days allows roughly 0.65 of a day. The budget reframes reliability from "never fail" to "you may fail this much, and here is how much you have left."

Q: Compute the error budget for "99% of business days fresh" over a 250-business-day year.
A: One minus 0.99 is 0.01, and 0.01 times 250 business days is 2.5 business days of allowable lateness per year. That means the freshness SLO tolerates roughly two-and-a-half late days annually before it is breached, which is the budget you spend down. If you burn a full late day in January you have about 1.5 days of budget left for the remaining eleven months, which is exactly the kind of pressure the budget is meant to make visible.

Q: What is the core operational purpose of an error budget for a data pipeline?
A: It governs the trade-off between shipping changes and protecting reliability: as long as budget remains you can take risks like deploying a new transformation, and when the budget is exhausted you freeze risky changes until reliability recovers. This turns "should we deploy?" from an argument into a policy keyed to a number. It aligns the team because everyone reads the same remaining-budget figure rather than debating gut feelings about stability.

Q: In this lesson, what happens when the error budget is burned?
A: A burned budget freezes risky pipeline changes — you stop deploying speculative transformations, schema changes, or new sources until attainment recovers and budget is restored. The change-freeze is the concrete error-budget response the "Do" step asks you to document during the synthetic bad week. The freeze is not punishment; it is a mechanical brake that redirects effort from features to reliability exactly when reliability is suffering.

Q: What is a "change-freeze note" and when do you write one?
A: A change-freeze note is the documented record that, because the error budget was exhausted, risky changes to the pipeline are paused until reliability is restored. You write it as the error-budget response when a bad week consumes the budget, and it names what is frozen, why (budget burned to zero), and the condition for lifting the freeze. It is the audit-friendly artifact that proves the SLO drove a real operational decision rather than sitting on a dashboard.

Q: Why is alerting on every individual late pipeline run a poor strategy?
A: Paging on each point failure floods responders with noise, treats a single blip the same as a sustained outage, and causes alert fatigue so real incidents get ignored. A low-traffic data product makes this worse: one failure in ten can look like a 10% error rate that is actually negligible in real terms. Burn-rate alerting fixes this by reacting to how fast the error budget is being consumed, not to any single run.

Q: What is burn rate, precisely?
A: Burn rate is how fast you are consuming the error budget relative to the rate that would exactly exhaust it over the SLO window — a burn rate of 1 spends the entire budget exactly at the end of the window, while a burn rate of 14.4 would exhaust it about 14 times faster. It is the velocity of failure, not the count of failures. Alerting on burn rate lets you tie alert severity to how urgently a human must respond.

Q: At burn rate 1, how long until the error budget is fully consumed for a 30-day SLO window?
A: A burn rate of exactly 1 means you are consuming budget at precisely the rate that would deplete it over the full SLO window, so it takes the whole 30 days. By definition, sustained burn rate 1 spends 100% of the budget right as the window closes — it is the boundary between "on track" and "overspending." Anything above 1 exhausts the budget early; anything below 1 leaves budget to spare.

Q: What does a burn rate of 14.4 signify in the SRE Workbook's recommended fast-burn alert?
A: A burn rate of 14.4 means you are spending budget about 14.4 times faster than sustainable, which for a 30-day window consumes roughly 2% of the entire month's budget in just one hour. That is fast enough to justify paging a human immediately, because at that pace the whole month's budget would be gone in about two days. It is the canonical fast-burn threshold paired with a 1-hour long window in the Workbook example.

Q: What are the three recommended multiwindow multi-burn-rate alert tiers from SRE Workbook chapter 5 (for a 99.9% SLO)?
A: The Workbook recommends three tiers: a page at burn rate 14.4 over a 1-hour long window (consuming ~2% of budget), a page at burn rate 6 over a 6-hour long window (~5% of budget), and a ticket at burn rate 1 over a 3-day long window (~10% of budget). The fast tiers page humans for urgent burns; the slow tier files a ticket for a grinding low-grade leak. Each tier is paired with a short confirmation window.

Q: Why does each burn-rate alert use a long window AND a short window?
A: The long window establishes that a meaningful amount of budget is being burned (so a brief blip does not fire), while the short window confirms the burn is still happening right now so the alert resets promptly once the problem stops. Without the short window an alert based on a 6-hour average could keep firing for hours after recovery. The pairing gives you both sensitivity to real sustained burns and a quick reset, dramatically reducing stale pages.

Q: What ratio does the SRE Workbook suggest between the short and long alert windows?
A: It suggests making the short window roughly 1/12 of the long window. So a 1-hour long window pairs with a 5-minute short window, a 6-hour long window pairs with a 30-minute short window, and a 3-day long window pairs with a 6-hour short window. The 1/12 ratio keeps the short window long enough to be statistically meaningful yet short enough to reset the alert quickly after recovery.

Q: For the fast-burn page in the Workbook example, what are the long and short windows and the budget consumed?
A: The fast-burn page uses a 1-hour long window with a 5-minute short window at burn rate 14.4, which corresponds to consuming about 2% of the 30-day error budget within that hour. The 5-minute short window is 1/12 of the hour, so the page clears within minutes of recovery. This tier exists to catch a sudden hard failure — like the whole NAV load aborting — fast enough to act before the morning deadline.

Q: For the slow-burn tier, what are the windows, burn rate, and why is it a ticket not a page?
A: The slow-burn tier uses a 3-day long window with a 6-hour short window at burn rate 1, consuming about 10% of the budget, and it files a ticket rather than paging because the burn is slow enough that nobody needs to be woken up. A grinding 1% completeness shortfall every day will not breach the SLO this hour, but left unattended it erodes the budget over weeks. The ticket ensures someone investigates the slow leak during business hours.

Q: Why is burn-rate alerting described as giving "appropriate priority based on how quickly you have to respond"?
A: Because the burn rate directly encodes urgency: a high burn rate means the budget will be gone soon so a human must respond immediately (page), while a low burn rate means there is time so a ticket suffices. This maps alert severity onto real-world consequence instead of treating every failure as equally urgent. The result is fewer, better-targeted alerts that respect responders' attention.

Q: A single NAV run fails at 02:00 but the rerun succeeds by 03:00, well before the 08:00 deadline. Should a well-designed burn-rate alert have paged?
A: Probably not — the freshness SLI for that day is still met because the data was fresh by the deadline, so almost no budget was consumed and the fast-burn page should not fire. Alerting on the individual failed run would have paged needlessly; burn-rate alerting tolerates a transient that self-heals within budget. This is precisely the noise-suppression benefit: the alert tracks the outcome consumers feel, not every internal hiccup.

Q: The NAV load has missed the 08:00 deadline three business days in a row. Which alert tier is most likely to fire and why?
A: Three consecutive missed deadlines is a sustained, fast burn of the freshness budget, so the fast-burn page (1-hour/5-minute, burn rate 14.4) and likely the medium tier (6-hour/30-minute, burn rate 6) will fire because budget is being consumed far faster than sustainable. Three late days against a 1% monthly budget almost certainly breaches the SLO outright. The pages demand immediate human response, and the burned budget triggers a change freeze.

Q: Where should measured SLO attainment be surfaced according to this lesson?
A: Attainment should be published in two places: on the data product's page in the catalog, so consumers can self-serve and see whether the product is meeting its promises, and inside the ODCS contract's SLA block as the formal contractual statement. The catalog page is the human-facing shop window; the contract is the machine-readable agreement. Surfacing in both keeps the marketing claim and the contractual commitment in sync.

Q: What is the ODCS, and why does this lesson reference it for SLOs?
A: ODCS is the Open Data Contract Standard, a machine-readable YAML standard (maintained under the Bitol project) for describing a data product's schema, quality, and service levels. This lesson references it because the SLA block is exactly where freshness, completeness, and availability SLOs are formally encoded so producers and consumers share one authoritative definition. Encoding the SLO in ODCS is what makes "fresh by 08:00 CET" a contract term rather than a slide.

Q: Which top-level ODCS key holds the list of SLA commitments?
A: The `slaProperties` key holds a list of key/value pairs for SLA-specific properties, and per the spec there is no fixed limit on the property types. Each entry describes one commitment such as latency or frequency. The older `slaDefaultElement` key, which named a default element to check, is deprecated since ODCS 3.1 and slated for removal in 4.0, so new contracts should set the element per property instead.

Q: What sub-fields does a single ODCS `slaProperties` entry have?
A: An SLA property entry has `property` (the named metric, e.g. latency, drawn from the spec's periodic table of properties), `value` (the agreement value), `valueExt` (an extended/second value), `unit` (ISO-style units like `d` for days), `element` (the element path the check applies to), and `driver`. The `driver` states the importance of the SLA. Together these let one YAML entry fully specify a measurable commitment against a specific column or table.

Q: What values can the ODCS SLA `driver` field take, and what does it signify?
A: The `driver` field accepts `regulatory`, `analytics`, or `operational`, and it describes the importance and motivation of the SLA commitment. A freshness SLA backing a filing deadline would carry `driver: regulatory`, a board-pack timeliness SLA might be `operational`, and a downstream-reporting freshness might be `analytics`. The driver makes the consumer need explicit in the contract, which directly supports the lesson's rule that every SLO be justified by a named need.

Q: Sketch an ODCS `slaProperties` entry expressing "NAV fresh within a deadline."
A: It would be an entry under `slaProperties` with `property: latency`, a `value` and `unit` such as `value: 8` and `unit: h` (or an absolute time expressed via the spec's conventions), an `element` pointing at the NAV table or valuation-time column, and `driver: operational` or `regulatory`. The exact property name comes from the ODCS periodic table of properties; the point is that freshness is encoded as a structured, machine-readable field, not prose. A reviewer can then validate the contract programmatically.

Q: Why is encoding the SLO in the contract better than only showing it on the product page?
A: The product page is human-facing and easy to let drift, whereas the ODCS contract is machine-readable, version-controlled, and can be validated and enforced in CI, so consumers can programmatically onboard to and check the commitment. Encoding it once in the contract and rendering the same value on the page keeps a single source of truth. A gotcha to avoid is publishing a generous number on the page while the contract says something stricter — they must reconcile.

Q: What does the "Done when" checklist mean by "a simulated bad week visibly consuming error budget"?
A: It means you replay synthetic history that includes a deliberately bad week, and you can show the daily attainment dipping and the remaining error budget being drawn down on the product page or dashboard. The visible drawdown proves your instrumentation actually reflects failures rather than always showing green. Paired with a documented response (the change-freeze note), it demonstrates the full SLO loop: measure, breach, alert, respond.

Q: The "Do" step says replay "one month of synthetic history including one bad week" — why synthetic and why a bad week?
A: Synthetic history lets you control the scenario and prove the whole machinery works without waiting a real month or risking production, and the deliberately bad week guarantees you exercise the failure path: attainment dropping, the burn-rate alert firing, and the error-budget response. If you only replayed good data you would never see the alert fire or the budget burn. The bad week is the test case that validates your alerting and freeze policy end to end.

Q: What three things should the synthetic replay record as evidence?
A: It should record the measured attainment over the month, the burn-rate alert firing during the bad week, and the error-budget response — specifically the change-freeze note triggered by the burned budget. These three are the proof points that the SLO is real: you measured it, the alert detected the breach, and a documented operational decision followed. Collecting them is what turns the exercise into demonstrable evidence rather than a claim.

Q: Justify why "99% of business days fresh by 08:00 CET" is a defensible SLO target.
A: It is defensible only if it traces to a named consumer need — for example the board-pack analyst's 09:00 deadline requiring NAV by 08:00, with the 99% reflecting how often a manual workaround can absorb a late day. The 99% is not a round number for its own sake; it is the point where the residual late-day risk is tolerable to that consumer. If you cannot name the consumer and the consequence, you cannot defend the digit.

Q: Justify why "100% of active share classes present" is a sensible completeness SLO for distributor ops.
A: Because distributor ops feeds every active share class downstream, any missing share class is a broken feed for some real fund, so the consumer genuinely needs full coverage rather than 99%. The 100% target is justified by the consumer need — there is no acceptable subset to drop — which is exactly the lesson's rule that targets come from needs. It is one of the rare cases where 100% is correct rather than a vanity number, precisely because the consequence of any gap is concrete.

Q: How do error budgets help an architect resist pressure to ship a risky change before a NAV release?
A: The budget gives an objective gate: if budget remains, the change can proceed with the residual risk it implies; if budget is exhausted, the change-freeze policy blocks it regardless of who is asking. This replaces a political argument with a number everyone agreed to in advance. It protects both reliability and the architect, because the freeze is policy, not a personal veto.

Q: What is the relationship between this lesson's SLO machinery and Phase 5's SLO vocabulary?
A: Phase 5 introduced the SLI/SLO/error-budget vocabulary as concepts; this lesson makes them contractual by tying them to a real data product, encoding them in the ODCS SLA block, and instrumenting them from the Phase-5/6 telemetry you already built. The progression is from learning the words to operationalising them with measured attainment and burn-rate alerts. The "Do" steps explicitly reuse your existing telemetry to compute the SLIs.

Q: What telemetry would you instrument to compute a freshness SLI day by day?
A: You need a per-day record of when the gold partition for the valuation date became fully populated and queryable, compared against the contractual deadline, which you derive from pipeline run timestamps and partition-completion events in your Phase-5/6 telemetry. Each day yields a boolean "met deadline," and the SLI is the rolling fraction of met days. The gotcha is to timestamp completion of the full load, not the first arriving row, so freshness reflects what consumers can actually query.

Q: Why store daily attainment rather than only the current rolling SLO?
A: Storing each day's value lets you recompute attainment over any window (28-day, monthly, quarterly), chart trends, and reconstruct exactly when budget was burned, which you need for the bad-week replay and for justifying a change freeze after the fact. A single live rolling number hides history and cannot be audited. The persisted daily series is the evidence base for both alerting and contractual reporting.

Q: A consumer complains the product "feels unreliable" but every freshness check is green. Which SLI should you suspect is missing?
A: Most likely completeness or serving-layer availability — the data lands on time (freshness green) but share classes are missing, or the serving endpoint times out when they query, neither of which a freshness-only SLI captures. The complaint signals you have instrumented only one dimension of quality. The fix is to add the SLI that matches the felt pain, since "reliable" to a consumer spans timeliness, coverage, correctness, and reachability.

Q: How does serving-layer availability interact with freshness for a consumer?
A: They are independent and both must hold for the consumer to be served: freshness guarantees the right data exists in time, while serving-layer availability guarantees the consumer can actually reach it. A product can be perfectly fresh yet unavailable if the API or warehouse endpoint is down, or available yet stale if loads are late. Consumers experience the conjunction, so a complete contract carries SLOs on both.

Q: Why might you set different SLO windows (monthly vs quarterly) for different SLIs?
A: The window should match how the consumer experiences and tolerates failure: a freshness deadline that matters every single business day argues for a tight monthly or rolling window, while a slow-moving accuracy proxy might be assessed quarterly. The window also shapes the error budget size, since the same percentage over a longer window allows more absolute failures. You pick the window that makes the budget reflect real tolerance.

Q: What is "alert fatigue" and how does multi-burn-rate alerting reduce it?
A: Alert fatigue is the desensitisation that happens when responders receive too many low-value alerts and start ignoring them, so a real incident gets missed. Multi-burn-rate alerting reduces it by only paging when budget is being consumed fast enough to matter and by resetting quickly via the short window, while routing slow leaks to tickets instead of pages. Fewer, more meaningful alerts keep responders attentive when it counts.

Q: How does a low-volume data product make naive failure-rate alerting misleading?
A: With few events, a single failure produces a large apparent error rate — one bad run out of ten is "10% errors" — so naive threshold alerts page for negligible real impact. Data pipelines are often low-volume (one NAV load per day), making this acute. Burn-rate alerting sidesteps it by measuring budget-consumption velocity over a window, which contextualises a single failure against the SLO rather than reacting to its raw rate.

Q: What is the difference between an SLO breach and an error budget being merely consumed?
A: Consuming budget is normal and expected — every late day or missing record spends some budget — whereas a breach is when you have consumed all of it and dropped below the SLO target for the window. The budget exists precisely so that ordinary, bounded failures do not count as breaking the promise. The change-freeze response is triggered by exhaustion (breach risk), not by every increment of consumption.

Q: Why does the lesson tie burn-rate alert design specifically to the freshness SLO in the "Do" step?
A: Freshness is the SLI with the hardest, most consumer-visible deadline (NAV by 08:00 CET), so a fast burn of freshness budget — repeated late loads — is the failure most worth paging on quickly. Adding a fast-plus-slow burn-rate alert there demonstrates the technique on the SLO that matters most operationally. It is the concrete, high-stakes case where catching a fast burn before the deadline has real value.

Q: When you publish SLO attainment on the catalog product page, what should a consumer be able to learn at a glance?
A: A consumer should see the current rolling attainment versus the target (e.g. 99.4% fresh vs 99% target), the remaining error budget, and ideally a trend so they can judge whether the product is healthy or degrading. This lets them self-assess fitness for their use before relying on the data. The page turns the SLO from an internal metric into a transparent promise consumers can verify.

Q: What is the risk of setting an SLO target equal to your current best-ever performance?
A: Setting the target at your peak leaves essentially no error budget, so any ordinary variation breaches it, triggers freezes, and burns out the team — you have committed to never having a bad day. SLO targets should sit below typical achievable performance, leaving budget for normal failures and planned risk. The target encodes "good enough for the consumer," not "the best we ever managed."

Q: How would you respond if a consumer demands 100% freshness?
A: You interview to find the real cost of a late day; usually 100% is a reflex, and the genuine need is "fresh by the deadline almost always, with a known manual fallback for the rare miss," which maps to 99% or 99.5% rather than 100%. A literal 100% leaves zero budget, forbids all risk, and is usually unaffordable. If a hard regulatory deadline truly tolerates zero misses, you document that named need and price the cost — but you do not accept 100% by default.

Q: What does "error budget policy" mean and why write it down before a bad week?
A: An error budget policy is the pre-agreed rule for what happens at various budget levels — most importantly, that exhausting the budget freezes risky changes — and writing it before a bad week removes the temptation to negotiate the response under pressure. Deciding the freeze rule in calm conditions makes it a neutral mechanism rather than a fight during an incident. The change-freeze note you produce in the replay is the policy being applied.

Q: A burn-rate alert fired overnight, but by morning attainment is back within target. Was the alert wrong?
A: Not necessarily — the long window detected a genuine fast burn during the incident, and the short window let it reset once the burn stopped, which is the design working as intended. A fired-then-cleared alert that prompted (or would have prompted) a look is correct behaviour, not a false positive. You only treat it as misconfigured if it fired with no meaningful budget consumed at all, which would point to too-sensitive thresholds.

Q: How does the `driver: regulatory` tag on an SLA property change how you treat a breach?
A: A `regulatory` driver signals the SLA backs a compliance obligation, so a breach carries regulatory exposure (e.g. a missed UCITS/AIFMD reporting deadline or a DORA operational-resilience concern), which usually warrants a tighter SLO, a stricter freeze, and formal incident reporting. Tagging the driver in the ODCS contract makes that stakes-level explicit to anyone reading the contract. It elevates the breach from an internal metric miss to a compliance event.

Q: Why is it useful that ODCS SLA `unit` follows ISO conventions like `d` for days?
A: ISO-style units make the contract unambiguous and machine-parseable, so tooling and different consumers interpret `value: 8, unit: h` identically without guessing whether "8" means hours, days, or business days. Standard units prevent the classic integration bug where one side reads a number in the wrong unit. It is part of what makes the SLA block a genuine machine-readable contract rather than free text.

Q: What is the deprecation status of `slaDefaultElement` in ODCS, and what should you do instead?
A: `slaDefaultElement` is deprecated since ODCS 3.1 and is slated for removal in ODCS 4.0; it named a single default element to run checks against. Instead you should specify the `element` on each individual `slaProperties` entry, which is more explicit and survives the deprecation. Writing a new contract against the default element would create rework when 4.0 lands.

Q: How do you decide whether a freshness shortfall should page or just file a ticket?
A: You let the burn rate decide: a fast burn (the deadline missed hard, budget draining within an hour) trips the fast-burn page tier, while a slow grind (a small recurring lateness that will only breach over days) trips the slow-burn ticket tier. The severity follows urgency, not the mere existence of a shortfall. This is exactly why the multi-burn-rate design pairs an immediate page tier with a business-hours ticket tier.

Q: What does it mean that an SLI must be a ratio of "good events" to "valid events," not just a raw count?
A: Expressing the SLI as a ratio normalises it so the SLO is comparable across days regardless of volume — 100% of 12 share classes present is as meaningful as 100% of 400. A raw count of failures cannot anchor a percentage SLO or an error budget, which are defined as fractions. The ratio also makes burn rate computable, since budget is a fraction of total valid events.

Q: How would you instrument a completeness SLI for "all active share classes present" daily?
A: You join the expected set of active share classes for the valuation date (the denominator) against the share classes that actually have a NAV row (the numerator), and store the ratio as that day's completeness SLI. The subtlety is sourcing an authoritative, dated list of active share classes, since funds open and close classes over time, so "active on this valuation date" must be point-in-time correct. A stale master of active classes will silently corrupt the SLI.

Q: Why might freshness and completeness need separate error budgets rather than one combined one?
A: Because consumers tolerate their failures differently — distributor ops may accept a slightly late but complete feed yet never accept a missing share class, so completeness needs a near-zero budget while freshness can carry a larger one. Combining them would let freshness budget mask completeness breaches or vice versa. Separate budgets keep each promise independently honest and independently alertable.

Q: What is the role of "interviewing yourself as three consumers" in the deliverable, methodologically?
A: It is a structured way to elicit distinct, named requirements without real consumers on hand, forcing you to articulate the board-pack analyst's, distributor ops's, and regulatory owner's actual deadlines and tolerances. Each persona yields a justified SLO with a traceable need, satisfying the "no vanity nines" rule. The exercise is a stand-in for the real BA interviews you would run in production.

Q: Why does the SRE Workbook recommend three alert tiers rather than just one fast-burn page?
A: One fast tier alone misses slow leaks that never breach within an hour but steadily erode the budget over days, and a single slow tier would react too late to a sudden hard failure. Three tiers cover the spectrum: catch sudden severe burns immediately (page), catch significant sustained burns soon (page), and catch slow grinding burns before the window closes (ticket). The layering balances fast detection against low noise.

Q: For a 99.9% SLO, roughly what fraction of the monthly budget does the burn-rate-6 page tier consume, and over what window?
A: The burn-rate-6 tier consumes about 5% of the 30-day error budget over its 6-hour long window (with a 30-minute short window), per the Workbook example. It pages because consuming a twentieth of the month's budget in six hours is a significant sustained burn that still warrants human attention, though slightly less acutely than the 14.4 tier. It sits between the 1-hour fast page and the 3-day slow ticket.

Q: How does encoding SLOs in a version-controlled ODCS contract support change management?
A: Because the contract lives in version control, any change to an SLO target is a reviewable diff with history, so tightening or loosening a promise is deliberate, attributable, and consumer-visible rather than a silent edit on a dashboard. Consumers can pin to a contract version and detect when their guarantees change. This ties SLO governance into the same evolution-policy discipline used for schema changes.

Q: What gotcha arises if your freshness SLI uses calendar days instead of business days?
A: Including weekends and holidays — when no NAV is expected — would either count non-business days as failures (deflating attainment unfairly) or as successes (inflating it), both of which distort the SLO and the budget. Fund valuation runs on a business-day calendar, often a specific market or Luxembourg holiday calendar, so the SLI denominator must be business days for that fund. Getting the calendar wrong is a classic source of misleading attainment.

Q: Why might two share classes of the same fund have different freshness SLOs?
A: They might serve different consumers or markets with different deadlines — for instance a share class feeding a regulatory filing has a harder cut-off (and tighter SLO) than one feeding an internal analytics dashboard. The SLO follows the consumer need, and consumers differ per share class. Tagging each with the appropriate ODCS `driver` (regulatory vs analytics vs operational) captures that difference in the contract.

Q: How do you avoid an SLO that is technically met but useless to consumers?
A: You derive the SLI and its deadline from the consumer's actual decision point — the board pack must be ready by 09:00, so measure "queryable by 08:00" — rather than an internal convenience like "loaded sometime today." An SLO measured at the wrong point (e.g. midnight UTC) can be green while consumers are still stranded at their deadline. Anchoring the SLI to the consumer's clock keeps it meaningful.

Q: What does the lesson mean by SLIs being "measurable indicators" — why stress measurable?
A: Measurable means the indicator must be computable objectively from telemetry, with no human judgement, so the SLO can be tracked, alerted, and audited consistently. "The data felt slow" is not an SLI; "fraction of business days complete by 08:00 CET" is. Insisting on measurability is what lets you build burn-rate alerts and publish defensible attainment rather than impressions.

Q: How would a burn-rate alert behave during the deliberately bad week of the replay?
A: As the bad week injects late loads and missing share classes, freshness and completeness budgets drain fast, so the short-and-long windows cross the burn-rate thresholds and the fast-burn page tier fires, demonstrating detection. The persisted attainment dips visibly and the remaining budget shrinks toward zero. Once the bad week ends and good data resumes, the short window lets the alert reset, leaving a recorded firing as evidence.

Q: Why must the change-freeze note name the condition for lifting the freeze?
A: A freeze without an exit condition risks becoming either permanent (blocking all progress) or arbitrary (lifted by whoever shouts loudest), so the note must state the objective restore condition — typically that attainment recovers and budget is rebuilt above a threshold. The stated condition keeps the freeze policy-driven and time-bounded. It also gives the team a concrete reliability target to work toward before resuming risky changes.

Q: What independent recomputation could back a NAV accuracy-proxy SLI in a fund-admin context?
A: A common approach is an independent shadow NAV calculation — recomputing NAV from positions, prices, and shares outstanding via a separate code path or oversight tool — and measuring the share of share-class NAVs that reconcile within tolerance to the published figure. The match rate is the accuracy proxy. It does not prove correctness absolutely, but a falling reconciliation rate is a strong early warning of a pricing or corporate-action error.

Q: How does the choice of SLO window affect how quickly a slow-burn ticket alert can warn you?
A: A longer SLO window yields a larger absolute budget, so a slow leak takes longer to threaten a breach, which means the 3-day slow-burn ticket has more runway to catch it before the window closes. A very short window leaves little budget, so even slow burns escalate fast and the slow tier may be redundant. You size the window and the slow-tier thresholds together so slow leaks are caught with time to respond.

Q: What is wrong with an SLO that has no associated error budget or burn-rate alert?
A: Without an error budget the SLO cannot govern the change-vs-reliability trade-off, and without burn-rate alerting you only discover a breach after the fact, at month-end, with no chance to react before the deadline. Such an SLO is a passive report card, not an operational control. The lesson's whole point is to couple the target with a budget and a burn-rate alert so the SLO drives decisions in real time.

Q: How would you explain to a non-technical fund manager why you alert on burn rate instead of "every time NAV is late"?
A: You would say that paging on every late run would mean crying wolf for transient hiccups that fix themselves before anyone is affected, while ignoring whether the lateness is a one-off or a worsening trend. Burn rate measures how fast you are using up the agreed allowance of late days, so you only raise the alarm when the trend genuinely threatens the deadline promise. That keeps responders trusted and reactions proportionate.

Q: Why is it important that the SLO target on the product page and in the ODCS contract match exactly?
A: If the page advertises a different (often more flattering) number than the contract enforces, consumers are misled and disputes follow when reality matches the contract but not the page. Keeping a single source of truth — ideally rendering the page from the contract — guarantees the human-facing claim and the machine-readable commitment agree. Divergence between marketing copy and contract is exactly the failure this lesson is built to prevent.

Q: What distinguishes a "page" alert from a "ticket" alert in burn-rate alerting policy?
A: A page demands immediate human response (it wakes someone) and is reserved for fast burns where the budget will be gone soon, while a ticket queues for business-hours investigation and is used for slow burns that erode budget without imminent breach. The distinction maps urgency to response channel. Mis-assigning them — paging on slow leaks or ticketing fast burns — reintroduces either fatigue or missed deadlines.

Q: If your completeness SLO is 100% and one share class is missing one day, what happens to the budget?
A: A 100% SLO has zero error budget by definition, so a single missing share class immediately breaches the SLO and exhausts the (nonexistent) budget, which under policy triggers the change-freeze response. That is the intended behaviour for a need that genuinely tolerates no gaps. It also means you must be confident the 100% is truly required, because every miss is a breach with no absorption.

Q: How does instrumenting SLIs from existing Phase-5/6 telemetry avoid building a parallel monitoring stack?
A: The pipeline run timestamps, partition-completion events, validation results, and serving probes you already emit contain everything needed to compute freshness, completeness, accuracy proxies, and availability, so you derive SLIs as queries over existing telemetry rather than new instrumentation. Reusing telemetry keeps one source of operational truth and avoids drift between monitoring systems. The "Do" step deliberately builds on what Phases 5 and 6 produced.

Q: A consumer asks for proof your product met its freshness promise last quarter. What do you show?
A: You show the persisted daily freshness attainment over the quarter, the rolling SLO figure versus the target, the error budget consumed, and any breach events with their documented responses — ideally rendered on the catalog product page and traceable to the ODCS contract's stated SLO. The stored daily series is what makes this auditable. Without persisted attainment you could only assert compliance, not evidence it.

Q: Why does the lesson frame missed deadlines as "a burn-rate alert with a documented response" rather than "finger-pointing"?
A: Because measured SLOs convert a missed NAV deadline from a blame argument into a defined operational event: the burn-rate alert detects it objectively, the error budget quantifies the impact, and the documented response (e.g. change freeze) shows what was done. Everyone reacts to the same numbers and the same playbook. The contrast highlights that the value of SLOs is procedural clarity, not just measurement.

Q: How could DORA expectations influence the SLOs you set on a NAV product's serving layer?
A: DORA (the EU Digital Operational Resilience Act) emphasises operational resilience and the ability to evidence it for financial entities, which pushes toward firm serving-layer availability SLOs, documented incident response, and demonstrable monitoring of critical functions. A NAV serving endpoint underpinning fund operations is a strong candidate for an availability SLO tagged `driver: regulatory`. The SLO and its evidence trail become part of resilience documentation rather than nice-to-have metrics.

Q: What is the consequence of choosing the wrong "valid total" denominator for an SLI?
A: If the denominator includes events that should not count (non-business days, retired share classes, test rows), the SLI is systematically distorted, making attainment and the error budget meaningless. For example counting deactivated share classes in completeness would deflate the ratio and burn budget for data that was never expected. Defining the valid total precisely — point-in-time correct and scoped to expected events — is as important as defining "good."

Q: How would you handle an SLO for a product whose consumers genuinely differ in tolerance?
A: You can either set the SLO to the strictest genuine need (protecting the most demanding consumer) or define per-consumer/per-element SLA properties in the contract, each with its own `driver` and target, so the regulatory consumer gets a tighter promise than the analytics consumer. ODCS supports multiple `slaProperties` entries scoped to elements, enabling this. The choice depends on whether the product is monolithic or can serve differentiated guarantees.

Q: Why is "fresh by 08:00 CET" expressed with an explicit timezone, and what breaks without it?
A: The timezone anchors the deadline to the consumers' actual operating clock (CET for a Luxembourg fund), and without it "08:00" is ambiguous across UTC, local server time, and consumer time, so the SLI could measure against the wrong instant. A pipeline timestamping completion in UTC while the SLO means CET will mis-measure freshness by an hour or two, including across daylight-saving shifts. Explicit timezone prevents silent off-by-hours errors in attainment.

Q: What single thing should you check first if your freshness attainment is unexpectedly stuck at 100%?
A: Check that the SLI is actually evaluating against the real deadline and a correct business-day calendar, because a constant 100% often means the comparison is misconfigured — for example measuring "loaded sometime today" instead of "by 08:00 CET," or counting only days that already passed. An always-green SLI hides breaches and defeats the whole point. Verify it can go red by replaying a known-late day, which the synthetic bad week does deliberately.

Q: What single thing should you check first if a burn-rate page fires but the dashboard shows attainment well within target?
A: First check the alert's window and threshold configuration against the SLO definition, because a page firing with healthy attainment usually means the burn-rate windows or the SLI feeding the alert are misconfigured (wrong window length, wrong good/total events, or stale metric). Confirm the alert reads the same SLI as the dashboard over a consistent window. A mismatch between the alert's metric and the published SLI is the classic cause of phantom pages.

Q: Summarise the full SLO loop this lesson asks you to demonstrate end to end.
A: You interview named consumers to derive justified SLOs (no vanity nines), encode them in the ODCS contract's `slaProperties` block and publish attainment on the catalog product page, instrument the SLIs — freshness, completeness, accuracy proxies, availability — from existing telemetry and store daily attainment, add a fast-plus-slow multi-burn-rate alert on freshness, then replay a month with a bad week to show budget consumed, the alert firing, and a documented change-freeze response. That loop — derive, encode, measure, alert, respond — is the productised-SLO discipline. Each step traces a target to a consumer and a breach to a documented action.

Q: How would you express a NAV serving-layer availability SLO in the ODCS `slaProperties` block?
A: You would add a `slaProperties` entry whose `property` is an availability metric from the spec's periodic table, with a `value` such as `99.9`, a percent `unit`, an `element` pointing at the served NAV view or endpoint, and a `driver` of `operational` or `regulatory` depending on the consumer. This records the uptime promise for the serving layer alongside the freshness and completeness entries. Keeping availability in the same contract block means consumers see one authoritative statement of all the guarantees they can rely on.

Q: Why is it risky to compute an accuracy-proxy SLI from the same code path that produces the data?
A: If the proxy validates the output using the very logic that generated it, a systematic bug shared by both will pass the check, giving false confidence — the proxy can only catch errors its own logic does not also make. That is why reconciliation against an independent shadow NAV calculation is stronger than re-running the same validation. The gotcha is "marking your own homework": an effective accuracy proxy needs at least partial independence from the producing pipeline.

Q: How does the `frequency` of a data product constrain the windows you can pick for a freshness SLO?
A: A once-daily NAV product produces one freshness event per business day, so very short SLO windows contain too few events to be statistically meaningful — a one-week window is just five data points. Low event frequency pushes you toward longer windows (monthly, quarterly) so the attainment percentage and error budget are stable rather than swinging wildly on a single miss. The mismatch between low frequency and short windows is exactly what makes naive per-run alerting noisy and why burn rate over a sensible window is preferable.

Q: A distributor disputes that last month's feed met its SLO. What ODCS and attainment artifacts settle the argument?
A: You point to the SLO target encoded in the version-controlled ODCS `slaProperties` entry (with its `driver` and `element`) as the agreed promise, and to the persisted daily attainment series plus the rolling monthly figure as the measured evidence. Because both the commitment and the measurement are stored and dated, the dispute is resolved by reference, not opinion. If a breach did occur, the recorded burn-rate alert and change-freeze note show it was detected and handled, which is itself part of the contractual evidence.
