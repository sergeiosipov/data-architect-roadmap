# Phase 4 · 1.8.2 Delivery semantics (at-most/at-least/exactly-once) — 150 self-test questions

Q: What are the three delivery guarantees a messaging or streaming system can offer?
A: At-most-once, at-least-once, and exactly-once. At-most-once may lose messages but never duplicates them; at-least-once never loses but may duplicate; exactly-once delivers each message effectively one time but is the most expensive to achieve. They are points on a trade-off curve between data loss, duplication, and cost.

Q: Define at-most-once delivery in one sentence.
A: At-most-once means each message is delivered zero or one times — it may be lost but is never duplicated. It is achieved by committing the read offset before processing, so a crash after commit but before processing silently drops the message. It is the cheapest guarantee and acceptable only where loss is tolerable, such as some metrics or sampled telemetry.

Q: Define at-least-once delivery in one sentence.
A: At-least-once means each message is delivered one or more times — it is never lost but may be duplicated. It is achieved by committing the offset only after processing, so a crash after processing but before commit causes the message to be reprocessed on restart. It is the default, practical guarantee for most pipelines and the foundation on which end-to-end exactly-once is built.

Q: Define exactly-once delivery in one sentence.
A: Exactly-once means each message takes effect exactly one time — no loss and no duplication of its observable effect. In practice it is effectively-once: the system may physically deliver more than once, but deduplication or transactional commit ensures the side effect happens once. It is the most expensive guarantee because it requires coordination between delivery and the sink's commit.

Q: Why is exactly-once usually called effectively-once by careful practitioners?
A: Because the network can always force a retry, so physical exactly-once delivery is impossible in a distributed system; what is achievable is exactly-once effect. The system tolerates duplicate delivery but ensures the side effect (a row written, a NAV updated) occurs once via idempotency or transactions. The term effectively-once makes explicit that the guarantee is about effects, not about how many bytes crossed the wire.

Q: Rank the three guarantees from cheapest to most expensive and say what you pay for the strongest.
A: At-most-once is cheapest, at-least-once is moderate, exactly-once is most expensive. For exactly-once you pay in latency (transactions add coordination round-trips), throughput (fewer in-flight batches, fencing), and operational complexity (transactional IDs, two-phase commit, idempotent sinks). As an architect you justify that cost only where a duplicate or a loss has a real consequence.

Q: For a metrics-sampling pipeline that drops the occasional point, which guarantee is appropriate and why?
A: At-most-once is appropriate. Losing a sampled metric point barely changes an aggregate, while the cost of exactly-once coordination would dominate the pipeline budget for no benefit. The principle is to match the guarantee to the consequence of failure, not to default to the strongest.

Q: For a settlement instruction or a NAV correction, which guarantee do you require and why?
A: You require exactly-once effect: a settlement must not be lost and must not be duplicated, because a duplicate is a double payment and a loss is a missed obligation. Since true exactly-once is effectively-once, you achieve it with at-least-once delivery plus an idempotent or transactional sink. The architect signs off that a duplicate cannot reach a non-idempotent settlement endpoint.

Q: An architect's guarantee diagram claims exactly-once on an edge into a non-idempotent sink. Why is that a defect?
A: Because the edge is really at-least-once into a sink that applies every duplicate, so a producer retry or consumer reprocess produces a duplicated effect — a duplicated settlement or a doubled NAV adjustment. Labelling it exactly-once hides where the guarantee actually degrades. The fix is to relabel the edge honestly and either make the sink idempotent or wrap it in a transaction.

Q: What is the architect skill this lesson is really training, beyond knowing the three terms?
A: Reading any pipeline diagram and marking precisely where each guarantee holds, where it degrades, and what restores it. A guarantee is not a global property of the system; it is a property of each edge and each commit boundary. The deliverable is an audited diagram, not a slogan, because exactly-once claims on settlement or NAV data must be proven, not believed.

Q: The two classic places where duplication enters a Kafka pipeline are what?
A: Producer retries and consumer reprocessing. A producer that does not get an ack resends, appending the same record twice unless deduplicated. A consumer that crashes after processing but before committing its offset reprocesses the same records on restart. Both are normal, healthy failure handling — the duplication is the price of not losing data.

Q: Why does a producer retry create a duplicate when the idempotent producer is not enabled?
A: If the producer sends a record, the broker writes it, but the ack is lost in the network, the producer assumes failure and resends. Without idempotence the broker cannot tell the resend from a new record, so it appends a second copy. The idempotent producer fixes this by tagging records so the broker can recognise and drop the retry.

Q: Why does a consumer that commits offsets after processing risk duplicates rather than loss?
A: Because the record is fully processed and its effect applied before the offset is saved, so a crash in that window leaves the offset pointing before the record. On restart the consumer re-reads and reprocesses it, producing a duplicate effect. This is the at-least-once configuration: you trade possible duplication for zero loss.

Q: Why does a consumer that commits offsets before processing risk loss rather than duplicates?
A: Because the offset advances first, so if the consumer crashes after committing but before finishing processing, the record is never (re)processed — its effect is lost. On restart the consumer reads past it. This is the at-most-once configuration: you trade possible loss for zero duplication.

Q: In Kafka, what does enabling the idempotent producer guarantee, and what is its scope?
A: It guarantees that producer retries do not create duplicate records and that records are not reordered, for writes to a single partition during the producer's session. The broker deduplicates resends using a producer ID and per-partition sequence numbers. Its scope is one producer to one partition — it does not by itself give multi-partition atomicity; transactions add that.

Q: What is the config key for the Kafka idempotent producer and its default in recent versions?
A: The key is `enable.idempotence`, and it defaults to `true` from Kafka 3.0 onward. When it is on, the producer also enforces the settings idempotence requires. Before 3.0 you had to set it explicitly, so version awareness matters when reading older configs.

Q: When `enable.idempotence=true`, which three other producer settings does Kafka require and to what values?
A: It requires `acks=all`, `retries` greater than `0`, and `max.in.flight.requests.per.connection` less than or equal to `5`. If you explicitly set a conflicting value, the producer throws a `ConfigException` at startup. These constraints ensure the broker has the in-sync replicas and bounded in-flight window needed to deduplicate and preserve order.

Q: How does the broker actually detect and drop a duplicate from an idempotent producer?
A: On its first connection the producer is assigned a Producer ID (PID), and every record carries the PID plus a monotonic per-partition sequence number. The broker tracks the last sequence number it accepted for each PID and partition; a resend arrives with a sequence number it has already seen, so the broker recognises the duplicate and does not append it again. A sequence number that is out of order triggers an `OutOfOrderSequenceException`.

Q: Why does `enable.idempotence` require `acks=all` specifically?
A: Because deduplication and durability depend on the leader and all in-sync replicas having a consistent view of the accepted sequence numbers. With weaker acks a leader change could lose the record and its sequence state, breaking the no-loss and no-duplicate guarantees. `acks=all` ensures the full in-sync replica set acknowledges before the producer considers the write done.

Q: Why is `max.in.flight.requests.per.connection` capped at 5 for the idempotent producer?
A: Because the broker tracks recent sequence numbers within a bounded window per partition, and allowing too many unacknowledged in-flight batches would let a retry land out of the deduplication window and break ordering. A cap of 5 lets the producer pipeline for throughput while the broker can still detect duplicates and preserve order. Setting it higher with idempotence on is rejected.

Q: What does an `acks=all` write actually wait for?
A: It waits for the partition leader plus the full set of in-sync replicas (ISR) to acknowledge the record before the producer's send completes. This guarantees the record survives the loss of any replica as long as one ISR member remains, which is the strongest durability Kafka offers. It is a precondition for the idempotent and transactional guarantees, not just a tuning knob.

Q: What does Kafka mean by EOS, and what config turns it on at the producer?
A: EOS is Exactly-Once Semantics — the combination of the idempotent producer with transactions so that a set of writes across partitions commits atomically. You enable it by setting a `transactional.id` on the producer (which implies `enable.idempotence=true`) and using the transactional API (`initTransactions`, `beginTransaction`, `commitTransaction`). Consumers must read with `isolation.level=read_committed` to honour it.

Q: What does setting `transactional.id` give you that `enable.idempotence` alone does not?
A: It gives multi-partition atomicity and cross-session fencing: a group of writes (and consumer offset commits) either all become visible or none do, and a new producer instance with the same `transactional.id` fences out a zombie old instance. Idempotence alone only deduplicates retries to a single partition within one session. The transactional ID is what makes consume-process-produce atomic.

Q: In Kafka transactions, what is fencing and why does it matter?
A: Fencing prevents a zombie or duplicated producer — for example one that hung and was presumed dead — from committing stale writes after a replacement has taken over the same `transactional.id`. When a new producer calls `initTransactions`, the broker bumps an epoch and rejects any write from the old epoch with a producer-fenced error. This keeps exactly-once intact across producer restarts and failovers.

Q: What is the consumer-side requirement to actually observe Kafka exactly-once, and what is the config key?
A: The consumer must set `isolation.level=read_committed`. With that, it only sees records from committed transactions and skips records from aborted or in-flight ones; the default `read_uncommitted` shows everything including aborted writes. Without `read_committed` on the consumer, the producer transactions are written but the reader still sees uncommitted data, so the end-to-end guarantee is lost.

Q: What is the difference between `read_committed` and `read_uncommitted` for a Kafka consumer?
A: `read_uncommitted` (the default) delivers all records up to the high watermark, including those from transactions that later abort. `read_committed` delivers only records from successfully committed transactions and never reads past the Last Stable Offset, the point before any open transaction. Choosing `read_committed` is what makes a downstream consumer honour producer EOS.

Q: What is the Last Stable Offset and why does a `read_committed` consumer care about it?
A: The Last Stable Offset (LSO) is the offset before the earliest still-open (uncommitted) transaction on a partition; a `read_committed` consumer will not return records beyond it. This means a long-running open transaction can stall `read_committed` consumers, because they cannot advance past data whose commit or abort outcome is unknown. It is a common cause of a stuck `read_committed` consumer.

Q: What are transaction markers (control batches) in a Kafka transactional topic?
A: They are special commit or abort records the transaction coordinator writes into each partition involved in a transaction to mark its outcome. A `read_committed` consumer uses these markers to decide whether to deliver or skip the preceding records. They are why transactional topics carry a small overhead even when payloads are small.

Q: What is the single most important limitation of Kafka EOS that an architect must state?
A: Kafka EOS is within-Kafka only: it guarantees exactly-once for the consume-transform-produce loop where both the input and the transactionally-committed output (and the consumer offsets) live in Kafka. The moment the data leaves Kafka into an external store, EOS no longer covers it. Claiming end-to-end exactly-once on the strength of Kafka transactions alone is the classic mislabelled-edge defect.

Q: Why can Kafka transactions atomically commit consumer offsets together with produced records?
A: Because consumer offsets are themselves stored in a Kafka topic (`__consumer_offsets`), the offset commit can be enrolled in the same transaction as the output records via `sendOffsetsToTransaction`. This makes the read these inputs and produced these outputs step atomic. It is the mechanism behind exactly-once stream processing inside Kafka Streams and the transactional consumer pattern.

Q: What does `sendOffsetsToTransaction` do and why is it essential for the consume-process-produce pattern?
A: It includes the consumer's read offsets in the producer transaction so that the offsets advance if and only if the output records commit. This guarantees you never advance past inputs whose outputs were not committed, eliminating both duplicate and lost processing within Kafka. Committing offsets separately from the output would reopen the duplicate or loss window.

Q: A producer with a `transactional.id` set never calls `initTransactions`. What happens?
A: The transactional producer is not usable until `initTransactions` runs; sending without it raises an error, because `initTransactions` is what registers the producer with the coordinator, fences prior instances by bumping the epoch, and recovers any pending transaction state. It must be called once at startup before `beginTransaction`. Skipping it is a common first-transaction bug.

Q: Define end-to-end exactly-once for a pipeline that ends in an external sink.
A: It is at-least-once delivery through the pipeline combined with a sink that applies each record's effect only once — either an idempotent sink or a transactional sink. The delivery layer is allowed to duplicate; the sink absorbs the duplicates or commits atomically. This is the precise meaning of effectively-once across the whole chain, not just the broker.

Q: What two sink properties can each independently turn at-least-once delivery into effectively-once?
A: Idempotency and transactionality. An idempotent sink produces the same final state no matter how many times a given record is applied (for example upsert by a stable key). A transactional sink commits a batch atomically and tied to the delivery layer's progress so a replay re-commits the same batch rather than adding to it. Either one closes the duplication window at the boundary.

Q: Give a concrete example of an idempotent sink write that survives duplicate delivery.
A: An upsert keyed by the business key — for instance `INSERT ... ON CONFLICT (isin, valuation_date) DO UPDATE SET nav = excluded.nav` for a NAV table. Replaying the same record just rewrites the same row to the same value, so the end state is identical whether it is applied once or three times. Contrast this with a blind `INSERT` or an `UPDATE balance = balance + amount`, which compounds on every duplicate.

Q: Why is `UPDATE balance = balance + :amount` a dangerous sink for at-least-once delivery?
A: Because it is not idempotent: each duplicate delivery adds the amount again, so a single replayed settlement double-credits the account. The effect is path-dependent on how many times the message arrives, which at-least-once explicitly does not bound. To make it safe you need a dedup key (for example the instruction reference) so a replayed instruction is recognised and ignored.

Q: How does a deduplication table make a non-idempotent sink effectively-once?
A: You record each message's unique key (for example event ID or instruction reference) in a dedup table inside the same transaction as the effect, and skip any key already present. A replay finds the key and does nothing, so the effect happens once. The key must be stable and carried by the message, and the insert-and-check must be atomic with the side effect or the window reopens.

Q: What property must the message carry for downstream deduplication to be possible at all?
A: A stable, unique idempotency key that is identical across retries of the same logical event — a business key like an instruction reference or a deterministic event ID. If the producer generates a fresh random ID on each retry, the duplicates look distinct and dedup fails. Designing this key into the event schema is an upstream decision the architect must mandate.

Q: Why is at-least-once plus an idempotent sink usually preferred over a full distributed transaction across systems?
A: Because idempotency is simpler, cheaper, and more robust than coordinating a transaction across heterogeneous systems that may not share a transaction manager. It tolerates retries, redeliveries, and even at-least-once from multiple producers, as long as keys are stable. Distributed transactions add coordinator complexity, blocking, and failure modes that idempotency sidesteps.

Q: What is a two-phase-commit sink in stream processing?
A: It is a sink that commits its output in two phases — a pre-commit (prepare) that durably stages the writes but does not make them visible, and a commit that atomically makes them visible — coordinated with the processing engine's checkpoints. If the prepare succeeds but the engine fails before commit, the staged data can be committed on recovery; if it fails before prepare, nothing is exposed. This ties the external sink's atomicity to the engine's fault-tolerance.

Q: How does Flink coordinate a transactional sink with its checkpoints to get end-to-end exactly-once?
A: Flink uses checkpoints as the commit coordinator: between checkpoints the sink writes into an open transaction; when a checkpoint barrier arrives the sink pre-commits (flushes and prepares but does not expose the data); and when Flink notifies that the checkpoint is globally complete the sink commits, making the data visible. The checkpoint is the single point that decides the outcome, so a recovery restores from the last complete checkpoint and re-commits any pre-committed-but-not-committed transaction.

Q: In Flink's two-phase-commit sink, what happens at `beginTransaction`?
A: It starts a fresh transaction that bundles all writes belonging to the current checkpoint interval — for example opening a database transaction, a Kafka transaction, or creating a temporary staging file. Every record processed until the next checkpoint barrier is written into this transaction. It is invoked at the start of each new checkpoint window.

Q: In Flink's two-phase-commit sink, what is the role of `preCommit`?
A: `preCommit` runs when the checkpoint barrier reaches the sink: it flushes and durably stages the current transaction's data (for example closing the temp file or flushing the Kafka transaction) and stops writing to it, then begins a new transaction for the next window. The data is durable but not yet visible. Its durability is captured in the checkpoint so it can be re-committed after a failure.

Q: In Flink's two-phase-commit sink, when does the actual `commit` happen and why then?
A: The actual `commit` happens in `notifyCheckpointComplete`, the callback fired after the checkpoint has been confirmed complete across the whole job. Committing only after global checkpoint completion guarantees every operator's state for that checkpoint is durable, so the visible output is consistent with the recorded position. Committing earlier could expose data the job cannot recover to.

Q: After a Flink restart from a checkpoint, why does the 2PC sink issue a preemptive commit, and what must that commit be?
A: On recovery Flink may have pre-committed a transaction in the last checkpoint but crashed before the commit notification, so it re-issues the commit for that transaction to make the staged data visible exactly once. That commit must be idempotent or safely repeatable, because Flink cannot always know whether the original commit already succeeded. If it is not idempotent, recovery can duplicate the output.

Q: In Flink, what happens if a commit in the 2PC sink fails after a successful pre-commit?
A: After a successful pre-commit the commit must eventually succeed, so a failed commit causes the Flink application to fail and restart per its restart strategy, and the commit is retried. The system never abandons a pre-committed transaction, because doing so would lose data that the checkpoint already accounts for. This is why commit must be guaranteed to eventually succeed is a design rule, not a hope.

Q: What is the modern Flink Sink API for two-phase committing, and how does it split responsibilities?
A: The `sink2` API's `TwoPhaseCommittingSink`, which splits responsibilities into a `SinkWriter` that produces pre-commits and a `Committer` that performs the commits. It implements the two-phase-commit algorithm over the `CheckpointedFunction` and `CheckpointListener` semantics. The older `TwoPhaseCommitSinkFunction` is the legacy single-class base, so version-aware reading matters when reviewing connector code.

Q: Why does pre-commit have to be durable rather than just buffered in memory?
A: Because the whole point of two-phase commit is that after a failure the engine can still commit a transaction it pre-committed; if the staged data lived only in memory it would be lost on crash, and the commit would have nothing to expose. Pre-commit therefore flushes to durable storage (file, broker transaction log, DB prepared transaction) and the checkpoint records that this transaction is pending. This is what makes recovery able to finish the commit.

Q: How does the Kafka sink in Flink achieve exactly-once, in terms of Kafka primitives?
A: It uses Kafka transactions: each checkpoint interval's records go into a Kafka transaction that is pre-committed at the barrier and committed on `notifyCheckpointComplete`, with `transactional.id` values managed across operator restarts to avoid zombies. Downstream consumers must read with `isolation.level=read_committed` to skip uncommitted or aborted output. So the engine's checkpoints drive Kafka's own transaction commit.

Q: Why must downstream consumers of a Flink-to-Kafka exactly-once pipeline use `read_committed`?
A: Because Flink stages output inside Kafka transactions that are only committed at checkpoint completion; a `read_uncommitted` consumer would see records from transactions that may later be aborted on recovery, reintroducing duplicates or phantom data. `read_committed` ensures the consumer only observes the committed, recovery-consistent output. The guarantee is only end-to-end if the final reader honours it.

Q: What is `transaction.timeout.ms` and why does it matter for a checkpointed transactional sink?
A: It bounds how long a Kafka transaction may stay open before the broker aborts it; if a Flink checkpoint interval (plus recovery delay) exceeds this timeout, the broker aborts the pre-committed transaction and the sink cannot commit it, causing data loss or job failure. So the transaction timeout must be set larger than the maximum expected checkpoint interval plus recovery time. It is a classic misconfiguration in long-checkpoint pipelines.

Q: A consumer uses auto-commit (`enable.auto.commit=true`). Which guarantee does this most resemble and what is the risk?
A: It gives weak, timing-dependent guarantees: offsets are committed on a timer (`auto.commit.interval.ms`) independent of whether processing finished. A crash can commit offsets for records not yet processed (loss) or fail to commit processed ones (duplicates). For any guarantee you actually care about, disable auto-commit and commit explicitly relative to processing.

Q: To get at-least-once with manual offset commits, do you commit before or after processing, and why?
A: After processing — commit the offset only once the record's effect is durably applied. That way a crash before commit causes reprocessing (a duplicate), never a loss, which is the at-least-once contract. Committing before processing would convert it to at-most-once.

Q: In the lesson's exercise you run the same consumer in auto-commit, manual-commit-after-process, and transactional EOS, then kill it mid-batch. What do you predict for each?
A: Auto-commit: a mix of losses or duplicates depending on where the timer fired relative to the crash. Manual commit-after-process: zero loss, some duplicates (at-least-once). Transactional EOS with a `read_committed` consumer and transactional output: zero loss and zero duplicates within Kafka. The exercise's value is that your observed duplicate and loss tabulation should match this prediction exactly.

Q: In that exercise, why does the manual-commit-after-process run show duplicates but no losses?
A: Because the record's effect is applied first and the offset is committed only afterward; killing the consumer between those steps leaves the offset behind the record, so on restart it is reprocessed. Nothing is lost because the offset never advances past unprocessed records. The duplicates are exactly the records processed but not yet acknowledged at the moment of the kill.

Q: In that exercise, why can the transactional EOS run show neither duplicates nor losses?
A: Because the output write and the consumer offset commit are enrolled in one Kafka transaction via `sendOffsetsToTransaction`, so a crash mid-batch aborts the open transaction — neither the partial output nor the offset advance becomes visible. On restart it reprocesses cleanly from the last committed offset, and the `read_committed` downstream never saw the aborted writes. The guarantee holds because delivery and commit move together.

Q: When you mark a diagram for the exercise, where exactly does the guarantee degrade if the downstream store is not idempotent?
A: At the edge from the consumer to the external (non-Kafka) store: Kafka EOS covers up to and including the transactional Kafka write and offset commit, but the moment the effect is applied to a non-idempotent external sink, a reprocess after crash duplicates that effect. The degradation point is the write into the external store, and what restores the guarantee is making that write idempotent (upsert or dedup) or transactional with the engine. That edge is precisely where the honest label changes from exactly-once to at-least-once.

Q: A NAV publication pipeline claims exactly-once into a downstream reporting database. What is the first thing to audit?
A: Whether the database write is idempotent or transactionally tied to the delivery layer, because Kafka EOS stops at Kafka. Check whether NAV rows are upserted by a stable key (for example ISIN plus valuation date) or whether a dedup key gates a plain insert; if neither, a consumer reprocess duplicates the NAV row. Without idempotency or two-phase commit at that edge, the exactly-once claim is false regardless of broker configuration.

Q: A transfer-agency order pipeline must never duplicate a subscription. Which design pattern do you mandate?
A: At-least-once delivery plus an idempotent sink keyed by the order's unique reference, so a replayed order is recognised and not booked twice. Concretely, dedup on the transfer-agency order reference inside the same transaction that books the order, or upsert the order by reference. This is cheaper and more robust than a cross-system distributed transaction and directly closes the double-subscription risk.

Q: Why is a stable, source-assigned reference (rather than a broker offset) the right idempotency key for fund instructions?
A: Because the business reference identifies the logical instruction across retries, reprocesses, and even reprocessing from a different topic or after a re-snapshot, whereas a broker offset can change when data is re-keyed, recompacted, or replayed from another source. Deduping on the instruction reference survives those events; deduping on offset does not. The architect should require this reference in the event schema.

Q: What is the danger of relying on Kafka offset as a dedup key across a CDC re-snapshot or topic rebuild?
A: Offsets are positional and not stable identities: a re-snapshot, partition reassignment, or topic rebuild can present the same logical change at a different offset, so offset-based dedup would treat it as new and duplicate the effect, or skip a genuinely new record. A content-based business key is stable across these events. This is why guarantee diagrams should annotate which key actually deduplicates each edge.

Q: Why does increasing throughput by raising in-flight requests conflict with exactly-once on the idempotent producer?
A: The idempotent producer caps `max.in.flight.requests.per.connection` at 5 so the broker can still deduplicate retries within its sequence-number window and preserve order; pushing it higher to chase throughput is rejected when idempotence is on. So you cannot trade the EOS guarantee away silently by tuning that knob — Kafka enforces the constraint. This is the throughput cost of exactly-once made concrete.

Q: How does latency increase when you move from at-least-once to Kafka EOS?
A: Transactions add coordination round-trips: `beginTransaction`, the writes, optional `sendOffsetsToTransaction`, then `commitTransaction`, plus the broker writing transaction markers to every involved partition. The consumer side also waits for committed data (it cannot read past the Last Stable Offset). These per-batch overheads raise end-to-end latency, which is the latency cost of exactly-once.

Q: An auditor asks you to prove a settlement pipeline cannot double-pay. What evidence do you present?
A: A guarantee diagram annotated edge by edge showing at-least-once delivery and an idempotent or transactional commit at the settlement endpoint, plus the dedup or upsert key used and proof it is stable across retries. You back it with the kill-and-restart test results showing zero duplicates at the sink, and the config (`enable.idempotence`, `transactional.id`, `isolation.level=read_committed`) where Kafka transactions are used. The claim must be demonstrated, not asserted.

Q: What is the difference between the broker received it once and the effect happened once?
A: Broker-level uniqueness (idempotent producer, transactions) guarantees the record appears once inside Kafka, but the business effect happens wherever the record is consumed and applied. If a consumer reprocesses that single record into a non-idempotent external sink, the effect happens twice even though the broker stored it once. Exactly-once must be reasoned about at the effect, which is why the external edge is where claims usually break.

Q: Why can you not get end-to-end exactly-once for free just by turning on Kafka transactions?
A: Because Kafka transactions only make the within-Kafka consume-process-produce loop atomic; the external sinks and sources at the ends of the pipeline are outside that transaction. End-to-end requires each external boundary to be idempotent or two-phase-commit-coordinated with the engine. Turning on transactions and declaring victory is exactly the mislabelled-edge mistake the lesson warns about.

Q: A `read_committed` consumer is stuck and not advancing while data is clearly being produced. What is the first thing to check?
A: Whether there is a long-running or hung open transaction holding the Last Stable Offset back, because `read_committed` will not deliver records beyond the LSO until the open transaction commits or aborts. Check for a stuck transactional producer, an oversized checkpoint interval, or a transaction that exceeded `transaction.timeout.ms`. Resolving or aborting the open transaction lets the LSO and the consumer advance.

Q: A transactional producer dies and is replaced; the old one revives and tries to commit. What protects you?
A: Epoch-based fencing: when the replacement called `initTransactions` with the same `transactional.id`, the coordinator bumped the epoch, so the revived old producer's writes and commits are rejected with a producer-fenced error. This prevents the zombie from committing stale data and corrupting the exactly-once guarantee. Fencing is the reason a fixed `transactional.id` is required, not a random one per instance.

Q: Why should the `transactional.id` be stable across restarts of the same logical task rather than randomly generated?
A: Because the stable ID is how the coordinator recognises a restarted instance as the same logical producer, lets it recover or abort the prior pending transaction, and fences the dead instance via the epoch. A random ID per start would orphan the previous transaction (leaving it to time out and possibly stall `read_committed` readers) and lose the fencing protection. Stable IDs are central to correct EOS across failures.

Q: What is a zombie producer in Kafka EOS terms?
A: A producer instance that was presumed dead and replaced but is still running — for example after a network partition or a stop-the-world pause — and might still attempt to write or commit. Without fencing it could commit stale data after the replacement has moved on, breaking exactly-once. The epoch bump on `initTransactions` fences it out, which is why zombie-fencing is a named feature of Kafka transactions.

Q: Why is exactly-once across two independent databases via application-level dual writes fundamentally unsafe?
A: Because a crash between the two writes leaves them inconsistent — one applied, one not — and there is no shared transaction to roll both back; retrying re-applies the already-applied side unless it is idempotent. This is the dual-write problem. The fix is a single source of truth plus CDC or outbox or a two-phase-commit sink, not two best-effort writes.

Q: How does the transactional-outbox pattern relate to end-to-end exactly-once?
A: The application writes its state change and an outbox event in one local database transaction, so the event is produced if and only if the state changed; a CDC relay then delivers the outbox event at least once to Kafka, and the downstream consumer dedups by the event ID. This avoids the dual-write race and yields effectively-once because the delivery is at-least-once over an idempotent boundary. It is the standard pattern for emitting events atomically with a state change.

Q: Why is idempotency at the sink generally more robust than a cross-system distributed transaction across heterogeneous stores?
A: Because heterogeneous XA transactions need every participant to support the protocol, introduce a blocking coordinator and in-doubt states, and fail in hard-to-recover ways under partitions. Idempotent sinks tolerate retries and redeliveries without coordination and degrade gracefully. As an architect you reach for idempotency first and reserve engine-coordinated two-phase commit for cases where the sink genuinely cannot be made idempotent.

Q: What does the guarantee is restored mean on a diagram, with a concrete example?
A: It means a downstream mechanism re-establishes exactly-once effect after an edge degraded it — for example, delivery is at-least-once (degraded) into a sink that upserts by ISIN plus date, so the duplicate is absorbed and the end state is exactly-once again (restored). You mark the degrade point (the edge) and the restore point (the idempotent write). A diagram with no restore mechanism after a degrade is a duplication waiting to happen.

Q: Can a pipeline be exactly-once at the start and end but at-least-once in the middle? What matters?
A: Yes — what matters is the observable effect at the sink, not that every internal hop is exactly-once. Internal at-least-once is fine as long as the final commit is idempotent or transactional so duplicates do not change the end state. The architect audits the boundaries where effects become visible, not every wire.

Q: Why does at-most-once never appear in the design of a settlement or NAV pipeline?
A: Because at-most-once permits silent loss, and losing a settlement instruction or a NAV correction is a regulatory and financial failure that auditors will surface. The acceptable trade in this domain is at-least-once (with idempotent sinks) — never loss. At-most-once belongs only to disposable, sampleable data.

Q: What is the relationship between idempotency and ordering for exactly-once?
A: Idempotency removes duplicates but does not by itself guarantee order; for some effects (for example applying NAV corrections in sequence) order also matters. The idempotent producer preserves per-partition order, and partitioning by the business key keeps related events ordered on one partition. Exactly-once effect plus correct ordering usually requires keyed partitioning, not just dedup.

Q: How does keying messages by the business key support both ordering and idempotency in Kafka?
A: Keying routes all records for one key (for example one ISIN or one account) to the same partition, so they are delivered and processed in produced order, and the idempotent producer preserves that order within the partition. Combined with a key-based upsert at the sink, you get ordered, deduplicated effects. Choosing the partition key is therefore a correctness decision, not just load balancing.

Q: What does `OutOfOrderSequenceException` from an idempotent producer indicate?
A: It indicates the broker received a record with a sequence number that does not follow the expected next value for that producer and partition — for example because an earlier batch was lost or the producer's state diverged. It signals the idempotency invariant was violated and the producer typically must be reset. It is a sign of a deeper delivery problem, not something to ignore.

Q: Why is exactly-once a property of a closed loop rather than a single component?
A: Because the guarantee depends on delivery, processing, and commit all agreeing on the same notion of progress; idempotence on the producer alone, or transactions without `read_committed` consumers, or a transactional engine with a non-idempotent sink each leaves a gap. Only the whole loop — consume, process, produce, commit offsets, expose to the sink — closed consistently yields exactly-once effect. Auditing one component in isolation cannot establish it.

Q: In Flink, why does committing at `notifyCheckpointComplete` rather than at the barrier matter for correctness?
A: The barrier (`preCommit`) means this transaction's data is durable for this checkpoint, but the checkpoint is only globally valid once every operator confirms; committing at `notifyCheckpointComplete` ensures the visible output corresponds to a recoverable global state. If the sink committed at the barrier and the checkpoint later failed elsewhere, the job would recover to an earlier state while output for the failed checkpoint was already visible — a duplicate. The completion callback aligns visibility with recoverability.

Q: What is the recovery sequence for a Flink 2PC sink that crashed between pre-commit and commit?
A: On restart Flink restores state from the last completed checkpoint, finds the pre-committed (pending) transaction recorded in that checkpoint, and preemptively re-issues its commit to make the staged data visible exactly once. Because it cannot be certain the original commit did not partially succeed, that commit must be idempotent. New processing then resumes from the restored position.

Q: Why must a Flink 2PC commit be idempotent specifically during recovery?
A: Because after a crash Flink may not know whether the pre-committed transaction was already committed before the failure, so it re-commits; if the commit is not idempotent, the data could be exposed twice. Designing the commit so that re-committing the same transaction is a no-op (for example committing an already-named atomic rename, or a Kafka transaction the coordinator treats correctly) keeps recovery exactly-once. This is the single most important contract for implementing such a sink.

Q: How would you make a file-based sink exactly-once using the 2PC pattern?
A: Write each checkpoint interval's data to a uniquely named temporary or staging file (begin), flush and close it on the barrier (pre-commit), and atomically rename or move it into the destination on checkpoint completion (commit). The atomic rename is idempotent — re-issuing it after recovery either completes the move or finds it already done. Readers only see fully renamed files, so partial files never become visible.

Q: How would you make a relational-database sink exactly-once with 2PC?
A: Use the database's prepared (XA-style) transactions: open a transaction per checkpoint, write into it, issue `PREPARE` at the barrier (pre-commit), and `COMMIT` the prepared transaction on checkpoint completion, re-committing the recorded prepared transaction ID on recovery. Alternatively, drop two-phase commit entirely and make the writes idempotent upserts keyed by a business key, which is simpler if the schema allows. The choice is between engine-coordinated commit and a self-contained idempotent sink.

Q: When is an idempotent sink preferable to a 2PC sink, and vice versa?
A: An idempotent sink is preferable when the data has a natural stable key and the operation can be expressed as an upsert or dedup, because it avoids coordinator complexity and tolerates redelivery from any source. A 2PC sink is needed when effects cannot be made idempotent (for example append-only outputs with no dedup key, or strict atomic visibility of a batch) and the engine can drive prepared commits. Architects default to idempotency and use two-phase commit where idempotency is genuinely impossible.

Q: Why does commit must eventually succeed make pre-commit a point of no return for the sink?
A: Once data is pre-committed, the checkpoint has accounted for it, so abandoning it would lose data the system believes is safe; therefore the design forbids giving up — a failed commit forces a job restart and retry until it succeeds. This asymmetry (you can abort before pre-commit, but not after) is the heart of two-phase commit. It is why the prepare step must only succeed when the subsequent commit is genuinely guaranteed to be completable.

Q: A pipeline shows exactly-once into Kafka but feeds a downstream analytics warehouse via a sink connector. Where do you re-audit the guarantee?
A: At the connector's write into the warehouse: Kafka EOS ends at Kafka, so you must check whether the sink connector does idempotent upserts (for example by primary key) or supports a two-phase-commit or exactly-once delivery mode, and how it tracks offsets on restart. If the connector is at-least-once into a non-idempotent table, duplicates land in the warehouse. The diagram's exactly-once label must stop at Kafka unless that connector edge restores it.

Q: Why can naive `INSERT`-based Kafka sink connectors duplicate rows on a connector restart?
A: Because on restart a sink connector may reprocess records between its last committed offset and the crash point (at-least-once), and a plain `INSERT` applies each one again, producing duplicate rows. Restoring exactly-once requires upsert-on-key, a dedup key, or the connector's exactly-once delivery feature. This is the same external-edge degradation, now inside the connector.

Q: What is the difference between idempotent delivery and idempotent processing?
A: Idempotent delivery means the transport does not duplicate the record (idempotent producer within Kafka), while idempotent processing means applying the same record any number of times yields the same effect at the sink. End-to-end exactly-once needs idempotent (or transactional) processing at the boundary, because delivery idempotency alone does not cover a consumer reprocessing into an external store. Conflating the two is a common reasoning error.

Q: Why is exactly-once harder for a process whose effect is sending an email or calling an external payment API?
A: Because those side effects are external, not transactional, and often not idempotent or reversible — you cannot enroll send email or call settlement API in your checkpoint or transaction. You must add an idempotency key the external system honours (many payment APIs accept one) or a dedup ledger so a replay is a no-op. Without external idempotency support, exactly-once cannot be guaranteed for that effect, only minimised.

Q: How does an idempotency key passed to an external API restore exactly-once at that boundary?
A: The API uses the key to recognise a retried request and return the original result instead of performing the action again, so a replayed call has no additional effect. You generate a stable key per logical operation (for example the instruction reference) and send it on every retry. This is how at-least-once delivery into an external action becomes effectively-once when the API cooperates.

Q: In a SWIFT message dispatch pipeline, why is exactly-once effect critical and how is it usually achieved?
A: Because dispatching the same payment message twice can trigger a duplicate settlement, and losing one misses an obligation, so neither loss nor duplication is acceptable. It is usually achieved with at-least-once delivery plus a dedup ledger keyed by the message reference (and the receiving system's own duplicate detection), so a replay is recognised and not resent. The architect documents this boundary as the restore point on the diagram.

Q: For an EMT or EPT file generation pipeline, where might exactly-once degrade and what restores it?
A: Degradation can occur if the file-writing step is triggered at-least-once and writes are not atomic, producing a partial or duplicated file the downstream picks up. Restoration uses atomic publish (write to a temp name, atomic rename on completion) or idempotent regeneration keyed by reporting period and fund, so re-running yields the same single file. The visible edge is the file becoming available to consumers, which must be atomic and idempotent.

Q: Why is atomic rename on completion a two-phase-commit-style idempotent restore for file outputs?
A: Because writing to a staging name and then atomically renaming means consumers never see a partial file (no half-written EMT or EPT report), and re-running the rename either completes or finds the destination already present — a no-op. The rename is the commit; the staging write is the pre-commit. It restores exactly-once visibility at the file boundary without a distributed transaction.

Q: Under DORA's operational-resilience expectations, why does mislabelling a guarantee carry regulatory weight?
A: Because DORA requires firms to understand and manage ICT risk to critical functions, and a guarantee diagram that claims exactly-once where it is really at-least-once into a non-idempotent settlement sink is an undocumented operational risk that can produce duplicated financial effects. An auditor finding the mislabel evidences inadequate control over a critical data flow. Accurate, provable guarantee diagrams are part of demonstrable resilience.

Q: Why must guarantee claims for a UCITS NAV-publication flow be auditable rather than asserted?
A: Because the NAV drives investor transactions and regulatory reporting, so a duplicated or lost NAV has direct investor-protection consequences and must be controllable and evidenced. Exactly-once must be backed by the idempotency or transaction mechanism at each boundary and by reproducible kill-restart evidence. An asserted but unproven claim is exactly what an auditor will challenge.

Q: How does an LEI or ISIN serve as a useful component of an idempotency key in fund-data sinks?
A: As stable, standardised identifiers, an ISIN (instrument) or LEI (legal entity) plus a period or event qualifier forms a deterministic business key on which to upsert or dedup, so replays of the same logical record collapse to one row. They are reliable because they do not change across retries or re-snapshots, unlike offsets or surrogate IDs. The architect mandates such keys in the event schema to make sinks idempotent.

Q: What is the danger of using a database auto-increment surrogate key as the dedup key for incoming events?
A: A surrogate generated at insert time is new on every insert, so it cannot detect that an incoming event is a replay — it would happily insert the duplicate and assign it a fresh ID. Dedup must key on the event's own stable business identity (instruction reference, ISIN plus date), checked before insert. Relying on the surrogate gives the illusion of uniqueness while permitting duplicates.

Q: What single mislabelling error is the costliest in a settlement pipeline, and how do you prevent it?
A: Labelling an edge exactly-once when it is at-least-once into a non-idempotent sink, which compliance discovers downstream as a duplicated settlement. Prevent it by always tracing the effect to its visible boundary, requiring a stable idempotency key or two-phase commit there, and proving with a kill-restart test that no duplicate reaches the sink. Honest, evidence-backed edge labelling is the whole point of the lesson.

Q: What is the default value of `delivery.timeout.ms` and why should you tune it rather than `retries` on an idempotent producer?
A: `delivery.timeout.ms` defaults to `120000` (two minutes) and is the upper bound on the total time `producer.send()` may take across the original send plus all retries and acks before it reports success or failure. Modern guidance is to leave `retries` at its high default and bound retry behaviour with `delivery.timeout.ms`, because it expresses the real business deadline directly. For a NAV-feed producer you set it to the longest delay you can tolerate before surfacing a send failure to ops.

Q: A producer that publishes only once a week suddenly starts failing with `ProducerFencedException`. What is the likely cause?
A: The transactional ID was expired by the broker because the producer was idle longer than `transactional.id.expiration.ms`, which defaults to `604800000` (one week); the next transaction then arrives with a stale epoch and is fenced. The coordinator garbage-collects transactional state for IDs it has not heard from within that window. For a low-frequency producer you either raise that broker setting or send a keep-alive transaction.

Q: What is `transactional.id.expiration.ms`, where is it configured, and what does it govern?
A: It is a broker-side setting (default `604800000` ms, one week) that controls how long the transaction coordinator retains a transactional ID's metadata after its last activity before expiring it. Once expired, a returning producer using that ID is treated as new or fenced, which can surface as a `ProducerFencedException`. It matters for any producer whose natural cadence is slower than a week, such as a monthly EPT regeneration job.

Q: What is the default `transaction.timeout.ms`, and what error appears if you request a value above the broker's cap?
A: `transaction.timeout.ms` defaults to `60000` (sixty seconds) and is the producer-side limit the coordinator uses before proactively aborting an open transaction. If you request a value larger than the broker's `transaction.max.timeout.ms`, the `initTransactions` call fails with an `InvalidTxnTimeoutException`. So raising the timeout for a long checkpoint interval also requires raising the broker cap.

Q: For a Flink-to-Kafka sink with a 10-minute checkpoint interval, how must you set the transaction timeout and why?
A: You must set `transaction.timeout.ms` comfortably above the checkpoint interval plus recovery time — well over ten minutes — and ensure the broker's `transaction.max.timeout.ms` permits it, because the Kafka transaction stays open for the whole interval. If the timeout is shorter, the broker aborts the pre-committed transaction before the checkpoint completes and the commit fails, losing that interval's data. This pairing of engine interval and broker timeout is a classic exactly-once misconfiguration.

Q: What CLI flag makes `kafka-console-consumer` read only committed transactional data, and what does omitting it show?
A: The flag is `--isolation-level read_committed`; with it the console consumer skips records from aborted or still-open transactions and never reads past the Last Stable Offset. Omitting it defaults to `read_uncommitted`, which displays every record up to the high watermark including writes that later abort. This flag is the quick way to confirm from the command line that a transactional producer's output is actually committed.

Q: How would you reproduce the at-least-once duplicate window on a NATIVE Ubuntu laptop using only the bundled Kafka CLI tools?
A: Start a `kafka-console-consumer` on the orders topic, disable auto-commit and commit offsets manually after a deliberate `sleep`, then `kill -9` the process during that sleep and restart it to watch the same records reappear. You drive input with `kafka-console-producer` and inspect committed offsets with `kafka-consumer-groups --describe --group <id>`. The reappearing records are precisely the processed-but-uncommitted ones at the moment of the kill.

Q: Which `kafka-consumer-groups` subcommand shows the lag and committed offset that reveal whether a consumer is at-least-once or at-most-once after a crash?
A: `kafka-consumer-groups --bootstrap-server <host> --describe --group <id>` prints `CURRENT-OFFSET`, `LOG-END-OFFSET`, and `LAG` per partition, so you can see whether the committed offset trails the processed position (at-least-once) or has run ahead of it (at-most-once). Comparing the committed offset against what the sink actually applied is how you audit the configuration empirically. It is the first command to run when diagnosing duplicate or lost records.

Q: What does `auto.offset.reset` control, and how can a misconfigured value masquerade as a delivery-semantics bug?
A: `auto.offset.reset` decides where a consumer starts when it has no committed offset or its offset is out of range — `earliest`, `latest`, or `none`; it has nothing to do with the per-record guarantee but is often blamed for one. A value of `latest` after an offset is lost silently skips a backlog (looks like loss), while `earliest` reprocesses from the start (looks like mass duplication). Diagnosing a delivery complaint means first confirming whether the offset was reset, not just how commits are timed.

Q: Why is `acks=1` insufficient for the idempotent producer even though it returns an ack?
A: `acks=1` acknowledges as soon as the partition leader writes the record, before in-sync replicas have it, so a leader failover can lose the record and its sequence-number state, breaking both no-loss and dedup. That is why `enable.idempotence=true` forces `acks=all` and rejects `acks=1` at startup with a `ConfigException`. The ack must reflect durable, replicated state for the guarantee to hold across a leader change.

Q: A producer log shows `OutOfOrderSequenceException`. Walk through your diagnosis before reaching for a restart.
A: First confirm whether earlier batches to that partition genuinely failed or were dropped (for example via a broker outage longer than the retention of producer state), because the broker saw a sequence number that skipped the expected next value. Check `delivery.timeout.ms` expiries and any `UnknownProducerIdException` that preceded it, since an expired producer ID resets the sequence baseline. Only after ruling out data loss do you reset or recreate the producer, because the exception signals a real gap, not a transient hiccup.

Q: What does `UnknownProducerIdException` indicate and how does it relate to topic retention?
A: It means the broker no longer has state for the producer's PID — typically because all records from that producer aged out of the partition under the topic's retention, so the broker discarded its sequence tracking. When the producer next sends, its sequence number no longer matches any known baseline. It is most common on very low-throughput topics with short retention and is usually handled by the client transparently re-initialising the PID.

Q: Contrast `min.insync.replicas` with `acks=all`: why do you need both for a durable idempotent write?
A: `acks=all` tells the producer to wait for all currently in-sync replicas, but if the ISR has shrunk to just the leader, all means one and durability is weak. `min.insync.replicas` (a topic or broker setting, commonly `2`) refuses the write with `NotEnoughReplicasException` unless at least that many replicas are in sync, so the two together guarantee the record is on multiple brokers before it is acknowledged. For settlement data you set `min.insync.replicas=2` with replication factor `3` so one broker can fail without blocking or losing writes.

Q: If `min.insync.replicas=2` and only the leader is in sync, what happens to an `acks=all` produce, and what is the semantics impact?
A: The produce is rejected with `NotEnoughReplicasException` (or `NotEnoughReplicasAfterAppendException`), so the producer retries rather than writing under-replicated data. This converts a silent durability gap into an explicit, retriable failure, preserving the no-loss property. The trade-off is availability: under replica loss the partition becomes unwritable, which for fund data is the correct, fail-safe choice.

Q: How does compacted-topic retention interact with idempotency keys for a NAV state topic?
A: A log-compacted topic keyed by, say, ISIN plus valuation date keeps only the latest value per key, which is itself an idempotent representation: replaying the same keyed record overwrites rather than appends. This makes the topic a natural at-least-once-safe store of current NAV state, since duplicates collapse to one entry. The key choice that gives idempotency at the sink is the same key choice that makes compaction meaningful.

Q: Why does enabling idempotence not protect against duplicates caused by application-level resends after `delivery.timeout.ms` expiry?
A: Idempotence dedups retries within a single send attempt's lifecycle, but once `delivery.timeout.ms` expires the send is reported as failed and the application's own retry is a brand-new send, potentially with a new sequence baseline, which the broker may accept as new data. So a timeout-then-resend at the app layer can still duplicate. The remedy is a transactional ID for atomic retry, or an application-level idempotency key that the sink dedups on.

Q: Distinguish a poison message from a delivery-semantics problem, and why does that distinction matter for guarantees?
A: A poison message is one that always fails processing (bad schema, unparseable EMT row), so an at-least-once consumer retries it forever and stalls the partition; this is a processing failure, not a delivery failure. The guarantee (no loss) is technically intact but progress is zero, so you route the message to a dead-letter topic after N attempts to unblock the partition. Mislabelling this as a duplication bug wastes time on the commit logic when the fix is dead-lettering.

Q: How does a dead-letter queue interact with the at-least-once guarantee for a poison message?
A: Moving a repeatedly-failing record to a dead-letter topic after a retry budget lets the consumer commit past it and keep delivering subsequent records, trading strict no-loss-of-this-record for liveness. The record is not lost — it is parked for human or automated inspection — so the guarantee becomes deliver-or-quarantine rather than silent drop. For regulated flows the DLQ itself must be durable and monitored, because a quarantined settlement still represents an unmet obligation.

Q: Why can a rebalance during processing cause duplicates even with manual commit-after-process, and what reduces the window?
A: If a partition is revoked and reassigned after a consumer processed records but before it committed, the new owner replays those records, duplicating their effect. A `ConsumerRebalanceListener` that commits offsets in `onPartitionsRevoked` shrinks the window, and cooperative incremental rebalancing avoids stopping the whole group. The duplicates are inherent to at-least-once; the listener and idempotent sink are what keep them harmless.

Q: What is the role of `max.poll.interval.ms` in spurious duplicates, and how do you tell it apart from a real crash?
A: If processing a batch takes longer than `max.poll.interval.ms` (default five minutes), the broker assumes the consumer is dead, kicks it from the group, and reassigns its partitions, so the uncommitted batch is reprocessed elsewhere — a duplicate with no actual crash. You tell it apart by spotting the rebalance and the slow-batch log rather than a process exit. The fix is to raise the interval, shrink `max.poll.records`, or speed up processing, not to touch commit timing.

Q: Why does a static consumer group membership (`group.instance.id`) help exactly-once-effect stability during rolling restarts?
A: Setting `group.instance.id` makes a consumer a static member, so a brief restart within `session.timeout.ms` does not trigger a partition reassignment and the consumer resumes its own partitions without other members reprocessing. This avoids the duplicate bursts that ordinary rebalances cause during deploys. For an idempotent sink the duplicates would be harmless anyway, but fewer reassignments mean less load and fewer LSO stalls.

Q: In the consume-process-produce loop, why must `sendOffsetsToTransaction` receive the consumer group metadata in newer Kafka, not just the offsets?
A: Newer Kafka passes a `ConsumerGroupMetadata` object so the broker can fence based on the consumer's generation, ensuring offsets committed inside the transaction belong to the current group membership and not a zombie consumer from a prior generation. Without the group metadata a stale consumer could enroll offsets that no longer reflect its ownership. This tightens the exactly-once guarantee across consumer rebalances, not just producer restarts.

Q: A transactional producer's `commitTransaction` throws an exception that is not a fencing error. What is the safe response?
A: For most non-fatal errors you abort the transaction and retry the whole consume-process-produce cycle, because a failed commit means the outputs and offsets are not visible and reprocessing is safe under exactly-once. If the error is a fencing or fatal error such as `ProducerFencedException`, you must close the producer and let a fresh instance take over rather than retry. The rule is: retriable commit failure means redo the transaction, fenced means step aside.

Q: Why is `abortTransaction` on the producer essential for liveness, not just correctness?
A: If processing fails mid-transaction and you neither commit nor abort, the transaction stays open until `transaction.timeout.ms`, holding back the Last Stable Offset and stalling every `read_committed` consumer on those partitions. Calling `abortTransaction` immediately releases the LSO and discards the partial writes. So prompt aborts are what keep downstream readers moving, which is why error paths must always reach an abort.

Q: How do transaction markers affect storage and a `read_committed` consumer's throughput on a high-transaction-rate topic?
A: Every commit or abort writes control batches (markers) into each involved partition, so a workload with many tiny transactions pays a per-transaction marker overhead in log size and in the consumer's need to scan and skip markers. This favours batching more records per transaction. For a high-rate fund-tick topic you size transactions to amortise the marker cost rather than committing per record.

Q: Why does committing one record per Kafka transaction defeat the purpose of EOS performance-wise?
A: Each transaction carries fixed overhead — coordinator round-trips for begin and commit plus a marker written to every partition — so one record per transaction maximises that overhead relative to payload and collapses throughput. Transactions are designed to wrap a batch so the cost is amortised. The architect picks a transaction granularity (a poll batch or a checkpoint interval) that balances latency against this overhead.

Q: Compare Kafka Streams `processing.guarantee=exactly_once_v2` with the older `exactly_once`: what changed and why does it matter?
A: `exactly_once_v2` (default in modern Kafka Streams) uses a single producer per instance with the newer thread-producer and group-metadata fencing, drastically reducing the number of transactional producers and improving scalability over the original `exactly_once`, which used a producer per task. The guarantee is the same; the resource cost and broker load are far lower. When reviewing a Streams config you prefer `exactly_once_v2` and flag the deprecated value.

Q: What does Kafka Streams give you for free regarding exactly-once that a hand-rolled consumer does not?
A: Kafka Streams wires the consume-process-produce transaction, offset commit via `sendOffsetsToTransaction`, and state-store changelog commits into one atomic unit under `processing.guarantee=exactly_once_v2`, so your stateful aggregations and their outputs commit together. A hand-rolled consumer must orchestrate all of that manually and is easy to get subtly wrong. The trade-off is that Streams EOS is still within-Kafka and does not cover an external sink.

Q: Why is a state store's changelog topic central to Kafka Streams exactly-once, and what happens on recovery?
A: Each state store is backed by a compacted changelog topic written inside the same transaction as the outputs, so the store's contents and the emitted records are consistent at every commit. On recovery the store is rebuilt by replaying the changelog up to the last committed offset, restoring exactly the state that matches the committed output. This is how stateful operations stay exactly-once across a crash inside Kafka.

Q: How would you design exactly-once-effect when the sink is an idempotent REST API that requires an `Idempotency-Key` header?
A: Generate one stable key per logical instruction — for example the transfer-agency order reference — and send it in the `Idempotency-Key` header on every retry, so the API returns the original outcome for a replay instead of acting again. Persist the mapping from instruction to key so restarts reuse the same key rather than minting a new one. This turns at-least-once delivery into effectively-once at a boundary you cannot enroll in a transaction.

Q: For a payment API that does NOT support idempotency keys, how do you still avoid double-payment under at-least-once?
A: You add your own dedup ledger: in one local database transaction you check-and-insert the instruction reference and only call the API if the insert was new, marking it sent on success. A replay finds the reference already present and skips the call. The residual risk is a crash after the API call but before marking sent, which you bound with a reconciliation step against the payment provider's statement.

Q: Why is the crash between external action and local bookkeeping the hardest residual in exactly-once-effect, and how is it managed?
A: Because the external side effect (a SWIFT message dispatched, a payment posted) is irreversible and you cannot atomically tie it to your local record, so a crash in that gap leaves you unsure whether to retry. It is managed with an idempotency key the receiver honours, or with reconciliation against the counterparty's records to detect and resolve the ambiguity. Exactly-once here is achieved by detection-and-correction, not by prevention alone.

Q: How does a watermark or processing-time window complicate exactly-once for late-arriving fund data?
A: Exactly-once governs whether each record's effect happens once, but a late record arriving after its window closed may be dropped or routed to a side output depending on allowed lateness, which is a separate completeness question. A duplicate of a late record is still deduped by the sink, but a dropped late record is a loss the delivery guarantee does not address. The architect separates exactly-once-effect from window-completeness when auditing a NAV-aggregation job.

Q: Why can an exactly-once pipeline still produce a wrong NAV even though no record is duplicated or lost?
A: Exactly-once is about delivery and effect counting, not correctness of the computation or completeness of inputs; a missing upstream source, a wrong reference rate, or an out-of-order correction applied before its base value all yield a wrong NAV with perfect delivery semantics. The guarantee says each record took effect once, not that the right records were present or ordered. Auditors must check completeness and ordering separately from the exactly-once claim.

Q: How does idempotency interact with deletes and tombstones for a CDC-fed NAV table?
A: An idempotent upsert by key handles inserts and updates, but a delete must also be idempotent — applying a tombstone (delete by key) twice must leave the row absent, which it naturally does. The danger is a duplicate delete-then-reinsert ordering issue, so deletes and upserts for the same key must stay ordered on one partition. Keying by the business key gives both the dedup and the ordering the delete needs.

Q: Why must ordering be preserved for a sequence of NAV corrections even when each write is idempotent?
A: Idempotent upserts collapse duplicates, but if two corrections to the same ISIN arrive out of order, the sink ends on the wrong final value because last-write-wins on a non-monotonic field is order-sensitive. You preserve order by partitioning on the ISIN so corrections are processed in produced sequence, or you carry a version and apply only if newer. Dedup alone does not give correctness when the effect depends on order.

Q: How does a monotonic version or sequence number on each event make an upsert safe against reordering as well as duplication?
A: You store the version with the row and apply an update only when the incoming version is strictly greater (`... WHERE incoming.version > existing.version`), so a stale or duplicate event is ignored and the latest always wins regardless of arrival order. This combines dedup and ordering correctness in one conditional upsert. For NAV corrections the version is naturally the correction sequence or a source timestamp guaranteed monotonic per ISIN.

Q: An idempotent sink upserts by `(isin, valuation_date)` but the source occasionally re-keys an instrument's ISIN. What guarantee breaks?
A: Dedup correctness breaks: if the same logical instrument is delivered under a changed ISIN, the upsert treats it as a new key and the duplicate is not absorbed, producing two rows for one economic instrument. The business key must be stable for the lifetime of the entity; an ISIN that can be reassigned is not fully stable. You either key on a stable internal instrument ID or maintain an ISIN-history mapping so re-keys collapse.

Q: Why is a UUID generated by the consumer a poor idempotency key, and what is the correct alternative?
A: A consumer-generated UUID is minted fresh on each processing attempt, so a reprocessed record gets a different UUID and the duplicate is not recognised — it provides uniqueness, not identity-across-retries. The correct key is one assigned at the source and carried in the event (instruction reference, ISIN plus period), identical on every redelivery. Idempotency keys must originate upstream of the duplication point, never downstream of it.

Q: In the transactional-outbox pattern, why does the relay only need at-least-once delivery to be safe?
A: Because the outbox event carries the source-assigned event ID and the downstream consumer dedups on it, so the relay duplicating an outbox row simply produces a duplicate the sink absorbs. The hard atomicity — state change and event emission together — was already solved by writing both in the same local transaction. The relay's job is just to never lose an outbox row, which at-least-once with offset tracking provides.

Q: Why does the outbox pattern solve the dual-write problem that a direct write-to-DB-then-publish-to-Kafka does not?
A: A direct write-then-publish has two independent commits, so a crash between them leaves the database updated but the event unpublished (or vice versa), with no way to atomically reconcile. The outbox writes the state change and the event into one local transaction, so they are atomic, and a separate relay later ships the event at-least-once. The single local transaction is what removes the race; the relay's duplicates are harmless because of dedup.

Q: How does the Debezium outbox event router fit the end-to-end exactly-once story for fund events?
A: It reads the outbox table via CDC, so an event is captured if and only if the state change committed, and routes each row to a topic, preserving the event ID for downstream dedup. This gives at-least-once delivery over a boundary made effectively-once by the consumer's idempotent apply. It is the standard way a transfer-agency database emits ordered, atomic events without dual writes.

Q: Why is exactly-once across two Kafka clusters (for example via MirrorMaker 2) not guaranteed out of the box?
A: MirrorMaker 2 replicates records and translates offsets but historically does not preserve transactional boundaries or producer transactional state across clusters, so the destination cluster sees at-least-once with possible duplicates after a replication restart. The mirrored topic's transaction markers do not carry the original atomicity. To get exactly-once-effect downstream you dedup on a business key at the consuming side of the target cluster.

Q: When mirroring topics between regions for DORA resilience, what delivery assumption should the consumer in the secondary region make?
A: It should assume at-least-once with possible reordering across the replication link and therefore apply idempotent, business-key-based processing so a failover or replication catch-up cannot duplicate effects. Cross-cluster replication does not carry the source's transactional exactly-once. Designing the secondary consumer to be idempotent is what makes a regional failover safe rather than a source of duplicated settlements.

Q: How do you prove to an auditor that your kill-restart test actually exercised the duplicate window rather than passing by luck?
A: You instrument the consumer to log the offset and the sink key at each step and `kill -9` it during the deliberate gap between applying the effect and committing the offset, then show the restart reprocessed exactly those keys and that the idempotent sink left identical row counts and values. Repeating across many kills and showing the sink invariant holds every time is the evidence. A single clean run could miss the window; reproducible kills inside the gap demonstrate the guarantee.

Q: What sink-side invariant do you assert in an automated test to confirm effectively-once after replays?
A: That the final state is a pure function of the set of distinct business keys delivered, independent of how many times each was delivered — for example, row count equals distinct instruction references and each NAV equals the last value for its key. You assert it after injecting deliberate duplicates and replays. If the invariant holds under arbitrary redelivery, the sink is effectively-once; if a count or sum drifts, it is not.

Q: Why is a checksum or row-count reconciliation between source and sink a necessary complement to an exactly-once claim?
A: Because configuration can be correct yet a bug, schema mismatch, or filtered record still cause divergence, so you periodically reconcile counts and hashes by business key between the source of record and the sink to catch silent drift. Exactly-once mechanics reduce the chance of duplicates and losses but do not by themselves prove the two stores agree. For regulated NAV data this reconciliation is the standing control that backs the design-time guarantee diagram.

Q: How does `acks=0` (fire-and-forget) on a producer create at-most-once at the producer edge, and when is it acceptable?
A: With `acks=0` the producer does not wait for any broker acknowledgement and never retries, so a record lost in flight or dropped by a momentarily unavailable broker is gone with no duplicate — pure at-most-once. It maximises throughput and minimises latency, which suits disposable, high-volume telemetry where an occasional gap is invisible in aggregate. It is categorically wrong for settlement, NAV, or any flow where a single lost record is a regulatory or financial event.

Q: What metric would you alert on to catch an open transaction stalling `read_committed` consumers before it becomes an incident?
A: You alert on the gap between the high watermark and the Last Stable Offset per partition (equivalently, growing lag on `read_committed` consumers while producers are clearly active), because a widening gap means an uncommitted transaction is holding the LSO back. Pairing this with an alert on long-lived transactions approaching `transaction.timeout.ms` catches a hung transactional producer early. Proactive LSO-lag monitoring turns a stuck-consumer incident into a caught-early warning.

Q: Summarise the architect's audit checklist for any edge claiming exactly-once into a sink.
A: Confirm the delivery into the edge is at-least-once (no silent loss), identify the stable business key, verify the sink applies effects idempotently or under a two-phase commit tied to the engine, and check ordering if the effect is order-sensitive. Then validate the configuration (`enable.idempotence`, `transactional.id`, `isolation.level=read_committed`, timeouts) and prove it with kill-restart and reconciliation evidence. Only an edge passing all of these earns the exactly-once label on the diagram.
