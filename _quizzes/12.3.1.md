# Phase 5 · 12.3.1 FinOps / cloud cost management — 100 self-test questions

Q: What is FinOps in one sentence, and what problem does it solve?
A: FinOps is the operational practice of bringing financial accountability to the variable spend of cloud, so engineering, finance, and business teams make cost-aware decisions together. It solves the core cloud problem that anyone can spin up resources and the bill is only discovered later, by making cost a continuously-managed engineering metric rather than a quarterly surprise. For a fund platform it turns "the NAV pipeline costs something" into "this run costs €X and here is why."

Q: Name the three phases of the FinOps lifecycle defined by the FinOps Foundation.
A: The three phases are Inform, Optimize, and Operate, and they form an iterative loop rather than a one-time sequence. Inform builds visibility and allocation, Optimize takes action to reduce or right-size cost, and Operate embeds the practice into ongoing governance and culture. Outputs from Operate feed back into Inform, so the organisation continuously cycles for improvement.

Q: What does the Inform phase of the FinOps lifecycle focus on?
A: Inform is about visibility, allocation, and benchmarking — getting accurate, timely, allocated cost and usage data into the hands of the people who can act on it. Without trustworthy allocation you cannot tell a business unit what it spends, so optimisation has no target. Typical Inform outputs are tagged cost reports, showback dashboards, and unit-economics baselines.

Q: What does the Optimize phase of the FinOps lifecycle focus on?
A: Optimize is where you take action to reduce waste and improve efficiency — right-sizing compute, buying commitments like reservations or savings plans, deleting idle resources, and choosing cheaper storage formats or tiers. It depends on the Inform phase, because you can only optimise what you can see and attribute. The output is concrete, quantified savings against a known baseline.

Q: What does the Operate phase of the FinOps lifecycle focus on?
A: Operate embeds FinOps into ongoing business processes — governance, policies, automation, budgets, and cultural accountability — so cost management is continuous rather than a one-off project. It establishes the rituals (monthly cost reviews, budget alerts, anomaly response) that keep the practice alive. Its outputs change the targets and scope that flow back into Inform.

Q: The FinOps Foundation describes a maturity model with three stages. What are they?
A: The maturity stages are Crawl, Walk, and Run, applied within each capability rather than to the organisation as a whole. Crawl means basic, manual, reactive practice; Walk means more automated and consistent; Run means fully automated, proactive, and embedded. The point is that you mature each capability incrementally rather than expecting full sophistication everywhere at once.

Q: What does "unit economics" mean in a FinOps context?
A: Unit economics expresses cost as a rate against a meaningful business unit of output rather than as a raw monthly total — for example € per pipeline run, € per report served, or € per GB delivered. It is the discipline that lets you compare designs, defend a build-vs-buy case, and tell whether growth is profitable. A falling total bill can still be bad if cost-per-unit is rising while volume is collapsing.

Q: Why is a defensible "€ per daily NAV pipeline run" figure so valuable to a fund-administration architect?
A: It converts an abstract architecture into a number the CFO can reason about, so the build-vs-buy and vendor-selection debates are decided on economics rather than aesthetics. If you can state and defend every line of "€ per NAV run," you win the argument against whoever cannot. It also makes regressions visible: if the per-run cost doubles after a change, that is an immediate signal.

Q: Why do cost models decide architectures, not just budgets?
A: Because the cheapest design at one volume can be the most expensive at another, and a chosen format, SKU, or egress path locks in a cost curve for years. An architect who cannot price a design will pick on elegance and discover the bill in production. Pricing the options up front turns a guess into an engineering decision.

Q: What is the difference between cost (the invoice total) and unit cost, and why does the distinction matter?
A: Cost is the absolute amount billed; unit cost is that amount divided by units of business value delivered. The distinction matters because a rising total with falling unit cost can be healthy growth, while a flat total hiding a rising unit cost is silent inefficiency. FinOps optimises for value, which is a unit-cost question, not just for a smaller invoice.

Q: How would you compute "€ per report served" for a regulatory reporting service?
A: Sum the allocated monthly cost of the compute, storage, query, and egress attributable to that service, then divide by the number of reports it produced that month. Be explicit about what is included (e.g. does it carry a share of the platform's fixed cost?) because the boundary choice changes the number. The figure is only defensible if the allocation behind it is traceable to tags or resource groups.

Q: What is "showback" in cost allocation?
A: Showback is reporting each team or business unit its share of cloud cost for visibility, without actually moving money or charging an internal budget. It creates accountability and behaviour change through transparency rather than through a billing transaction. It is usually the first step before an organisation is ready for chargeback.

Q: What is "chargeback" and how does it differ from showback?
A: Chargeback actually allocates and bills cloud cost back to each business unit's budget, so the cost lands on their P&L, whereas showback only reports it for visibility. Chargeback creates stronger incentives because real money moves, but it demands very accurate allocation or it triggers disputes. Many organisations run showback first and graduate to chargeback once tagging is trustworthy.

Q: Why is accurate tagging the foundation of cost allocation?
A: Because allocation rules and showback/chargeback reports are built by grouping and filtering on tags (or resource groups and subscriptions), untagged resources become an unallocatable "unknown" bucket that erodes trust in every number. If 30% of spend is untagged, no business unit's figure is defensible. Tagging discipline is therefore an Inform-phase prerequisite, not a cosmetic nicety.

Q: In Azure, name three mechanisms you can use to allocate cost to a business unit.
A: Tags (key/value metadata on resources), resource groups, and the subscription or management-group hierarchy are the three primary allocation handles. Azure Cost Management lets you group and filter by all of them and define cost-allocation rules to redistribute shared costs. A common pattern is one subscription per business unit plus tags for finer cost-centre and environment splits.

Q: What is a "cost allocation rule" in Azure Cost Management used for?
A: It redistributes shared or common costs — like a shared monitoring workspace, networking, or a platform team's resources — from a source onto target subscriptions or resource groups by a chosen percentage or proportional split. This stops shared infrastructure sitting in an unallocated bucket and makes chargeback fair. Without it, the platform team appears to consume cost that really belongs to its consumers.

Q: A fund admin runs one Azure subscription per fund family for chargeback. What is one risk of relying solely on subscription boundaries for allocation?
A: Shared platform resources (the orchestration layer, shared storage, monitoring) do not belong to any single fund and would either skew one subscription or fall into an unallocated bucket. You also lose finer-grained views like cost per environment or per data domain that tags would give. The fix is to combine subscription boundaries with consistent tags and a cost-allocation rule for shared services.

Q: What are the main Azure Blob Storage access tiers, ordered from most to least expensive to store?
A: From most to least expensive per GB to store: Hot, Cool, Cold, then Archive. Hot has the highest storage price but no retrieval charge; each colder tier lowers storage cost but adds retrieval cost and latency, with Archive being offline and needing a rehydration step. The Cold tier was added in 2023 to sit between Cool and Archive.

Q: What is the key cost trade-off as you move from Hot to Cool to Cold to Archive storage?
A: You trade lower per-GB storage cost for higher per-GB retrieval (read/access) cost and increasing minimum-retention requirements and latency. Hot is cheap to read but expensive to store; Archive is the cheapest to store but expensive and slow to read and is offline. The right tier therefore depends on how often the data is actually accessed, not just on how old it is.

Q: Each cooler Azure Blob tier has a minimum storage duration. What are they and what happens if you delete early?
A: Cool requires a 30-day minimum, Cold a 90-day minimum, and Archive a 180-day minimum. Deleting or moving a blob before its minimum incurs an early-deletion charge prorated for the remaining days, as if it had stayed the full minimum. This is the classic gotcha that makes "just archive everything" backfire on short-lived data.

Q: Why can moving infrequently-read data to the Archive tier sometimes increase cost?
A: If the data is actually read more often than expected, the per-GB retrieval charges plus rehydration latency can exceed what you saved on storage, and early deletion before 180 days triggers a penalty. Archive only wins for data that is written, rarely if ever read, and kept long. Tiering decisions must be driven by measured access patterns, not assumptions.

Q: What is Azure Storage lifecycle management and how does it help FinOps?
A: It is a policy engine that automatically transitions blobs between tiers (Hot to Cool to Cold to Archive) or deletes them based on rules like last-modified or last-accessed age. It removes the manual effort of tiering and captures storage savings continuously, which is exactly the Operate-phase automation FinOps wants. For NAV history, a policy could keep recent files Hot and archive year-old files automatically.

Q: What is data egress in cloud pricing and why is it a notorious cost driver?
A: Egress is data leaving the cloud provider's network — to the internet or across regions — and it is metered per GB, often at a far higher rate than the storage itself. It is notorious because it is easy to incur accidentally (cross-region reads, chatty APIs, large downloads) and hard to see until the bill arrives. Ingress (data in) is typically free, which fools people into ignoring data-movement design.

Q: How does cross-region data transfer differ from internet egress in Azure pricing?
A: Internet egress is data leaving Azure to the public internet, while cross-region transfer is data moving between Azure regions; both are billed per GB but at different rates, and data within the same region is often free or cheaper. Inter-region traffic is a frequent hidden cost when a workload reads from storage in another region. The cheapest design keeps compute and the data it reads in the same region.

Q: Name three concrete techniques to reduce egress cost.
A: Co-locate compute with the data it reads so transfers stay in-region; cache or use a CDN so repeated reads do not re-egress from origin; and compress or use columnar formats with predicate pushdown so less data physically moves. Avoiding unnecessary cross-region replication and batching API responses also help. The principle is to move less data and move it shorter distances.

Q: What is a compute SKU and why does choosing the wrong one waste money?
A: A SKU (stock-keeping unit) is a specific priced configuration of a service — for a VM it is the family, size, vCPU, memory, and sometimes accelerator. Choosing the wrong one wastes money because an oversized SKU pays for headroom you never use, while a wrong-family SKU (e.g. memory-optimised for a CPU-bound job) pays a premium for the wrong resource. Matching the SKU to the actual workload shape is core right-sizing.

Q: What is an Azure Reservation (Reserved Instance) and what discount can it give?
A: A reservation is a one-year or three-year commitment to a specific resource configuration (e.g. a particular VM size in a region) in exchange for a deep discount versus pay-as-you-go, with Reserved VM Instances reaching up to around 72% off, or higher in some Hybrid Benefit cases. The trade is flexibility for price: you save a lot but lock into that exact config. It suits steady, predictable workloads unlikely to change.

Q: What is an Azure Savings Plan for Compute and how does it differ from a reservation?
A: A savings plan commits you to a fixed hourly compute spend ($/hour) for one or three years, and that commitment applies automatically to any eligible compute across VM sizes, types, and regions, with discounts up to around 65%. Unlike a reservation, it is not tied to a specific configuration, so it is more flexible but usually a slightly smaller discount. Reservations win on depth; savings plans win on flexibility.

Q: When would you choose a reservation over a savings plan, and vice versa?
A: Choose a reservation when the workload is steady and the exact SKU/region is stable, because it gives the deeper discount; choose a savings plan when the compute mix changes across sizes, families, or regions, because the commitment follows the spend rather than a config. Many estates blend both: reservations for the rock-stable baseline and a savings plan for the variable remainder. Both come in one- and three-year terms.

Q: What are Azure Spot VMs and what is the catch that makes them cheap?
A: Spot VMs use Azure's spare capacity at a steep discount, but Azure can evict them with little notice when it needs the capacity back, and they can be unavailable when capacity is tight. The catch is that they are interruptible, so they only suit fault-tolerant, restartable, or batch workloads. They are wrong for a latency-sensitive or stateful service that cannot tolerate sudden eviction.

Q: Could a daily NAV calculation batch run on Spot VMs? What must be true first?
A: It can, if the job is checkpointed/idempotent and the schedule has enough slack to retry after an eviction without breaking the NAV publication deadline. The risk is that a regulated daily NAV has a hard cut-off, so an eviction near the deadline with no fallback is a correctness/timeliness failure, not just a cost event. A common pattern is spot with an on-demand fallback so the deadline is always met.

Q: What is the difference between provisioned (dedicated) and serverless compute pricing models?
A: Provisioned compute is billed for the capacity you reserve whether or not you use it (you pay for the VM or cluster while it is on), whereas serverless is billed per unit of actual work — per request, per second, or per data scanned — and idles to near-zero. Provisioned rewards high, steady utilisation; serverless rewards spiky or low-duty-cycle workloads. The art is finding where one becomes cheaper than the other.

Q: What is the "serverless-vs-provisioned crossover" and why must an architect find it?
A: The crossover is the utilisation point where the cost of pay-per-use serverless equals the cost of always-on provisioned capacity; below it serverless is cheaper, above it provisioned wins. The architect must find it because picking the wrong side at the actual duty cycle can double the bill. A workload running 5% of the day is usually serverless territory; one running 80% steadily favours provisioned with a commitment.

Q: A NAV pipeline runs intensely for 90 minutes once a day. Serverless or provisioned, and why?
A: Serverless or auto-suspending compute is usually right, because the workload is below the crossover — paying for capacity 24 hours to use it for 90 minutes wastes roughly 94% of a provisioned bill. You pay only for the burst plus startup overhead. The exception is if cold-start latency or per-second compute premiums for that 90 minutes exceed the idle savings, which you verify by pricing both.

Q: How does storage format efficiency act as a FinOps lever?
A: Columnar, compressed formats like Parquet shrink the bytes stored and, crucially, the bytes scanned per query, which lowers storage, egress, and per-TB-scanned query costs all at once. Row formats like CSV store and scan far more data for the same logical content. Converting a hot CSV landing zone to compressed Parquet is often one of the highest-leverage, lowest-risk cost wins.

Q: Why does columnar format reduce query cost in scan-priced engines, not just storage cost?
A: Engines priced per TB scanned (and even cluster-based ones) only read the columns a query needs and can skip row groups via statistics/predicate pushdown, so a query touching 3 of 50 columns scans a fraction of the data. Compression further reduces the physical bytes read. CSV forces a full read of every row and column, so the same query scans and bills far more.

Q: What is cluster right-sizing and what waste does it eliminate?
A: Right-sizing matches a cluster's node count and size to the workload's real resource needs, eliminating the waste of idle vCPUs and unused memory you pay for in an oversized cluster. It also covers shutting clusters down when idle. Telemetry-driven sizing typically finds clusters running at low utilisation that can shrink with no performance loss.

Q: List the four "big levers" this lesson highlights for cutting data-platform cost.
A: Storage-format efficiency (columnar/compressed), cluster right-sizing (and idle shutdown), choosing correctly across the serverless-vs-provisioned crossover, and egress avoidance (co-location, caching, less data movement). They are called the big levers because each can move the bill by a large fraction with modest engineering effort. The Optimize phase is largely about pulling these.

Q: When asked to quantify a lever's saving, what is the disciplined way to express it?
A: Price the model with the lever pulled and without it under the same assumptions, then state the delta in both absolute terms and as a percentage of the baseline, naming the line items that changed. For example "moving the landing zone to Parquet cut monthly query scan from 40 TB to 9 TB, saving roughly €X (Y%) on query." A saving claim with no measured before/after is not defensible.

Q: What is Azure Cost Management and what is it for?
A: Azure Cost Management (part of Cost Management + Billing) is Microsoft's native tooling for analysing, monitoring, allocating, and governing Azure spend, with cost analysis views, budgets, alerts, allocation rules, and exports. It is the primary corporate tool for the Inform and Operate phases on Azure. It answers "where is the money going and is it within budget?"

Q: What is an Azure budget and what does it actually do when exceeded?
A: A budget is a spending threshold you set on a scope (subscription, resource group, or tag filter) over a time period, and it triggers alerts (and optionally automation via action groups) when actual or forecast spend crosses configured percentages. Crucially, a standard budget does not stop spending by itself — it notifies; enforcement requires you to wire an action like scaling down or disabling resources. So a budget is a tripwire, not a hard cap by default.

Q: What is the difference between an actual-cost alert and a forecast alert in Azure budgets?
A: An actual-cost alert fires when spend already incurred crosses the threshold, whereas a forecast alert fires when Azure predicts spend will cross it by the end of the period based on the current trend. Forecast alerts give earlier warning so you can act before the budget is blown. Using both gives an early heads-up and a confirmation when the line is actually crossed.

Q: What is a cost anomaly and why does anomaly detection matter even with budgets in place?
A: A cost anomaly is an unexpected, statistically unusual change in spend — a sudden spike or a new resource burning money — that a fixed budget threshold might not catch if it is still under the monthly limit. Anomaly detection flags the shape of the change, catching a runaway job or a mis-tier early. It matters because a slow leak or a one-off spike can stay under budget yet still be pure waste.

Q: What is FOCUS in the FinOps world?
A: FOCUS is the FinOps Open Cost and Usage Specification, an open, vendor-neutral schema that normalises cost and usage billing data into a common set of columns and definitions across clouds and SaaS providers. It exists so practitioners can analyse multi-cloud spend without learning each provider's idiosyncratic billing export format. It is governed under the FinOps Foundation and the Linux Foundation.

Q: Why does a multi-cloud or multi-vendor fund platform benefit from FOCUS?
A: Because each provider's native billing export uses different column names, units, and semantics, building one allocation and reporting pipeline normally means custom parsers per vendor. FOCUS gives one schema so the same showback/chargeback logic works across Azure, AWS, GCP, and conformant SaaS. For an estate spanning a primary cloud plus SaaS data tools, that is a large maintenance saving.

Q: At what level should an architect know FOCUS for this lesson?
A: At awareness level — you should know what FOCUS is, why a normalised billing schema matters, and that major clouds offer FOCUS-conformant exports, without needing to memorise every column. The lesson lists it explicitly as "awareness." Knowing it exists lets you design a vendor-neutral cost pipeline rather than hand-rolling per-cloud parsers.

Q: Roughly where is the FOCUS specification in its release history as of 2026?
A: FOCUS reached 1.0 in 2024, 1.2 became generally available in May 2025 (adding SaaS/PaaS support and multi-currency), and 1.3 was ratified in December 2025 (adding a contract-commitment dataset and allocation columns). The practical takeaway is that it is a maturing, actively-versioned standard with growing cloud and SaaS coverage. Cite a specific version only when you have checked the current release.

Q: What problem does FOCUS's multi-currency support solve for a Luxembourg fund administrator?
A: A Lux fund admin reports in EUR but may consume cloud and SaaS billed in USD, GBP, or other currencies across regions, so normalising currency in the billing schema lets cost reports roll up consistently in the reporting currency. Without it, FX conversion is bolted on inconsistently and chargeback figures drift. FOCUS 1.2 added the columns to express both billing and pricing currency.

Q: What are the four "FinOps Framework Domains" introduced in the 2024 framework update?
A: The four domains are Understand Cloud Usage & Cost, Quantify Business Value, Optimize Cloud Usage & Cost, and Manage the FinOps Practice. They represent the fundamental business outcomes a FinOps practice should deliver and group the underlying Capabilities. They complement, rather than replace, the Inform/Optimize/Operate lifecycle.

Q: What are "Scopes" in the FinOps Framework as of the 2025 update?
A: Scopes were added as a core element to extend FinOps beyond public cloud to other technology spend such as SaaS, data centres, AI, and licensing. The idea is that the same FinOps disciplines (visibility, allocation, optimisation) apply across more cost domains than just IaaS/PaaS. It reflects the framework maturing from "cloud cost" toward broader "technology value" management.

Q: What is a "FinOps Capability" in the framework?
A: A Capability is a specific, repeatable functional activity the FinOps practice performs — for example allocation, anomaly management, forecasting, or rate optimisation — each with its own Crawl/Walk/Run maturity. Capabilities are the building blocks grouped under the Domains. You assess and improve the practice one capability at a time.

Q: Who are the typical "personas" FinOps coordinates between, and why does this matter?
A: FinOps coordinates engineering, finance, and business/product (and leadership/procurement), because cloud cost decisions cut across all of them and none can optimise alone. Engineers control the resources, finance owns the budget and reporting, and the business defines what value is worth paying for. The discipline exists precisely because these groups historically did not speak a common cost language.

Q: Why is "FinOps is a culture, not a tool" a meaningful statement?
A: Because buying a cost dashboard does not by itself change behaviour — savings come from engineers and finance routinely making cost-aware decisions, which is the Operate-phase cultural work. A tool surfaces data, but humans must own budgets, run reviews, and act on alerts. Many organisations buy the tool and stall because they skip the accountability and ritual.

Q: What is the Azure Pricing Calculator and what is it the right tool for?
A: It is Microsoft's interactive estimator where you configure services (region, SKU, hours, storage, egress) and it produces a monthly cost estimate, making it the lab bench for building a cost model before deploying. It is right for designing and comparing architectures up front. It is an estimate, so real bills can differ due to actual usage, tiering, and discounts not modelled.

Q: When building a cost model in the calculator, what assumptions must you write down to keep it defensible?
A: Region, data volume, number of runs/queries, storage tier and retention, compute SKU and hours/duty cycle, egress GB, and any committed-discount assumption (reservation/savings plan). Writing them down is what lets you defend each line to a CFO and re-run the model at a new volume. An estimate with hidden assumptions is just a number nobody can challenge.

Q: The lesson asks you to price the architecture at 1x and again at 10x volume. What is the purpose?
A: The purpose is to see the cost shape — which lines scale linearly with volume, which step at thresholds (a bigger SKU, a new cluster), and which stay flat — so you understand how the bill behaves as the fund business grows. A design that is cheap at 1x can be ruinous at 10x if a dominant line scales super-linearly. It exposes the cost curve, not just a point.

Q: What does it mean for a cost line to "step" rather than scale when volume increases?
A: A line steps when cost jumps discretely at a capacity threshold — for example moving from one cluster node to two, or up a SKU tier — rather than rising smoothly with each extra GB or run. Stepping lines are dangerous because the next unit of growth can trigger a large discrete jump. Identifying which lines step at 10x tells you where headroom runs out.

Q: At 10x volume, which cost lines typically scale linearly in a data platform?
A: Storage volume, per-TB-scanned query cost, and per-GB egress typically scale roughly linearly with data volume, because they are metered per unit. Compute can scale linearly too if you simply run more of the same job, but often steps when you must size up clusters. Knowing the linear lines lets you forecast the bill from a volume forecast.

Q: How do you identify "the three biggest levers" in a given cost model?
A: Sort the model lines by their share of total cost (and by how they scale at 10x), then target the largest, most volume-sensitive lines where a known technique applies — usually storage format, compute sizing/commitment, and egress. The biggest lever is rarely the smallest line. You confirm by re-pricing with each pulled and measuring the delta.

Q: What is the FinOps "Crawl/Walk/Run" relevance to a brand-new fund data platform?
A: A new platform should start at Crawl: get basic tagging and a single cost dashboard working before attempting automated anomaly response or sophisticated chargeback. Trying to "Run" every capability on day one usually fails because the allocation data is not trustworthy yet. You mature each capability as the data and culture catch up.

Q: What is "rate optimization" versus "usage optimization" in FinOps?
A: Rate optimization lowers the price you pay per unit (reservations, savings plans, spot, negotiated discounts) without changing what you run, while usage optimization reduces how much you consume (right-sizing, deleting idle resources, efficient formats). Both reduce cost but through different levers and owners. Mature practices pursue both; rate optimization alone can lock in commitments for wasteful usage.

Q: Why can buying reservations before right-sizing be a mistake?
A: Because a reservation locks in a commitment to a specific (often oversized) configuration for one to three years, so you would be paying a discounted rate for capacity you should have shrunk. The correct order is usually usage optimization first (right-size), then rate optimization (commit) on the reduced, stable baseline. Committing to waste just makes the waste cheaper, not gone.

Q: What is "showback/chargeback maturity" and how do tags gate it?
A: It is the progression from merely reporting costs (showback) to billing them back to budgets (chargeback), and tag quality gates it because chargeback disputes erupt the moment an unallocated or mistagged cost lands on the wrong unit's P&L. You generally cannot do credible chargeback until tagging coverage is high and consistent. So tag-policy enforcement is the prerequisite investment.

Q: What is an Azure tag policy and how does it improve cost allocation?
A: Azure Policy can require specific tags on resources, deny creation of untagged resources, or inherit tags from resource groups, which raises tagging coverage and consistency. Better coverage shrinks the unallocatable bucket and makes showback/chargeback trustworthy. Enforcing tags at creation is far cheaper than retrofitting tags across a sprawling estate later.

Q: A cost report shows 25% of spend as "untagged." Why is this a FinOps red flag and what do you do?
A: It is a red flag because a quarter of the bill cannot be attributed, so no business unit's figure is defensible and chargeback is impossible for that slice. First find the largest untagged resources and tag them or apply a cost-allocation rule, then enforce a tag policy so new resources cannot be created untagged. The leak is in governance, not just reporting.

Q: What does "the bill is discovered in production" mean and how does FinOps prevent it?
A: It means a design's true cost only becomes visible once it is running at scale, after the architecture is locked in and expensive to change. FinOps prevents it by pricing the model up front (calculator, unit economics) and monitoring with budgets and anomaly alerts so cost is a design input and an operational metric, not a postmortem. The cheapest time to fix a cost mistake is before deployment.

Q: Why is egress sometimes called an "architectural" cost rather than an operational one?
A: Because egress is largely determined by where you place compute and data and how components talk to each other — decisions baked into the architecture — rather than by a tuning knob you turn later. A chatty cross-region design egresses constantly no matter how you operate it. The cheapest egress is the transfer the design never makes, which is why co-location is an architecture choice.

Q: How does a CDN reduce both egress and latency for a served dataset?
A: A CDN caches content at edge locations close to users, so repeated requests are served from the edge instead of re-egressing from origin storage each time, cutting origin egress and per-request latency. For a frequently-downloaded fund factsheet or EMT file, caching the static artefact avoids paying origin egress on every download. The trade-off is cache freshness for data that changes.

Q: What is the FinOps risk of serving frequently-read fund data straight from cold/archive storage?
A: Cold and Archive tiers charge high per-GB retrieval (and Archive requires slow rehydration), so frequently-read data there can cost far more in retrieval than it saves in storage, and Archive cannot meet interactive latency at all. Frequently-read data belongs in Hot (optionally behind a cache). Tiering must follow the measured access pattern, and "old" does not always mean "cold-accessible."

Q: What does "right-sizing" mean for a managed query/warehouse service versus a VM?
A: For a VM it means choosing an appropriately small SKU; for a managed warehouse it means choosing the right compute size/class and ensuring it auto-suspends when idle so you stop paying between queries. The shared idea is to stop paying for idle or oversized capacity. The mechanism differs by service, but the lever — match paid capacity to real demand — is the same.

Q: What is auto-suspend (auto-pause) and why is it a key FinOps control for bursty data workloads?
A: Auto-suspend stops a compute resource (warehouse, cluster, serverless pool) after a period of inactivity so you stop being billed while it sits idle, then resumes on the next request. It is key for bursty workloads — like a NAV job that runs minutes a day — because the dominant waste in provisioned data compute is idle uptime. A short auto-suspend timer can eliminate most of that idle cost.

Q: An idle dev cluster ran all weekend and blew the budget. What is the first FinOps control to add?
A: Add auto-suspend/auto-shutdown so the cluster stops after a short idle period, and pair it with a budget/anomaly alert scoped to that resource group so the next leak is caught early. The root issue is paying for idle capacity, which auto-suspend directly fixes. A schedule that shuts non-prod down outside business hours is a complementary control.

Q: What is the difference between "forecast" and "budget" in cost governance?
A: A forecast is a prediction of where spend is heading based on current usage trends, while a budget is a chosen target/threshold you measure actual and forecast spend against. The forecast tells you the likely outcome; the budget tells you whether that outcome is acceptable and triggers alerts. Forecast alerts let you intervene before the budget is actually breached.

Q: Why does FinOps treat cost as a "shared responsibility"?
A: Because the people who create cost (engineers provisioning resources) are not usually the people who pay it (finance) or who define its value (the business), so no single team can optimise alone. Making it shared means engineers see and own the cost of their choices while finance and business set targets and value. Centralising it in finance alone removes the leverage of the people who actually control spend.

Q: What is "cost avoidance" versus "cost savings"?
A: Cost savings reduce a bill you are already paying (e.g. deleting an idle resource), whereas cost avoidance prevents future cost you would otherwise incur (e.g. designing out an egress path before launch). Both add value but avoidance is harder to measure because the cost never appears. FinOps counts both, and architecture-stage decisions are mostly avoidance.

Q: How does compression interact with both storage and egress pricing?
A: Compression reduces the physical bytes stored and the bytes transferred, so it lowers both per-GB storage and per-GB egress simultaneously, and in scan-priced engines it reduces bytes scanned too. The cost is CPU to compress/decompress, which is usually far cheaper than the storage and transfer it saves. For large fund datasets this is a near-free, high-leverage win.

Q: What is "predicate pushdown" and how does it lower query cost?
A: Predicate pushdown means the engine applies filters as early as possible — using file/column statistics to skip row groups or partitions that cannot match — so it reads less data from storage. Less data read means lower per-TB-scanned cost and less egress/IO. It works best with columnar, statistics-rich formats like Parquet and good partitioning.

Q: How does partitioning a dataset by date reduce query cost for time-bounded reports?
A: Partitioning physically organises data by a key like date so a query for one day only scans that partition instead of the whole dataset, cutting bytes scanned and therefore scan-priced query cost. For daily NAV or transaction queries this is a large saving because reports are usually time-bounded. Over-partitioning, though, creates tiny files and metadata overhead, so balance is needed.

Q: What is the "small files problem" and how does it affect cost?
A: It is when a dataset is split into many tiny files, which forces engines to open and list huge numbers of objects, increasing per-operation and metadata costs and hurting scan efficiency. It commonly arises from over-partitioning or streaming writes. Compaction into right-sized files restores scan efficiency and lowers both query time and cost.

Q: Why does FinOps recommend tagging an environment dimension (prod/dev/test) on resources?
A: Because it lets you see and act on non-production spend separately — non-prod is often a large, easily-cut share that can be auto-shut-down outside hours without business risk. Without an environment tag you cannot tell prod from dev in the bill. It also prevents accidentally cost-optimising production into a performance incident.

Q: What is a "reservation utilisation" metric and why monitor it?
A: Reservation utilisation measures how much of a purchased commitment is actually being matched to eligible usage; under-utilisation means you bought a commitment you are not consuming, wasting the discount. You monitor it because workloads change and a once-perfect reservation can drift to idle. Low utilisation signals you should exchange, resize, or switch to a more flexible savings plan.

Q: What is "reservation coverage" and how does it relate to utilisation?
A: Coverage is the fraction of eligible usage that is covered by commitments (reservations/savings plans) versus paid at on-demand rates; utilisation is how much of the commitments you bought is being used. You want high coverage of the stable baseline and high utilisation of what you bought. Low coverage means you are leaving on-demand savings on the table; low utilisation means you over-committed.

Q: For a stable 24x7 baseline plus a spiky daily peak, what commitment strategy is sensible?
A: Cover the stable 24x7 baseline with reservations or a savings plan for the deep discount, and serve the spiky peak with on-demand or spot/serverless so you do not commit to capacity you only need briefly. Committing to the peak would leave the discount idle most of the day. This baseline-plus-burst split is the standard rate-optimisation pattern.

Q: Why must you re-validate commitments periodically rather than "buy and forget"?
A: Because workloads, prices, SKUs, and discount instruments change, so a reservation that was optimal can become idle (low utilisation) or be superseded by a cheaper savings plan or newer SKU. Periodic review keeps coverage and utilisation high and catches drift. "Buy and forget" silently converts a saving into waste over the commitment term.

Q: What is OpenCost and where does it fit in this lesson?
A: OpenCost is an open-source project for Kubernetes cost allocation that attributes cluster cost down to namespaces, workloads, and labels, giving showback/chargeback for containerised platforms. In this lesson it is awareness-level FOSS, comparable to commercial tools like Vantage or Cloudability. It matters where a data platform runs on Kubernetes and you need per-team cluster cost.

Q: Why is Kubernetes cost allocation harder than VM cost allocation, motivating tools like OpenCost?
A: Because many workloads share the same nodes, the cloud bill is per-node, not per-pod, so you must apportion shared node cost to namespaces/pods by their resource requests and usage. The cloud provider's bill cannot do that attribution. OpenCost models requests, usage, and labels to split shared cluster cost back to the teams that caused it.

Q: How would you allocate the cost of a shared orchestration cluster across several funds?
A: Tag/label each workload with its fund, then apportion the cluster's cost by each fund's share of resource requests or usage (via a tool like OpenCost or an Azure cost-allocation rule), rather than splitting it evenly. Even splits are unfair when funds use very different amounts. The allocation must trace to a measurable usage signal to survive a chargeback dispute.

Q: What is the FinOps argument against choosing an architecture on "aesthetics"?
A: Aesthetics (elegance, fashion, familiarity) say nothing about the bill, so an aesthetically pleasing design can be the expensive one, and the cost is only discovered in production when it is hard to change. The FinOps argument is to price each option's unit economics so the decision is made on defensible numbers. Elegance is a tiebreaker, not the selection criterion.

Q: How does a cost model strengthen a build-vs-buy decision with a vendor?
A: It lets you put your in-house "€ per NAV run" next to the vendor's quoted price on the same basis, so the decision is a like-for-like economic comparison rather than a sales pitch. The architect who can price the build wins the argument; the one who cannot defers to whoever can. It also exposes hidden costs (egress, support, integration) the vendor quote may omit.

Q: What is a "blended rate" and why can it mislead unit-economics analysis?
A: A blended rate averages discounted and on-demand prices (or different SKUs) into one figure, which hides that some usage is paying full price; relying on it can mask where rate optimisation is still available. For accurate unit economics you often want unblended rates so you see the true marginal cost. Blended figures are fine for high-level reporting but poor for optimisation decisions.

Q: Why does FinOps emphasise "timely" cost data, not just accurate data?
A: Because cloud spend accrues continuously and a leak left for a month is a month of waste, so data that arrives too late to act on loses most of its value. Near-real-time visibility lets you catch a runaway job in hours, not at month-end close. Accuracy with no timeliness still discovers the bill after it is spent.

Q: What is an Azure cost export and why set one up?
A: A cost export is a scheduled push of detailed cost and usage data (increasingly in FOCUS-conformant form) to storage, enabling custom analysis, long-term retention, and integration into your own dashboards or warehouse. You set it up when the built-in views are not enough — for cross-cloud normalisation, custom chargeback, or feeding a BI tool. It is the data-pipeline foundation for a bespoke FinOps reporting layer.

Q: How can choosing the wrong region raise cost even at identical SKUs?
A: Prices vary by region, and a region far from your data or users increases cross-region transfer and egress; some services or SKUs are also unavailable or pricier in certain regions. So an identical SKU can simply cost more in one region, and the data-movement cost around it can dominate. Region choice is both a rate and an egress decision.

Q: For a Luxembourg-domiciled fund with EU data-residency requirements, how do compliance and FinOps interact on region choice?
A: Data-residency or sovereignty rules (and DORA/regulatory expectations) may mandate an EU region, which constrains the cheapest-region option, so FinOps optimises within the compliant region set rather than globally. You cannot chase a cheaper region that breaches residency. The cost model must therefore start from the allowed regions, then optimise SKUs, tiers, and egress inside them.

Q: Why might "egress avoidance" specifically matter when distributing EMT/EPT files to many counterparties?
A: Because each download of a distributed file is metered egress, so distributing large EMT/EPT files to many counterparties repeatedly can accumulate significant egress cost. Caching/CDN, compression, and serving deltas instead of full files reduce the bytes egressed per recipient. The architecture that minimises repeated full-file transfers minimises this cost.

Q: What is the cost reasoning for storing many years of NAV history in Archive but keeping the current period Hot?
A: Current-period NAV is read frequently for reporting and so belongs in Hot (cheap reads), while multi-year history is rarely accessed and kept for regulatory retention, making Archive's low storage cost worthwhile despite slow, costly retrieval. A lifecycle policy can transition files automatically as they age past their active window. This matches each tier's cost profile to the data's real access pattern and retention obligation.

Q: Why is a long regulatory retention period (e.g. for fund records) a FinOps consideration?
A: Because data you are legally required to keep for years accrues storage cost the whole time, so the tier and format you keep it in materially affects the long-run bill. Archiving compressed, rarely-read records minimises that cost while meeting retention. The FinOps move is to satisfy the retention rule at the cheapest defensible storage profile, not to keep everything Hot.

Q: What does "FinOps shifts cost left" mean for data architecture?
A: "Shifting left" means moving cost consideration earlier — into design and code review — so cost is evaluated before deployment rather than discovered on the bill, mirroring how security shifts left. For data architecture it means pricing formats, SKUs, and egress paths in the design phase. The earlier a cost decision is made, the cheaper it is to get right.

Q: How would you present a 1x-versus-10x cost shape to a CFO in one defensible sentence?
A: Something like "At today's volume the pipeline costs €X per NAV run dominated by compute; at 10x it costs €Y per run because storage and query scan scale linearly while compute steps up two cluster sizes, and pulling format efficiency and a savings plan removes most of the step." It names the unit, the dominant lines, what scales versus steps, and the levers. Every figure in it must trace back to the documented model assumptions.

Q: What is the single most important habit FinOps asks a data architect to adopt for every new design?
A: To build and document a unit-economics cost model (€ per run/report/GB) at expected and stressed volumes before committing to the architecture, then re-check it against real spend in operation. This makes cost a first-class design input and a monitored metric rather than a production surprise. The architect who does this consistently can defend any design to finance and win the build-vs-buy argument.
