# Phase 0 · 0.8 Batch processing & OLTP vs OLAP — 100 self-test questions

Q: What does OLTP stand for, and what kind of work is it for?
A: Online Transaction Processing; it handles many small, frequent reads and writes that capture the day-to-day operations of a business, like a transfer agency recording subscription and redemption orders.

Q: What does OLAP stand for, and what kind of work is it for?
A: Online Analytical Processing; it answers analytical questions by scanning and aggregating large amounts of data, like computing average NAV per fund per month across years of history.

Q: In one phrase, what is the shape of a typical OLTP read?
A: A small, indexed lookup that touches very few rows, e.g. fetch one investor's current holding by primary key.

Q: In one phrase, what is the shape of a typical OLAP read?
A: A big scan that reads many rows across few columns and aggregates them, e.g. `SUM`/`AVG` over millions of NAV records.

Q: OLTP is described as "latency-bound" — what does that mean?
A: The thing you care about is how fast a single small operation completes (milliseconds per query), because users or systems are waiting on each transaction.

Q: OLAP is described as "throughput-bound" — what does that mean?
A: The thing you care about is how much data you can crunch per unit time, because the query touches huge volumes and you optimise bytes-scanned-per-second rather than per-row latency.

Q: Why is response time the key OLTP metric while data volume processed is the key OLAP metric?
A: OLTP serves interactive operations where each must feel instant, so per-operation latency dominates; OLAP serves bulk analysis where total work is enormous, so sustained throughput dominates.

Q: Give a fund-industry example of an OLTP operation.
A: Recording a single subscription order into the transfer-agency order book, or looking up one investor's account balance by ID.

Q: Give a fund-industry example of an OLAP operation.
A: Month-end analysis computing average NAV, total assets under management, or flow trends per fund across all share classes and dates.

Q: Roughly how many rows does a healthy OLTP query touch versus an OLAP query?
A: OLTP touches a handful (often one) row via an index; OLAP touches thousands to millions of rows in a scan.

Q: Why do OLTP systems lean heavily on indexes?
A: Indexes let the engine jump straight to the few rows a transaction needs without scanning the whole table, keeping each operation low-latency.

Q: Why are indexes less central to pure OLAP scans?
A: Analytical queries read most of a table anyway, so a full sequential scan is often cheaper than walking an index for nearly every row.

Q: What is the "access pattern" difference between OLTP and OLAP in one sentence?
A: OLTP does point/small-range access on rows; OLAP does wide scans over columns.

Q: Why does mixing heavy OLAP queries onto your OLTP database hurt operations?
A: A big analytical scan consumes CPU, memory, and I/O and can contend for locks/cache, slowing the small latency-sensitive transactions, e.g. orders crawling at the subscription cutoff.

Q: An architect runs month-end reporting directly on the order-capture database and operations complain subscriptions slowed at cutoff — what happened?
A: The throughput-hungry OLAP scan saturated the resources the latency-bound OLTP workload needed, so transactional queries queued behind the analytics.

Q: What is the standard architectural fix for the "analytics is slowing my transactions" problem?
A: Separate the workloads — keep transactions on the OLTP system and copy/extract data into a separate analytical store (warehouse/columnar engine) for OLAP.

Q: Why does "one database to rule them all" rarely serve both OLTP and OLAP well?
A: The two workloads want opposite physical layouts and tuning — row storage and indexes for small writes/lookups versus columnar storage for wide scans — so optimising for one penalises the other.

Q: What is row-oriented storage?
A: A layout that stores all the values of one row contiguously on disk, so a whole record is fetched together.

Q: What is column-oriented (columnar) storage?
A: A layout that stores all the values of one column contiguously, so a single field across many rows is fetched together.

Q: What does row storage make cheap?
A: Reading or writing an entire single record at once — ideal for OLTP point lookups and inserts/updates of whole rows.

Q: What does column storage make cheap?
A: Reading just a few columns across many rows for aggregation, since you only touch the bytes you need — ideal for OLAP scans.

Q: Why is a row store inefficient for `SELECT AVG(nav) FROM prices` over a wide table?
A: It must read every row's full record off disk just to extract one column, wasting I/O on the columns you didn't ask for.

Q: Why is a column store inefficient for "fetch this one investor's whole record"?
A: The investor's fields are scattered across many separate column files, so assembling one row means reading from many places instead of one contiguous block.

Q: How does columnar storage enable better compression?
A: Values in a single column are the same type and often similar, so run-length, dictionary, and delta encodings compress far better than mixed-type rows.

Q: What is dictionary encoding and why does it help columnar stores?
A: It replaces repeated column values (like ISINs or fund names) with small integer codes referencing a value dictionary, shrinking storage and speeding scans.

Q: What is run-length encoding and where does it shine in column storage?
A: It stores a value plus how many times it repeats consecutively; it shines on sorted or low-cardinality columns like a currency or share-class field.

Q: Why does better compression directly speed up OLAP queries?
A: Less data on disk means fewer bytes to read into memory per scan, and I/O is usually the bottleneck for analytics, so smaller equals faster.

Q: What is "column pruning" and which storage layout benefits from it?
A: Reading only the columns a query references and skipping the rest; columnar storage benefits because columns are physically separate.

Q: What is vectorized execution?
A: Processing data in batches of column values at a time rather than row-by-row, so CPU caches and SIMD instructions are used efficiently — a hallmark of analytical engines.

Q: Why is row-by-row processing slower than vectorized column processing for analytics?
A: Per-row processing pays interpreter/function-call overhead on every row; vectorized engines amortise that over a whole batch of values, hitting CPU caches well.

Q: Which storage layout is better for frequent single-row `UPDATE` statements, and why?
A: Row storage, because the whole record sits together so updating it touches one place instead of rewriting many separate column segments.

Q: Why are columnar stores often append-mostly rather than update-heavy?
A: Updating one row means touching every column segment, so columnar engines favour bulk appends and treat updates as expensive or rewrite-based.

Q: Is Postgres a row store or a column store by default?
A: Row store — it stores tuples (rows) together, which suits its OLTP heritage.

Q: Is DuckDB a row store or a column store?
A: Column store with vectorized execution, purpose-built for analytical (OLAP) queries.

Q: In one line, when would you reach for Postgres over DuckDB?
A: When you need concurrent transactional reads/writes, point lookups, constraints, and many clients — the OLTP shape.

Q: In one line, when would you reach for DuckDB over Postgres?
A: When you need fast single-node analytics — big scans and aggregations over files like Parquet — without standing up a server.

Q: What does it mean that DuckDB is "in-process" / embedded?
A: It runs inside your application or REPL as a library with no separate server to install, connect to, or manage — like SQLite but for analytics.

Q: Postgres uses a client-server model — what does that imply operationally?
A: You run a long-lived server process that clients connect to over a socket/network, giving shared concurrent access, authentication, and durability guarantees.

Q: Why is DuckDB often called "the SQLite of analytics"?
A: It's a single-file, zero-dependency, embedded SQL engine like SQLite, but columnar and tuned for OLAP scans instead of OLTP transactions.

Q: Can DuckDB query a Parquet file directly without importing it first?
A: Yes — e.g. `SELECT * FROM 'prices.parquet'`, it reads the file in place using its columnar layout.

Q: Why is Parquet a natural pairing with DuckDB?
A: Parquet is itself a columnar, compressed file format, so DuckDB can prune columns and read only what a query needs without loading everything.

Q: Why might `SELECT AVG(nav) ...` be much faster on DuckDB-over-Parquet than on Postgres for 1M rows?
A: DuckDB reads only the `nav` column (and any filter columns) in compressed vectorized batches, while Postgres reads full rows and processes them one at a time.

Q: Why might a single-row lookup by fund key be faster on Postgres than on DuckDB?
A: Postgres can use a B-tree index to jump straight to the row, while a columnar scan engine may scan to find one row and pay column-reassembly cost.

Q: After timing both engines you see the winner reverses between the scan and the point lookup — what does that prove?
A: That neither engine is "faster" in the abstract; storage layout matches workload — columnar wins scans, row+index wins point lookups.

Q: A teammate says "DuckDB is just fast" — give the real reason it wins an aggregation.
A: Columnar layout lets it read only needed columns, compression shrinks I/O, and vectorized execution processes batches efficiently — it's layout and execution, not magic.

Q: What is a batch processing job, in one sentence?
A: A job that processes a bounded, finite set of data on a schedule and publishes a result, rather than reacting to events continuously.

Q: Name the three-part rhythm of a batch job taught in this lesson.
A: Schedule, then process a bounded slice of data, then publish the result.

Q: What does "bounded data" mean for a batch job?
A: The input has a known, finite extent — e.g. "all trades for 2026-06-12" — so the job has a clear start and end, unlike an unbounded stream.

Q: How does batch processing differ from stream processing?
A: Batch processes a finite dataset all at once on a schedule; streaming processes unbounded events continuously as they arrive.

Q: Why is batch the "default rhythm" of the fund industry?
A: Core outputs like the daily NAV are produced once per valuation cycle on bounded end-of-day data, which maps naturally onto a scheduled batch.

Q: Describe the daily-NAV cycle as a batch job.
A: On a schedule (after market close/cutoff), take the bounded set of that day's prices and positions, compute each fund's NAV, then publish it to downstream consumers.

Q: What is a "cutoff" in fund operations and why does it matter for batch boundaries?
A: A cutoff is the deadline after which the day's orders/prices are fixed; it defines the bounded input for that day's NAV batch so the job knows exactly what to process.

Q: What is a scheduler's role in batch processing?
A: It triggers the job at the right time or on the right dependency (e.g. daily after cutoff) so the job runs reliably without manual kicks.

Q: Give two ways a batch job might be scheduled.
A: By the clock (e.g. cron at a fixed time) or by an event/dependency (e.g. "run when the upstream price file lands").

Q: What does "publish" mean as the final step of a batch job?
A: Making the computed result available to downstream consumers — writing it to a table, file, or feed that others read, ideally only once it's complete and correct.

Q: Why should a batch job publish results atomically (all-or-nothing) rather than incrementally visible?
A: So downstream consumers never read a half-finished result; they should see either the previous complete output or the new complete one.

Q: What is idempotency for a batch job?
A: The property that running the job again with the same input produces the same end state — running it twice is safe and leaves no duplicates or drift.

Q: Why does this lesson insist that "run it twice must be safe"?
A: Batch jobs fail and get retried (crashes, partial runs, manual reruns); if a rerun double-counts or corrupts data, every failure becomes a data-quality incident.

Q: Give a concrete way a NON-idempotent NAV job goes wrong on rerun.
A: If it blindly `INSERT`s each day's NAVs, a rerun adds a second set of rows for the same day, so downstream `SUM`/`AVG` double-counts.

Q: Name one technique to make a batch load idempotent.
A: Use upsert/merge keyed on the natural key (e.g. fund + date), or delete-then-insert the target partition, so a rerun overwrites rather than appends.

Q: What is "delete-then-insert by partition" and why is it idempotent?
A: Before writing a slice (say one date), you delete any existing rows for that slice and then insert fresh ones, so the result is identical no matter how many times you run it.

Q: What is an `UPSERT` (e.g. `INSERT ... ON CONFLICT ... DO UPDATE` in Postgres) and how does it help idempotency?
A: It inserts a row or updates the existing one on key conflict, so re-running with the same keys overwrites instead of duplicating.

Q: Why is a "natural key" important for idempotent fund-data writes?
A: A stable key like (ISIN or fund id, valuation date) lets the job recognise and replace the prior version of a row instead of appending a duplicate.

Q: How does idempotency relate to retries and failure recovery?
A: Because reruns are safe, you can retry a failed or half-completed job freely without manual cleanup, which makes the whole pipeline robust.

Q: Is appending rows to a log table idempotent by default? Why or why not?
A: No — a plain append adds new rows every run, so a rerun produces duplicates unless you dedupe by key or guard with an insert-if-not-exists.

Q: What is a "partition" in the context of a batch job?
A: A logical slice of the data, typically by date, that the job processes and can publish/replace independently — e.g. one day's NAVs.

Q: Sort these into OLTP/OLAP/batch: "insert one redemption order".
A: OLTP — a single small write.

Q: Sort into OLTP/OLAP/batch: "compute AUM per fund family for the year".
A: OLAP — a large scan-and-aggregate analytical query.

Q: Sort into OLTP/OLAP/batch: "every night at 22:00, recompute and publish all funds' NAV".
A: Batch (whose internal computation is OLAP-shaped) — scheduled processing of bounded daily data.

Q: Sort into OLTP/OLAP/batch: "look up an investor's current balance for the call-centre screen".
A: OLTP — a latency-bound point read.

Q: Sort into OLTP/OLAP/batch: "month-end report of average NAV per fund per month".
A: OLAP — aggregation over a large historical scan.

Q: Sort into OLTP/OLAP/batch: "update a single fund's management fee field".
A: OLTP — a small single-row write.

Q: Where on the OLTP/OLAP split does the daily-NAV batch sit, and what does that imply for where it runs?
A: Its computation is OLAP-shaped (scan positions and prices, aggregate per fund), so it should run against an analytical/columnar store or extract, not on the live transactional order book.

Q: Why should the daily-NAV calculation generally not run on the transactional order-capture database?
A: Its big scans are throughput-bound and would contend with latency-bound order processing; it belongs on a separate analytical copy.

Q: An EMT (European MiFID Template) file is a periodic vendor feed of product data — is loading it a batch or a stream job?
A: Batch — it arrives as a bounded file on a schedule, so you load and publish it as a scheduled batch.

Q: Why must an EMT-file load be idempotent if the vendor can resend a corrected file?
A: A resend or rerun must replace the prior version for that period rather than create duplicate product rows, so the load should upsert/replace by key.

Q: What natural key would make an ISIN-keyed reference load idempotent?
A: The ISIN (plus an effective date or version) — so reloading the same file overwrites each instrument's row instead of appending a copy.

Q: Why is an ISIN a good candidate key for instrument reference data?
A: It is a standardised, unique 12-character identifier per security, so it stably identifies the same instrument across reloads and sources.

Q: For "show one fund's latest NAV on a web page" which engine/layout fits, and why?
A: A row store with an index (e.g. Postgres) — it's a latency-bound point lookup, exactly what row+index storage makes cheap.

Q: For "chart 5 years of monthly average NAV across all funds" which engine/layout fits, and why?
A: A columnar engine (e.g. DuckDB over Parquet) — it's a throughput-bound scan/aggregation, exactly what columnar storage makes cheap.

Q: Your "average NAV per fund per month" query is slow on Postgres over 1M rows — what is the first layout-level thing to suspect?
A: Row storage is reading full rows to get a couple of columns; the workload is OLAP-shaped and would suit a columnar engine or at least a covering/columnar approach.

Q: A point lookup that was fast on Postgres is slow on DuckDB — first thing to check?
A: That you actually need a point lookup here; DuckDB scans rather than seeks, so for single-key lookups a row store with an index is the right tool.

Q: Your nightly NAV batch produced double the expected rows for today — first thing to check?
A: Whether the job ran twice (or was retried) and is not idempotent — i.e. it appends instead of upserting/replacing the day's partition.

Q: A batch job "succeeded" but downstream sees yesterday's numbers — first thing to check?
A: Whether publish was atomic and pointed at the new partition/date, and whether the schedule actually triggered today's run on the right bounded input.

Q: A rerun of a batch job for a single bad day corrupted other days too — what design flaw does this reveal?
A: The job isn't partition-scoped; it should delete/replace only the targeted day's slice, not touch the whole dataset.

Q: Why is "cold vs warm" timing worth recording when benchmarking Postgres vs DuckDB?
A: The first (cold) run reads from disk while a warm run hits cache; comparing both separates I/O cost from CPU/execution cost so you don't misattribute speed.

Q: What does a big cold-vs-warm gap on a scan query usually indicate?
A: That the query is I/O-bound — most of the cold time is reading bytes from disk, which caching then removes on the warm run.

Q: Why does columnar compression matter most on the cold run?
A: Compression reduces bytes read from disk, which is exactly the cost dominating a cold, I/O-bound scan.

Q: Two engines give identical query results but very different timings — is the slower one "wrong"?
A: No — correctness is the same; the difference is performance driven by storage layout and execution model for that workload.

Q: Why generate ~1M synthetic NAV rows for this exercise instead of using 100 rows?
A: Storage-layout effects (scan I/O, compression, vectorization) only become visible at scale; tiny tables fit in cache and hide the difference.

Q: What is a "covering index" and which workload does it help?
A: An index that includes all columns a query needs so the engine answers from the index alone; it helps OLTP-ish reads but doesn't turn a row store into a columnar scanner.

Q: Why won't simply adding indexes make Postgres beat DuckDB on a full-table aggregation?
A: An aggregation scans most rows anyway, so an index adds overhead without avoiding the scan; the win comes from columnar layout, not indexing.

Q: What is a materialized view and how does it relate to batch + OLAP?
A: A precomputed, stored query result refreshed on a schedule; it's effectively a batch job that publishes an OLAP aggregate for fast reads.

Q: Why might you precompute (materialize) "monthly average NAV per fund" rather than query it live each time?
A: The aggregation is expensive to recompute repeatedly; a scheduled batch materializes it once so many readers get fast, consistent results.

Q: What is the trade-off of materializing aggregates?
A: You gain read speed and consistency but add staleness (results lag the refresh) and the cost/complexity of keeping the materialization current.

Q: How does the OLTP-vs-OLAP split motivate the classic "extract from the source database" step?
A: You copy operational data out of the latency-bound OLTP system into an analytical store so heavy scans never touch live transactions.

Q: What does "throughput vs latency" tuning mean you optimise differently for each system?
A: For OLTP you minimise per-query latency (indexes, small transactions, caching hot rows); for OLAP you maximise bytes-scanned-per-second (columnar layout, compression, parallel scans).

Q: Why are write amplification and lock contention bigger concerns for OLTP than OLAP?
A: OLTP has many concurrent small writes competing for the same rows/pages, so locking and write overhead per transaction matter; OLAP is mostly read-heavy bulk scans.

Q: Postgres vs DuckDB: which would you expect to handle 500 concurrent writing clients, and why?
A: Postgres — its client-server, transactional, row-store design supports high-concurrency reads/writes, which an embedded single-process analytical engine is not built for.

Q: Summarise the core lesson of 0.8 in one sentence.
A: Match the system to the workload — row stores and indexes for latency-bound OLTP, columnar engines for throughput-bound OLAP — and run analytics on bounded, idempotent batch jobs off a separate copy, as the daily NAV does.
