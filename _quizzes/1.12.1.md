# Phase 2 · 1.12.1 ISO 20022 — 150 self-test questions

Q: What is ISO 20022 in one sentence?
A: ISO 20022 is an international standard for electronic data interchange between financial institutions, defining a common modelling methodology and a library of XML message schemas. It is the successor to legacy formats like SWIFT MT, and post-migration it is the lingua franca of EU payments and securities messaging. For a fund platform it matters because subscription orders, statements, and settlement instructions arrive and depart as ISO 20022 messages your canonical model must align to.

Q: What are the three layers of the ISO 20022 standard?
A: ISO 20022 is layered as a business model (conceptual, the business processes and concepts), logical messages (the structured message definitions independent of syntax), and physical messages (the concrete XML schemas, the XSDs). This separation means the same business meaning can in principle be expressed in different syntaxes. In practice the physical layer you work with day to day is XML validated against an XSD.

Q: Decode the message identifier `setr.010.001.04` part by part.
A: The four dot-separated tokens are business area (`setr`, securities trade), message number (`010`, unique within that business area), variant (`001`, a constrained flavour of the global definition), and version (`04`, the schema version). So `setr.010.001.04` is version 4 of the SubscriptionOrder message in the securities-trade area. Reading the identifier tells you exactly which schema you must validate against.

Q: In the identifier `pacs.008.001.13`, what does the `008` represent and what does the final `13` represent?
A: The `008` is the message number, which is unique inside the `pacs` business area and identifies the specific message function — here FIToFICustomerCreditTransfer. The final `13` is the version number of that message definition. The third token (`001`) is the variant; together the four tokens pin down one exact XSD.

Q: What does the four-letter prefix in an ISO 20022 message name tell you?
A: The four-letter prefix is the business area (sometimes called the business domain), grouping messages that serve a related purpose. Examples are `pacs` for payments clearing and settlement, `camt` for cash management, and `setr`, `semt`, `sese` for securities trade, management, and settlement. Recognising the prefix immediately tells you which functional domain a message belongs to before you read any payload.

Q: What does the business area `setr` cover, and why does it matter for funds?
A: `setr` is the securities-trade business area, and it carries investment-fund order messages — subscriptions, redemptions, switches, and their confirmations. It matters for funds because transfer-agency order flow is expressed in `setr` messages, so your platform must ingest and emit them. The canonical example is `setr.010`, the SubscriptionOrder.

Q: What does the business area `semt` cover, and which fund artefacts use it?
A: `semt` is the securities-management business area, carrying statements and reporting — holdings statements, transaction statements, and reconciliation reporting. For funds it underpins the periodic position and movement statements a custodian or transfer agent sends. Think of `semt` whenever you need a snapshot or movement report rather than an order instruction.

Q: What does the business area `sese` cover?
A: `sese` is the securities-settlement business area, carrying settlement instructions and their statuses and confirmations — the messages that move securities and cash against each other after a trade. For a fund, `sese` is where the settlement leg of a subscription or redemption is instructed and confirmed. It is distinct from `setr`, which carries the order itself rather than its settlement.

Q: Sketch the fund order message flow and name the message family at each hop.
A: A typical flow is order → status → confirmation → settlement: a `setr` order (for example `setr.010` SubscriptionOrder) is sent, an order-status message reports acceptance or rejection, a `setr` confirmation (for example `setr.012` SubscriptionOrderConfirmation) reports execution, and settlement is instructed and confirmed in the `sese` family. A `semt` statement may later report the resulting holding. Being able to name the type at each hop is exactly the Done-when criterion for this lesson.

Q: What does the business area `seev` cover, and why should a fund security master care?
A: `seev` is the securities-events business area, carrying corporate-action messages — notifications, instructions, and confirmations for events like dividends, splits, mergers, and reorganisations. A fund security master cares because corporate actions can rename or retire an ISIN, so `seev` messages drive identifier-lifecycle handling. Ignoring them is how a single instrument silently becomes two rows in your warehouse.

Q: What does the business area `pacs` cover?
A: `pacs` (payments clearing and settlement) carries interbank payment messages — for example FIToFICustomerCreditTransfer (`pacs.008`) and the payment status report (`pacs.002`). These move funds between financial institutions rather than instructing a customer's bank. They are the high-value-payment backbone in the post-MT world but are less central to fund-order processing than `setr`.

Q: What does the business area `camt` cover, and give a fund-relevant example.
A: `camt` (cash management) carries account-reporting and cash-management messages, including bank-to-customer statements and notifications. The classic examples are `camt.053` (bank-to-customer statement) and `camt.052` (intraday report). For a fund, `camt.053` is how you receive the daily cash statement you reconcile against the NAV cash position.

Q: What does the business area `pain` cover?
A: `pain` (payments initiation) carries customer-to-bank messages — the customer instructing its bank to make a payment or set up a direct debit, for example `pain.001` (CustomerCreditTransferInitiation). It is the initiation leg, upstream of the interbank `pacs` leg. A fund's treasury function uses `pain` messages to instruct payments out of fund accounts.

Q: Which three securities business areas are most central to fund order processing, and why?
A: `setr`, `semt`, and `sese` — `setr` carries the fund orders and confirmations, `semt` carries the statements and reporting, and `sese` carries the settlement instructions. Together they cover the order-to-settlement lifecycle that a transfer-agency platform must capture. The payments families `pacs`, `camt`, and `pain` are relevant for the cash legs but secondary to the order flow itself.

Q: What message is `setr.010` and who sends it to whom?
A: `setr.010` is the SubscriptionOrder. It is sent by an instructing party — for example an investment manager or its authorised representative — to the executing party, typically a transfer agent, to instruct the subscription of one or more financial instruments for an investment-fund account. It is the canonical sample message this lesson asks you to parse and map.

Q: Can a single `setr.010` SubscriptionOrder contain more than one order?
A: Yes. The SubscriptionOrder message can carry a single order (one financial instrument for one investment account) or multiple orders (several instruments for the same investment account in one message). This matters for your model because the order is a repeating block, not a one-to-one record. A naive flat mapping that assumes one order per message will lose data on bulk files.

Q: What is `setr.012` and how does it relate to `setr.010`?
A: `setr.012` is the SubscriptionOrderConfirmation, sent by the executing party (the transfer agent) back to the instructing party to confirm execution details of a subscription. It is the confirmation hop that follows the `setr.010` order and any status message. Linking the confirmation back to the original order is a key join your model must support.

Q: Why is XSD the syntax layer you actually validate against?
A: The XSD (XML Schema Definition) is the physical-message representation of an ISO 20022 logical message — it defines element names, nesting, cardinalities, and data types. You validate an incoming XML instance against the corresponding XSD to confirm it is structurally well-formed and conformant. With `lxml` in Python you load the schema with `etree.XMLSchema` and call `.validate()` or `.assertValid()` on the parsed tree.

Q: What is the difference between a business component and a message component in ISO 20022?
A: Business components belong to the conceptual business model and capture reusable real-world concepts (for example a party, an account, or a financial instrument). Message components are the message-level building blocks derived from them that actually appear in the schema, reused across many messages. The reuse is why the same address or party structure looks identical across `setr`, `semt`, and `sese` payloads.

Q: What is an external code set in ISO 20022, and why is it kept external?
A: An external code set is a list of allowed code values (for example currency, country, or a status reason code) that is maintained outside the message XSD and published separately, often as a spreadsheet on the ISO 20022 site. It is kept external so the values can be updated on their own cadence without reissuing every message schema. The gotcha is that an XSD validates the field as a string of the right pattern but does not itself enforce the current external code list, so a value can be schema-valid yet not a real code.

Q: If an XSD passes validation but a field still holds an invalid code, what is likely going on?
A: The field is almost certainly governed by an external code set rather than an inline enumeration, so the XSD only checks the lexical pattern (for example a length-restricted token), not membership in the live list. You must validate that value against the published external code set separately. This is a classic data-quality trap: structurally valid, semantically wrong.

Q: What is the e-Repository in ISO 20022?
A: The e-Repository is the machine-readable master dictionary of the entire ISO 20022 standard — all registered business and message components, message definitions, and data types — published by the Registration Authority. It is the authoritative source from which the schemas and documentation are generated. When a flag, a cardinality, or a code is uncertain, the e-Repository (not a third-party site) is the place to settle it.

Q: What is a Message Definition Report (MDR) and what does it contain?
A: A Message Definition Report (MDR) is the human-readable specification document for a group of related messages, generated from the e-Repository. It contains the message scope and usage, the structure of each message, element-by-element definitions, cardinalities, and the rules and code sets that apply. For mapping `setr.010` onto your model, the MDR is the document you read field by field.

Q: Why treat the e-Repository and MDRs as the authoritative source rather than a convenient third-party reference site?
A: Third-party reference sites can lag, paraphrase, or omit constraints, whereas the e-Repository and the official MDRs are generated from the registered master and carry the exact cardinalities, rules, and code sets. For a regulated fund-administration context, you must be able to trace a design decision to the authoritative definition, not to a blog. Use third-party sites to orient yourself, then confirm against the MDR.

Q: Where do you obtain the official message definitions and MDRs?
A: From the ISO 20022 message-definitions catalogue at `iso20022.org`, which publishes Message Definition Reports including the `setr` investment-funds set, alongside the XSD schemas and external code sets. SWIFT also publishes standards material for the MX-over-SWIFT context. The lesson's primary resource is the ISO 20022 catalogue.

Q: What is a "variant" (the third token) in a message identifier, and why does it exist?
A: A variant is a constrained version of a global message definition — typically a subset with tighter cardinalities or fewer optional elements — registered for a specific community or usage. It exists so a market practice can pin down exactly which optional elements are used without inventing a new message. The third token in the identifier (commonly `001`) names the variant.

Q: How do you parse and validate a `setr.010` instance with Python and `lxml` via `uv`?
A: You parse the XML with `etree.parse('setr010.xml')`, load the schema with `schema = etree.XMLSchema(etree.parse('setr.010.001.04.xsd'))`, and call `schema.assertValid(tree)` to raise on the first violation or `schema.validate(tree)` for a boolean. You run it under the project toolchain with `uv run python parse.py` rather than a bare `python`. The `assertValid` exception message points you to the offending element, which is invaluable when mapping.

Q: ISO 20022 XML uses namespaces; what does that mean for your XPath queries in `lxml`?
A: Each ISO 20022 document declares a default namespace such as `urn:iso:std:iso:20022:tech:xsd:setr.010.001.04`, so element names are namespace-qualified. In `lxml` your XPath must bind a prefix to that URI (for example `tree.xpath('//x:SbcptOrdr', namespaces={'x': 'urn:...'})`) or it will return nothing. Forgetting the namespace is the single most common reason an XPath silently matches zero nodes.

Q: What does "mapping a message to a canonical model" mean in this lesson's terms?
A: It means deciding, for every business field in the message, which table and column of your warehouse model it lands in — and recording that mapping. The point is that the canonical model, not the message, is your stable internal representation, and messages are mapped onto it at ingestion. The deliverable is a documented field-to-column mapping for `setr.010` against your Phase-1 model.

Q: Why is it dangerous if your warehouse model cannot hold a field present in the message?
A: Because dropping a field at ingestion means it is gone for downstream lineage, reconciliation, and audit — and auditors in a fund-administration context expect to trace fields end to end. An unmappable field is a gap you must document along with what extending the model would require. Designing without checking the message against the model is how you build something that silently loses data auditors want.

Q: A `setr.010` carries a repeating order block. How should that influence your canonical-model design?
A: The repeating block means the message maps to a parent (the order message / bulk) and a child (the individual order line), so you need a one-to-many relationship rather than a single flat table. Modelling it flat forces you either to keep only the first order or to denormalise message-level fields onto every line. A header table plus an order-line table keyed back to it preserves the structure cleanly.

Q: How would you record an ISO 20022 field-to-model mapping so it is auditable?
A: Keep a mapping artefact (for example a versioned spreadsheet, a dbt model with documented columns, or a YAML mapping) that lists, per field, the XPath in the message, the business meaning from the MDR, the target table and column, any transformation, and a gap flag if it cannot be held. Versioning it lets you show how the mapping changed when a new message version arrives. This is what "record the mapping" in the Do steps means concretely.

Q: What does "MX" mean in the SWIFT context?
A: MX refers to the XML-based ISO 20022 messages carried over SWIFT, as opposed to MT, the legacy text-based FIN message formats. So when people say "MX-over-SWIFT" they mean ISO 20022 XML payloads transported through SWIFT's network. Your `setr`, `semt`, and `sese` fund messages are MX messages.

Q: How do MT and MX messages differ at the format level?
A: MT messages are SWIFT's legacy fixed-tag text format (for example `:20:` reference fields), while MX messages are ISO 20022 XML validated against XSDs. MX carries richer, more structured and granular data than the terse MT tags. The migration from MT to MX is precisely why ISO 20022 has become the standard your canonical model must align to.

Q: What are SWIFT's InterAct, FileAct, and FIN services, and which carry MX?
A: FIN is the legacy store-and-forward service that carries MT text messages; InterAct is the XML messaging service that carries MX (ISO 20022) messages; FileAct is a bulk file-transfer service that is format-agnostic and can carry MT, MX, or proprietary files. So MX travels over InterAct for real-time messaging or over FileAct when sent as files. Knowing the transport helps you reason about batching, acknowledgements, and where files land on your platform.

Q: What is the Business Application Header (BAH) and why does it matter?
A: The Business Application Header is a standard ISO 20022 header (`head.001`) that wraps a message document, carrying routing and business metadata such as sender, receiver, message definition identifier, business message identifier, and creation timestamp. Many newer ISO 20022 services mandate it, so a valid payload is the AppHdr plus the Document. For your platform it is where you reliably read who sent what and which message type without parsing the body.

Q: A counterparty sends an MX file but your parser only sees envelope metadata and no business content. What is the likely cause?
A: You are probably parsing only the Business Application Header (the AppHdr) and not unwrapping the Document, or the file is an envelope (BAH plus Document, or a SWIFT/FileAct envelope) whose inner payload you have not descended into. Inspect the structure: the business content lives under the `Document` element with its own namespace. Bind the correct namespace and target the document body, not the header.

Q: Why did ISO 20022 adoption accelerate in EU payments and securities messaging?
A: Because the industry committed to migrating off legacy MT formats onto richer, structured ISO 20022 messages, with coordinated programmes driving high-value payments and securities flows onto the standard. Richer structured data improves automation, reconciliation, and regulatory reporting. For an architect this means ISO 20022 is now the reference your canonical model aligns to rather than an optional add-on.

Q: True or false: after the MT decommissioning for cross-border payments, every ISO 20022 securities message is automatically mandatory too. Explain.
A: False. The widely cited MT-to-MX decommissioning timeline applies to specific programmes such as cross-border payments and reporting (CBPR+); securities messaging migration follows its own governance and timelines. Do not assume a single cut-over date covers `setr`, `semt`, and `sese`. When the exact mandate matters, check the relevant SWIFT and market-practice schedule rather than generalising from the payments deadline.

Q: What does an XSD `minOccurs`/`maxOccurs` pair tell you when mapping a field?
A: They define the element's cardinality — `minOccurs="0"` means optional, `minOccurs="1"` means mandatory, and `maxOccurs="unbounded"` means it repeats with no fixed limit. This directly drives your model: a mandatory single field can be a not-null column, while an unbounded element needs a child table. Misreading cardinality is how you end up with a column that cannot hold legitimately repeating data.

Q: How does ISO 20022 typically represent a monetary amount, and why is that significant for NAV work?
A: A currency-and-amount element carries the numeric amount together with a currency code as an attribute (commonly `Ccy`), so the currency is inseparable from the figure. This is significant for NAV work because you must persist both the amount and its currency, never an amount alone. Storing the number without the currency is a subtle data-loss bug that surfaces in multi-currency funds.

Q: In an ISO 20022 instrument identification block, where does the ISIN appear and why care?
A: The financial-instrument identification typically offers an `ISIN` element plus optional other identifications, so an instrument can be carried by ISIN and supplementary codes. You care because your security master should key on the ISIN you extract here while preserving any other identifiers for cross-reference. Picking the wrong identification node, or assuming ISIN is always present, breaks the join to your instrument master.

Q: How is a date typically expressed in ISO 20022 XML, and what is the parsing gotcha?
A: Dates use ISO 8601 types — an `ISODate` like `2026-06-13` or an `ISODateTime` with time and possibly a timezone offset. The gotcha is mixing a date-only field with a datetime field, or ignoring the timezone on a datetime, which shifts a NAV or settlement date across a day boundary. Parse with timezone awareness and store dates and timestamps in distinct, well-typed columns.

Q: What is the difference between the `setr` order and the `sese` settlement instruction conceptually?
A: The `setr` order is the business instruction to subscribe to or redeem fund units; the `sese` settlement instruction is the post-trade message that actually moves the securities and cash to settle the resulting transaction. One expresses intent in the fund-order domain, the other expresses the settlement mechanics. Conflating them in your model collapses two distinct lifecycle stages auditors expect to see separately.

Q: Where does an order-status message fit in the lifecycle and which family carries it?
A: After an order is sent and before its confirmation, an order-status (or order-instruction-status) message reports acceptance, rejection, or pending status; for fund orders this sits in the `setr` family alongside the order and confirmation. It is the hop that tells the instructing party whether the transfer agent accepted the instruction. Capturing status is essential for an end-to-end order state machine.

Q: Two fund order messages share the same address and party structure. Why, in ISO 20022 terms?
A: Because both reuse the same registered message components (derived from shared business components) for party and postal-address concepts, the reuse is by design across the catalogue. This is the methodology's whole point: define a concept once and reuse it everywhere. For you it means a single canonical party/address mapping can be reused across `setr`, `semt`, and `sese`.

Q: What does it mean that ISO 20022 is "syntax-independent" at the logical layer?
A: It means the logical message definition describes structure and meaning without committing to a wire format, so in principle it could be rendered in XML or another syntax. In practice the registered physical representation is XML (XSD), which is what you exchange. The separation is why the same business message can survive a syntax change without redefining the business meaning.

Q: You receive `setr.010.001.04` but your mapping was built for `setr.010.001.08`. What must you check?
A: Check the differences between the two versions in their MDRs — added, removed, or recardinalised elements and changed code sets — because a higher version can introduce mandatory fields or restructure blocks. Validate the incoming instance against the version-4 XSD, not the version-8 one. Treat the version token as load-bearing: silently reusing a mapping across versions is how subtle field shifts slip through.

Q: Why might an incoming ISO 20022 instance fail XSD validation on a perfectly real-looking value?
A: Common causes are a value that violates a type restriction (wrong length, pattern, or fraction digits), an element out of sequence, a missing mandatory element, or the wrong namespace/version of the schema. The XSD enforces structure and lexical types strictly, so a "real-looking" amount with too many decimals or a code of the wrong length will fail. The validator's error message names the element and the rule it broke — read it first.

Q: First thing to check when `lxml` reports an empty result for an XPath against an ISO 20022 file?
A: Check the namespace. ISO 20022 documents carry a default namespace on the `Document` element, so an unqualified XPath matches nothing; you must register a prefix bound to that namespace URI and use it in the query. Confirm the exact URI from the document root, since it encodes the message version. This single issue accounts for most "my XPath returns nothing" reports.

Q: A subscription's amount imported as a bare number with no currency. Where did it go wrong and what is the fix?
A: The currency lives in an attribute (commonly `Ccy`) on the amount element, and the import read the element text but not the attribute. The fix is to extract both the numeric value and the `Ccy` attribute and persist them together. In multi-currency fund accounting, an amount without its currency is unusable and a reconciliation break waiting to happen.

Q: What is the practical risk of relying on a third-party site for an exact ISO 20022 cardinality or code value?
A: The site may be out of date or may have simplified the constraint, so you could encode a wrong cardinality or accept a retired code, which then propagates into your model and validation. In a regulated context you need traceability to the registered definition. Use third-party material to orient, then confirm the exact value against the official MDR, XSD, or external code set.

Q: How does the e-Repository help when you are unsure whether a field is mandatory?
A: The e-Repository holds the registered cardinality for every element, and the MDR generated from it states `minOccurs`/`maxOccurs` per field, so you can confirm whether the field is mandatory rather than guessing. Cross-check against the XSD, which encodes the same constraint. This authoritative check is exactly the discipline a fund architect needs when a missing field could block downstream processing.

Q: In the order-to-settlement flow, where would a `semt` statement typically appear?
A: A `semt` statement (for example a holdings or transaction statement) typically appears after settlement, reporting the resulting position or the movements over a period. It is the reporting hop rather than an instruction in the active order flow. You use it to reconcile that your booked holding matches what the custodian or transfer agent reports.

Q: Why is being able to "sketch the message flow and name the type at each hop" a design skill, not trivia?
A: Because the message at each hop tells you what your platform must capture, link, and persist — order reference, status, confirmation details, settlement reference, statement position — and missing any hop leaves a gap in lineage. Naming the types forces you to design the joins between order, status, confirmation, and settlement. It is the reasoning that prevents a model that drops fields auditors expect to trace.

Q: What is the relationship between a message component and the XSD complex types you see in the schema?
A: A message component in the e-Repository is realised in the XSD as a complex type (a reusable element group) that the message references wherever that concept appears. So the same complex type, such as a party or financial-instrument block, recurs across messages by reference. Recognising shared complex types lets you write one parsing routine and reuse it across `setr`, `semt`, and `sese`.

Q: Distinguish `pacs.008` from `pacs.002`.
A: `pacs.008` is the FIToFICustomerCreditTransfer, the interbank instruction that moves a customer credit transfer between financial institutions. `pacs.002` is the FIToFIPaymentStatusReport, which reports the status (accepted, rejected, pending) of a prior payment instruction. One initiates the interbank movement, the other reports back on its fate.

Q: Distinguish `camt.052` from `camt.053`.
A: `camt.052` is the BankToCustomerAccountReport, typically an intraday or interim account report, while `camt.053` is the BankToCustomerStatement, the end-of-day statement. For fund cash reconciliation you generally reconcile against the `camt.053` statement and may use `camt.052` for intraday visibility. Choosing the wrong one means reconciling against an incomplete picture.

Q: A counterparty says they will send you a `pain.001`. What should you expect?
A: A `pain.001` is a CustomerCreditTransferInitiation — a customer instructing its bank to make one or more credit transfers, the initiation leg upstream of the interbank `pacs.008`. Expect an XML message with debtor, creditor, and payment-instruction blocks, validated against the relevant `pain.001` XSD version. It is an instruction to pay, not a confirmation that money has moved.

Q: Why must your platform persist the message version it received, not just the parsed business data?
A: Because reprocessing, dispute resolution, and audit may require knowing exactly which schema version and variant governed the original instance, including which optional fields could legally appear. Storing only parsed data loses the provenance needed to re-validate or explain a historical decision. Keep the raw message and its message-definition identifier alongside the extracted fields.

Q: What does the message-definition identifier in the BAH let you do before parsing the body?
A: It tells you the exact message type, variant, and version (for example `setr.010.001.04`), so you can route the message to the right parser and schema without inspecting the body. This is efficient and safe: you select the correct XSD up front rather than guessing from the payload. It is also a reliable field for logging and lineage.

Q: How do you validate an external-code-set value in practice when the XSD will not?
A: You load the published external code-set list (often a spreadsheet or its registered form) and check the field's value against that list as a separate validation step after XSD validation. In a pipeline this is typically a reference table you join against, raising a data-quality error on no match. This catches schema-valid but semantically invalid codes such as a retired status reason.

Q: What is the difference between a maintenance version bump and a new message number?
A: A version bump (the last token) keeps the same business message but revises its definition — added fields, relaxed or tightened cardinalities, new code values — under the same message number. A new message number is a different message function entirely within the business area. Your mapping can often be migrated across version bumps with diffs, but a new number means a new mapping.

Q: Why is ISO 20022 described as a "methodology" and not just a set of messages?
A: Because it defines a modelling approach — a data dictionary of reusable business and message components and a process for registering new messages — from which the actual message schemas are derived. The library of messages is the output of the methodology, not the whole of it. Understanding it as a methodology explains why components are so consistently reused across business areas.

Q: A Luxembourg transfer agent sends bulk subscription orders as one file. Which message and which transport are most likely?
A: The orders are most likely `setr.010` SubscriptionOrder messages, and a bulk file is most likely delivered over SWIFT FileAct (or an equivalent file channel) rather than single InterAct messages. A single `setr.010` can itself carry multiple orders, and many such messages can be bundled into one file. Your ingestion must handle both the multi-order message and the file-of-messages dimension.

Q: What is the risk of mapping ISO 20022 party identifications onto a single "name" column?
A: Party blocks carry structured identification — names, postal-address sub-elements, and identifiers such as a BIC or LEI — so collapsing them into one free-text name discards structure you may need for matching, regulatory reporting, and entity resolution. In a fund context the management company's LEI is a join key you would lose. Map party structure into structured columns, preserving identifiers separately.

Q: How would you handle an optional repeating element that your model currently cannot store?
A: Record it as a gap: note the XPath, the business meaning from the MDR, its cardinality, and what extending the model would require (for example a new child table). Until the model is extended, decide explicitly whether to reject the message, quarantine the data, or store the raw element for later. Silently dropping it is the failure mode this lesson warns against.

Q: What does it mean that ISO 20022 message components are "registered"?
A: It means each component and message definition is submitted to and approved by the Registration Authority and recorded in the e-Repository, giving it a single authoritative definition. Registration is what makes the standard interoperable: everyone refers to the same registered concept. It is also why you should treat the registered definition, not a copy, as ground truth.

Q: Why might two valid ISO 20022 messages of the same type look structurally different?
A: They may use different versions or variants, so optional elements present in one are absent in another, or a variant constrains cardinalities differently. Both can be schema-valid against their respective XSDs. Your parser must therefore be tolerant of optional elements and explicit about which version/variant it expects.

Q: What is the practical first step the lesson's Do section asks for, and why start there?
A: The first step is to obtain a sample `setr.010` subscription-order message and its schema. You start there because you cannot meaningfully map fields, find gaps, or validate without a concrete instance and the XSD that governs it. Working from a real message keeps the exercise grounded in actual structure rather than assumptions.

Q: After parsing a `setr.010`, what is the deliverable for the mapping step?
A: The deliverable is a recorded mapping of every business field onto your Phase-1 model — field, meaning, target table and column — plus a documented list of gaps (fields the model cannot hold and what they would require). This satisfies the Done-when criterion of being able to map a `setr.010` and list the unmappable fields. The mapping is an artefact, not a mental note.

Q: How does knowing the business area help you triage an unfamiliar message file?
A: The four-letter business-area prefix immediately tells you the domain — `setr` is a fund order, `semt` a statement, `sese` a settlement, `camt`/`pacs`/`pain` a cash or payment flow — so you know which subsystem and parser it belongs to before reading the body. This lets you route and prioritise without deep inspection. It is the fastest reliable signal in the identifier.

Q: What is the difference between the AppHdr (BAH) namespace and the Document namespace in an MX message?
A: The BAH has its own namespace (the `head.001` schema) while the business document under `Document` has the message-specific namespace such as `urn:iso:std:iso:20022:tech:xsd:setr.010.001.04`. They are two distinct schemas in one envelope, so you validate and parse each against its own XSD. Mixing them up is a common reason header parsing or body parsing fails.

Q: Why is ISO 20022 considered better suited to regulatory reporting than legacy MT?
A: Because its richer, structured, and explicitly typed data captures granular business detail — parties, identifiers, amounts with currency, reasons — that terse MT tags compressed or omitted. More structure means more fields can be carried, validated, and traced end to end. For DORA-era operational resilience and audit expectations, that traceability is a direct benefit.

Q: A redemption order and a subscription order in `setr` — are they the same message number?
A: No. Subscription and redemption are distinct functions with their own message numbers within `setr` (for example SubscriptionOrder versus RedemptionOrder), each with its own confirmation message. They share reusable components but are separate definitions. Your model should distinguish order type explicitly rather than inferring it.

Q: What is the consequence of ignoring `maxOccurs="unbounded"` on an order element when designing tables?
A: You will model a one-to-many relationship as one-to-one, so a message carrying several order lines either overwrites or truncates to one. The unbounded cardinality is the schema telling you a child table is required. Catching this at design time, by reading the XSD, prevents silent data loss on bulk files.

Q: How do you confirm the exact namespace URI to use in your XPath for a given message?
A: Read it from the root element of the instance (the `xmlns` on `Document`) or from the target namespace declared in the message's XSD, which both encode the message identifier and version. Do not hard-code a remembered URI, because the version token changes it. Binding the exact URI is what makes your XPath match nodes.

Q: Why does the standard keep code sets external rather than enumerating them inline in every schema?
A: To decouple the cadence of code-value changes from schema releases — a currency or status-reason list can be updated without reissuing and re-deploying every message XSD that uses it. This keeps schemas stable and code lists current. The trade-off is that validation against the live list becomes a separate step you must implement.

Q: What is the role of SWIFT relative to ISO 20022?
A: SWIFT is a network and standards body that transports ISO 20022 (MX) messages — over InterAct and FileAct — and historically carried MT over FIN, and it publishes standards and market-practice material. ISO 20022 is the message standard; SWIFT is one major rail that carries it. Other rails and market infrastructures also use ISO 20022.

Q: A field is present in the MDR but absent from a received valid instance. Is the message wrong?
A: Not necessarily — if the field is optional (`minOccurs="0"`), a conformant sender can omit it, so the instance is still valid. Your model and code must treat such fields as nullable and not assume presence. Treating an optional field as mandatory causes spurious validation failures and brittle pipelines.

Q: What does it mean to "align your canonical model to ISO 20022"?
A: It means designing your internal warehouse model so that the business concepts ISO 20022 carries — orders, parties, instruments, amounts, statuses, settlement references — all have a stable home, so incoming messages map cleanly and nothing auditors expect is dropped. It does not mean copying the XML structure verbatim; it means covering its business content. Alignment is about coverage and traceability, not literal shape.

Q: How can you tell, from the identifier alone, whether a message is securities or payments?
A: The business-area prefix tells you: securities messages start with `se` (`setr`, `semt`, `sese`, `seev`, `secl`, `sevt`), while payments use `pain`, `pacs`, and cash management uses `camt`. So `setr.010` is securities and `pacs.008` is payments at a glance. This lets you route by prefix before any parsing.

Q: Why is it risky to assume the FIN MT network is still your fallback for fund messages indefinitely?
A: Because the industry is decommissioning MT in favour of MX on a programme-by-programme basis, so relying on MT as a permanent fallback builds in obsolescence. The exact timeline depends on the message domain, so you should confirm the relevant schedule rather than assume MT persists. Designing for MX-first avoids a forced rework later.

Q: What is a Business Message Identifier, and how is it useful operationally?
A: The Business Message Identifier is a sender-assigned unique reference in the BAH for a specific message instance, distinct from the message-definition identifier that names the type. Operationally it is a natural key for deduplication, acknowledgement matching, and tracing a single message through your pipeline. Persisting it lets you answer "did we already process this exact message?".

Q: In mapping `setr.010`, where would the investment-account identification typically land in your model?
A: It lands on the order or order-line as a reference to the investment account, which you would resolve to your account dimension via a surrogate key. The message carries the account identification in the order block; your model stores both the raw identifier and the resolved internal key. This preserves the source value while enabling joins.

Q: What is the difference between validating well-formedness and validating against the XSD?
A: Well-formedness checks that the XML is syntactically correct (proper nesting, closed tags, single root), which any parser does on load; XSD validation additionally checks the document against the schema's element structure, cardinalities, and data types. A document can be well-formed but schema-invalid. For ISO 20022 you need both, and `lxml`'s `XMLSchema` gives you the second.

Q: Why might persisting the raw message alongside parsed fields be a regulatory expectation, not just good hygiene?
A: Because audit and dispute processes may require reproducing exactly what was received, byte for byte, including elements you did not map. Keeping the raw message preserves the authoritative record and supports re-parsing if your model later extends. In a regulated fund-administration context, that immutable source record underpins lineage and defensibility.

Q: How would you approach documenting "gaps" as required by the Do steps?
A: For each unmappable field, record the XPath, the MDR business definition, its cardinality and data type, why the current model cannot hold it, and what change would be needed (new column, new child table, new code-set reference). Group gaps by the model change they imply so you can scope work. This gap log is a direct deliverable and a design backlog.

Q: A new external code set version retires a code your historical data uses. How should your model cope?
A: Your model should store the code as received and validate against the code-set version in force at the time, not only the latest, so historical records remain explainable. Keep the code-set lists versioned as reference data. This avoids rejecting or mislabelling valid historical values when the live list changes.

Q: Why does ISO 20022 separate the order (`setr`) from its confirmation (`setr.012`) rather than using one round-trip message?
A: Because the order and its execution are distinct events that can be separated in time and may have different statuses, so each warrants its own message with its own reference and lifecycle. Separating them lets the platform track state — instructed, accepted, executed — explicitly. Your model should mirror this with linked but distinct order and confirmation records.

Q: What does it tell you if an MX message validates against the XSD but downstream systems still reject it?
A: It usually means the message breaks a usage rule, market-practice constraint, or external-code-set requirement that the XSD alone does not enforce, or it omits an element a particular community mandates. Schema validity is necessary but not sufficient; the MDR's textual rules and the relevant market practice add constraints. Check those rules and code sets beyond bare XSD validation.

Q: How does the layered model help when a new fund-order requirement appears?
A: You can reason about the change at the business layer (what concept is needed), find or define the message component, and then see it reflected in the logical and physical (XSD) layers, with a clear path to your canonical model. The separation keeps business intent from being tangled with XML mechanics. It is why an architect benefits from understanding all three layers.

Q: Why is the `Ccy` attribute placement on amounts a frequent source of mapping bugs?
A: Because developers naturally read element text and overlook attributes, so an XPath that grabs the amount text alone silently drops the currency held in `Ccy`. The fix is to extract the attribute explicitly and store currency with every amount. In multi-currency fund accounting this bug corrupts valuations and reconciliations.

Q: What distinguishes a "global" message definition from a registered variant in practice?
A: The global definition is the full registered message with all optional elements available; a variant is a constrained subset registered for a community, typically removing optionality or tightening cardinalities. In practice you may be required to send or accept a specific variant, so you validate against the variant's XSD. The variant token in the identifier signals which you are dealing with.

Q: How would you sanity-check that you have mapped every field of a `setr.010`, not just the obvious ones?
A: Drive the check from the schema, not the sample: enumerate every element and attribute in the `setr.010` XSD (including optional and repeating ones), and confirm each has a target column or a documented gap. A sample instance only exercises the fields that sender used. Schema-driven coverage is the only way to be confident nothing is silently unmapped.

Q: Why is the message-definition identifier preferable to filename or sender convention for routing?
A: Because the message-definition identifier (for example `setr.010.001.04`) is a standardised, unambiguous statement of type, variant, and version carried inside the message, whereas filenames and sender conventions are arbitrary and can change. Routing on the identifier is robust to renamed files and new counterparties. Read it from the BAH or the document namespace.

Q: A subscription order references a management company. Where might its LEI live in the message and why capture it?
A: The management company would appear as a party block, which can carry identifiers including an LEI, so the LEI lives in that party's identification sub-element. You capture it because the LEI is the industry join key for entities and links your fund data to the GLEIF registry. Discarding it forces fragile name-based matching later.

Q: What is the safest default when you encounter an ISO 20022 element you do not yet understand during mapping?
A: Look it up in the authoritative MDR (and the e-Repository definition) for its exact business meaning, cardinality, and any governing code set, rather than inferring from the element name. Names can be abbreviated or ambiguous, and guessing risks a wrong mapping. Confirming against the registered definition is the discipline this lesson is teaching.

Q: Why might an order-status reason in a `setr` status message be governed by an external code set, and what does that imply for storage?
A: Reason and status codes are typically external code-set values so the list can evolve without reissuing the message schema, which means the XSD only checks the lexical token, not the live membership. The implication is that you store the raw code as received, validate it against the versioned code set as a separate step, and keep the reason text or mapping for human readability. This preserves an explainable audit trail even after a code is retired.

Q: Summarise why an architect who cannot map a subscription order onto the warehouse model is a liability.
A: Because the mapping is what guarantees every business field the message carries has a home in the model, so nothing auditors expect to trace is dropped at ingestion. Without that skill the architect designs a model that silently loses fields, breaks lineage, and fails regulatory scrutiny. Mapping `setr.010` and listing the unmappable fields is precisely the competence this lesson certifies.

Q: What message number is the RedemptionOrder, and how does it pair with its confirmation?
A: The RedemptionOrder is `setr.004` and its confirmation is `setr.006` (RedemptionOrderConfirmation), mirroring the `setr.010`/`setr.012` subscription pair. The redemption order is sent by an instructing party to the executing transfer agent to redeem units, and one redemption order with several individual orders may be answered by more than one `setr.006` because the instruments can have different valuation cycles. Your model should link confirmations back to the originating order by reference rather than assuming one-to-one.

Q: Which `sese` message numbers carry the settlement instruction, its status, and its confirmation?
A: `sese.023` is the SecuritiesSettlementTransactionInstruction, `sese.024` is the SecuritiesSettlementTransactionStatusAdvice, and `sese.025` is the SecuritiesSettlementTransactionConfirmation. So the settlement leg of a fund subscription is instructed with `sese.023`, its progress is advised with `sese.024`, and final settlement is confirmed with `sese.025`. Knowing the triplet lets you build a settlement state machine that mirrors the order-side `setr` flow.

Q: A counterparty references `setr.016`. What message is that and where does it sit in the lifecycle?
A: `setr.016` is the OrderInstructionStatusReport, the `setr`-family status hop that reports whether an order instruction was accepted, rejected, or is pending, sitting between the order and its confirmation. It is how the transfer agent tells the instructing party the fate of the instruction before any execution detail exists. Capturing it is what lets your platform represent the intermediate "accepted/rejected" state rather than jumping straight from order to confirmation.

Q: How would you structure a parser so one routine handles `setr.010`, `setr.004`, and their confirmations?
A: Factor the shared message components — party, account, financial-instrument, and amount blocks — into reusable extraction functions keyed by element local-name, then have a thin per-message wrapper that knows the order-type and the order-line XPath. Because subscription and redemption reuse the same registered components, the inner extractors are identical and only the message-level framing differs. This is the practical payoff of ISO 20022's component reuse: write the party extractor once and call it across `setr`, `semt`, and `sese`.

Q: Your `lxml` validation raises `DocumentInvalid` but the exception string is terse. How do you get the exact failing element and line?
A: Catch `etree.DocumentInvalid` and iterate `schema.error_log` (or the exception's `error_log`), reading `err.line`, `err.column`, `err.message`, and `err.type_name` on each entry. The error message names the offending element and the schema rule it broke, for example `SCHEMAV_ELEMENT_CONTENT` for an unexpected child or `SCHEMAV_CVC_DATATYPE_VALID_1_2_1` for a bad type. Logging the full `error_log` rather than just `str(e)` is what turns a vague failure into an actionable field-level diagnosis.

Q: What does the lxml error code `SCHEMAV_CVC_PATTERN_VALID` typically signal in an ISO 20022 instance?
A: It signals that an element or attribute value failed the schema's regex pattern restriction — for example an ISIN that is not 12 alphanumeric characters, or a code token of the wrong shape. The XSD enforces these lexical patterns strictly even though the value may look plausible to a human. The fix is to inspect the offending value against the type's `xs:pattern` facet in the schema, not to relax the schema.

Q: Why might `schema.validate(tree)` return `False` while no exception is raised, and how do you then see why?
A: `validate()` returns a boolean and never raises, so a `False` result just means the document is invalid without telling you why inline; you inspect `schema.error_log` afterwards to read the accumulated errors. By contrast `assertValid()` raises `DocumentInvalid` on the first failure. Use `validate()` plus `error_log` when you want to collect all errors, and `assertValid()` when you want to fail fast.

Q: How do you confirm the exact default-namespace URI of a received instance before writing any XPath in `lxml`?
A: Parse the file and read `tree.getroot().nsmap` (or `root.tag`, which carries the namespace in Clark notation `{uri}LocalName`), which exposes the URI declared on `Document`. That URI encodes the message identifier and version, for example `urn:iso:std:iso:20022:tech:xsd:setr.010.001.04`. Binding that exact string into your `namespaces` dict is what makes a prefixed XPath match, and hard-coding a remembered URI across versions is the classic zero-match bug.

Q: In `lxml`, what is the difference between an XPath using `local-name()` and one binding the real namespace, for ISO 20022 work?
A: Binding the real namespace (for example `namespaces={'x': 'urn:...setr.010.001.04'}` and querying `//x:FinInstrm`) is precise and fails loudly if the URI is wrong, whereas `//*[local-name()="FinInstrm"]` ignores namespaces entirely and matches regardless of version. The `local-name()` trick is convenient for quick exploration but dangerous in production because it would silently match a different message version. Prefer explicit namespace binding once you know the exact URI; reserve `local-name()` for ad-hoc inspection.

Q: A `setr.010` subscription is expressed as an amount-to-invest in some files and as a number of units in others. How does the schema express that, and what does it mean for your model?
A: The subscription quantity is typically a choice between a cash amount (a currency-and-amount element) and a unit quantity, so the schema offers a choice group and only one branch is present per order. Your model must hold both possibilities — an amount column with its currency and a units column — and record which branch was used. Assuming every order carries cash, or every order carries units, will null out legitimate data on the other kind.

Q: How does ISO 20022 encode a percentage such as a fee rate, and what is the storage gotcha?
A: Percentages use a decimal type (commonly a `PercentageRate`) carrying the rate as a plain number, where the convention is the human percentage value (for example `1.5` for 1.5%), not a fraction. The gotcha is silently dividing by 100 or not, so you must read the MDR definition to know whether the field is already a percentage and store it consistently. Mixing `1.5` and `0.015` across feeds is how a management-fee column becomes meaningless.

Q: A monetary amount in a fund file fails XSD validation with a fraction-digits error. What is the likely cause?
A: The currency-and-amount type restricts the number of fraction digits (commonly to a maximum, often five for the generic amount type), so a value with too many decimals trips `SCHEMAV_CVC_DATATYPE_VALID` on the `fractionDigits` facet. This often happens when an upstream system emits full floating-point precision instead of rounding to the type's limit. The fix is at the producing system: round to the schema's allowed precision before serialising, never widen the schema.

Q: How would you diff `setr.010.001.04` against `setr.010.001.08` in practice to migrate a mapping?
A: Compare the two MDRs element by element for added, removed, or recardinalised fields and changed code sets, and optionally diff the two XSDs structurally to spot new complex types or sequence changes. Pay special attention to elements that became mandatory or to a previously single element that became repeating, since those break a flat mapping. Drive the migration from the schema diff, not from sample instances, because a sample only exercises the optional fields its sender happened to populate.

Q: An incoming file has the BAH declaring `setr.010.001.08` but the Document body validates only against the `04` XSD. What is wrong and how do you resolve it?
A: The header and body disagree on version, which means either the envelope was assembled incorrectly or you selected the wrong XSD; the message-definition identifier in the BAH (`BizMsgIdr`/`MsgDefIdr`) is what should drive schema selection. Read the `Document` namespace URI to confirm the body's true version and validate against that exact XSD, then reconcile with the BAH. Trusting the filename or a default schema instead of the in-message identifier is how version mismatches slip through.

Q: How do you validate the AppHdr and the Document together when they live in one envelope?
A: They are two distinct schemas, so you validate the `AppHdr` subtree against the `head.001` XSD and the `Document` subtree against the message-specific XSD (for example `setr.010.001.04`) separately, each with its own namespace. There is no single schema that covers both halves of the business message. Treating the envelope as one document against one schema is why combined validation fails even when each half is conformant.

Q: In `lxml`, how do you isolate the `Document` subtree from an envelope so you can validate just the business payload?
A: Locate the `Document` element by its namespace-qualified name (for example via an XPath bound to the message namespace, or by iterating children of the envelope root) and pass that element — not the envelope root — to `schema.assertValid()`. You can validate a subtree directly because `lxml`'s `XMLSchema.validate`/`assertValid` accept any element, not only the document root. Forgetting to descend into `Document` is why a parser sometimes "sees only header metadata".

Q: A bulk FileAct delivery contains many `setr.010` documents concatenated. What is the parsing pitfall and the fix?
A: A well-formed XML document has exactly one root, so multiple `Document` roots concatenated in one physical file are not a single valid XML document and a naive `etree.parse` fails on the second root. The fix is to know the envelope/manifest format FileAct used — each message may be a separate logical unit, framed by the channel — and split or iterate accordingly rather than parsing the whole file as one tree. Assuming one file equals one XML document is a common bulk-ingestion bug.

Q: How would you stream-parse a very large `semt` statement without loading it all into memory?
A: Use `lxml.etree.iterparse(source, events=('end',), tag='{namespace}StmtLine')` to process each line element as it closes, then call `elem.clear()` (and delete preceding siblings) to free memory as you go. This event-driven approach keeps memory bounded regardless of statement size, unlike `etree.parse`, which builds the whole tree. For a multi-hundred-megabyte holdings statement this is the difference between a working pipeline and an out-of-memory crash.

Q: Why must the tag filter in `iterparse` be namespace-qualified for ISO 20022, and what happens if it is not?
A: ISO 20022 elements are namespace-qualified, so the `tag` argument must use Clark notation `{urn:iso:std:...}LocalName`; an unqualified tag matches nothing and the iterator yields no events. This is the streaming analogue of the unqualified-XPath zero-match problem. Confirm the URI from the root and build the Clark-notation tag from it rather than passing the bare local name.

Q: What does a `pacs.004` message do, and when would a fund cash flow encounter one?
A: `pacs.004` is the PaymentReturn, used to return funds of a previously settled value message such as a `pacs.008` customer credit transfer or a `pacs.009` financial-institution credit transfer. A fund encounters it when a redemption payout or a capital movement is returned — for example a closed or incorrect beneficiary account — and the cash comes back. Your reconciliation must link the return to the original outbound payment, not treat it as an unexplained credit.

Q: Distinguish `pacs.008` from `pacs.009`.
A: `pacs.008` is the FIToFICustomerCreditTransfer, moving a customer's credit transfer between financial institutions, while `pacs.009` is the FinancialInstitutionCreditTransfer, moving funds between the financial institutions themselves (a bank-to-bank movement). One has an underlying customer; the other is the institutions' own liquidity movement. For a fund the inbound subscription cash typically arrives via the customer flow, but cover/liquidity legs can be `pacs.009`.

Q: What is a UETR, where does it live, and why is it operationally valuable?
A: The UETR (Unique End-to-End Transaction Reference) is a UUID-format reference mandatory on value messages such as `pacs.008`, `pacs.009`, and `pacs.004`, carried in the transaction information. It lets you trace a single payment end to end across every institution in the chain, which underpins payment tracking and exception handling. Persisting the UETR gives you a reliable natural key to correlate an outbound fund payment with its status reports and any return.

Q: What does `camt.054` carry, and how does it differ from `camt.053`?
A: `camt.054` is the BankToCustomerDebitCreditNotification — individual debit/credit notifications, often used to advise specific entries such as an incoming subscription or a payment return — whereas `camt.053` is the end-of-day statement of the whole account. You use `camt.054` for near-real-time, per-item awareness and `camt.053` for the authoritative daily reconciliation. Reconciling solely on `camt.054` notifications risks missing entries that only appear consolidated on the statement.

Q: For fund subscription cash reconciliation, in what order do the cash messages typically arrive and what do you reconcile against?
A: An intraday `camt.052` report may show pending or provisional entries, a `camt.054` notification may advise the specific incoming credit, and the end-of-day `camt.053` statement is the authoritative record you reconcile the NAV cash position against. You treat `camt.052`/`camt.054` as early visibility and `camt.053` as the system of record. Booking off the intraday view without confirming against `camt.053` is how a provisional entry that later reverses corrupts the cash ledger.

Q: A `camt.053` statement has entries (`Ntry`) and underlying transactions (`TxDtls`). How should that nesting drive your model?
A: An entry can aggregate several underlying transactions, so the statement is a three-level hierarchy — statement, entry, transaction detail — and you need a parent statement table, an entry table, and a transaction-detail child table. Flattening entries and transaction details into one row either loses the breakdown or duplicates entry-level fields. Modelling the levels separately preserves the ability to reconcile both at the booked-entry and the individual-transaction grain.

Q: How do you reliably tell a debit from a credit in a `camt.053` entry?
A: Read the `CdtDbtInd` element, whose values are `CRDT` (credit) or `DBIT` (debit); the amount itself is unsigned, so sign must be derived from this indicator. Inferring direction from context or assuming a sign on the amount is a bug, because the same `Amt` value appears for both directions. Store the indicator alongside the amount and apply the sign at the point you post to a ledger.

Q: A corporate action renames an ISIN carried in your fund holdings. Which ISO 20022 family signals this and what must your security master do?
A: The `seev` (securities-events) family carries corporate-action notifications and confirmations, which is where a reorganisation that retires or replaces an ISIN is announced. Your security master must treat the ISIN as a versioned identifier with effective dates, linking the old and new ISIN as the same economic instrument rather than creating a disconnected second row. Ignoring `seev` is precisely how one instrument silently becomes two in the warehouse.

Q: Why is mapping driven from the XSD safer than mapping driven from a sample instance, restated as a concrete failure mode?
A: A sample only contains the optional and repeating elements that one sender chose to populate, so a sample-driven mapping silently omits every legally-allowed field the sample lacked. Months later a different counterparty sends one of those fields and it is dropped at ingestion with no error. Enumerating every element and attribute from the XSD — including `minOccurs="0"` and `maxOccurs="unbounded"` ones — and assigning each a column or a documented gap is the only coverage-complete method.

Q: How would you programmatically enumerate every element and attribute defined by the `setr.010` XSD to drive coverage?
A: Load the XSD with `lxml` and walk the `xs:element`, `xs:attribute`, and `xs:complexType` definitions, resolving `ref`s and recursing into referenced complex types to produce a flat list of fully-qualified paths with their cardinalities. Because the schema reuses complex types by reference, you must follow the references rather than just reading top-level elements, or you will miss the bulk of the fields. The output is a checklist you reconcile against your mapping artefact, one row per schema path.

Q: An element is defined in the XSD as `nillable="true"`. What does that mean for parsing and storage?
A: A nillable element can appear with `xsi:nil="true"` and empty content to explicitly assert "no value" as distinct from the element being absent, so your parser must distinguish present-but-nil from missing. In your model this maps to storing a genuine NULL for the nilled case while perhaps treating absence differently if the MDR gives it separate meaning. Treating a nilled element as if it carried empty-string content is a subtle data-quality error.

Q: How do you handle an element whose actual type is chosen at runtime via `xsi:type` in an ISO 20022 instance?
A: When the schema uses a type substitution, the instance declares the concrete type with an `xsi:type` attribute, and your parser must read that attribute to know which sub-structure to expect. Hard-coding one branch and ignoring `xsi:type` will mis-parse instances that use the alternative. This pattern appears where a generic identification or value can take several shapes, so branch your extraction on the declared type.

Q: A party identification block offers a choice between an organisation identification and a private identification. How do you map it without losing structure?
A: Read which branch is present — organisation identification (carrying BIC, LEI, and other org identifiers) versus private/person identification — and map each to its own structured columns rather than collapsing both into one generic id. The schema models this as a choice, so exactly one branch appears, and which branch it is carries meaning. Flattening both into a single "party_id" string discards the type distinction that regulatory reporting and entity resolution depend on.

Q: Where in a party block does a BIC live, and how does it differ structurally from an LEI in ISO 20022?
A: A BIC typically appears as a dedicated financial-institution identifier element (an `AnyBIC`/`BICFI`-style element constrained to the BIC pattern), while an LEI appears as its own element constrained to the 20-character LEI pattern, often within the organisation identification. They are separate, separately-typed fields, not interchangeable, so you map them to distinct columns. Conflating BIC and LEI loses the ability to route on the bank identifier while keeping the legal-entity key.

Q: What is the structural shape of an LEI and a BIC, so you can pattern-validate them before insert?
A: An LEI is exactly 20 alphanumeric characters (a 4-char LOU prefix, 2 reserved zeros, a 12-char entity portion, and 2 ISO 17442 check digits), and a BIC is 8 or 11 characters (4-char institution, 2-char ISO country, 2-char location, optional 3-char branch). Validating these patterns at ingestion catches transposition and truncation before they poison joins. The XSD enforces the lexical pattern, but adding the check-digit validation for the LEI catches values that match the pattern yet are not genuine.

Q: An ISIN in a `setr.010` passes XSD pattern validation but fails your check-digit test. What is happening and what do you do?
A: The XSD only enforces the ISIN's lexical pattern (12 alphanumeric characters), not the Luhn-based check digit, so a typo'd ISIN can be schema-valid yet arithmetically invalid. You run an independent check-digit validation after XSD validation and raise a data-quality error on failure, quarantining rather than silently loading. This is the same pattern as external-code-set validation: schema validity is necessary but not sufficient for semantic correctness.

Q: How do you persist provenance so a historical `setr.010` can be re-validated years later?
A: Store the raw message bytes (or the canonical XML), the message-definition identifier and version (for example `setr.010.001.04`), the Business Message Identifier, the receipt timestamp, and the version of the XSD and external code sets in force at receipt. Together these let you reconstruct exactly what was received and re-run validation under the historical schema, which audit and dispute resolution may demand. Keeping only parsed fields discards the provenance needed to defend a past decision.

Q: Two `setr.010` files arrive with the same Business Message Identifier from the same sender. What does that mean and how do you handle it?
A: The Business Message Identifier (`BizMsgIdr` in the BAH) is the sender-assigned unique reference for a message instance, so a repeat from the same sender is almost certainly a redelivery or duplicate, not a new business event. You deduplicate on the sender plus `BizMsgIdr` key, treating the second arrival as idempotent rather than booking the orders twice. Failing to deduplicate is how a network retransmission becomes a double subscription.

Q: How do you distinguish a genuine duplicate from a legitimate amendment of an order?
A: A duplicate repeats the same `BizMsgIdr` with identical content and should be ignored, whereas an amendment or cancellation is a distinct message — with its own identifier and a function code or a reference to the original order — that explicitly signals the change. You rely on the message type and its reference-to-original field, not on guessing from content similarity. Treating an amendment as a duplicate (or vice versa) corrupts the order state.

Q: A counterparty must cancel an already-sent subscription. How is that expressed in ISO 20022 terms rather than resending the order?
A: Cancellation is a separate, dedicated message in the `setr` family that references the original order's identifier and instructs its cancellation, rather than a modified copy of the order. The instructing party cannot un-send the original, so the lifecycle models cancellation as its own event with its own status and confirmation. Your model must represent cancellation as a distinct linked record so the order's terminal state (cancelled) is explicit and auditable.

Q: How do you link a `setr.012` confirmation back to its originating `setr.010` order in your model?
A: The confirmation carries references to the order — typically the order reference and the deal/order identification assigned by the instructing and/or executing parties — which you store as foreign keys back to the order line. Because one multi-order `setr.010` can spawn several confirmations, the join is by order-line reference, not by message. Persisting these references is what lets you assemble the full order → status → confirmation chain for lineage.

Q: When mapping a NAV-relevant date such as the requested settlement date, why must you keep date and datetime fields distinctly typed?
A: ISO 20022 uses `ISODate` (date only) and `ISODateTime` (date plus time, possibly with a timezone offset) as different types, and a settlement or NAV date carried as a plain date must not be coerced into a timestamp that invents a midnight time. Mixing them, or dropping a timezone on a datetime, can shift a date across a day boundary and mis-bucket a NAV. Map each to a matching column type — `DATE` versus `TIMESTAMP`/`TIMESTAMPTZ` — and preserve any offset.

Q: A `setr.012` carries a NAV (price per unit) used to compute the executed amount. What must your model capture beyond the number?
A: You must capture the price together with its currency, the price type/basis if the MDR distinguishes them, and the related dealing/valuation date, because a bare price is ambiguous about currency and as-of date. The amount element carries the currency in its `Ccy` attribute, which you must read, not just the element text. Storing the price without currency and valuation date makes the executed-amount reconciliation unverifiable.

Q: A field is schema-valid and a real code, yet a downstream market-infrastructure still rejects the message. What category of constraint did you miss?
A: You likely violated a textual usage rule or market-practice constraint from the MDR (for example a conditional "if A is present then B is mandatory" rule) or a community variant restriction that the generic XSD does not encode. Schema validity covers structure, cardinality, and lexical type only; cross-element rules and market practice are additional layers. Validating against the variant's XSD and checking the MDR's usage rules is what catches these before submission.

Q: How does a registered variant differ from the global message in a way that can cause a rejection, with an example?
A: A variant typically removes optionality or tightens cardinality for a community — for example making an element that is optional in the global definition mandatory in the variant — so an instance that is valid against the global XSD can be invalid against the variant. If a counterparty expects the variant and you send a global-shaped message missing the variant-mandatory element, they reject it. The third token of the identifier signals the variant, and you must validate against the variant's XSD when a community mandates it.

Q: What is the difference between `setr.010`'s order reference assigned by the sender and the deal reference assigned by the executor, and why keep both?
A: The instructing party assigns its own order identification when it sends `setr.010`, and the executing transfer agent assigns its own deal/order reference on execution returned in `setr.012`; they are two distinct keys for the two parties' books. You keep both so you can match the order across both perspectives and answer queries from either side. Storing only one breaks reconciliation whenever a counterparty refers to the transaction by the reference you discarded.

Q: How would you generate a synthetic but schema-valid `setr.010` to test your pipeline without real client data?
A: Build the XML against the `setr.010.001.04` XSD by populating every mandatory element with pattern-valid placeholder values — a valid 12-char ISIN, an amount with a `Ccy` attribute and allowed fraction digits, ISO-date fields — then validate it with `lxml`'s `assertValid` until it passes. Using Faker-style fictive funds and LEIs keeps it realistic yet non-confidential, matching the study plan's approach. A schema-valid synthetic instance lets you exercise the full parse-and-map path safely in a regulated context.

Q: Summarise the end-to-end fund-order lifecycle naming the exact message numbers at each securities-side hop.
A: For a subscription: `setr.010` SubscriptionOrder is sent, `setr.016` OrderInstructionStatusReport reports acceptance or rejection, `setr.012` SubscriptionOrderConfirmation reports execution, then settlement runs `sese.023` instruction, `sese.024` status advice, and `sese.025` confirmation, with a `semt` holdings statement later reporting the resulting position. The redemption path substitutes `setr.004` and `setr.006` for the order and confirmation. Being able to name each number is exactly the "sketch the flow and name the type at each hop" Done-when criterion.

Q: How would you store the ISO 20022 to canonical-model mapping as code so it is testable, not just a spreadsheet?
A: Express each field mapping as a row of structured config — source XPath, MDR meaning, target table and column, transform, cardinality, and a gap flag — in a versioned YAML or a dbt model with documented columns, then write a test that asserts every schema-enumerated path is either mapped or explicitly flagged as a gap. Driving the mapping from data means a CI check can fail when a new schema version introduces an unmapped element. This turns "record the mapping" into an enforceable contract rather than a stale document.

Q: A `setr.010` arrives with an unexpected supplementary-data element your mapping does not cover. What is that element and how should you treat it?
A: ISO 20022 messages include a `SplmtryData` (SupplementaryData) extension point that lets a community carry agreed additional content the base schema does not define, validated loosely as `any`-style content. Because the XSD permits arbitrary registered extensions there, you must not assume it is empty: capture the raw `SplmtryData` for provenance and decide per market practice whether any of it must be mapped. Silently discarding supplementary data can drop community-mandated fields auditors expect.

Q: When validating against the variant XSD versus the global XSD, which do you choose for an instance whose BAH names a specific variant, and why?
A: Validate against the schema matching the full message-definition identifier in the BAH, including its variant token, because a community variant can tighten cardinalities such that the instance is only conformant under that variant's XSD. Using the global XSD instead can let through an instance that the variant would reject, or reject one the variant constrains differently. The identifier's third token is load-bearing: select the exact XSD it names rather than a generic one.
