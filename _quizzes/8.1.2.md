# Phase 5 · 8.1.2 Vendor reference architectures — 150 self-test questions

Q: What is a "vendor reference architecture" and why does this lesson tell you to read one critically rather than reverently?
A: A vendor reference architecture is a blueprint diagram a vendor (Databricks, Snowflake, Microsoft Fabric) publishes showing the recommended way to build a data platform on their stack. You read it critically because each blueprint is optimised for the vendor's lock-in, not for your freedom to leave, so the architect's job is to translate it into open-component vocabulary and circle the lock-in points live in a meeting before the org buys the diagram and discovers the exit cost three years later.

Q: The lesson warns that "the org buys the diagram and discovers the exit cost three years later." What does that sentence mean in practice?
A: It means a stakeholder approves a vendor blueprint on its aesthetics and feature list without anyone pricing or designing the path off it, so by the time the data lives in a proprietary catalog and proprietary format the cost to migrate out is huge. The architect prevents this by naming each lock-in point up front and writing the open-stack equivalent, so the exit cost is a conscious decision rather than a surprise.

Q: Name the three vendor blueprints this lesson asks you to be able to read and translate.
A: The three are the Databricks lakehouse reference (Unity Catalog-centric, medallion layering, DBU-priced compute), Snowflake data-cloud patterns (secure data sharing, zero-copy clones, listings and exchanges), and Microsoft Fabric end-to-end (OneLake-centric, capacity-based, experiences stitched together). The lesson's goal is that you can map all three onto your open-stack mental model.

Q: What is the single most important skill the "Done when" criteria want you to demonstrate for this lesson?
A: You must be able to translate any vendor diagram into open-component vocabulary live in a meeting, and name the single biggest lock-in point in each of the three blueprints. The point is fluency under pressure: an architect who can do this in real time controls the build-vs-buy conversation instead of being sold to.

Q: What does the Databricks "well-architected lakehouse" reference centre its governance on?
A: It centres governance on Unity Catalog, Databricks' unified metastore for access control, lineage, and discovery across tables, files, models, and dashboards. Unity Catalog is the linchpin of the blueprint because it is where data and AI governance is enforced, and it is also one of the primary lock-in points since it is a Databricks-proprietary catalog.

Q: What is the medallion architecture in the Databricks reference, and what do bronze, silver, and gold mean?
A: Medallion is a layered data-quality pattern: bronze holds raw ingested data, silver holds validated and cleansed/conformed data, and gold holds enriched, business-ready aggregates and marts. It is not Databricks-proprietary — you can implement the same bronze/silver/gold layering on any lakehouse — so when you translate the blueprint, medallion is the part that ports cleanly while the catalog and compute pricing do not.

Q: How is compute priced in the Databricks blueprint, and what is the unit called?
A: Compute is priced in DBUs (Databricks Units), a normalised unit of processing consumed per second while compute is running, multiplied by a rate that varies by workload type (jobs, all-purpose, SQL) and tier (Standard, Premium, Enterprise). You also pay the underlying cloud VM cost separately, so the effective bill is DBUs times the Databricks rate plus the cloud infrastructure cost.

Q: The lesson says the Databricks well-architected lakehouse has more pillars than the WAF five. How many, and which are the extra ones?
A: It has seven pillars: the five shared cloud pillars (operational excellence, security/privacy/compliance, reliability, performance efficiency, cost optimisation) plus two lakehouse-specific ones — data and AI governance, and interoperability and usability. The two extra pillars are exactly the areas a lakehouse most needs and are where Unity Catalog and open-format interoperability get argued.

Q: When you translate the Databricks medallion blueprint to open components, what maps to bronze/silver/gold and to the table format?
A: The bronze/silver/gold layering maps directly to layered tables in an open table format such as Apache Iceberg or Delta Lake on object storage, rather than Databricks-managed Delta tables. The proprietary surface to flag is Unity Catalog and Databricks-managed compute; the layering concept itself is vendor-neutral and carries over unchanged.

Q: In the Databricks blueprint, which single element is the biggest lock-in point and why?
A: Unity Catalog is the biggest lock-in point because once it owns your access control, lineage, and table registration, every consumer and pipeline binds to a Databricks-proprietary metastore, and there is no clean export of those grants and lineage into an open catalog. Open-table data in Delta/Parquet is comparatively portable; the catalog layer is what holds you.

Q: What open-stack catalog would you name as the equivalent of Unity Catalog when circling lock-in?
A: You would name an open catalog such as Apache Polaris, Project Nessie, the Hive Metastore, or AWS Glue Data Catalog depending on the estate, paired with Iceberg tables. The teaching point is that the catalog is a replaceable component in the open stack, whereas Unity Catalog is a Databricks-bound one; naming a concrete open alternative is what makes the translation credible in a meeting.

Q: What is the core mechanism behind Snowflake "secure data sharing"?
A: Secure data sharing gives a consumer account live, read-only access to a provider's datasets using metadata pointers rather than copying or moving the data. The shared objects (often secure views or tables) reference the provider's storage directly, so the consumer queries them as if local while the provider pays the single storage cost and retains full control.

Q: What is a Snowflake "zero-copy clone" and why is it called that?
A: A zero-copy clone creates a new, independently writable object (table, schema, or database) that initially shares the same underlying micro-partitions as the source via metadata, so no data is physically duplicated at clone time. It is "zero-copy" because storage is only consumed when cloned or source data subsequently diverges (copy-on-write), making it near-instant and cheap for creating dev/test or point-in-time copies.

Q: How do Snowflake "listings" and "data exchanges" differ from a direct share?
A: A direct share grants specific named accounts access, typically within the same region. Listings publish data through the Snowflake Marketplace and can be public (visible to all customers) or private (visible to selected accounts), while a data exchange is a private, curated hub for sharing among a defined group such as a company and its partners. All three sit on the same zero-copy sharing engine.

Q: In the Snowflake data-cloud blueprint, what is the single biggest lock-in point?
A: The biggest lock-in is that data lives in Snowflake's proprietary micro-partition storage format inside Snowflake-managed storage, and the sharing/clone features only work account-to-account within Snowflake. Zero-copy sharing and clones are powerful but they are network effects that bind both provider and consumer to the platform; the data is not in an open format you can point Trino at directly.

Q: What open-stack equivalents would you offer for Snowflake's zero-copy clone and secure sharing?
A: For zero-copy clone you would name Iceberg or Delta table snapshots and branching/tagging (e.g. Nessie branches), which give metadata-only point-in-time copies on object storage. For secure cross-party sharing you would name the open table format on shared object storage governed by a catalog with access policies, or the Delta Sharing open protocol, accepting that none replicate Snowflake's seamless marketplace network.

Q: Snowflake has begun supporting Apache Iceberg tables. How does that change your lock-in argument?
A: It softens but does not remove it: Iceberg tables let Snowflake read/write data in an open format on your own storage, reducing storage-format lock-in, but the compute, the secure-sharing/clone features, and often the catalog still bind you to Snowflake. The honest verdict in a meeting is that Iceberg support is a partial exit ramp, not an open architecture by default.

Q: What is OneLake in the Microsoft Fabric blueprint?
A: OneLake is Fabric's single, tenant-wide logical data lake — "one drive for data" — that every Fabric workload reads from and writes to, stored in open Delta-Parquet format. It is the centrepiece of the Fabric reference: all experiences (Data Factory, Synapse engineering/warehouse, Power BI) share OneLake rather than each maintaining its own storage.

Q: How is Microsoft Fabric priced, and what does "capacity-based" mean here?
A: Fabric is capacity-based: you buy a capacity SKU (F-SKUs such as F2, F8, F64, measured in Capacity Units) and all Fabric workloads draw from that shared pool of compute, rather than paying per service. This means a single noisy workload can starve others on the same capacity, and right-sizing the F-SKU plus smoothing/bursting behaviour is the cost and performance lever.

Q: The Fabric blueprint is described as "experiences stitched together." What does that phrase capture?
A: It captures that Fabric is a unification of previously separate products — Data Factory (ingestion/orchestration), Synapse Data Engineering and Data Warehouse, Real-Time Intelligence, Data Science, and Power BI — presented as one SaaS surface over OneLake. The "stitched together" wording is a warning: the experiences have different maturity and lineage, and the seams show, so the blueprint's neatness can oversell the integration.

Q: What is the single biggest lock-in point in the Microsoft Fabric blueprint?
A: The biggest lock-in is the capacity SKU and the all-in-one SaaS coupling to OneLake plus the Power BI/Direct Lake consumption layer, which ties analytics, storage, and compute to a Microsoft tenant and billing model. OneLake's Delta-Parquet storage is open enough to read elsewhere, but the capacity-based compute and the tightly integrated Power BI semantic layer are what hold the estate.

Q: Fabric stores data in Delta-Parquet. Why does that make storage-format lock-in lower than Snowflake's?
A: Because Delta Lake (Parquet files plus a transaction log) is an open format readable by Spark, Databricks, Trino, and other engines, so the bytes in OneLake are not trapped in a proprietary on-disk format the way Snowflake's micro-partitions are. The lock-in in Fabric therefore lives more in the compute capacity model and the consumption layer than in the storage format itself.

Q: What is a OneLake "shortcut" and why does it matter when reading the Fabric blueprint?
A: A shortcut is a pointer in OneLake to data stored elsewhere — another OneLake location, ADLS Gen2, Amazon S3, or Google Cloud Storage — so the data appears in Fabric without being copied. It matters because it is the part of Fabric that fights lock-in: it lets external open storage be queried in place, and you should highlight it as the seam where Fabric can interoperate with an open lake.

Q: Map all three blueprints onto a single "open-stack mental model." What are the four open components you keep returning to?
A: The recurring open components are an open table format (Apache Iceberg), an open catalog (Polaris/Nessie/Hive Metastore), an open query/compute engine (Trino, plus Spark/Flink), and open object storage (MinIO or any S3-compatible store). Every vendor blueprint can be re-expressed as some proprietary substitution for one or more of these four, which is exactly the translation the lesson trains.

Q: What are the four categories of lock-in point the "Do" step tells you to circle in a blueprint?
A: Proprietary catalog, proprietary format, capacity SKU, and egress trap. These four cover where the data's metadata is held, what format the bytes are in, how compute is billed, and what it costs to move data out — together they describe how hard and expensive it is to leave the vendor.

Q: What is an "egress trap" and why is it a lock-in point even when the data format is open?
A: An egress trap is the cloud charge for moving data out of the provider's network (and sometimes out of a region), which can make a technically-portable open format economically expensive to actually export. Even if your data is in open Iceberg/Parquet, if pulling petabytes out incurs large egress fees the architecture is still sticky, so egress economics belong in the lock-in analysis.

Q: Why is a "proprietary catalog" often a stronger lock-in than a "proprietary format"?
A: Because formats like Parquet/Delta/Iceberg can be read by many engines, so data in an open format is portable, but the catalog holds the table definitions, partitioning metadata, access grants, and lineage that every consumer depends on, and these rarely export cleanly. You can copy files; you cannot easily copy three years of accumulated grants and lineage out of a proprietary metastore.

Q: The "Do" step says to read one vendor blueprint "in a single sitting and annotate it." Why a single sitting?
A: Reading it end-to-end in one pass lets you see the whole shape and the vendor's narrative arc — how each piece justifies the next and funnels you toward proprietary components — rather than getting lost in individual service details. Annotating as you go captures lock-in points and open-stack equivalents while the overall argument is fresh, which is the artefact you bring to the meeting.

Q: Give the open-stack equivalent for each lock-in category: proprietary catalog, proprietary format, proprietary compute, proprietary storage.
A: Proprietary catalog maps to an open catalog (Polaris/Nessie/Hive Metastore); proprietary format maps to Apache Iceberg or Delta on Parquet; proprietary compute maps to Trino, Spark, or Flink you control; proprietary storage maps to MinIO or any S3-compatible object store. This four-way substitution table is the core deliverable of the lesson.

Q: A vendor diagram shows "Unity Catalog → Delta tables → DBU clusters → Databricks SQL." Translate it live into open components.
A: That reads as: an open catalog (Polaris/Nessie) over Iceberg/Delta tables on MinIO object storage, queried by Trino (or Spark for ETL) instead of DBU-priced clusters, with a BI tool hitting Trino in place of Databricks SQL. You would then circle Unity Catalog and the DBU compute as the two lock-in points and note the medallion layering carries over unchanged.

Q: Why does the architect translate blueprints to "open-component vocabulary" specifically, rather than to a rival vendor's stack?
A: Because open-component vocabulary is the neutral coordinate system that lets you compare any vendor against any other and against build-it-yourself on equal terms, and it is the only framing where lock-in is visible. Translating Databricks into Snowflake terms just swaps one lock-in for another; translating into Iceberg/catalog/Trino/MinIO exposes what each vendor is actually charging you to avoid building.

Q: In a Luxembourg fund-administration estate, why does vendor lock-in analysis carry extra regulatory weight?
A: Because regulated fund administrators are subject to outsourcing and exit-strategy expectations (e.g. CSSF circulars and DORA's third-party/ICT requirements), and a regulator will ask for a documented, tested exit plan from any critical cloud or data-platform provider. A blueprint with an un-priced exit cost is not just a commercial risk but a potential supervisory finding, so circling lock-in points feeds directly into the exit-strategy deliverable.

Q: How does DORA change how you read a vendor reference architecture for a fund platform?
A: DORA (the EU Digital Operational Resilience Act) requires financial entities to manage ICT third-party risk, including concentration risk and the ability to exit a critical provider without undue disruption. So when you annotate a Databricks/Snowflake/Fabric blueprint you must explicitly assess whether it creates a critical-provider dependency and whether a tested exit to an alternative (open stack or another vendor) is feasible within DORA's expectations.

Q: A fund's NAV-calculation data flows through a Snowflake share to the transfer agent. What lock-in concern does that raise?
A: The share binds both the fund administrator and the transfer agent to Snowflake — the consumer must have a Snowflake account to read the live share, creating a two-sided dependency that is hard to unwind. The architect should flag that this is convenient but couples a regulated data exchange to a single vendor, and the open-stack alternative would be sharing Iceberg data via the open Delta Sharing protocol or governed object storage.

Q: Why might storing fund data (ISINs, NAVs, holdings) in OneLake's Delta-Parquet be reassuring for data residency and exit, but the Fabric capacity SKU still a worry?
A: The Delta-Parquet bytes are in an open format and, with the right region configuration, can be kept inside the EU residency boundary and read by non-Microsoft engines, which eases the exit-of-storage concern. The capacity SKU remains a worry because the compute that processes NAV pipelines and the Power BI consumption layer are billed and locked to Microsoft's capacity model, so the processing tier is still single-vendor even when the storage is open.

Q: An EMT file feed lands daily and is processed in a Databricks medallion pipeline. Which parts are portable and which are lock-in?
A: The medallion layering (bronze raw EMT, silver validated, gold reporting marts) and the underlying Delta/Parquet files are portable. The lock-in is Unity Catalog governing those tables and the DBU-priced jobs/clusters running the transforms, so a true exit would re-point an open catalog and Iceberg/Delta at the same files and re-implement the jobs in Spark/Trino you control.

Q: What does it mean that "each blueprint is optimized for the vendor's lock-in"? Give the mechanism for one vendor.
A: It means the blueprint's recommended defaults steer you toward the components that are hardest to leave. For Snowflake, the mechanism is that secure data sharing and zero-copy clones are so frictionless that teams build workflows around them, and those features only function inside Snowflake, so the very productivity gain deepens the dependency the more you use it.

Q: Why does the lesson insist you can name the open-stack equivalent for "each lock-in point" rather than just identify the lock-in?
A: Because identifying lock-in without an alternative is just complaining, whereas naming the concrete open substitute (Iceberg for the format, a catalog for the metastore, Trino for query, MinIO for storage) turns the critique into an actionable build-vs-buy comparison. The equivalent is what lets you price the exit and argue the trade-off, which is the whole point of the exercise.

Q: When is buying into a vendor blueprint's lock-in actually the right call?
A: When the productivity, time-to-market, and operational-burden savings outweigh the priced exit cost and the org has consciously accepted the dependency — for example a small team with no platform engineers may rationally choose managed Fabric or Snowflake over running an open stack. The lesson teaches critical reading, not reflexive rejection; the goal is a documented, eyes-open decision rather than an accidental one.

Q: What is the danger of "learning a vendor blueprint reverently"?
A: Reverent learning treats the diagram as the definition of good architecture, so you absorb the vendor's framing — including its proprietary components as if they were neutral best practice — and lose the ability to see or price the lock-in. The architect must hold the blueprint at arm's length, mapping it to open components, so the vendor's marketing does not become your mental model of how data platforms must be built.

Q: What is Apache Iceberg and why is it the recurring "open format" answer in this lesson?
A: Apache Iceberg is an open table format that adds a metadata layer (manifests, snapshots, schema/partition evolution, ACID commits) over Parquet/ORC files on object storage, readable by Spark, Trino, Flink, and many engines. It is the recurring answer because it gives you warehouse-like table semantics without binding the data to any single vendor's engine or catalog, making it the natural open substitute for proprietary table formats.

Q: Why is Trino the open-stack stand-in for the vendor query engines in this lesson?
A: Trino is a distributed open-source SQL query engine that runs federated queries over object storage and many catalogs without owning the storage, so it plays the role that Databricks SQL, Snowflake compute, or Fabric warehouse play in the blueprints. Naming Trino over MinIO with an open catalog is the canonical "this is what the proprietary compute box really is" translation.

Q: Why is MinIO the open-stack stand-in for the vendor's storage layer?
A: MinIO is an S3-compatible open-source object store you can run yourself, so it stands in for ADLS, Snowflake-managed storage, or OneLake as the place the bytes live. Using MinIO in the translation makes the point that object storage is a commodity, and that the vendor's storage tier is a replaceable component rather than something only the vendor can provide.

Q: A stakeholder says "the Fabric diagram already does everything, why build an open stack?" How do you respond as the architect?
A: You acknowledge Fabric's integration genuinely reduces engineering effort, then translate the diagram to show that OneLake is Delta-Parquet (portable), but the capacity SKU and Power BI consumption layer are the lock-in, and you put a number on the exit cost and the concentration/DORA risk. The response is a priced trade-off, not a yes or no, so the org chooses with the exit cost visible.

Q: What is the difference between Delta Lake and Apache Iceberg as open table formats, briefly?
A: Both add ACID transaction logs and snapshots over Parquet on object storage; Delta Lake originated at Databricks and is tightly integrated with Spark, while Iceberg originated at Netflix and is engine-neutral with strong support across Trino, Flink, and Spark. For lock-in analysis both are "open format" answers, but Iceberg is generally seen as the more vendor-neutral default, and Delta's openness depends on using the open protocol rather than Databricks-managed features.

Q: Why does the Databricks blueprint add "data and AI governance" as a distinct pillar, and what does that imply for translation?
A: It adds the pillar because a lakehouse mixes data and ML assets that both need access control, lineage, and quality oversight, and Unity Catalog is positioned as the answer. For translation it implies that when you replace Unity Catalog with an open catalog you must also account for how you will deliver governance — access policies, lineage, discovery — since the open catalog alone may not match Unity Catalog's breadth.

Q: Why does the Databricks blueprint add "interoperability and usability" as a pillar, and how is that ironic for lock-in?
A: The pillar covers open standards, open formats, and ease of use so the lakehouse plays well with other tools and users. It is ironic for lock-in because the same blueprint that preaches interoperability still centres a proprietary catalog and DBU compute, so the architect should note where the vendor genuinely is open (Delta/Parquet) versus where the interoperability claim stops (Unity Catalog).

Q: What does "Direct Lake" mode in Fabric/Power BI do, and why is it relevant to lock-in?
A: Direct Lake lets Power BI read Delta tables in OneLake directly without importing or running a separate query, giving import-like performance over lake data. It is relevant because it makes the Power BI consumption layer extremely sticky — analysts get fast dashboards straight off OneLake — which strengthens the consumption-layer lock-in even though the underlying storage format is open.

Q: A blueprint shows data egressing to a partner via the vendor's marketplace. What two costs do you flag?
A: You flag the egress/data-transfer cost of moving data out of the vendor's network, and the dependency cost that the partner must be a customer of the same vendor to consume the marketplace listing. Together they show the sharing convenience carries both a per-byte economic trap and a two-sided platform lock-in, which is exactly what the "egress trap" lock-in category is about.

Q: Why is "live in a meeting" a deliberately chosen acceptance criterion for this lesson?
A: Because vendor selection decisions are made in rooms with sales engineers and executives, not in a quiet review afterwards, and the architect who can translate the diagram and name the lock-in in real time shapes the decision. Being able to do it offline is not enough; the value is intercepting the pitch as it happens so the lock-in is on the whiteboard before the org commits.

Q: What is a "secure view" in Snowflake sharing and why does it matter for a shared dataset?
A: A secure view hides its underlying SQL definition and base tables from the consumer, and supports row- and column-level security, so a provider can expose a filtered slice without revealing the full data or the logic. It matters because it is how a fund administrator could share, say, only a transfer agent's own positions from a larger dataset, controlling visibility while still using the zero-copy sharing mechanism.

Q: In the open stack, what replaces Snowflake's marketplace/listing distribution model, and what is the honest gap?
A: You replace it with sharing open-format data (Iceberg/Delta) on governed object storage, or the open Delta Sharing protocol, with a catalog enforcing access. The honest gap is that none of these reproduce Snowflake Marketplace's discovery and network effect — the convenience of a curated public catalog of monetisable datasets — so the open alternative trades that ecosystem for portability.

Q: What is the "capacity SKU" lock-in mechanism in Fabric, concretely?
A: You commit to an F-SKU (e.g. F64) that provides a fixed pool of Capacity Units shared across all Fabric workloads, billed whether or not fully used, and scaling means buying a bigger SKU. The lock-in is that your entire data estate's compute economics are expressed in Microsoft's capacity currency on a Microsoft tenant, so cost optimisation and exit both run through that single proprietary dial.

Q: How would you put a number on "exit cost" for a Databricks estate when challenging a blueprint?
A: You estimate the engineering effort to re-implement jobs in Spark/Trino, the cost to migrate table definitions and grants from Unity Catalog to an open catalog, the data egress charges to move (or the parallel-run period to re-derive) the storage, and the retraining/operational change. Even a rough figure turns "we're locked in" into a defensible cost line that decision-makers can weigh against the convenience.

Q: Why does the lesson treat medallion (bronze/silver/gold) as vendor-neutral while treating Unity Catalog as vendor-specific?
A: Because medallion is a layering convention — an idea about staging data by quality — that you can implement in any table format on any storage, whereas Unity Catalog is a concrete Databricks product with proprietary APIs, metadata, and grants. The lesson wants you to separate portable ideas from proprietary implementations so you don't mistake a vendor's branding of a generic pattern for lock-in or vice versa.

Q: When mapping a Snowflake blueprint, what do you say about its separation of storage and compute?
A: You note that separation of storage and compute (independently scalable warehouses over centralised storage) is a genuine architectural good and is replicated in the open stack by Trino/Spark compute over object storage. So that design principle ports cleanly; the lock-in is not the separation itself but that Snowflake's storage format and warehouse compute are proprietary, which you flag separately.

Q: A fund-tech team already pays for Snowflake and wants to add Databricks for ML. What lock-in tension do you raise?
A: You raise concentration and integration tension: running both means two proprietary catalogs and two compute-billing models, and moving data between them risks duplication, egress, and governance gaps. The architect's translation would show whether a shared open layer (Iceberg with both engines reading it, or Snowflake's Iceberg support) could reduce the double lock-in rather than stacking two vendor blueprints.

Q: Why are "zero-copy clones" attractive for a regulated fund's testing, and what is the catch?
A: They let you instantly create an isolated, writable copy of production data (e.g. a NAV dataset) for testing or a point-in-time investigation without duplicating storage or affecting prod, which is excellent for reproducibility and audit. The catch is that this beloved capability only exists inside Snowflake, so the more your test/audit processes depend on it, the harder the platform is to leave.

Q: What open-format feature gives you something similar to Snowflake's point-in-time clone for audit?
A: Iceberg (and Delta) snapshots plus time-travel queries let you read a table as of a previous snapshot or timestamp, and Iceberg/Nessie branches let you create isolated lines of development on the same data. These give the audit and reproducibility benefits in an open format, though they are less seamless than Snowflake's instant cloneable databases.

Q: In a single sentence each, name the biggest lock-in for all three vendors — the lesson's headline deliverable.
A: Databricks: Unity Catalog (the proprietary catalog binding grants and lineage); Snowflake: the proprietary micro-partition storage format plus account-bound sharing/clones; Fabric: the capacity SKU and OneLake/Power BI SaaS coupling to the Microsoft tenant. Being able to state these three crisply is exactly what the "Done when" checklist requires.

Q: Why is it risky to evaluate a blueprint only on its feature list rather than its lock-in profile?
A: Because feature lists are where vendors compete and look best, so an evaluation that stops there reliably picks the slickest pitch and ignores the cost of being unable to leave. The architect's discipline is to read past the features to the catalog/format/SKU/egress profile, because those determine the multi-year total cost and the regulatory exit position.

Q: What does the Fabric blueprint mean by "end-to-end," and what should make you cautious about that claim?
A: "End-to-end" means one SaaS surface covers ingestion, engineering, warehousing, real-time, data science, and BI over OneLake, so a team never leaves Fabric. You should be cautious because end-to-end coverage maximises the surface bound to one vendor, and because the experiences have uneven maturity, so the seamlessness is partly aspirational and the all-in dependency is real.

Q: How do you handle a blueprint component that has no clean open-stack equivalent?
A: You name it honestly as a genuine vendor advantage with no free substitute (for example Snowflake Marketplace's network or Fabric's tight Power BI integration), and you price what re-creating or living without it would cost. The translation exercise is not to pretend everything is replaceable, but to be precise about which dependencies are commodity-replaceable and which are real, paid-for value.

Q: Why does "proprietary format" appear as a lock-in category even though Parquet underlies Delta and Iceberg?
A: Because not every vendor stores in an open format — Snowflake's micro-partitions are proprietary on disk, and some warehouse formats are too — so "is the on-disk format open and readable by other engines?" is a real differentiator. Parquet underlying Delta/Iceberg is why those are scored as open; a format you can only read through the vendor's own engine is the lock-in the category targets.

Q: A blueprint claims "open by design." How do you stress-test that claim?
A: You ask: in what open format is the data on disk, can a third-party engine read it without the vendor's runtime, is the catalog exportable to an open standard, and what does egress cost to leave? If the answers are open format yes but catalog and compute no, then "open by design" is true only for storage, and you say so precisely rather than accepting the slogan.

Q: Why does this lesson sit in Phase 5 alongside landing zones, IaC, and DR rather than in an early phase?
A: Because by Phase 5 you have built a real open stack (Iceberg/Trino/MinIO/Dagster) over Phases 2–4, so you have the open-component mental model to translate against, and you are now making cloud-platform and vendor-selection decisions. Reading vendor blueprints critically only works once you have a concrete open baseline to compare them to, which the earlier phases provide.

Q: What is the relationship between this lesson and the Capstone-5 azurerm/WAF work?
A: Capstone 5 has you express a target architecture in IaC and run a Well-Architected review, and this lesson supplies the critical-reading muscle to evaluate whether vendor blueprints belong in that target and at what lock-in cost. Together they ensure the capstone architecture is a conscious composition of open and proprietary components with documented trade-offs, not a copied vendor diagram.

Q: A vendor's reference shows their proprietary catalog as the governance hub. Why is "we'll just use the open catalog later" usually naive?
A: Because once pipelines, BI tools, and access grants bind to the proprietary catalog, migrating them later means re-registering every table, re-creating every grant, and re-pointing every consumer — work that grows with adoption and is rarely prioritised. The realistic moment to choose the catalog is up front; deferring the open-catalog decision usually means never doing it.

Q: What is the open-stack answer when a blueprint centres a proprietary semantic/metrics layer?
A: You name an open semantic layer such as a dbt semantic layer or Cube, or model the metrics as governed gold tables/views queried by Trino, accepting that the integration with BI may be looser. The point is to show the metrics layer is a replaceable component rather than something only the vendor's stack can provide, even if the open version takes more assembly.

Q: How does the "interoperability" claim of a lakehouse blueprint interact with Iceberg's rise?
A: Iceberg's broad engine support has pushed vendors to add Iceberg read/write (Databricks, Snowflake, Fabric all have moves here), which genuinely increases interoperability at the storage layer and weakens format lock-in. The architect should reward this in the evaluation but still test the catalog and compute layers, because vendors open the format faster than they open the parts that actually retain customers.

Q: Why is naming the open-stack equivalent useful even for an org that will definitely buy the vendor product?
A: Because it establishes the baseline cost and capability to build it yourself, which is your negotiating leverage and your sanity check on the vendor's price, and it defines the exit path the regulator (and DORA) will ask about. Even a committed buyer benefits from a documented "here is what we would do instead," since that is what makes the dependency defensible.

Q: What common mistake do junior architects make when first reading a Snowflake or Databricks blueprint?
A: They internalise the vendor's component names as the architecture itself — talking in "warehouses," "DBUs," "Unity Catalog" — instead of in neutral roles (compute, billing unit, catalog), which makes lock-in invisible and cross-vendor comparison impossible. The fix is to immediately re-label every proprietary box with its generic role before evaluating, which is the habit this lesson builds.

Q: Why does the lesson pair "circle the lock-in" with "write the open-stack equivalent" as two steps, not one?
A: Because circling lock-in identifies the problem and writing the equivalent quantifies the alternative, and you need both to make a decision: the first shows where you are bound, the second shows the feasibility and cost of being free. Doing only the first produces fear; doing both produces a priced, actionable build-vs-buy recommendation.

Q: A Fabric blueprint relies on OneLake shortcuts to an external S3 bucket of fund data. Is that lock-in or anti-lock-in?
A: It is anti-lock-in for that data: the shortcut means the bytes stay in your own open object store and are merely surfaced in Fabric, so you could detach Fabric without moving the data. You would highlight it positively, while noting that any data created and stored natively in OneLake, plus the capacity-based compute and Power BI layer, remain the lock-in to watch.

Q: How do you explain to a CFO why a "cheaper-looking" vendor blueprint may be more expensive over five years?
A: You show that the headline price omits the exit cost (re-platforming, egress, re-training), the concentration risk premium, and the way capacity/credit pricing scales with growth, then contrast it with an open stack's higher upfront engineering but lower long-run lock-in. Framing it as five-year total cost including a priced exit reframes "cheap" as "cheap until you try to leave."

Q: What does "translate the diagram" actually look like as a written artefact?
A: It is the annotated blueprint plus a two-column table: each proprietary component on the left, its open-component role and concrete substitute on the right, with lock-in points circled and an exit-cost note. This artefact is what you walk through in the meeting and what feeds the ADR and the DORA exit-strategy documentation.

Q: Why might an org legitimately choose Fabric despite the capacity-SKU lock-in?
A: If the org is already deep in the Microsoft estate (Entra, Power BI, Office), has limited platform-engineering capacity, and values one integrated SaaS surface, the operational savings and faster delivery can outweigh the priced lock-in, and OneLake's open Delta storage keeps the data partially portable. The lesson supports such a choice as long as it is made with the capacity lock-in and exit cost explicit.

Q: How does Snowflake's cross-cloud/cross-region replication feature factor into both DR and lock-in?
A: It is a DR positive — you can replicate databases and shares across clouds/regions for failover — but it deepens lock-in because the replication, failover, and the shares it carries are all Snowflake-internal mechanisms. So you credit it for resilience while noting it makes the platform's DR posture itself dependent on staying with Snowflake, which matters under DORA's resilience expectations.

Q: What is the risk of mapping all three blueprints onto the open stack too glibly?
A: The risk is implying the open stack is a drop-in replacement with no cost, when in reality you take on catalog operation, upgrade burden, integration, and support that the vendor handled. An honest translation pairs each open substitute with the operational cost of running it yourself, so the build-vs-buy argument stays credible rather than ideological.

Q: Why is the egress trap especially relevant when evaluating a multi-cloud blueprint?
A: Because multi-cloud designs move data between providers and regions, and inter-cloud or cross-region egress is among the most expensive line items, so a blueprint that casually shuttles data can hide a large recurring bill. When you annotate, you mark every data-movement arrow that crosses a cloud or region boundary as a potential egress trap to be priced.

Q: A blueprint shows the vendor's proprietary ingestion tool feeding the lake. What is the translation and the caution?
A: The translation replaces it with an open ingestion/orchestration stack such as Airbyte plus Dagster (the FOSS comparison in this phase) writing Iceberg tables. The caution is that proprietary ingestion is usually the least sticky layer — easy to swap — so you note it as low lock-in and focus the lock-in argument on the catalog, format, and compute downstream.

Q: How does this lesson's "open-component vocabulary" connect to ADRs in the capstone?
A: The open-component framing is what you write the ADRs in — for example ADR-014 (OpenTofu vs Bicep) and the Kubernetes-vs-managed-PaaS ADR — because comparing options in neutral component roles plus priced lock-in is exactly what an Architecture Decision Record captures. The translation exercise produces the comparison that the ADR records and the review board approves.

Q: What single question best exposes a blueprint's lock-in in a meeting?
A: "If we had to leave this vendor in 18 months, what would it cost and how long would it take?" The answer immediately surfaces the catalog/format/compute/egress dependencies, and a vendor or advocate who cannot answer it concretely has just demonstrated that the exit cost is unknown — which is the finding you wanted.

Q: Why does the lesson emphasise reading the blueprint "critically" rather than rejecting vendors on principle?
A: Because reflexive rejection of all proprietary tooling is as poor as reverent acceptance — it forfeits real productivity and pushes the org toward an over-engineered open stack it cannot operate. Critical reading means weighing each component's lock-in against its value, so the recommendation is evidence-based and survives challenge from both the vendor camp and the open-source purists.

Q: When translating, how do you treat a vendor's managed governance/catalog if the org has no team to run an open one?
A: You acknowledge that the proprietary catalog's lock-in buys operated governance the org cannot otherwise staff, and you price that as a genuine value, not just a trap. The honest translation says "the open equivalent exists but requires a platform team to run it," letting the org decide whether to pay the lock-in for managed governance or invest in the capability.

Q: What is the difference between a "platform" lock-in and a "data" lock-in, and which matters more for exit?
A: Platform lock-in is dependence on the vendor's compute, orchestration, and tooling; data lock-in is your bytes and metadata being in a format/catalog you cannot read elsewhere. Data lock-in matters more for exit because you can rebuild platform tooling, but if the data and its governing metadata are trapped you cannot move at all — which is why catalog and format dominate the analysis.

Q: A blueprint pitches "zero-ETL" sharing as a benefit. How do you frame it critically?
A: You agree it removes pipeline cost and latency for sharing within the vendor's ecosystem, then frame it as a network-effect lock-in: zero-ETL works because both sides are on the same platform, so the benefit is realised only by deepening mutual dependence. The open-stack framing is shared open-format data on common storage, which trades some of that seamlessness for portability.

Q: How does the rise of open catalogs (Polaris, Nessie, Unity Catalog OSS) affect the lock-in story?
A: It is narrowing the catalog lock-in: vendors are open-sourcing or supporting REST catalog standards (the Iceberg REST catalog API), so catalogs are becoming more interchangeable. The architect should track this because the strongest historical lock-in (the catalog) is the one most in flux, and a blueprint built on an open catalog standard is materially less sticky than one on a closed metastore.

Q: When you "map all three onto your open-stack mental model," what does the resulting picture have in common across vendors?
A: In all three the picture reduces to the same skeleton — object storage, an open table format, a catalog, a compute/query engine, and a consumption/BI layer — with each vendor substituting proprietary components for some of those boxes. Seeing the common skeleton is the payoff: it proves the vendors are selling variations on one architecture, and the differences are precisely where the lock-in lives.

Q: Why is it important to identify which lock-in points are "reversible with effort" versus "effectively permanent"?
A: Because the decision changes with reversibility: a swappable ingestion tool or an open-format storage layer is a low-stakes commitment, while a proprietary catalog holding years of grants and lineage approaches permanent. Grading lock-in by reversibility lets the org accept the cheap, reversible dependencies and resist or mitigate the expensive, sticky ones rather than treating all lock-in as equal.

Q: A NAV reporting mart is published to investors via a Snowflake listing. What open alternative preserves the use case, and what is lost?
A: The open alternative is publishing the gold mart as Iceberg/Delta on governed object storage and sharing it via the Delta Sharing protocol or signed access, with a catalog enforcing entitlements. What is lost is the Snowflake Marketplace's frictionless discovery and the consumer experience of querying it as if local without any setup, which the open path makes the consumer assemble themselves.

Q: How would you summarise the architect's posture toward vendor blueprints in one sentence?
A: Treat every vendor blueprint as a persuasive argument optimised for the vendor's retention, translate it into neutral open-component roles, circle and price each lock-in point, and recommend the mix of proprietary and open that the org can operate and exit on defensible terms. That posture is the entire skill this lesson certifies.

Q: Why does running an open stack yourself still count as a "lock-in" of a different kind, and how do you present that fairly?
A: Running Iceberg/Trino/catalog/MinIO yourself locks you into an operational burden — upgrades, tuning, on-call, integration — which is a real cost even though no vendor owns you. You present it fairly by putting that operational cost beside the vendor's lock-in cost, so the choice is "who do you want to depend on, a vendor or your own platform team?" rather than a one-sided pitch for open source.

Q: What does the lesson imply about the half-life of any specific vendor-feature fact you memorise?
A: It implies the specifics — exact SKU names, which vendor just added Iceberg support, current pricing units — change fast, so memorising them reverently is fragile, whereas the durable skill is the translation method and the lock-in categories. That is why the lesson trains a way of reading blueprints rather than a snapshot of three products, and why you verify current details before quoting them in a meeting.

Q: Before quoting a blueprint detail (a SKU, a pricing unit, a new Iceberg capability) in a client meeting, what should you do?
A: Verify it against the vendor's current documentation rather than relying on memory, because these details change between releases and an out-of-date claim undermines your credibility and the recommendation. The disciplined architect checks the primary docs (Databricks well-architected, Snowflake sharing, Fabric tutorials) for the current state before asserting specifics live.

Q: What does the Iceberg REST catalog API change about catalog lock-in, and why should an architect care?
A: The Iceberg REST catalog API is an open specification for how engines talk to a catalog, so any compliant catalog (Polaris, Nessie, Unity Catalog OSS, vendor implementations) can be swapped behind it without rewriting every client. An architect should care because it turns the historically stickiest layer — the catalog — into a more interchangeable component, so a blueprint that speaks the REST catalog standard is materially less locked-in than one on a closed proprietary metastore.

Q: A blueprint shows a proprietary streaming/real-time tier feeding the lakehouse. How do you translate and grade it?
A: You translate it to the open streaming stack from earlier phases — Apache Kafka or Redpanda for the log and Apache Flink for processing — landing results as Iceberg/Delta tables. You grade the streaming tier as moderate lock-in: the ingestion path is replaceable, but if the vendor's stream processor stores state or offsets in a proprietary way, migration of in-flight stateful jobs is the part to price, not the broker itself.

Q: Why is "concentration risk" a distinct concern from ordinary lock-in when a single vendor underpins multiple critical functions?
A: Concentration risk is the systemic exposure of having many critical functions depend on one provider, so an outage, price change, or contractual dispute hits everything at once rather than one workload. It is distinct from lock-in because even a fully portable estate still carries concentration risk while it runs on one vendor, and DORA explicitly requires financial entities to assess and limit concentration on critical ICT third parties — making it an architecture-board and supervisory concern, not just a commercial one.

Q: After translating a blueprint, what is the final recommendation format the lesson is steering you toward?
A: The final format is a documented build-vs-buy recommendation: the annotated blueprint, a proprietary-to-open component table, each lock-in point circled and graded by reversibility and priced for exit, the concentration/DORA position, and a clear recommendation with its rationale captured as an ADR. This is what turns "I read the diagram critically" into an artefact an architecture review board and a regulator can act on.

Q: Pulling it together: a Databricks blueprint, a Snowflake share to the transfer agent, and a Fabric Power BI layer all appear in one fund estate. What is your top-line critique?
A: The top-line critique is triple concentration — three proprietary catalogs/compute models with overlapping function and three separate exit costs — which raises DORA concentration risk and total cost while fragmenting governance. Your translation would propose a shared open storage/format layer (Iceberg on object storage with an open catalog) that all three can read, shrinking the lock-in to compute/consumption and giving the estate one coherent, exitable data foundation.

Q: The Delta Sharing open protocol is the open-stack answer to Snowflake secure sharing — describe concretely how a recipient with no Databricks account reads a share.
A: The provider sends the recipient a credential/profile file (often via an `activation link`) containing the sharing-server endpoint URL and a `bearer token`, which the recipient's client puts in the HTTP `Authorization` header to authenticate. The sharing server logs the request, checks entitlements, and returns short-lived presigned cloud-storage URLs so the actual Parquet bytes stream directly from object storage to the recipient, not through the server — meaning a fund's transfer agent could consume a NAV share using any Delta Sharing client (pandas, Spark, Power BI) without a vendor account.

Q: What is the difference between Delta Sharing "open sharing" and "Databricks-to-Databricks" sharing, and why does it matter for your lock-in argument?
A: Open sharing (the `OpenSharing` protocol) uses a bearer-token profile file so any client on any platform can read the share, whereas Databricks-to-Databricks sharing requires the recipient to be on a Unity-Catalog-enabled Databricks workspace and identifies them by their metastore ID. It matters because only the open variant is genuinely anti-lock-in: the D2D path is more governed and performant but binds both sides to Databricks, so when you annotate a blueprint you note which Delta Sharing mode is in use before crediting it as "open."

Q: A blueprint shows Databricks reading a Snowflake table without copying it. Which feature is this, and is it lock-in relief or a new dependency?
A: This is Databricks Lakehouse Federation, where a foreign catalog is a read-only virtual representation of the Snowflake schema inside Unity Catalog, queried with Databricks compute. It is partial relief — you avoid duplicating data and egress for read access — but it is not an exit: the federation is read-only, the source still lives in Snowflake's proprietary format, and you have now added Unity Catalog as a second proprietary catalog in the path, so you flag it as convenience that deepens rather than reduces overall coupling.

Q: Fabric Mirroring is increasingly shown in Fabric blueprints. What does it do and what lock-in nuance does it carry?
A: Mirroring continuously replicates an operational source (Snowflake, Azure SQL, Cosmos DB, PostgreSQL) into OneLake as open Delta tables in near real time, converting the data to Parquet without you building ETL. The nuance is two-faced: the mirrored output is in open Delta-Parquet (portable, anti-lock-in for that copy), but it makes Fabric the analytical landing zone for every operational system and the replication engine itself is Fabric-managed, so adoption still funnels the estate's analytics into the capacity model.

Q: A Power BI report on Fabric suddenly slows down and you suspect it stopped using Direct Lake. Name three concrete triggers that force a fallback to DirectQuery.
A: Common triggers are: the SQL analytics endpoint object is a `view` (especially one doing joins/transforms) rather than a base table; the table enforces row-level security (RLS); and the Delta table exceeds the capacity's Direct Lake guardrails (max rows, row-groups, Parquet file count, or model-size-on-disk for the F-SKU). The first check is whether the semantic model targets a view or an RLS-protected table and whether the fact table outgrew its F-SKU guardrails, because DirectQuery routes each query back through the slower SQL endpoint.

Q: Why is Direct Lake fallback to DirectQuery a lock-in-relevant detail and not just a performance footnote?
A: Because the fix for fallback is usually "buy a bigger F-SKU" so the table fits the guardrails, which directly couples your reporting performance to Microsoft's capacity-pricing dial — the consumption layer's speed becomes a function of how much capacity you commit. For a fund's NAV dashboard, a growing fact table silently dropping to DirectQuery and then being "fixed" by upsizing capacity is exactly the capacity-SKU lock-in expressing itself through performance.

Q: What are Snowflake Data Clean Rooms and how do they differ from an ordinary secure share for lock-in purposes?
A: Data Clean Rooms (built on Snowflake's acquisition of Samooha and the Native App Framework) let two parties run approved, privacy-preserving analyses on each other's data without either seeing the other's raw rows — collaborators get only aggregated results from templates the data owner authorises. For lock-in they are stickier than a plain share: they sit on Secure Data Sharing but add a proprietary app-framework workflow, so the more a fund builds privacy-controlled partner analytics on clean rooms, the deeper the Snowflake dependency, with no clean open-protocol equivalent.

Q: Snowpipe vs Snowpipe Streaming appears in a Snowflake ingestion blueprint. Translate each and grade its stickiness.
A: Snowpipe is serverless micro-batch loading from cloud storage and Snowpipe Streaming is a low-latency row-level ingest API; you translate both to the open streaming stack (Kafka/Redpanda plus Flink, or a loader like Airbyte) landing Iceberg/Delta tables. Ingestion is the least sticky tier — you flag it as low lock-in and swappable — so the lock-in argument should stay focused on the catalog, format, and warehouse compute downstream, not the loader.

Q: A vendor demo shows querying data "in place" via Iceberg without moving it. What single question proves whether this is real openness?
A: Ask: "which catalog registers these Iceberg tables, and can a non-vendor engine read them through the `Iceberg REST catalog` API without the vendor's runtime?" If the tables are open Iceberg but the only catalog that knows about them is the vendor's proprietary metastore, the format is open while the catalog still locks you, so in-place Iceberg querying is openness only when both the format and the catalog interface are open.

Q: Snowflake offers both a proprietary catalog and "catalog-linked" / external Iceberg modes. Why does the catalog choice change the lock-in verdict more than the Iceberg adoption itself?
A: Because Iceberg-on-your-storage solves format lock-in but the metadata pointer — which catalog is the source of truth for snapshots and table state — decides who can write and who can read. If Snowflake is the managing catalog for the Iceberg tables you get open bytes but a proprietary control plane; if an open REST catalog manages them and Snowflake is just one reader, the table is genuinely portable, so you always trace the catalog ownership before declaring the Iceberg adoption a real exit ramp.

Q: A stakeholder says "we'll avoid lock-in by keeping everything in Delta Lake." Where is the hole in that reasoning?
A: Delta-Parquet on disk is open, but openness depends on using the open protocol rather than Databricks-managed features — Delta Live Tables, Unity Catalog grants, materialized views, and managed lineage are proprietary on top of the open files. The hole is conflating "the storage format is open" with "the platform is open": the bytes port cleanly while the catalog, the pipeline orchestration, and the governance layered above them do not, which is where the real exit cost sits.

Q: How would you quantify the DORA exit-strategy "testing" obligation against a Snowflake-centric blueprint, concretely?
A: DORA expects not just a documented exit plan but evidence it is feasible, so you would design and time-box a tested exit: export the gold marts to Iceberg/Parquet on your own object storage, re-point an open catalog and Trino at them, reproduce a representative NAV query, and record the elapsed time, data-egress cost, and any features (clones, marketplace) lost. The deliverable is a rehearsed runbook with real numbers, because a regulator distinguishes a paper exit plan from a demonstrated one.

Q: A blueprint puts the vendor's notebook/ML environment at the centre of the data-science tier. How do you translate and grade it?
A: You translate it to open notebooks (JupyterLab) plus an open experiment-tracking/registry tool (MLflow, which is itself open-source, or alternatives) over the same Iceberg tables, with orchestration in Dagster. You grade ML tooling as moderate lock-in: notebooks and tracking are replaceable, but if model lineage, feature tables, and serving are wired into the vendor's catalog (e.g. Unity Catalog models) the governance binding is the part to price, not the notebook UI.

Q: When you re-label a blueprint into neutral roles, what generic role does "Databricks SQL Warehouse," "Snowflake Virtual Warehouse," and "Fabric SQL endpoint/Warehouse" all share?
A: They are all the interactive SQL query/compute engine — the role that Trino plays in the open stack — sitting over storage and serving BI and ad-hoc queries with elastic, separately-billed compute. Re-labelling all three as "the query engine box" is exactly the habit the lesson builds: once they share a generic name, you can compare their billing units and their format/catalog bindings on equal terms instead of arguing vendor feature lists.

Q: A blueprint markets "auto-scaling serverless compute" as a differentiator. What is your critical framing?
A: Serverless auto-scaling genuinely removes cluster-management toil and you credit it, but it intensifies the billing-unit lock-in because consumption becomes opaque and entirely expressed in the vendor's currency (DBUs, credits, CUs) with no infrastructure cost you control. The open-stack framing is autoscaling Trino/Spark on Kubernetes you operate, trading the operational burden for transparent, portable compute economics — so serverless is convenience paid for with deeper billing dependency.

Q: A fund's auditor asks to reproduce yesterday's NAV exactly. Compare how Snowflake, an open Iceberg stack, and a vendor without time-travel each answer.
A: Snowflake answers with a zero-copy clone or Time Travel (`AT`/`BEFORE` query) to read the table as of yesterday; an open Iceberg/Delta stack answers with snapshot time-travel (read as of a snapshot ID or timestamp) plus optionally a Nessie branch for isolation; a vendor without time-travel forces you to rely on external backups or a separate audit copy. The teaching point is that audit reproducibility is a portable capability in the open stack, so it should not be the reason you accept the proprietary clone's lock-in.

Q: Why is "the vendor just added Iceberg support" a fact you should re-verify rather than memorise before a meeting?
A: Because Iceberg-support claims change between releases and differ by mode (read-only vs read-write, managed-catalog vs external-catalog, GA vs preview), and asserting an out-of-date or over-broad capability live undermines your credibility. The durable skill is the translation method and lock-in categories; the specific "X now supports Iceberg write via the REST catalog" detail must be checked against the vendor's current docs immediately before you quote it.

Q: A blueprint shows a single sign-on / identity binding (Entra, Snowflake users) as the access layer. Is identity a lock-in point, and how do you treat it?
A: Identity integration is usually low-to-moderate lock-in because SSO via standards like SAML/OIDC and SCIM provisioning is portable, but it becomes sticky when authorization grants and row/column policies live inside the vendor's proprietary catalog rather than in an external policy engine. You treat the SSO plumbing as portable while flagging that the actual entitlements — who can see which fund's positions — may be trapped in the proprietary catalog, which is the grant-migration cost you already price under catalog lock-in.

Q: A blueprint claims "no egress fees because data never leaves." How do you stress-test that for a regulated fund?
A: You ask where "never leaves" holds: it may be true for intra-cloud, intra-region access, but a tested DORA exit, a cross-region DR copy, or sharing with an off-platform counterparty all cross a boundary that does incur egress. For a Luxembourg fund you also check EU data-residency: "never leaves" must mean never leaves the EU region, not merely never leaves the vendor, so you separate the residency claim from the egress-economics claim and verify both.

Q: Two teams propose Iceberg but one keeps the catalog in the vendor and one runs Polaris. Why is this the decisive difference?
A: Because with Iceberg the catalog is the write-coordination and source-of-truth layer, so whoever owns the catalog controls table evolution, commits, and access — vendor-owned catalog means the open format still has a proprietary control plane, while a self-run Polaris (or Nessie) means any compliant engine can be the writer/reader. The decisive question is never "is it Iceberg?" but "who owns the catalog?", which is why the lesson ranks proprietary catalog above proprietary format as a lock-in.

Q: A blueprint bundles data quality / expectations into the vendor's pipeline product. Translate it and note the risk.
A: You translate it to an open data-quality layer — Great Expectations or `dbt test`/`dbt build` assertions, or Soda — running against the same Iceberg tables in your Dagster orchestration. The risk is that quality rules and their run history embedded in a vendor's proprietary pipeline product are an under-counted migration item: the checks are re-implementable, but the accumulated rule library and quality lineage are work to extract, so you list them in the exit cost rather than assuming the open tool is free.

Q: How do you respond when a vendor argues "open formats are slower, so our proprietary format is worth the lock-in"?
A: You concede that a vendor's tuned proprietary format can outperform a naïve open-table setup, then reframe: the comparison must be against a properly-configured Iceberg/Parquet layout (good partitioning, compaction, sorting, Z-ordering/clustering) and the question is whether the measured performance delta justifies the priced multi-year exit cost. You ask for a benchmark on representative fund workloads, because "faster" without numbers is a slogan and the trade is performance-now versus portability-and-exit-later.

Q: A vendor blueprint shows cross-region replication for DR. What two DORA-relevant points must your annotation make?
A: First, credit the resilience but flag that the replication, failover, and any shares it carries are vendor-internal mechanisms, so the DR posture itself depends on staying with the vendor — a concentration-risk concern. Second, verify the replica regions keep the data inside the required residency boundary (EU for a Luxembourg fund) and that the failover is actually tested, because DORA wants demonstrated resilience, not a replication checkbox.

Q: An architect annotates a blueprint and finds the only proprietary lock-in is the BI/consumption layer (e.g. Power BI Direct Lake). How sticky is that, really?
A: Moderately to very sticky in practice, because dashboards, semantic models, RLS rules, and report definitions accumulate and re-platforming BI means rebuilding all of them and retraining users — even though the underlying lake data is open. You grade it as reversible-with-significant-effort: the data exits cleanly, but the consumption layer is where user habit and a large catalogue of reports create real switching friction, so you price the report-rebuild and retraining explicitly.

Q: Compare the lock-in profile of "managed open" offerings (e.g. managed Iceberg/Trino services) against a fully proprietary warehouse.
A: A managed-open service runs open formats and engines (Iceberg, Trino/Spark) so your data and queries are portable even though the operation is outsourced — exit means moving the same artefacts to another runtime. A fully proprietary warehouse traps the format and/or catalog, so exit means re-deriving data and metadata. The verdict: managed-open trades operational lock-in (which is reversible) for far less data lock-in, which is usually the better deal for a regulated estate that needs a demonstrable exit.

Q: A NAV pipeline must hit a cut-off time. How does the capacity-SKU model create a hidden risk the architect should surface?
A: On a shared Fabric capacity, a noisy neighbour workload can consume Capacity Units and throttle or queue your NAV job, so a fixed-pool SKU couples your time-critical pipeline's reliability to unrelated workloads' behaviour. The architect surfaces this as a reliability-plus-lock-in finding: meeting the NAV cut-off may force you to upsize the F-SKU or isolate capacity, both of which deepen the capacity commitment, so the SLA and the lock-in dial are the same dial.

Q: A blueprint uses the vendor's marketplace to buy third-party reference data (e.g. an ISIN/LEI or pricing feed). What lock-in and what alternative?
A: The lock-in is two-sided: consuming the marketplace listing requires being a customer of that vendor, so a convenient data-acquisition channel quietly mandates the platform. The alternative is sourcing the same reference data via the provider's API/files or the open Delta Sharing protocol and landing it as Iceberg, accepting you lose the marketplace's frictionless discovery and billing in exchange for not tying your reference-data supply to a single platform.

Q: When translating, why should you distinguish "control plane" from "data plane" lock-in?
A: The data plane is where the bytes live and their format; the control plane is the catalog, governance, orchestration, and billing that coordinate access. Data-plane lock-in (proprietary format) is often shallower than control-plane lock-in (proprietary catalog/governance) because you can copy bytes but cannot easily export grants, lineage, and policies — so naming which plane each lock-in point sits in tells the org which dependencies are cheap to leave and which are not.

Q: A vendor offers to "import your existing Iceberg tables" into its managed catalog. What is the subtle lock-in to watch?
A: The subtlety is whether the vendor becomes the managing catalog (the sole writer/source-of-truth) for those tables or merely a federated reader — if it takes over catalog management, future commits and the table's control plane move into the vendor even though the files stay open. You watch for "managed" vs "external/foreign" registration, because an import that converts your open-catalog tables into vendor-managed ones quietly relocates the stickiest layer.

Q: How do you grade lock-in for a vendor's orchestration/workflow tool (e.g. Databricks Workflows, Fabric Data Factory pipelines)?
A: Orchestration is moderate, reversible-with-effort lock-in: the schedules, dependencies, and pipeline definitions are proprietary and must be re-authored, but the underlying tasks are portable to an open orchestrator (Dagster/Airflow). You price the re-authoring of the DAGs and any vendor-specific activities, and note that orchestration rarely traps data — so it ranks below catalog and format but above pure ingestion in stickiness.

Q: A team says zero-copy clones make their dev/test workflow effortless and they cannot give it up. How do you handle the dependency honestly?
A: You acknowledge the clone is a genuine, hard-to-replicate convenience and name the nearest open equivalents (Iceberg/Delta snapshot reads, Nessie branches) while being honest that they are less seamless than instant cloneable databases. Then you price living without it: the question is whether the productivity of clones is worth the share of exit cost they create, framed as a conscious trade rather than pretending the open stack matches it.

Q: A blueprint shows the vendor's lineage/governance graph as a selling point. Why is lineage a particularly painful exit item?
A: Because lineage is accumulated metadata describing how every table derives from sources across years of pipeline runs, and it is held in the vendor's proprietary graph with no standard export, so leaving means either abandoning historical lineage or rebuilding it. For a regulated fund that must evidence data provenance for NAV figures, losing lineage on exit is a compliance cost, which is why catalog/governance lock-in is weighted heavily in the analysis.

Q: A vendor claims "we support the Iceberg REST catalog, so you're never locked in." How do you verify the claim is meaningful?
A: You check whether the vendor exposes its catalog through the open REST API such that an external engine (Trino, Spark) can both read and write through it, or whether REST support is read-only, partial, or limited to specific table types. Meaningful openness means another engine can be a first-class writer via the standard API; if the REST endpoint is a constrained read shim around a proprietary metastore, the standard is being used as marketing rather than a real exit interface.

Q: Two blueprints both store open Parquet but only one lets a foreign engine write. Why does write-access change the lock-in grade?
A: Read-only openness lets you copy data out but you still must produce and update tables through the vendor, so the vendor remains the system of record and the write path is proprietary. Write-access through an open catalog means another engine can maintain the tables, making the vendor genuinely optional — so multi-engine write capability, not just read, is the bar for calling a format-plus-catalog combination truly portable.

Q: A fund's EMT/EPT/EET regulatory files must be retained for years. How does retention interact with format and egress lock-in?
A: Long retention multiplies the data volume that any exit must move, so a proprietary on-disk format plus per-byte egress turns "keep seven years of EMT/EET history" into a large, growing exit liability. Storing the retained files in open Iceberg/Parquet on object storage you control decouples retention from the compute vendor, so the regulatory archive does not become the heaviest anchor when you re-platform the processing tier.

Q: When mapping all three vendors, where does the "consumption/BI layer" sit and why is it the box most often left proprietary?
A: It sits at the top of the skeleton (object storage → format → catalog → compute → consumption/BI), and it is most often left proprietary because BI is where users live, semantic models and reports accumulate, and the integration (Direct Lake, Snowsight, Databricks dashboards) is genuinely convenient. The architect notes that even an otherwise-open stack often keeps a proprietary BI tool, so the consumption layer is frequently the last and stickiest lock-in to address.

Q: A vendor's "data contract" or schema-management feature is in the blueprint. Translate it and assess lock-in.
A: You translate it to open contract tooling — schema-as-code in dbt, a schema registry for streaming, or open data-contract specs validated in CI — over the same tables. Lock-in is low-to-moderate: contracts are largely declarative and portable, but if enforcement is wired into the vendor's pipeline runtime you re-implement the gate. You credit the discipline while keeping the enforcement mechanism vendor-neutral so contracts survive an exit.

Q: How would you explain to a risk committee the difference between "vendor outage risk" and "vendor exit risk"?
A: Outage risk is short-term availability — the platform goes down and your NAV pipeline stalls — mitigated by DR, multi-region, and SLAs. Exit risk is the structural cost and feasibility of leaving the vendor at all, driven by catalog/format/compute lock-in and egress. They are distinct: a perfectly available vendor can still pose severe exit risk, and DORA requires you to manage both, so the committee must see availability and exitability as separate columns.

Q: A blueprint shows the vendor handling encryption and key management. What do you check before crediting it as "secure and portable"?
A: You check who controls the keys: customer-managed keys (CMK/BYOK) in your own key vault preserve some control and ease exit, whereas vendor-managed keys mean the encryption is bound to the vendor's KMS and a migration must re-encrypt. For a regulated fund you verify that key custody, rotation, and revocation meet policy and that an exit does not strand data behind keys only the vendor can produce, so encryption is assessed as both a security and a portability question.

Q: Why might "the open stack" itself fail a DORA exit test if implemented carelessly?
A: Because running Iceberg/Trino/catalog/MinIO yourself can still concentrate everything on one cloud's object storage and one managed Kubernetes, and if you never tested moving it, the exit is theoretical. DORA cares about demonstrated exitability and concentration regardless of openness, so an undocumented, untested open stack on a single provider can be as much a supervisory finding as a proprietary one — openness is necessary but not sufficient; you still rehearse the exit.

Q: A vendor pitches "we'll migrate your data in for free." How does this asymmetry inform your lock-in reading?
A: Free ingress and easy import are deliberate — the vendor subsidises getting data in because the friction and cost are designed to be on the way out (egress, format conversion, catalog re-registration). The asymmetry between cheap import and expensive export is itself a lock-in signal, so when import is free you scrutinise the exit path harder and explicitly price the reverse journey the vendor is conspicuously not subsidising.

Q: A blueprint relies on the vendor's materialized views / auto-optimization for performance. What is the translation and the hidden cost?
A: You translate it to open equivalents — Iceberg table maintenance (compaction, sorting/clustering, snapshot expiry) and possibly pre-aggregated gold tables refreshed by Dagster — accepting more manual tuning. The hidden cost is that proprietary auto-optimization makes the vendor's performance feel effortless, so its absence in the open stack is real operational work, which you put beside the lock-in cost rather than pretending the open path is performance-equivalent for free.

Q: When does naming the open-stack equivalent actually weaken your recommendation, and how do you avoid that?
A: It weakens it when you name a substitute glibly without the operational reality — implying Trino/catalog/MinIO is a free drop-in — so a sceptical CTO who knows the on-call and integration burden discounts your whole analysis. You avoid it by pairing every substitute with its run-cost and maturity caveat, so the translation reads as an honest engineer's trade rather than an open-source advocate's wish-list, which is what survives challenge in the room.

Q: A blueprint shows a "bring your own storage" option but the catalog stays managed. Why is this the most common partial-openness pattern, and how do you score it?
A: It is common because vendors can cheaply let you keep bytes in your own bucket (low-value, commodity layer) while retaining the catalog/control plane (high-value, sticky layer) that actually retains customers. You score it as storage-portable but control-plane-locked: a real improvement on residency and egress, but the table state, grants, and write coordination still live with the vendor, so the headline "bring your own storage" must not be read as "bring your own platform."

Q: A fund-tech lead asks which single artefact best satisfies both the architecture board and the DORA examiner. What do you produce?
A: A single annotated-blueprint package: the diagram re-labelled into open-component roles, a proprietary-to-open substitution table, each lock-in point circled and graded by reversibility with a priced exit estimate, an explicit concentration-risk and EU-residency note, and a tested-exit runbook with timings — captured as an ADR. The same document answers the board's build-vs-buy question and the examiner's outsourcing/exit-strategy question, which is why the lesson drives toward it as the terminal deliverable.

Q: Across Databricks, Snowflake, and Fabric, give the one-line "neutral role" name for each vendor's catalog, and why naming them identically is the point.
A: Unity Catalog, Snowflake Horizon/the account metastore, and the Fabric/OneLake catalog are all simply "the catalog — the metadata control plane holding table definitions, grants, and lineage," the role an open `Iceberg REST catalog` (Polaris/Nessie) fills. Naming them identically is the whole trick: it collapses three marketing vocabularies into one comparable box, exposes that the catalog is the dominant lock-in in every blueprint, and lets you ask the same exit question of all three.

Q: A SWIFT-driven settlement feed lands hourly and a vendor blueprint routes it through the vendor's proprietary CDC/change-feed tool. Translate and grade it.
A: You translate the change-data-capture tier to an open path — Debezium emitting change events to Kafka/Redpanda, processed by Flink and landed as Iceberg upserts (or Delta merge) — keeping the open table format as the system of record. You grade CDC as moderate lock-in: the connector and stream are replaceable, but if the vendor's CDC stores offsets, checkpoints, or schema history in a proprietary store, re-establishing exactly-once delivery on migration is the part to price, not the act of capturing changes itself.

Q: A blueprint shows the same data shared to two consumers, one via Snowflake share and one via the vendor marketplace. Why might the architect still flag concentration even though sharing "saves copies"?
A: Because both sharing paths require every consumer to hold an account on the same vendor, so the network effect that avoids data copies simultaneously multiplies the number of external parties bound to one platform — convenience and concentration grow together. Under DORA that is exactly the systemic exposure to watch: a single provider now underpins an expanding web of counterparties, so saving storage copies does not reduce concentration risk and can quietly increase it.

Q: A vendor demo shows an AI/LLM assistant generating SQL and pipelines natively in the platform. How do you read this for lock-in and for the EU AI Act?
A: For lock-in you note that an embedded copilot deepens stickiness by training users to author work inside the vendor's surface and by coupling generated assets to proprietary APIs, while the underlying open data stays portable — so you grade the assistant as consumption-layer convenience, not data lock-in. For the EU AI Act, an assistant that merely helps engineers write SQL is low-risk, but if its outputs feed decisions about investors or fund operations you assess transparency, human-oversight, and logging obligations rather than assuming a productivity feature is regulation-free.

Q: Summarise the exact decision rule the lesson wants you to apply to every box in a vendor blueprint.
A: For each box, ask three questions in order: what neutral role does it play (storage, format, catalog, compute, consumption), is it open or proprietary in that role, and if proprietary how reversible and how costly is leaving it (egress, re-registration, rebuild). The rule is to accept cheap, reversible, high-value proprietary dependencies and resist or mitigate expensive, sticky ones — turning the whole blueprint into a graded, priced build-vs-buy map rather than a yes/no on the vendor.
