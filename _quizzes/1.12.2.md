# Phase 2 · 1.12.2 + 1.12.3 LEI & ISIN (entity and instrument identity) — 150 self-test questions

Q: What is an ISIN and which ISO standard defines it?
A: An ISIN (International Securities Identification Number) is a 12-character alphanumeric code that uniquely identifies a security, defined by ISO 6166. It is the global identifier for a fungible instrument such as a fund share class, bond, or equity. In a fund platform it is the join key that lets you tie a NAV, a holding, and an order to the same instrument across systems.

Q: How many characters does an ISIN contain and how are they laid out?
A: An ISIN is exactly 12 characters: a 2-letter ISO 3166 country (prefix) code, then a 9-character NSIN (National Securities Identifying Number), then a single check digit. For example `LU1234567890` would be a Luxembourg-domiciled instrument with `1234567890` split as a 9-char NSIN plus a trailing check digit. The fixed width and structure make it easy to validate length before any check-digit math.

Q: What does the two-letter prefix of an ISIN represent?
A: The first two characters are an ISO 3166 alpha-2 country code that identifies the National Numbering Agency's jurisdiction, not necessarily where the security trades. Many Luxembourg fund share classes carry the `LU` prefix because the Luxembourg NNA allocated them, even though they may be distributed and traded across Europe. The prefix tells you who assigned the ISIN, which is a common source of confusion when people assume it means the listing country.

Q: What is the NSIN portion of an ISIN?
A: The NSIN (National Securities Identifying Number) is the middle 9 characters of an ISIN, allocated by the country's National Numbering Agency. It often wraps an existing national code — for example a US ISIN embeds the 9-character CUSIP, and a UK ISIN embeds the 7-character SEDOL padded with leading zeros. The NSIN is what actually distinguishes one instrument from another within a country's namespace.

Q: Who allocates ISINs and how is allocation coordinated globally?
A: ISINs are allocated by National Numbering Agencies (NNAs), with generally one NNA per country, coordinated by ANNA (the Association of National Numbering Agencies). ANNA acts as the ISO 6166 Registration Authority and operates the ANNA Service Bureau that consolidates ISIN data globally. This federated model is why you cannot mint an ISIN yourself — you request one from the relevant NNA.

Q: Which numbering agency typically allocates ISINs for Luxembourg-domiciled funds?
A: For Luxembourg-domiciled instruments the relevant National Numbering Agency allocates `LU`-prefixed ISINs, historically handled through the Luxembourg numbering body associated with the central securities depository. Each new fund share class gets its own ISIN from that NNA. This matters because a single fund umbrella can spawn dozens of `LU` ISINs as new share classes are launched.

Q: Why is it said that "ISIN ≠ listing"?
A: An ISIN identifies the instrument itself, but the same ISIN can be listed and traded on many venues, so the ISIN alone cannot tell you where a trade occurred or at what venue-level price. Venue-level identity needs a different identifier such as a MIC for the market and FIGI for the listing line. Treating an ISIN as if it were a venue identifier silently collapses multiple listings into one and loses price-formation detail.

Q: What is a MIC and how does it relate to an ISIN?
A: A MIC (Market Identifier Code, ISO 10383) is a 4-character code identifying a trading venue or market segment, such as `XLUX` for the Luxembourg Stock Exchange. While the ISIN says which instrument, the MIC says which market. You combine ISIN plus MIC when you need to know which specific exchange a quote, trade, or listing belongs to.

Q: What is a FIGI and why is it the venue-level complement to ISIN?
A: A FIGI (Financial Instrument Global Identifier) is an open, 12-character identifier from the Object Management Group and Bloomberg's OpenFIGI that can identify instruments down to the individual listing on a specific venue. Because one ISIN can have many venue listings, FIGI provides distinct identifiers per listing where ISIN does not. It is the answer when you need a stable key for a specific exchange line rather than the instrument as a whole.

Q: Why is treating an ISIN as a unique key for "where it trades" a modeling mistake?
A: Because an ISIN identifies the instrument, not the listing, the same ISIN appears across multiple exchanges, each with its own MIC, currency, and microstructure. If your model uses ISIN as the venue key you will double-count or merge venue-distinct rows. The correct design keys instrument-level facts on ISIN and venue-level facts on ISIN plus MIC (or a FIGI).

Q: What algorithm computes the ISIN check digit?
A: The ISIN check digit is computed with the Luhn algorithm (modulus 10) after converting every letter to a number. Each letter is replaced by its position-derived value where `A`=10, `B`=11, through `Z`=35, producing a long digit string. The Luhn mod-10 process then yields the single check digit that makes the whole 12-character string valid.

Q: Walk through the steps to validate an ISIN check digit.
A: First convert each letter to two digits using `A`=10 … `Z`=35, leaving digits as-is, to get a numeric string from the first 11 characters. Then apply the Luhn algorithm: starting from the rightmost digit of that string, double every second digit, sum the digits of any result over 9, and add everything up. The check digit is the amount needed to bring the total to the next multiple of 10, i.e. `(10 - (sum mod 10)) mod 10`.

Q: In ISIN letter-to-digit conversion, what numeric value does the letter `A` take?
A: In ISIN check-digit calculation `A` is converted to `10`, `B` to `11`, and so on up to `Z` = `35`. This is the same scheme used by CUSIP-style alphanumeric Luhn checks. So a country prefix like `LU` becomes `21` `30` before the Luhn pass, because `L`=21 and `U`=30.

Q: Why does an ISIN carry a check digit at all?
A: The check digit is a self-validation mechanism that catches the most common manual-entry errors — single-digit typos and most adjacent transpositions — before the identifier is trusted as a key. In a fund pipeline this lets you reject a malformed ISIN at ingestion rather than discovering a phantom instrument downstream. It is cheap insurance: a length check plus one Luhn computation rejects a large fraction of corrupted identifiers.

Q: Does a valid ISIN check digit prove the security actually exists?
A: No — the check digit only proves the string is internally consistent (correct length and a Luhn-valid trailing digit), not that any NNA ever issued that ISIN. A randomly constructed code with a correct check digit will still pass validation while referencing nothing real. To confirm existence you must look the ISIN up against an authoritative reference source such as an NNA or vendor security master.

Q: An ingested ISIN passes a length check but fails check-digit validation — what is the likely cause?
A: A length-valid but check-digit-invalid ISIN almost always means a transcription or transformation error: a transposed pair, a dropped leading zero in the NSIN, or a letter-case or character-set mangling during a CSV/Excel round-trip. The first thing to check is whether a spreadsheet stripped leading zeros or coerced the value to a number. Re-pull the raw source and compare byte-for-byte before assuming the source itself is wrong.

Q: What is an LEI and which standard defines it?
A: An LEI (Legal Entity Identifier) is a 20-character alphanumeric code that uniquely identifies a legal entity participating in financial transactions, defined by ISO 17442. Unlike an ISIN, which identifies an instrument, an LEI identifies an organisation — for example a fund's management company or a counterparty. It is the global "who" key that complements the instrument-level "what" key.

Q: How many characters does an LEI have and what is its segment structure?
A: An LEI is exactly 20 characters: a 4-character LOU prefix, two reserved characters that are always `00`, a 12-character entity-specific part, and two trailing check digits. So the layout is positions 1–4 (LOU), 5–6 (`00`), 7–18 (entity), 19–20 (check digits). The reserved `00` is a fixed feature of the format that you can use as a quick structural sanity check.

Q: What is the LOU prefix in an LEI?
A: The first four characters of an LEI identify the LOU (Local Operating Unit) — the issuing organisation that allocated that LEI under GLEIF accreditation. It ensures globally unique allocation by partitioning the namespace per issuer. Knowing the prefix tells you which registrar minted the code but says nothing about the entity's industry or country.

Q: What occupies characters 5 and 6 of an LEI?
A: Characters 5 and 6 of an LEI are reserved and are always set to `00` in the current ISO 17442 format. They carry no information today and exist as reserved positions in the standard. A quick way to flag an obviously malformed LEI is to check that positions 5–6 are `00`.

Q: How is the LEI check digit computed?
A: The final two characters of an LEI are check digits computed using the ISO/IEC 7064, MOD 97-10 scheme — the same family as IBAN validation. Letters in the first 18 characters are converted to numbers (`A`=10 … `Z`=35), the two check positions are treated as appended, and the whole number must satisfy `mod 97 == 1`. This two-digit checksum catches more errors than a single Luhn digit, which is appropriate for a 20-character identifier.

Q: How do you verify an LEI's check digits in practice?
A: Take the 20-character LEI, move nothing (the check digits are already at the end), convert all letters to digits with `A`=10 … `Z`=35, and interpret the result as one large integer. The LEI is valid if that integer modulo 97 equals 1. If you are generating a check digit instead, you append `00`, compute `98 - (n mod 97)`, and zero-pad to two digits.

Q: Why does an LEI use two check digits while an ISIN uses only one?
A: The LEI uses ISO 7064 MOD 97-10, which yields two check digits and detects essentially all single-character errors and transpositions, whereas the ISIN's single Luhn digit catches most but not all such errors. The stronger scheme reflects the LEI's role as a regulatory identity key where a misidentified counterparty has serious consequences. The trade-off is two characters of overhead for materially better error detection.

Q: What is GLEIF and what role does it play?
A: GLEIF (the Global Legal Entity Identifier Foundation) is the not-for-profit body that oversees the Global LEI System, maintains the central registry, and accredits the LOUs that issue LEIs. It does not issue LEIs directly to most entities; instead it governs the system and publishes the consolidated reference data. GLEIF is the authoritative source you reconcile against when validating that an LEI is real and current.

Q: What is the GLEIF golden copy?
A: The GLEIF golden copy is the consolidated, de-duplicated reference dataset that GLEIF publishes combining all LEI records ever issued by all LOUs into a single authoritative file set. It removes technical duplicates and guarantees completeness across issuers, which the raw per-issuer files do not. For a lakehouse it is the ideal free dataset to land in bronze as your entity reference source.

Q: In what formats and how often is the GLEIF golden copy published?
A: The golden copy is published in XML, CSV, and JSON, three times a day at fixed UTC times (02:00, 10:00, and 18:00), with the underlying index updated up to ten times daily. GLEIF also publishes delta files containing only newly issued or revised LEIs so you can apply incremental updates. Choosing CSV or JSON over XML often simplifies loading into a columnar bronze table.

Q: What are GLEIF delta files and why use them?
A: Delta files contain only the LEIs that were newly issued or revised since a prior golden-copy release, rather than the full dataset. Loading deltas lets you keep your bronze entity table current with a small daily download instead of re-ingesting the entire registry each cycle. The gotcha is that you must track which baseline version your deltas apply to, or you risk gaps and out-of-order application.

Q: What is "Level 1" data in the Global LEI System?
A: Level 1 data is the "who is who" reference data: the foundational attributes of each legal entity tied to its LEI, such as legal name, registered and headquarters address, jurisdiction, registration status, and registration authority details. GLEIF enriches it with normalized addresses and geocoding. It answers identity questions about a single entity in isolation.

Q: What is "Level 2" data in the Global LEI System?
A: Level 2 data is the "who owns whom" relationship data: records that link an entity's LEI to its direct parent and ultimate parent entities, enabling reconstruction of corporate hierarchies. It is captured as relationship records rather than flat attributes on one entity. This is what lets you roll exposures up an ownership tree across the global LEI population.

Q: Distinguish what Level 1 versus Level 2 data each enable.
A: Level 1 enables you to identify and describe a single entity — confirm its legal name, country, and status from its LEI. Level 2 enables you to traverse ownership, answering "which group ultimately owns this management company?" by following direct- and ultimate-parent relationship records. In practice Level 1 powers entity master data and Level 2 powers group-level aggregation and concentration analysis.

Q: In Level 2 data, what is the difference between a direct parent and an ultimate parent?
A: The direct parent is the immediate accounting-consolidating parent one level up, while the ultimate parent is the entity at the very top of the consolidation chain. An entity can have a direct parent that is itself owned by a different ultimate parent several levels higher. Modeling both lets you answer immediate-owner questions and top-of-house exposure questions separately.

Q: Why might a legal entity have no parent relationship recorded in Level 2 data?
A: An entity may report that it has no parent (it is the ultimate parent itself) or may report a reason why parent information is not provided, such as legal obstacles or consent constraints. GLEIF captures these "reporting exceptions" explicitly rather than leaving a silent gap. When building hierarchies you must handle these exception codes, or you will mistake a deliberate non-disclosure for missing data.

Q: How does an LEI relate to an ISIN conceptually?
A: An LEI identifies a legal entity (the issuer, management company, or counterparty), while an ISIN identifies a specific instrument that entity may issue or manage. They operate at different levels: one entity (one LEI) can be associated with many instruments (many ISINs). In a security master you link the instrument's ISIN to the issuing or managing entity's LEI to connect the "what" to the "who".

Q: In the study task, what does "link your Faker funds to real LEIs of their management companies" achieve?
A: It teaches you to attach a real, validatable entity identifier (the LEI of a management company) to your synthetic fund records, so the entity side of your model is anchored to GLEIF-validatable identity even though the fund itself is fictional. This mirrors production, where fund records reference real ManCo LEIs. It also forces you to handle the join between an instrument-level fund and an entity-level LEI cleanly.

Q: Why does a security master need both an ISIN and an internal surrogate key?
A: The ISIN is a meaningful external identifier, but it can change or be retired over an instrument's life due to corporate actions, so it is unstable as a permanent primary key. An internal surrogate key is a system-generated value that never changes, preserving continuity and history even when the ISIN does change. Keeping both lets you join to external feeds on ISIN while maintaining stable internal lineage on the surrogate.

Q: What is a surrogate key in the context of a security master?
A: A surrogate key is an internally generated, business-meaningless identifier (such as a sequence or hash) assigned to each instrument and never reused or altered. Unlike the ISIN, it is under your control and immune to external lifecycle events. It becomes the true primary key for all internal facts and relationships, with the ISIN stored as a mutable attribute alongside it.

Q: What is an "identifier lifecycle event" for an ISIN?
A: An identifier lifecycle event is any change to an instrument's ISIN over time — issuance, reassignment, or retirement — typically driven by corporate actions such as mergers, redenominations, or share-class restructurings. The instrument may persist economically while its ISIN changes, or an ISIN may be retired and a new one issued. Your model must treat the ISIN as a time-bounded attribute, not an immortal key.

Q: Give an example of a corporate action that causes an ISIN to change.
A: A reverse stock split, a redomiciliation, or a merger can cause the issuer to retire one ISIN and have a new ISIN allocated for the resulting instrument, even though holders' economic position continues. For a fund, a share-class restructuring or currency redenomination can trigger a new ISIN. The continuity is economic and legal, but the external identifier breaks.

Q: How does a surrogate key preserve continuity when an ISIN changes?
A: Because the surrogate key never changes, all historical facts (NAVs, holdings, transactions) stay attached to the same surrogate even as the ISIN attribute is updated from old to new. You record the ISIN change as a time-bounded mapping (old ISIN valid until date X, new ISIN valid from date X) keyed on the surrogate. This keeps one continuous instrument record instead of splitting it into two unrelated rows.

Q: What goes wrong if you use the ISIN itself as the primary key and the ISIN later changes?
A: Using the ISIN as the primary key means a corporate-action ISIN change creates what looks like a brand-new instrument, silently splitting one economic instrument's history into two disconnected records. NAV series and holdings before and after the change no longer join, breaking time-series continuity and performance calculations. This is the classic "one instrument becomes two in the warehouse" failure the surrogate key prevents.

Q: How would you model the old-to-new ISIN relationship during a corporate action?
A: Store the ISIN as a slowly changing attribute on the instrument's surrogate, with validity dates, and keep a separate cross-reference table mapping old ISIN to new ISIN with the effective date and the corporate-action reason. Internal facts key on the surrogate, so they are unaffected; only the external-identifier lookup changes. This lets feeds arriving under either the old or new ISIN resolve to the same surrogate.

Q: A NAV time series shows a gap exactly on a corporate-action date — what is the first thing to check?
A: First check whether the instrument's ISIN changed on that date and whether your join is keyed on ISIN rather than a stable surrogate, because an ISIN change keyed on ISIN will sever the series. Confirm by looking up the corporate action and the old/new ISIN mapping for that instrument. If the surrogate is intact but the feed switched ISINs, the fix is to map the new ISIN to the existing surrogate rather than create a new record.

Q: Why is ANNA the right authority to consult for ISIN allocation and structure questions?
A: ANNA is the ISO-designated Registration Authority for ISO 6166 (ISIN) and coordinates the National Numbering Agencies that allocate ISINs, so it is the canonical source for structure, allocation rules, and check-digit definition. Vendor descriptions can be approximate, whereas ANNA's standards pages reflect the normative rules. When a flag or rule is uncertain, ANNA's standards documentation is the reference of record.

Q: Why is GLEIF the right authority to consult for LEI questions?
A: GLEIF governs the Global LEI System, accredits the issuing LOUs, and publishes the authoritative consolidated reference data, so it defines what an LEI means, how it is structured, and what current data is available. Third-party LEI lookup sites resell or mirror GLEIF data and can lag or differ. For structure (ISO 17442), validation, and golden-copy access, GLEIF is the primary source.

Q: What regulatory driver makes the LEI mandatory in EU financial reporting?
A: EU regimes such as MiFID II / MiFIR transaction reporting and EMIR require counterparties and issuers to be identified by LEI, under the principle "no LEI, no trade" for in-scope reporting. This regulatory mandate is why LEIs are pervasive in fund operations and why your entity master must store and validate them. The LEI thus functions as a compliance-grade identity key, not merely a convenience.

Q: Under DORA and similar regimes, why does identifier discipline matter for a fund platform?
A: Regulations like DORA emphasise operational resilience and accurate, traceable data, and consistent identifiers (LEI for entities, ISIN for instruments) are the backbone of traceable, auditable records. If identifiers drift or split, regulators cannot reconcile your reported exposures to reality. Strong identifier governance — surrogate keys, validation, lifecycle handling — is therefore part of regulatory data integrity, not just engineering hygiene.

Q: How do ISIN and LEI relate to an EMT (European MiFID Template) file?
A: An EMT carries instrument-level reference and cost data for distribution, keyed on the share-class ISIN, while the LEI identifies the manufacturer or management company entity behind it. So an EMT row joins the instrument identity (ISIN) to entity identity (LEI) plus regulatory cost fields. Correctly resolving both identifiers is essential so that the EMT's ISIN and any embedded LEI match your security master.

Q: In transfer agency, why are stable instrument identifiers critical?
A: Transfer agency processes subscriptions, redemptions, and switches against specific fund share classes, each identified by an ISIN, so an unstable or mismatched ISIN can route an investor's order to the wrong share class. A surrogate-backed master ensures that even across an ISIN change the register stays attached to the right instrument. Errors here directly affect investor holdings and NAV allocation.

Q: What is the relationship between a CSD and ISIN allocation in markets like Luxembourg?
A: In several markets the National Numbering Agency function sits with or alongside the Central Securities Depository, which both allocates ISINs and settles the instruments. This couples identity allocation with settlement infrastructure, so a Luxembourg `LU` ISIN is closely tied to the local CSD's records. For an architect it means the authoritative source for `LU` ISINs is that CSD/NNA, not a generic vendor feed.

Q: Why can two different securities never share the same valid ISIN?
A: ISINs are allocated to be unique per instrument by the NNA within its country namespace, and the global federation of NNAs partitions the space by country prefix so allocations cannot collide. Therefore a correctly issued ISIN maps to exactly one instrument at a point in time. The only ambiguity comes from lifecycle reuse over time, which is why time-bounding the ISIN attribute matters.

Q: Can an ISIN ever be reassigned to a different instrument over time?
A: In principle an ISIN should not be reused, but lifecycle events and historical practice mean you should not assume an ISIN is eternally bound to one economic instrument; treat it as valid for a time window. This is precisely why the surrogate key, not the ISIN, anchors your records. Always store effective-from/effective-to dates on the ISIN attribute so historical joins resolve to the correct instrument-as-of-then.

Q: What is the difference between an instrument identifier and an entity identifier?
A: An instrument identifier (ISIN, FIGI) names a tradable thing, while an entity identifier (LEI) names a legal organisation. They answer different questions: "what is being traded?" versus "who is the legal party?". Conflating them — for example trying to key a fund's NAV on a ManCo LEI — produces nonsense joins, so your model must keep the two levels distinct.

Q: Why is FIGI considered "open" and how does that contrast with some other identifiers?
A: FIGI is an open standard with freely available identifiers and no licensing fee for use, administered through OpenFIGI, in contrast to some national codes (like CUSIP) that carry licensing restrictions. Openness makes FIGI attractive as a venue-level key you can use and redistribute freely. For a free-tooling study stack this matters because you can ingest FIGI mappings without licensing friction.

Q: What is a CFI code and how does it complement an ISIN?
A: A CFI (Classification of Financial Instruments, ISO 10962) is a 6-character code that classifies an instrument's type and attributes — for example whether it is an equity, debt, or fund and its key features. It complements the ISIN, which identifies but does not describe the instrument. Carrying both means you know which instrument it is (ISIN) and what kind of instrument it is (CFI) in your reference data.

Q: Why is it risky to infer an instrument's type purely from its ISIN?
A: The ISIN encodes country and a national number plus a check digit, but it does not reliably encode instrument type, so parsing the ISIN to guess "this is a bond" or "this is a fund" is unsafe. The correct field for type is the CFI code or your own classification attribute. Inferring type from ISIN substrings is a brittle hack that breaks across NNAs.

Q: When loading the GLEIF golden copy to bronze, why keep it raw and unmodified?
A: Bronze is the landing layer whose job is to preserve the source exactly as received, so you can re-derive everything downstream and audit against the original. Keeping the golden copy raw means a later mapping bug can be fixed by reprocessing without re-downloading. It also gives you a defensible, regulator-friendly record of exactly what GLEIF published and when.

Q: What validation would you apply to LEIs after loading the golden copy?
A: At minimum confirm each LEI is 20 characters, that positions 5–6 are `00`, that all characters are in the allowed uppercase-alphanumeric set, and that the ISO 7064 MOD 97-10 check digits pass. You would also flag entities whose registration status is lapsed or retired. These checks turn a raw dump into a trustworthy entity reference table.

Q: What does it mean for an LEI's registration status to be "lapsed"?
A: A lapsed LEI is one whose registration has not been renewed by the annual deadline, so its reference data is no longer being maintained and may be stale. The LEI string remains structurally valid, but its Level 1 data is unverified. In risk and compliance contexts a lapsed status is a flag, because counterparties are generally expected to keep their LEI in good standing.

Q: Why must LEIs be renewed periodically?
A: GLEIF requires annual renewal so that an entity's Level 1 reference data (name, address, status) is re-validated against an authoritative registration source and kept current. Without renewal the data drifts as entities move, rename, or restructure. For your platform this means LEI validity has a time dimension: a once-valid record can become stale, so store and monitor the status field.

Q: How would you handle a fund record whose management company's LEI is not found in the golden copy?
A: First confirm you loaded the correct, current golden-copy version and applied any deltas, since a very recently issued LEI might only appear in a delta file. Then re-validate the LEI string's structure and check digits to rule out a transcription error. If it is genuinely absent, treat it as an unresolved entity reference and quarantine the record rather than silently joining to nothing.

Q: What is the practical reason to convert letters to digits before the Luhn step in ISIN validation?
A: Luhn arithmetic operates on decimal digits, but ISINs contain letters in the country prefix and often the NSIN, so the letters must first be expanded to their numeric values (`A`=10 … `Z`=35) to produce a pure digit string. Skipping this step or using ASCII codes gives a wrong checksum. The expansion is the bridge that lets a numeric checksum cover an alphanumeric identifier.

Q: For the ISIN `US0378331005`, what national identifier is embedded in the NSIN?
A: For a US ISIN the 9-character NSIN is the CUSIP, so `037833100` in `US0378331005` is the CUSIP for that security, with `5` as the ISIN check digit. This illustrates how ISINs wrap pre-existing national codes rather than inventing new ones. Recognising this lets you cross-reference a US ISIN to its CUSIP-keyed data sources.

Q: Why might the same instrument have different national identifiers embedded across countries' ISINs?
A: Because each NNA wraps its own national numbering scheme (CUSIP in the US, SEDOL in the UK, WKN-derived codes in Germany) into the NSIN, the embedded national code differs by jurisdiction even for economically similar instruments. The ISIN harmonises these into one format, but the inner NSIN still reflects local conventions. This is why you should map on the full ISIN, not on a presumed-universal inner code.

Q: What is the danger of storing an ISIN in a spreadsheet column without text formatting?
A: Spreadsheets may interpret an all-numeric NSIN or a code with leading zeros as a number, stripping leading zeros or applying scientific notation, which corrupts the ISIN. For example a leading-zero NSIN can lose its first character, breaking both length and check-digit validation. Always import and store ISINs as text, and validate length and check digit immediately after ingestion.

Q: How does the LEI's MOD 97-10 scheme relate to IBAN validation?
A: Both LEI and IBAN use the ISO/IEC 7064 MOD 97-10 check-character system, so the underlying arithmetic — convert letters to digits, interpret as a big integer, test against 97 — is essentially the same. Recognising this lets you reuse a single validation routine for both identifier types. The familiar IBAN "mod 97 equals 1" test is exactly the LEI test once the check digits are in place.

Q: If an LEI's positions 5–6 are not `00`, what should you conclude?
A: Under the current ISO 17442 format, characters 5 and 6 are reserved and must be `00`, so a non-`00` value means the string is not a validly formatted LEI — likely a typo, truncation, or a non-LEI value placed in the LEI field. It is a fast structural red flag you can apply before the full check-digit computation. Quarantine such rows rather than attempting to join them.

Q: Why is a single LEI insufficient to understand a fund's group-level exposure?
A: A single LEI gives you one entity's identity (Level 1) but not its place in an ownership hierarchy, so to aggregate exposure to a whole group you must traverse Level 2 direct- and ultimate-parent relationships. Without Level 2 you would treat each subsidiary as independent and undercount group concentration. Level 2 is what turns a flat list of entities into a navigable corporate tree.

Q: How would you use Level 2 data to answer "how much do we hold across one corporate group?"
A: You resolve each holding's issuer LEI, then walk the Level 2 ultimate-parent relationships to map every issuer to its top-of-house entity, and finally aggregate exposures by ultimate parent. This collapses many subsidiary issuers into one group total. The accuracy depends on Level 2 completeness, so you must account for entities that report parent exceptions.

Q: What is the risk of building a parent hierarchy and ignoring GLEIF reporting exceptions?
A: Reporting exceptions (entity declares no parent, or cites a legal/consent reason for not reporting one) are not the same as a missing edge, and treating them as missing can either truncate a real hierarchy or fabricate one. Ignoring them produces an inaccurate ownership tree and wrong group aggregations. You must branch on the exception reason and decide explicitly how to treat each case.

Q: Why is the GLEIF golden copy preferred over individual LOU files for loading to bronze?
A: The golden copy consolidates and de-duplicates records from all LOUs and guarantees completeness — every LEI ever published in that version is present — whereas individual LOU files are partial and may contain technical duplicates. Loading the golden copy gives you one authoritative, complete snapshot rather than stitching many partial feeds. That simplicity and completeness is exactly what a reference dataset needs.

Q: At what UTC times does GLEIF publish golden-copy files, and why does that matter for scheduling?
A: GLEIF publishes golden-copy file sets at 02:00, 10:00, and 18:00 UTC. Knowing the publish cadence lets you schedule your ingestion job just after a release rather than polling blindly, and to pick the release that aligns with your downstream cutoffs. Aligning your pipeline to these fixed times avoids fetching a half-updated dataset.

Q: How does using a stable surrogate key help with auditability in a regulated fund platform?
A: A surrogate key gives every internal fact a permanent, unchanging anchor, so an auditor can follow an instrument's full history even across ISIN changes without the trail breaking. External identifiers can be shown as time-bounded attributes mapped to that anchor. This continuous lineage is exactly what regulators expect when they ask you to reconstruct an instrument's record over time.

Q: When ingesting both ISIN and LEI, why validate before joining rather than after?
A: Validating identifiers (length, structure, check digits) at ingestion stops malformed values from creating phantom dimension rows or silently failing joins downstream. A single invalid ISIN that slips through can spawn an orphaned instrument or merge two real ones. Catching it at the boundary keeps the silver/gold layers clean and your joins trustworthy.

Q: What is the consequence of an ISIN-to-surrogate mapping that is not time-bounded?
A: Without effective-from/effective-to dates, a reused or changed ISIN will map ambiguously to more than one surrogate, and a point-in-time join cannot tell which instrument was meant on a given date. This produces incorrect historical NAVs and holdings. Time-bounding the mapping makes "ISIN X as of date D" resolve to exactly one surrogate.

Q: Why is the LEI described as a "join key of the industry" alongside the ISIN?
A: Together the LEI (entities) and ISIN (instruments) let disparate systems and firms reconcile the same parties and securities without bilateral mappings, which is the foundation of industry-wide data integration. They are the shared vocabulary that makes a feed from one firm joinable to another's. Where these keys are clean, integration is straightforward; where they drift, you get a data swamp.

Q: How would you detect that two warehouse rows are actually the same instrument split by an ISIN change?
A: You would look for two surrogate records (or two ISINs) sharing the same underlying attributes — issuer LEI, name, currency, and a corporate-action event linking an old ISIN to a new one on a specific date. A cross-reference of old-to-new ISINs from corporate-action data confirms the split. Once confirmed, you merge them under one surrogate and time-bound the two ISINs.

Q: Why is `persist`-ing the ISIN as a mutable attribute, rather than as the key, the safer schema choice?
A: Storing the ISIN as an attribute means a lifecycle change is a simple update or a new validity-dated row, leaving all foreign keys (which point to the surrogate) untouched. If the ISIN were the key, every referencing table would have to cascade an update on each corporate action, which is fragile and history-destroying. The attribute approach localises change to one column and preserves referential continuity.

Q: What minimum fields would you keep in a security master to satisfy this lesson's "done when" criteria?
A: At least an internal surrogate primary key, the current ISIN with validity dates, the CFI or instrument-type, the issuing/managing entity's LEI, and a cross-reference of historical ISINs to the surrogate. This lets you join external feeds on ISIN, aggregate by entity on LEI, and preserve continuity across ISIN changes. It directly supports explaining a corporate-action scenario and distinguishing the two identifier levels.

Q: Why is it incorrect to assume a fund's `LU` ISIN means the fund only trades in Luxembourg?
A: The `LU` prefix only records that the Luxembourg NNA allocated the ISIN, reflecting the fund's domicile, not its distribution or trading footprint, which can span many countries and venues. Cross-border-distributed UCITS routinely carry `LU` ISINs while being sold across the EU. Confusing allocation country with trading venue leads to wrong assumptions about market data and venue identifiers.

Q: How do MIC and FIGI together cover what an ISIN cannot at the venue level?
A: The MIC names the venue (which market a listing belongs to) and the FIGI provides a distinct identifier for each venue-level listing line, so together they pin down "this instrument on this venue", which a single ISIN cannot. ISIN stays at the instrument level. Your model therefore layers venue-level keys (ISIN + MIC, or FIGI) on top of the instrument-level ISIN.

Q: What would you check first if a freshly downloaded golden-copy CSV fails to parse?
A: First confirm you downloaded the intended format and a complete, non-truncated file, since the golden copy is large and an interrupted download is common; verify the file size and any provided checksum. Then check delimiter and encoding assumptions, because LEI reference data includes non-ASCII names and embedded delimiters that need proper quoting. A parser configured for naive comma-splitting will choke on quoted, accented entity names.

Q: Why is character encoding a concern when loading LEI Level 1 data?
A: Legal entity names in Level 1 data are global and include accented and non-Latin characters, so loading them with the wrong encoding (e.g. assuming Latin-1 instead of UTF-8) mangles names. Mangled names then fail downstream matching and reconciliation. Always load LEI reference data as UTF-8 and verify a few known multi-byte names after ingestion.

Q: How does an LEI improve counterparty identification compared with using a free-text name?
A: An LEI is a globally unique, validatable code, whereas a legal name varies in spelling, language, abbreviation, and over time, making name-based matching unreliable. Using the LEI as the join key eliminates the fuzzy-matching guesswork and ties directly to GLEIF-maintained reference data. This is why regulators mandate LEIs rather than names for counterparty identification.

Q: What is the relationship between an LEI and the entities required in EMT/EPT distribution data?
A: EMT and EPT templates carry identifiers for the parties involved in manufacturing and distributing a fund, where the LEI is the standard way to identify the management company or manufacturer entity. The instrument is identified by ISIN, the entity by LEI. Correctly populating and validating the LEI in these templates is part of producing compliant distribution data.

Q: Why might you keep historical Level 1 attributes rather than only the current ones?
A: An entity's name, address, or status changes over time, and reconstructing a past report or audit requires knowing the attributes as they stood then, not just now. Keeping versioned Level 1 history lets you answer "what was this entity called on the trade date?". Overwriting in place destroys the as-of view that regulated reporting often needs.

Q: How would you reconcile a vendor security master against the GLEIF golden copy?
A: You join the vendor's entity LEIs to the golden copy on the LEI, then compare Level 1 attributes (name, country, status) and flag mismatches, missing LEIs, and lapsed registrations. The golden copy acts as the authoritative reference, so discrepancies usually indicate vendor staleness. This reconciliation surfaces silent drift before it contaminates downstream reporting.

Q: Why is it dangerous to silently drop rows that fail ISIN check-digit validation?
A: Silently dropping rows hides data-quality problems and can omit real instruments whose ISINs were merely mis-transcribed upstream, leading to understated holdings or NAV coverage. The right behaviour is to quarantine and alert, so a human can investigate the source. Visibility of rejects is itself a governance requirement in a regulated platform.

Q: What is the role of effective dating when an instrument moves from one ISIN to another?
A: Effective dating records that the old ISIN was valid up to a cutover date and the new ISIN from that date forward, both pointing to the same surrogate. This lets any historical query resolve the correct ISIN as of any date and lets feeds under either ISIN find the right instrument. Without it, you cannot answer "which ISIN was active on date D?" deterministically.

Q: Why does the architect, not the analyst, own the identifier-lifecycle design?
A: Lifecycle handling — surrogate keys, time-bounded identifiers, corporate-action cross-references — is a foundational data-architecture decision that shapes every downstream model, so it must be designed centrally and consistently. If left to ad hoc analyst fixes, different teams will handle ISIN changes differently and history will fragment. Owning it centrally is what keeps the platform a coherent master rather than a swamp.

Q: How can you quickly sanity-check a 20-character string before treating it as an LEI?
A: Verify it is exactly 20 uppercase-alphanumeric characters, that positions 5–6 are `00`, and only then run the ISO 7064 MOD 97-10 check. The cheap structural checks reject most bad inputs before the more expensive checksum. This layered validation is fast at ingestion scale and gives a clear reason code for each rejection.

Q: Why is the ISIN check digit alone an insufficient guarantee of data quality at ingestion?
A: A valid check digit only proves internal consistency of the string, not that the ISIN is current, correctly mapped to your surrogate, or even issued, so you still need reference lookups and lifecycle handling on top. Relying on the check digit as your sole gate lets well-formed but nonexistent or stale ISINs through. It is a necessary first filter, not a sufficient one.

Q: In what way is FIGI's per-listing granularity useful for fund trade reconciliation?
A: When the same fund or instrument is traded on multiple venues, FIGI gives each venue listing its own identifier, so you can reconcile executions to the exact listing rather than collapsing them under one ISIN. This preserves venue-specific price and microstructure context. Where reconciliation must distinguish venues, FIGI (with MIC) is the appropriate key.

Q: How does the principle "docs/identifiers that drift are worse than none" apply to a security master?
A: If your ISIN-to-surrogate mappings or entity references silently go stale, downstream consumers trust wrong joins and make decisions on corrupted lineage, which is more harmful than having no mapping and knowing it. Drift creates false confidence. The mitigation is validation, reconciliation against authoritative sources like the golden copy, and explicit lifecycle handling rather than passive reuse.

Q: Why must you map a Faker-generated fund to a real LEI rather than a fake one?
A: Using a real, GLEIF-validatable LEI exercises the actual validation and golden-copy reconciliation path, proving your entity side works against authoritative data, whereas a fabricated LEI would pass structure checks but fail any lookup. It mirrors production where fund records reference real management-company LEIs. The synthetic fund stays fake, but the entity identity is anchored to reality.

Q: What distinguishes the "who is who" question from the "who owns whom" question operationally?
A: "Who is who" (Level 1) is answered by an entity's own reference attributes and is used for identification, naming, and KYC-style checks, while "who owns whom" (Level 2) is answered by relationship records and is used for hierarchy, consolidation, and group-risk aggregation. They are stored and queried differently. Knowing which question you are answering tells you which dataset and which joins to use.

Q: How would you design ingestion so that a late-arriving LEI (only in a delta) does not break a fund-to-LEI join?
A: Apply golden-copy deltas before resolving fund-to-LEI joins, and if a referenced LEI is still unresolved, hold the fund record in a pending/quarantine state rather than dropping or hard-failing it. Re-resolve on the next delta load. This way a newly issued ManCo LEI that has not yet reached your baseline is recovered automatically once the delta lands.

Q: Why is it important that the internal surrogate key be opaque and carry no business meaning?
A: An opaque surrogate has nothing to "go stale" — it never needs to change because it never encoded a fact that could change, so it remains a permanent anchor. If you embedded meaning (like an ISIN or a date) into the key, a lifecycle event would tempt you to change it, reintroducing the instability you were avoiding. Opacity is precisely what guarantees the key's stability.

Q: What single design decision most directly prevents "one instrument becoming two" after a corporate action?
A: Keying all internal facts on a stable internal surrogate while treating the ISIN as a time-bounded attribute is the decision that prevents the split, because an ISIN change updates an attribute instead of creating a new key. Combined with an old-to-new ISIN cross-reference, it keeps one continuous instrument record. This is the core "done when" the lesson asks you to be able to explain and demonstrate.

Q: When during a new fund share class's life is its ISIN typically allocated, and why does that timing matter?
A: An ISIN is allocated by the relevant National Numbering Agency at or before the instrument's launch, so it must exist before the share class can be distributed, traded, or reported. For your pipeline this means you may receive setup data referencing a brand-new `LU` ISIN that is genuinely valid even though it is absent from older vendor snapshots. The fix is to source ISINs from the authoritative NNA/CSD feed rather than relying solely on a possibly-stale vendor master.

Q: How does the LEI appear in SWIFT and ISO 20022 messaging, and why is that relevant to a fund data architect?
A: ISO 20022 (MX) messages and increasingly SWIFT flows carry the LEI as the standard structured identifier for parties such as the issuer, management company, or counterparty, while instruments are carried by ISIN. As an architect you map these inbound LEIs and ISINs to your surrogate-backed master so that a message resolves cleanly to known entities and instruments. A common gotcha is a message carrying a lapsed or newly issued LEI, which you must validate against the GLEIF golden copy rather than trust blindly.

Q: How many characters does a FIGI have and how is the 12-character layout structured?
A: A FIGI is exactly 12 characters: a fixed 3-character prefix that is always `BBG`, then 8 alphanumeric characters drawn from consonants and digits only, then a single numeric check digit in position 12. Crucially the 8 body characters never contain the English vowels `A`, `E`, `I`, `O`, or `U`, which is a deliberate rule to avoid the prefix `BBG` accidentally re-appearing and to prevent forming words. This vowel-exclusion is a quick structural test: a candidate FIGI containing a vowel in positions 4–11 is malformed.

Q: What algorithm computes the FIGI check digit, and how does it differ from the ISIN's?
A: The FIGI check digit uses the Modulus 10 Double-Add-Double technique, which is the same Luhn-family arithmetic the ISIN uses, applied after mapping each letter to a numeric value. The practical difference is the input alphabet and ordering, not the core math, so you cannot reuse an ISIN routine verbatim — the letter-to-number mapping and the fixed `BBG` prefix handling differ. Both still detect single-digit typos and most transpositions via the same mod-10 mechanism.

Q: Why does the FIGI body deliberately exclude vowels from its character set?
A: Excluding `A`, `E`, `I`, `O`, and `U` from positions 4–11 prevents the identifier from spelling real or offensive words and stops the reserved prefix `BBG` pattern from recurring inside the body. It keeps FIGIs as meaningless opaque tokens, which is the point of a system-assigned identifier. For validation it gives you a cheap rejection rule before any check-digit math runs.

Q: In Python, how would you guard an ISIN field on ingestion before running the check digit?
A: First assert the value is a `str` of length exactly 12 with `len(isin) == 12`, then test the prefix with `isin[:2].isalpha()` and that all characters match `re.fullmatch(r'[A-Z0-9]{12}', isin)` so case and stray whitespace are caught. Only after these structural gates do you run the Luhn computation, because feeding a mangled string into the checksum wastes work and produces a confusing failure. Reading the column as `dtype=str` in `pandas.read_csv` prevents the silent integer coercion that strips leading zeros.

Q: When reading a security-master CSV with `pandas`, which argument prevents ISINs from being corrupted, and how?
A: Pass `dtype=str` (or `dtype={'isin': str}`) to `pandas.read_csv` so the parser never infers a numeric type for an all-digit NSIN and therefore never strips leading zeros or applies scientific notation. Without it, a value like `0037833100`-style NSIN can lose its leading zero, breaking both the length and check-digit tests. You can additionally set `keep_default_na=False` so a code that looks like `NA` is not turned into a missing value.

Q: A vendor sends ISINs in an Excel `.xlsx` and several now read as floats like `1.23457E+11` — what happened and how do you recover?
A: Excel coerced the all-numeric NSIN to a number and displayed it in scientific notation, irreversibly losing precision and any leading zeros once the cell was stored as a numeric type. The recovery is not to reformat the displayed float but to re-request the data as text (or CSV with the column quoted), because the original digits cannot be reconstructed from the rounded float. The lesson is to insist on text-typed identifier columns at the source rather than fixing them downstream.

Q: How would you batch-validate a million ISINs efficiently rather than one Python call per row?
A: Vectorise the work: filter by length and regex in a single columnar pass, then apply the Luhn computation as a numpy/pandas vectorised operation or a compiled UDF rather than a per-row Python loop. Pushing the structural checks into the query engine (a SQL `LENGTH` and `REGEXP` predicate) eliminates most candidates before the expensive checksum. The gotcha is that a naive `df.apply(validate, axis=1)` is orders of magnitude slower than a set-based approach at that scale.

Q: Write the formula, in words, for generating an LEI's two check digits from the first 18 characters.
A: Append `00` to the 18-character base, convert every letter to digits using `A`=10 … `Z`=35, interpret the whole string as one large integer `n`, and the check digits are `98 - (n mod 97)`, zero-padded to two digits. This is the ISO 7064 MOD 97-10 generation step, the mirror of the validation test where the full 20-character number must satisfy `n mod 97 == 1`. A worked gotcha: if `98 - (n mod 97)` yields a single digit you must left-pad with `0` so the result is always two characters.

Q: Why can you reuse one Python function to validate both LEIs and IBANs but not ISINs?
A: LEIs and IBANs both use the ISO 7064 MOD 97-10 scheme — convert letters to digits and test `mod 97 == 1` — so the same big-integer routine validates both once you handle IBAN's leading rearrangement. ISINs instead use a Luhn mod-10 single check digit, which is a different algorithm entirely, so it needs its own function. Recognising the shared MOD 97-10 family avoids duplicating validation logic for the two entity-style codes.

Q: A counterparty LEI passes the `00`-in-positions-5-6 structural check but fails `mod 97 == 1` — what is the most likely cause?
A: A structurally well-formed LEI that fails the MOD 97-10 test almost always indicates a single-character transcription error or a transposed pair within the 18-character body, since the checksum is designed precisely to catch those. The first thing to check is whether an `O`/`0` or `I`/`1` confusion occurred during manual entry or OCR. Re-pull the LEI from the GLEIF source and compare character by character before assuming the entity is unknown.

Q: How would you fetch a single LEI's Level 1 record from the GLEIF REST API, and what is the base path?
A: Issue an HTTP GET to `https://api.gleif.org/api/v1/lei-records/{lei}` (or filter the collection with `?filter[lei]=...`), which returns the entity's Level 1 reference data as JSON-API formatted JSON. The API is free and requires no key for basic lookups, making it convenient for spot-checks even when you batch-load the golden copy for bulk work. The gotcha is rate limiting: for validating a whole portfolio you should reconcile against the downloaded golden copy, not hammer the live API row by row.

Q: When would you prefer the GLEIF live API over the downloaded golden copy, and vice versa?
A: Use the live `api.gleif.org` endpoint for low-volume, on-demand lookups where freshness matters (a single just-issued LEI you cannot find locally), and use the golden copy for bulk reconciliation of an entire entity master where you need a complete, point-in-time snapshot. The API is convenient but rate-limited and per-record; the golden copy is one large authoritative file ideal for set-based joins. Mixing them sensibly — bulk golden copy plus API top-up for gaps — gives both completeness and freshness.

Q: What are the three Level 2 relationship outcomes a single entity's parent reporting can take?
A: An entity can report an actual parent relationship (a relationship record linking it to that parent's LEI), report a reporting exception with a reason code explaining why no parent is given, or — for the ultimate level — itself be the top of the tree. So for each of the direct and ultimate parent slots you may find a relationship record, an exception, or self-as-ultimate. Your hierarchy loader must branch on all three rather than assuming every entity has a populated parent edge.

Q: Name three GLEIF Level 2 reporting-exception reason codes and what each means.
A: `NO_KNOWN_PERSON` means there is no known controlling person, such as diversified shareholding; `NATURAL_PERSONS` means the entity is controlled only by individuals with no intermediate legal entity to name; and `NON_CONSOLIDATING` means the controlling entity does not prepare consolidated financial statements that would require disclosure. Other codes like `LEGAL_OBSTACLES` and `CONSENT_NOT_OBTAINED` cover legal or consent barriers. These are explicit data, not missing values, so you must store and branch on the code rather than treating the absent edge as a gap.

Q: How does the exception code `NON_CONSOLIDATING` change how you build a group hierarchy?
A: `NON_CONSOLIDATING` tells you the entity's controller exists but does not produce consolidated accounts, so GLEIF holds no parent LEI to link — the absence is legitimate and final, not pending data. You should record the entity as a hierarchy root for aggregation purposes while flagging that a controller exists outside the LEI consolidation scope. Treating it as a missing edge to be back-filled later would be wrong, because no parent LEI is expected to appear.

Q: What two relationship types underlie GLEIF Level 2 direct- and ultimate-parent links conceptually?
A: They correspond to direct-consolidation and ultimate-consolidation relationships: one record links a child LEI to its immediate accounting-consolidating parent, the other to the entity at the top of the consolidation chain. Both are accounting-consolidation based, which is why the test is "who consolidates this entity's financials", not mere shareholding percentages. Your hierarchy logic should follow ultimate-consolidation links for top-of-house roll-ups and direct links for one-level-up questions.

Q: Why are GLEIF parent relationships based on accounting consolidation rather than share ownership percentages?
A: GLEIF Level 2 uses the accounting definition of a consolidating parent — the entity that includes the child in its consolidated financial statements — because that is an objective, regulator-recognised boundary, whereas raw ownership percentages can be ambiguous across jurisdictions. This means a 30%-owned associate may not appear as a parent if it is not consolidated, which can surprise someone expecting shareholding-based trees. For exposure aggregation you must understand you are rolling up the consolidation perimeter, not every economic interest.

Q: How would you load the GLEIF Level 2 relationship records into a queryable hierarchy in a lakehouse?
A: Land the raw relationship-record file in bronze, then in silver model each record as an edge `(child_lei, parent_lei, relationship_type, status)` plus a separate exceptions table keyed on `lei` with its reason code. A recursive CTE or graph traversal over the edges then resolves ultimate parents, with the exceptions table telling you where to stop. The gotcha is that edges and exceptions live in separate GLEIF files, so a complete picture requires joining both, not just the relationship records.

Q: Writing a recursive CTE to find ultimate parents from Level 2 edges, what guards against infinite loops?
A: Guard against cycles by tracking the visited-LEI path and stopping when a node repeats, and cap recursion depth, because erroneous or circular consolidation data can otherwise loop forever. In practice you terminate when an entity is an ultimate parent (no outgoing edge) or carries an ultimate-parent exception. Without a cycle guard a single bad self-referential or mutual edge will spin the query indefinitely or exhaust the engine's recursion limit.

Q: How would you detect that a fund's ManCo LEI has lapsed before you trust its Level 1 attributes?
A: Join the LEI to the golden copy and inspect the registration-status field, treating a status such as `LAPSED` as a flag that the name and address may be stale even though the 20-character string still validates. You would surface lapsed ManCo LEIs in a data-quality report rather than silently using their possibly-outdated attributes. The mechanism is that lapse means the annual renewal was missed, so GLEIF is no longer re-validating that entity's reference data.

Q: A nightly GLEIF golden-copy ingestion suddenly loads far fewer rows than yesterday — what do you check first?
A: First confirm whether you accidentally loaded a delta file instead of the full golden copy, since deltas contain only changed records and are legitimately tiny compared with the full set. Then verify the download completed (file size and any published checksum) and that you pointed at the correct release time rather than a half-published one. A delta-versus-full mix-up is the classic cause of a sudden, dramatic row-count drop.

Q: Why must you record which baseline version a GLEIF delta applies to before merging it?
A: A delta only contains records changed since a specific prior golden-copy version, so applying it on top of the wrong baseline silently produces gaps or applies stale changes out of order. Tracking the baseline version (and applying deltas strictly in sequence) guarantees your bronze entity table is a faithful reconstruction of the latest full set. The failure mode is subtle: nothing errors, but your registry quietly diverges from GLEIF's authoritative state.

Q: How would you reconcile yesterday's full golden copy plus today's delta against today's full golden copy?
A: Apply the delta to yesterday's snapshot to produce a derived "today" set, then row-compare that against the independently downloaded today full copy on the LEI key, expecting zero differences. Any discrepancy points to a missed delta, an out-of-order application, or a baseline mismatch. This periodic full-versus-derived reconciliation is the control that proves your incremental delta pipeline has not silently drifted.

Q: Two fund share classes differ only by currency hedging — should they share one ISIN or have separate ISINs, and why?
A: They must have separate ISINs because each distinct, separately subscribable share class is a different fungible instrument with its own NAV, currency exposure, and economics, and the NNA allocates one ISIN per share class. A hedged and an unhedged class of the same fund are not interchangeable, so collapsing them under one ISIN would merge two different NAV series. This is why a single fund umbrella legitimately produces many `LU` ISINs.

Q: In your security master, what is the cardinality between a fund umbrella, sub-funds, share classes, and ISINs?
A: A fund umbrella contains many sub-funds (compartments), each sub-fund has one or more share classes, and each distinct share class maps to exactly one current ISIN, giving a one-ISIN-per-share-class relationship at a point in time. So one umbrella entity fans out to many ISINs through the sub-fund and share-class layers. Modeling these levels explicitly prevents the common error of attaching a NAV to the umbrella when it belongs to a specific share class.

Q: A NAV feed arrives keyed only on fund name, not ISIN — how do you safely resolve it to the right share class?
A: Do not fuzzy-match on the free-text fund name alone, because names collide across share classes and currencies; instead require at least the share-class-distinguishing attributes (currency, hedged/unhedged, dist/acc) to resolve a unique ISIN, and quarantine anything ambiguous. Build a deterministic mapping from the source's class labels to ISINs maintained in your master. The gotcha is that "the EUR fund" can map to several ISINs, so name-only resolution will silently misallocate NAVs.

Q: When linking your Faker funds to real ManCo LEIs, why might you pick LEIs of actual Luxembourg management companies specifically?
A: Choosing real `LU`-domiciled ManCo LEIs makes the synthetic platform realistic for the regulated Luxembourg fund context, so the entity side exercises genuine golden-copy lookups, jurisdiction fields, and renewal-status handling exactly as production would. The funds stay fictional while the entity identity is fully GLEIF-validatable. It also lets you practise Level 2 traversal if those ManCos sit under real group ultimate parents.

Q: How would you store the ISIN-to-surrogate mapping so a feed under either an old or new ISIN resolves correctly as of a date?
A: Keep a cross-reference table of `(isin, surrogate_key, effective_from, effective_to, change_reason)` where each ISIN row is time-bounded, so a lookup of "ISIN X as of date D" filters on `effective_from <= D < effective_to` and returns exactly one surrogate. Both the old and new ISIN rows point to the same surrogate across the cutover. Without the validity window a reused or changed ISIN would map ambiguously to more than one surrogate.

Q: What SQL predicate resolves "which surrogate did ISIN X map to on date D?" given a time-bounded cross-reference?
A: Use `WHERE isin = :x AND :d >= effective_from AND (:d < effective_to OR effective_to IS NULL)`, treating a null `effective_to` as the still-current row. This half-open interval convention (`effective_from` inclusive, `effective_to` exclusive) prevents an as-of date on a cutover boundary from matching two rows. The gotcha is mixing inclusive/exclusive bounds, which produces either gaps or overlaps exactly on change dates.

Q: How does treating `effective_to` as a half-open exclusive bound prevent double-counting on a corporate-action date?
A: With a half-open convention the old ISIN row is valid `[from, to)` and the new row is valid `[to, next)`, so the cutover date D belongs to exactly one row — the new one — and never to both. If both bounds were inclusive, a query as of D would match the old and the new row and return two surrogates. Consistent half-open intervals are the standard way to make point-in-time joins deterministic across change boundaries.

Q: A merger combines two funds into one surviving share class — how do you model the ISIN and surrogate outcome?
A: The surviving share class keeps (or is assigned) its own surrogate, the merged-away fund's ISIN is closed with an `effective_to` on the merger date and cross-referenced to the survivor's surrogate with a `MERGER` reason, and historical facts of the absorbed fund either remain on their own surrogate or are linked via the cross-reference. The economic continuity for surviving holders maps to the survivor surrogate going forward. The gotcha is performance history: you must decide and document whether the absorbed fund's track record carries forward.

Q: A fund redenominates its base currency and gets a new ISIN — does its surrogate key change?
A: No — the surrogate key is opaque and permanent, so a currency redenomination updates the time-bounded ISIN attribute and adds an old-to-new cross-reference row with a `REDENOMINATION` reason, while every NAV and holding stays attached to the unchanged surrogate. The instrument is economically continuous for holders, so its internal anchor must be too. Changing the surrogate would reintroduce exactly the history-splitting bug the surrogate exists to prevent.

Q: How do you distinguish, in data, an ISIN that was retired-and-replaced from one that was retired with no successor?
A: A replaced ISIN has a cross-reference row mapping it to a successor ISIN (or the same surrogate's new ISIN) with a change reason, whereas a terminated instrument's ISIN is simply closed with an `effective_to` and no successor mapping, often with a `LIQUIDATION` or `TERMINATION` reason. The presence or absence of a successor edge is the discriminator. Conflating the two would either invent a phantom successor or lose the fact that an instrument ceased to exist.

Q: Why is it unsafe to derive a fund's domicile solely from the `LU` prefix of its ISIN?
A: The prefix records which National Numbering Agency allocated the ISIN, which usually but not always tracks domicile, and it tells you nothing about the fund's distribution countries or trading venues. A more authoritative domicile source is the fund's own legal registration and the entity reference data, not a two-letter ISIN prefix. Treating the prefix as the definitive domicile field is a brittle inference that can mislead regulatory and tax classification.

Q: How would you validate at ingestion that a claimed ISIN country prefix is a real ISO 3166 alpha-2 code?
A: Check the first two characters against a maintained set of valid ISO 3166-1 alpha-2 codes (plus the few special allocations like `XS` for international ISINs), rejecting anything outside it before the check-digit step. A prefix like `ZZ` or a lowercased `lu` is an immediate structural failure. The gotcha is forgetting non-country allocations such as `XS`, which are valid ISIN prefixes despite not being a country.

Q: What does the ISIN prefix `XS` signify and why must your validation allow it?
A: `XS` is allocated for internationally cleared securities (issued through international CSDs rather than a single national numbering agency), so it is a legitimate ISIN prefix that is not an ISO 3166 country code. If your country-code whitelist only contains real countries, valid `XS` Eurobond ISINs will be wrongly rejected. You must include the recognised special prefixes alongside the country list when validating.

Q: Why might a `pandas` join between a fund table and the GLEIF golden copy silently produce zero matches despite valid LEIs?
A: The usual cause is a key mismatch from invisible differences — trailing whitespace, lowercase versus uppercase, or a non-breaking space introduced by a copy-paste or Excel round-trip — so the strings look equal to the eye but differ as bytes. Normalise both sides with `.str.strip().str.upper()` before joining and verify a known LEI matches. An encoding mismatch on entity names can also cause apparent non-matches when you join on name instead of LEI.

Q: How would you normalise an LEI column before reconciliation to avoid spurious mismatches?
A: Apply `.str.strip()` to drop surrounding whitespace, `.str.upper()` to enforce the uppercase-only alphabet, and reject any value not matching `^[A-Z0-9]{20}$`, so only canonical LEIs reach the join. This guards against the common copy-paste artefacts that make identical-looking codes fail equality. The same normalisation belongs on the golden-copy side so both join keys share one canonical form.

Q: Why is UTF-8 specifically the safe encoding for GLEIF Level 1 entity names, and what breaks otherwise?
A: GLEIF entity names are global and contain accented Latin, Cyrillic, CJK, and other multi-byte characters that UTF-8 represents losslessly, whereas decoding the file as Latin-1 or Windows-1252 mangles those bytes into mojibake. Mangled names then fail any name-based matching and corrupt regulatory output. Always specify `encoding='utf-8'` when reading the file and spot-check a known non-Latin name after load.

Q: After loading the golden copy, how would you quickly confirm encoding is correct without reading every row?
A: Pick a few entities you know contain multi-byte characters (an accented French ManCo name or a CJK entity) and assert their names render correctly, plus scan for the replacement character `�` or telltale mojibake sequences like `Ã©` that indicate a Latin-1/UTF-8 mismatch. The presence of `�` anywhere is a strong signal of a decoding error. This targeted check is far cheaper than validating every name and catches the common encoding fault.

Q: What is the BIC-to-LEI relationship and why might a fund platform need a mapping between them?
A: A BIC (ISO 9362 Business Identifier Code) identifies a financial institution for SWIFT messaging, while the LEI identifies the same institution as a legal entity, and GLEIF publishes a free BIC-to-LEI mapping so you can pivot between the messaging world and the legal-entity world. A fund platform needs this when a SWIFT MT/MX message identifies a party by BIC but your entity master is keyed on LEI. The gotcha is that the relationship is not always one-to-one — one entity can hold multiple BICs — so the mapping must be many-to-one into the LEI.

Q: For the LEI `529900T8BM49AURSDO55`, what do positions 5–6 tell you and is it structurally plausible?
A: Positions 5–6 are `00`, which is the mandatory reserved value in the current ISO 17442 format, so the string passes the cheap structural sanity check before any checksum. The first four characters `5299` are the LOU prefix identifying the issuing organisation. You would still run the MOD 97-10 test on all 20 characters to confirm the trailing check digits are valid, since structure alone does not prove the checksum.

Q: How does a CFI code let you answer "is this ISIN a fund?" more reliably than parsing the ISIN?
A: The ISO 10962 CFI code's first character classifies the instrument category — `C` for collective investment vehicles, for example — so reading the CFI tells you it is a fund directly, whereas the ISIN encodes only country, national number, and a check digit. Parsing ISIN substrings to guess type is unsafe because no type information is reliably embedded there. Carry the CFI (or your own classification) alongside the ISIN so type questions have an authoritative field.

Q: What is the first character of a CFI code for a collective investment vehicle, and why store CFI next to ISIN?
A: For a fund or collective investment vehicle the CFI's first character is `C` under ISO 10962, which immediately classifies the instrument category that the ISIN itself does not encode. Storing the CFI beside the ISIN means your reference data answers both "which instrument" (ISIN) and "what kind" (CFI) without brittle string parsing. The gotcha is relying on ISIN prefixes or NSIN patterns to infer fund-ness, which breaks across numbering agencies.

Q: An EMT row's ISIN does not match any ISIN in your security master — what is your first diagnostic step?
A: First normalise and re-validate the EMT ISIN (length, character set, check digit) to rule out a transcription or spreadsheet-coercion error in the template, then check whether it is a brand-new share-class ISIN absent from a stale master snapshot. If it validates and is genuinely new, refresh from the authoritative NNA/CSD feed and quarantine the EMT row meanwhile rather than dropping it. The common cause is a newly launched class whose ISIN has not yet propagated to your master.

Q: How would you handle an inbound ISO 20022 message whose party LEI is structurally valid but absent from your golden copy?
A: Validate the LEI's structure and MOD 97-10 check digit first, then attempt a live `api.gleif.org` lookup in case it is newly issued and only present in a delta not yet loaded; if it resolves there, ingest it and let the next golden-copy load confirm it. If it is genuinely unknown everywhere, quarantine the message as an unresolved-entity exception rather than auto-creating a phantom entity. Auto-creating entities from unvalidated message data is how a master becomes a swamp.

Q: Why should rejected ISINs and LEIs go to a quarantine table with reason codes rather than being dropped?
A: Quarantining with an explicit reason code (`BAD_CHECK_DIGIT`, `WRONG_LENGTH`, `LEI_NOT_IN_GOLDEN_COPY`) preserves the rejected row for human investigation, makes data-quality measurable, and satisfies the regulatory expectation that you can show what was excluded and why. Silently dropping rows hides real instruments mis-transcribed upstream and understates holdings or NAV coverage. Visibility of rejects is itself a governance control, not just a debugging convenience.

Q: What metrics would you put on a dashboard to monitor ISIN/LEI identifier health over time?
A: Track the ingestion check-digit pass rate for ISINs and LEIs, the count of LEIs in lapsed or other non-issued status, the number of unresolved fund-to-LEI joins, the count of quarantined rows by reason code, and the age of the loaded golden-copy version. Trends in these expose drift — a rising lapsed-LEI count or a stale golden copy — before it corrupts reporting. The point is to make identifier integrity a measured, alertable property rather than an assumption.

Q: How would you write a single SQL data-quality test that flags any ISIN-to-surrogate mapping with overlapping validity windows?
A: Self-join the cross-reference table on equal `isin` where the rows differ and their `[effective_from, effective_to)` intervals intersect — `a.effective_from < b.effective_to AND b.effective_from < a.effective_to` — and return any matched pair as a violation. Any row returned means one ISIN resolves to two surrogates on some date, which breaks deterministic point-in-time joins. Running this as a `dbt` test or a scheduled assertion catches accidental overlapping windows before they corrupt historical NAV lookups.

Q: If GLEIF lists a fund's ManCo as the ultimate parent of several other entities, how does that help you size group exposure, and what is the caveat?
A: Following the ultimate-consolidation Level 2 edges from each held issuer up to that ManCo lets you sum exposures across every subsidiary into one group total, turning many subsidiary LEIs into a single top-of-house figure. The caveat is completeness: entities that file a reporting exception (such as `NO_KNOWN_PERSON` or `NON_CONSOLIDATING`) contribute no edge, so a naive roll-up silently undercounts unless you account for those exceptions. You should reconcile the share of exposure that is hierarchy-resolved versus exception-blocked so the group total's coverage is explicit.
