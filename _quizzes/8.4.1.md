# Phase 8 · 8.4.1 Domain-driven design — 150 self-test questions

Q: What is "ubiquitous language" in domain-driven design?
A: Ubiquitous language is a rigorous, shared vocabulary used by both domain experts and engineers within a single bounded context, where each term has one precise, agreed meaning. The goal is to eliminate translation between business and code, so the language in conversations, documentation, and the model are the same. The classic gotcha is that the language is only ubiquitous *inside* one context, not across the whole organisation.

Q: Why are "NAV" in fund accounting and "NAV" in client reporting legitimately different objects rather than the same thing named twice?
A: In fund accounting, NAV is the authoritative struck value computed from the books of the fund — a number with a valuation point, rounding rules, and audit trail. In client reporting, NAV is a presented figure consumers see on a statement, possibly indicative, lagged, or per-share rounded differently. Because the invariants, lifecycle, and precision differ, DDD treats them as two models living in two contexts that share a name but not a definition.

Q: What does it mean that ubiquitous language has a "context-local" scope?
A: It means a term's definition is bound to one bounded context and need not — and often must not — match the same term elsewhere. Trying to force a single global definition of "policy", "account", or "NAV" across the whole enterprise produces a watered-down model that fits nowhere. Each context keeps its own precise meaning, and translation happens explicitly at context boundaries.

Q: A domain expert and a developer use the word "subscription" to mean different things in a meeting. What DDD problem does this signal?
A: It signals a breakdown of ubiquitous language, and frequently a hidden bounded-context boundary running through the conversation. When one stakeholder means an investor's buy order into a fund and another means a recurring billing arrangement, the two are different models. The fix is to surface the ambiguity, name each meaning precisely, and assign each to its own context rather than papering over it.

Q: How does ubiquitous language relate to naming in the actual codebase and database?
A: The language should appear verbatim in class names, table names, column names, and API fields, so the model is traceable back to the business term. When code says `settlement_date` but experts say "value date", the mismatch is a defect waiting to cause miscommunication. Keeping names aligned is what lets a new engineer read the schema and the domain conversation as one vocabulary.

Q: Define a bounded context.
A: A bounded context is a boundary within which a particular model and its ubiquitous language are consistent and valid. Inside it, every term has one meaning and the model holds together; outside it, the same terms may mean something else. It is the fundamental strategic-DDD unit for cutting where one model ends and another begins.

Q: Is a bounded context the same as a microservice or a deployment unit?
A: No — that is the most common misconception. A bounded context is a boundary of language and model consistency, a *logical* concept, while a microservice is a physical deployment choice. One bounded context may be implemented as several services, or several small contexts may share one deployment; conflating the two leads to splitting models for infrastructure reasons rather than domain reasons.

Q: How can you spot a bounded context boundary purely from listening to a conversation?
A: Listen for the same word carrying different meanings, or different words describing the same thing, as the discussion moves between groups of people. When the meaning of "trade", "position", or "NAV" shifts as you cross from custody people to fund-accounting people, you have likely crossed a context boundary. Recurring need for translation or qualification ("the accounting NAV, not the reporting one") is the tell.

Q: What is the relationship between a bounded context and ubiquitous language?
A: They are two sides of the same idea: a bounded context is precisely the scope within which one ubiquitous language is internally consistent. The language defines the context, and the context bounds the language. You cannot have a sound ubiquitous language without delimiting where it applies, and the boundary of where it stops applying is the context edge.

Q: Why do bounded contexts that follow domain lines survive reorganisations better than ones drawn on the org chart?
A: Domain lines reflect stable differences in language, invariants, and business meaning, which change slowly; org charts change with every reorganisation. If you cut contexts along reporting lines, a reshuffle redraws your data landscape and integrations rot. Cutting along domain meaning means a team move does not invalidate the model, because the boundary was never about who reports to whom.

Q: What is context mapping?
A: Context mapping is the practice of identifying all bounded contexts in a system and documenting the relationships and integration patterns between them. It produces a context map: a diagram where each edge is labelled with a specific relationship pattern that says how the two contexts collaborate and how models flow across the boundary. It is the strategic tool for seeing the whole landscape and its dependencies.

Q: Describe the Partnership context-mapping relationship.
A: Partnership is a symmetric relationship where two teams succeed or fail together and coordinate closely, with no single upstream or downstream party. They jointly plan changes, integrate continuously, and absorb each other's needs because the cost of mis-coordination is high. It implies tight communication and shared roadmaps, suitable only when the teams genuinely have aligned goals.

Q: Describe the Customer–Supplier relationship in context mapping.
A: Customer–Supplier is an upstream/downstream relationship where the upstream (supplier) provides something the downstream (customer) depends on, and the downstream's needs are given real weight in the upstream's planning. The supplier accommodates the customer's requirements through negotiation, unlike a pure conformist setup. In a fund administrator, custody supplying positions to fund accounting is a natural Customer–Supplier edge.

Q: What is the Conformist relationship and when is it appropriate?
A: Conformist is a downstream context that adopts the upstream's model wholesale, with no translation layer and no influence over the upstream. It is appropriate when the upstream is powerful, stable, or external enough that negotiating or translating is not worth the cost, and the upstream model is good enough to live with. The downside is that you inherit every quirk and breaking change from upstream.

Q: What is an Anticorruption Layer (ACL) and what problem does it solve?
A: An ACL is a translation layer the downstream context builds to convert the upstream's model into its own terms, so the upstream's concepts never leak into the downstream model. It prevents *model corruption*: stops a foreign or legacy model from polluting your clean ubiquitous language. A typical use is wrapping a legacy transfer-agency feed so its idiosyncratic codes are mapped into your domain before any of your logic touches them.

Q: What is the "Published Language" relationship pattern?
A: Published Language is a well-documented, shared interchange format that multiple contexts agree to communicate in, independent of any one party's internal model. It decouples integrators from each producer's private model and gives a stable contract for exchange. In EU fund data, EMT and EPT are textbook published languages: standardised templates that manufacturers and distributors exchange without exposing internal schemas.

Q: What does the "Separate Ways" relationship signify?
A: Separate Ways means two contexts deliberately do not integrate at all, because the cost of integration outweighs the benefit. Each solves its overlapping need independently rather than building and maintaining a connection. It is a legitimate, sometimes wise decision — recognising that not every duplication must be eliminated through integration.

Q: In context mapping, what do "upstream" and "downstream" mean?
A: Upstream is the context whose changes flow toward and affect the other; downstream is the context that consumes and must react to those changes. The upstream sets the pace and the model; the downstream lives with the consequences. Identifying the direction of influence on each edge is essential because it tells you who absorbs the cost of change.

Q: Open Host Service and Published Language are which kind of role on a context-map edge?
A: They are *upstream* roles, describing how a supplying context exposes itself to consumers. An Open Host Service publishes a stable, well-defined protocol for many downstream consumers, and a Published Language is the shared format they speak. In Context Mapper DSL these appear as the `OHS` and `PL` abbreviations on the upstream side of a relationship.

Q: Conformist and Anticorruption Layer are which kind of role on a context-map edge?
A: They are *downstream* roles, describing how a consuming context copes with an upstream it depends on. Conformist (`CF`) accepts the upstream model as-is; Anticorruption Layer (`ACL`) translates it into the downstream's own model. The choice between them is essentially "absorb the foreign model" versus "defend against it".

Q: What are the three subdomain types in strategic DDD?
A: Core, supporting, and generic. Core subdomains are where you create competitive advantage and the problem is hard; supporting subdomains are necessary but not differentiating; generic subdomains are solved problems common to many businesses. Classifying each part of the domain into one of these three drives the build-versus-buy and where-to-invest decisions.

Q: What is a core subdomain?
A: A core subdomain is the part of the business that provides competitive advantage and where the hard, differentiating problems live. It deserves your best people, deepest modelling, and custom-built software, because it is the reason the organisation wins. Getting the core right with rich DDD modelling is where strategic design pays off most.

Q: What is a supporting subdomain?
A: A supporting subdomain is necessary for the business to function but does not differentiate it — it supports the core without being a competitive edge. The problem is usually simpler and more stable than the core. You typically build it with less sophistication than the core, or assemble it cheaply, reserving deep modelling effort for the core.

Q: What is a generic subdomain?
A: A generic subdomain is a solved, commodity problem common to many businesses — authentication, payroll, accounting ledgers — where off-the-shelf solutions exist. There is no advantage in building it yourself, so the investment logic says buy or adopt an existing product. Spending custom engineering on a generic subdomain is wasted differentiation budget.

Q: What is the build-versus-buy logic that follows from subdomain classification?
A: Build the core, buy the generic, and pragmatically assemble or lightly build the supporting. The reasoning is that custom engineering only pays off where it creates advantage (the core), while generic problems are cheaper and safer to purchase. Misclassifying — building a generic subdomain or buying your core — is a classic strategic mistake that wastes money or surrenders advantage.

Q: For a fund administrator, what is a defensible candidate for the core subdomain?
A: NAV calculation and the fund-accounting/oversight engine are strong core candidates, because accurate, timely, defensible valuation is the differentiating, hard, regulated capability a fund administrator sells. Getting NAV right under complex instruments, fees, and corporate actions is where expertise and custom modelling create advantage. Generic billing or document storage, by contrast, would sit in supporting or generic.

Q: Why might general-ledger accounting be a generic subdomain even in a fund administrator?
A: Double-entry bookkeeping is a solved, standardised problem with mature off-the-shelf engines, so it offers no competitive differentiation by being custom-built. The fund-specific valuation logic on top may be core, but the underlying ledger mechanics are commodity. The investment logic therefore says buy the ledger and concentrate custom effort on the fund-specific core.

Q: What is EventStorming?
A: EventStorming is a collaborative workshop technique, invented by Alberto Brandolini, for exploring a business domain by mapping it as a series of domain events on sticky notes along a timeline. It brings domain experts and technical people to one wall to build shared understanding fast, surface disagreements, and discover boundaries. The big-picture variant is the broad, exploratory format used for discovery.

Q: In big-picture EventStorming, what do the orange sticky notes represent?
A: Orange stickies represent domain events — things that have happened in the domain, written in past tense, such as "order accepted", "trade settled", or "NAV struck". They are the backbone of the workshop, arranged left to right along a timeline. Starting with events keeps the focus on what actually occurs in the business rather than on systems or UI.

Q: Why are domain events written in the past tense?
A: Past tense forces participants to describe facts that have already occurred in the business — "payment received", not "receive payment" — which keeps the language behavioural and unambiguous. It avoids drifting into commands, UI, or wishful design and anchors the conversation in real outcomes. The consistent grammar also makes the timeline easy to read across many stickies.

Q: What is a "hotspot" in EventStorming and how is it marked?
A: A hotspot is a point of confusion, disagreement, risk, or an open question that the workshop surfaces but does not resolve on the spot. It is marked with a distinctly coloured sticky — conventionally purple or magenta, often rotated as a diamond — so it stands out against the orange event flow. Hotspots are valuable output: they show exactly where the domain is fuzzy and needs follow-up.

Q: What are "pivotal events" in big-picture EventStorming?
A: Pivotal events are the small number of especially significant domain events that mark major transitions or phase changes in the process, dividing the timeline into meaningful segments. They often coincide with boundaries between bounded contexts or with key state changes like "order accepted", "trade settled", or "NAV struck". Marking them helps structure the flow and frequently reveals candidate context boundaries.

Q: What is the value of EventStorming to a data architect specifically?
A: It is a fast, low-cost way to elicit the real domain flow, surface where language and meaning shift, and discover candidate bounded contexts before committing to data-product boundaries. It formalises the business-analysis instinct of "follow the events" into a repeatable workshop. For a data architect it directly informs where to cut data products and which context owns which data.

Q: Why start a big-picture EventStorming with events rather than with data models or systems?
A: Events describe what the business actually does in language everyone shares, whereas data models and systems are implementation artefacts that experts may not relate to. Starting with events keeps non-technical participants engaged and avoids prematurely freezing structure. The structure — aggregates, contexts, data — emerges *from* the event flow rather than being imposed on it.

Q: How would you run a *solo* big-picture EventStorming of the subscription-order-to-NAV flow?
A: Lay out domain events left to right in past tense — "order received", "order accepted", "cash received", "trade placed", "trade settled", "valuation point reached", "NAV struck", "NAV published" — using orange stickies. Flag uncertainties as hotspots and mark the pivotal events that divide the flow. Even alone, the discipline of externalising the timeline reveals gaps, ordering questions, and natural context seams.

Q: What is an aggregate in DDD, at the conceptual level?
A: An aggregate is a cluster of related domain objects treated as a single unit for the purpose of enforcing invariants, with one entity acting as the root through which all changes go. Conceptually it is a *consistency boundary*: everything inside must be kept consistent together within one transaction. At the strategic/design-review level you care about where these boundaries fall, not the tactical code patterns.

Q: What does "consistency boundary" mean and why does it matter in design reviews?
A: A consistency boundary is the scope within which a set of business rules (invariants) must hold true atomically — all changes inside happen together or not at all. It matters in reviews because it tells you what must be transactionally consistent versus what can be eventually consistent across boundaries. Drawing it too large hurts scalability; too small lets invariants be violated.

Q: Why do you need the aggregate concept for design reviews without the tactical code patterns?
A: Because the architect's job is to judge where consistency must be enforced and how boundaries affect data ownership and contracts, which is a conceptual decision independent of repositories, factories, or entity frameworks. You can critique whether a proposed boundary protects the right invariant without writing the implementation. The tactical patterns are for the implementing team; the boundary judgement is the architectural contribution.

Q: For a NAV strike, what invariant might define an aggregate consistency boundary?
A: The rule that a fund's NAV per share is consistent with the sum of valued positions, fees, and shares in issue at the valuation point — all those must agree atomically for a single struck NAV. That mutual consistency at the moment of striking defines a natural consistency boundary. Anything that need not be consistent in that same instant, like downstream distribution, sits outside the aggregate.

Q: What is the data-mesh idea of "data products" and how does it connect to DDD?
A: A data product is a curated, owned, consumable unit of analytical data treated as a product with a clear owner, contract, and SLOs. Data mesh draws data-product boundaries from DDD bounded contexts, so each domain owns and publishes the data it is the authority on. The connection is that DDD gives the principled way to decide where one product ends and another begins.

Q: What is a source-aligned data product?
A: A source-aligned (native) data product exposes data closely reflecting the operational system or domain that originates it, with minimal transformation. It is owned by the context that produces the source facts, such as transfer agency publishing raw subscription/redemption orders. It represents the domain's facts "as they happen" and is the upstream raw material other products build on.

Q: What is an aggregate data product in data-mesh terms?
A: An aggregate data product combines and reshapes data from multiple source-aligned products into a new, value-added dataset that no single source owns alone. It sits in the middle, integrating across domains — for example a product joining custody positions with fund-accounting valuations. Note this "aggregate" is the data-mesh alignment type, distinct from the DDD tactical aggregate.

Q: What is a consumer-aligned data product?
A: A consumer-aligned (fit-for-purpose) data product is shaped specifically for the needs of particular consumers or use cases, optimised for how it will be read rather than how it is produced. An example is a client-reporting dataset modelled exactly for statement generation. It trades generality for being directly usable by its intended audience.

Q: Which context should own a source-aligned data product?
A: The context that is the authoritative source of those facts — the one whose operational domain originates the data. Transfer agency owns the source-aligned order data; fund accounting owns the source-aligned NAV facts. Ownership follows domain authority so that the producer of the truth is also accountable for the product that publishes it.

Q: How do the three data-product alignment types map onto a typical lakehouse medallion architecture?
A: Roughly, source-aligned products correspond to bronze/raw-to-silver domain data near the operational source, aggregate products correspond to integrated silver/gold marts joining domains, and consumer-aligned products correspond to gold datasets shaped for specific consumers. The mapping is approximate, not one-to-one, but it helps locate where each alignment type lives. The key is that alignment is about ownership and shaping intent, not just storage layer.

Q: Name five candidate bounded contexts for a fund administrator.
A: Transfer agency (TA), fund accounting, custody, regulatory reporting, and client reporting. Each has its own ubiquitous language, invariants, and authoritative data: TA owns investor registers and orders, fund accounting owns valuation and NAV, custody owns positions and settlement, regulatory reporting owns filings, and client reporting owns investor-facing presentation. They are candidates because they exhibit distinct language and model consistency.

Q: How would you answer the challenge "why is fund accounting not part of the TA context?" with a domain answer?
A: Because they have different ubiquitous languages and different invariants: TA speaks of investors, registers, orders, and dealing; fund accounting speaks of valuations, the books of the fund, NAV per share, and accounting periods. A "subscription" in TA is an investor order, while in fund accounting the relevant event is cash and shares hitting the books. The boundary is justified by language and invariants, not by which department they sit in.

Q: Why is an org-chart answer ("they report to different managers") a poor justification for a context boundary?
A: Because org structure is incidental and unstable — it changes with reorganisations — whereas a sound boundary rests on durable differences in language and model consistency. If the only reason TA and fund accounting are separate is reporting lines, the boundary will be redrawn the moment the org changes, fracturing your data landscape. DDD insists the justification be about meaning and invariants.

Q: How do EMT and EPT function as "published languages" in the fund-administration domain map?
A: EMT (European MiFID Template) and EPT (European PRIIPs Template) are standardised FinDatEx interchange formats that fund manufacturers publish for distributors, decoupling each party's internal model from the exchange format. On a context map they sit on edges as Published Language: the agreed, documented contract that crosses the boundary. This lets a manufacturer change internals without breaking distributors, as long as the template contract holds.

Q: On the fund-administration context map, what relationship type fits custody supplying positions to fund accounting?
A: Customer–Supplier, with custody upstream as the supplier and fund accounting downstream as the customer. Fund accounting depends on custody's position and settlement data to value the fund, and its needs (timeliness, completeness for the valuation point) are negotiated into custody's delivery. The direction of influence is clear: custody changes flow downstream into fund accounting.

Q: On the context map, what relationship fits a legacy transfer-agency feed consumed by a modern context?
A: An Anticorruption Layer on the consuming side, because the legacy feed carries an old, idiosyncratic model you do not want leaking into your clean domain. The ACL translates legacy codes and structures into your ubiquitous language before any domain logic sees them. This isolates you from the legacy model so a change there cannot corrupt yours.

Q: What model corruption does an ACL in front of a legacy TA feed specifically prevent?
A: It prevents the legacy feed's terminology, status codes, and structural quirks — say a proprietary order-status enumeration or a denormalised investor record — from infiltrating and reshaping your fund-data model. Without the ACL, downstream logic would start branching on legacy codes and your ubiquitous language would erode into the legacy one. The ACL keeps your model defined by your domain, not by the upstream's accidents.

Q: How is a strategic DDD analysis "the intellectual foundation under data mesh"?
A: Data mesh's core principle of domain-oriented decentralised data ownership is essentially DDD applied to analytical data — bounded contexts become data domains, and ubiquitous language and context mapping govern the products and their interfaces. Without DDD, "domains" in a mesh degenerate into arbitrary partitions. Strategic DDD provides the principled basis for where to cut and who owns what.

Q: What is the difference between true domain-oriented design and "folder renaming"?
A: Domain-oriented design cuts boundaries along genuine differences in language, invariants, and ownership, producing models and products that are internally coherent and independently evolvable. Folder renaming merely relabels existing technical structure with domain-sounding names while the underlying coupling and shared model remain. The test is whether each boundary protects a distinct ubiquitous language and consistency rule, not just whether the directory is named after a domain.

Q: What is a "big ball of mud" and how does DDD help avoid it?
A: A big ball of mud is a system with no discernible boundaries, where everything depends on everything and the model is a tangle. DDD avoids it by establishing explicit bounded contexts with clear context-map relationships, so models stay internally consistent and integrations are deliberate. The boundaries and relationship patterns are precisely the discipline that stops integrations from rotting into mud.

Q: What is Context Mapper and what artefact does it produce?
A: Context Mapper is an open-source DSL (CML) and toolset for expressing bounded contexts and their relationships as text. It produces a versionable, reviewable context-map artefact that can be diffed in code review and committed alongside ADRs. Turning the map into code makes it a first-class, evolvable design document rather than a stale diagram.

Q: In Context Mapper DSL, what keyword adds bounded contexts to a context map?
A: The `contains` keyword, used inside a `ContextMap` block, lists the bounded contexts that participate in the map. For example a `ContextMap` declares `contains FundAccountingContext` and `contains CustodyContext`. The contexts themselves are declared separately with `BoundedContext`, and the map references them.

Q: In Context Mapper DSL, how is a Customer–Supplier relationship typically written?
A: As an upstream/downstream edge using the `U`/`D` roles, for example `FundAccountingContext [D]<-[U] CustodyContext`, optionally with `S`/`C` for Supplier/Customer or `[U,S]`/`[D,C]`. The arrow direction encodes that upstream influences downstream. You can attach attributes in braces, such as `{ implementationTechnology = "REST" }`.

Q: What do the abbreviations `OHS`, `PL`, `CF`, and `ACL` mean in Context Mapper DSL?
A: `OHS` is Open Host Service and `PL` is Published Language — both upstream roles describing how a supplier exposes itself; `CF` is Conformist and `ACL` is Anticorruption Layer — both downstream roles describing how a consumer copes. A relationship like `[D,ACL]<-[U,OHS,PL] CustodyContext` reads as a downstream context defending itself with an ACL against an upstream that offers an open host service in a published language. These abbreviations make the map's intent explicit and reviewable.

Q: Why commit the Context Mapper map alongside your ADR log?
A: So the context map evolves as a versioned, reviewable artefact in lockstep with the architectural decisions that shaped it, giving traceability between "why" (ADRs) and "what" (the map). A reviewer can see a relationship change as a diff and tie it to the decision record. This turns architecture into something testable and auditable rather than a diagram that drifts out of date.

Q: When annotating a context map with data products, what three alignment classifications would you apply?
A: Source-aligned, aggregate, and consumer-aligned, the data-mesh alignment types. For each product you note which context owns it and which alignment it is — TA's raw orders as source-aligned, a cross-domain valuation mart as aggregate, a client-statement dataset as consumer-aligned. This makes ownership and shaping intent explicit on the map.

Q: A consumer complains that "NAV" from your data product does not match the NAV on the client statement. Where do you look first through a DDD lens?
A: First check whether the two NAVs belong to different bounded contexts — the fund-accounting struck NAV versus the client-reporting presented NAV — because they may legitimately differ in timing, rounding, or status. If they are meant to be the same number, then verify the translation at the context boundary and the published-language contract between them. The DDD instinct is to confirm which model each value comes from before assuming a bug.

Q: What is the danger of treating a bounded context as identical to a database schema?
A: While a context often maps to its own storage, equating context with schema can lead to either splitting one coherent model across schemas for technical reasons or cramming two distinct models into one schema. The context is defined by model and language consistency, not by where bytes live. The correct sequence is to find the context first, then decide storage, not infer the context from existing schemas.

Q: In context mapping, why is identifying the direction of dependency on each edge so important for data flows?
A: Because the upstream/downstream direction tells you who absorbs the cost of change and which way data and breaking changes propagate. If custody is upstream of fund accounting, a custody schema change threatens fund accounting, so you place contracts and ACLs accordingly. Mislabelling direction leads to defences pointing the wrong way and surprise breakages.

Q: What does "one term, one precise meaning inside a context" rule out that beginners often do?
A: It rules out overloading a single term with multiple meanings within the same context, and rules out using several interchangeable synonyms for one concept. Beginners often let "client", "customer", and "investor" float as loose synonyms, or use "order" to mean both a subscription and a redemption. The rule forces disambiguation so the model and language are exact.

Q: How would EventStorming help you discover that "trade settled" sits on a context boundary?
A: As you lay out events, you would notice that the people and language around "trade placed" (custody/dealing) differ from those around "NAV struck" (fund accounting), with "trade settled" being the pivotal handoff between them. The shift in vocabulary and ownership at that pivotal event flags a candidate boundary. Pivotal events frequently mark exactly where one context hands off to the next.

Q: What is the relationship between pivotal events and bounded-context boundaries?
A: Pivotal events often coincide with bounded-context boundaries because they mark significant state transitions where responsibility and language hand off from one part of the business to another. Not every pivotal event is a boundary and not every boundary has a pivotal event, but they correlate strongly. That correlation makes pivotal events a practical heuristic for proposing context seams.

Q: Why is a hotspot considered a valuable *output* of EventStorming rather than a failure?
A: Because a hotspot captures a precise, located point of disagreement, risk, or unknown that would otherwise stay implicit and surface later as a defect or argument. Making it explicit on the wall means it can be assigned, investigated, and resolved deliberately. A workshop that surfaces many honest hotspots has done its discovery job well.

Q: How do bounded contexts help "every reorg" not "redraw your data landscape along org-chart lines"?
A: When data-product and integration boundaries follow domain meaning, an org reshuffle moves people without invalidating the model, because the model was never defined by reporting lines. The contexts, contracts, and data products remain coherent regardless of who manages whom. This stability is precisely the architect's defence against reorg-driven churn in the data estate.

Q: What is the difference between the DDD tactical "aggregate" and the data-mesh "aggregate data product"?
A: The DDD aggregate is a transactional consistency boundary around domain objects in operational software; the data-mesh aggregate data product is an analytical dataset that integrates multiple source-aligned products. They share a word but solve different problems at different layers. Conflating them in a design review causes confusion, so it is worth naming which one you mean.

Q: Why might client reporting be a supporting rather than core subdomain for a fund administrator?
A: Because while necessary and visible, client reporting largely presents and formats data the core valuation and accounting contexts already produce, rather than creating the differentiating value. Its problems are real but more about presentation and distribution than hard domain logic. That makes it a candidate for supporting, with effort concentrated on getting the core NAV right.

Q: When is Conformist the right choice over building an ACL?
A: Conformist is right when the upstream model is good enough, stable, and the cost of building and maintaining a translation layer is not justified — often when the upstream is a strong external standard or platform. You accept its model wholesale to save effort. The trade-off is loss of insulation: you inherit upstream changes directly, so it suits low-volatility, acceptable-model upstreams.

Q: What practical risk comes with a Conformist relationship to an external regulatory schema?
A: You bind your downstream model to the regulator's schema, so any change to that schema flows straight into your context with no buffer. If the regulator revises an XSD or field definition, your model and code must change in lockstep. That is acceptable when the schema is stable and authoritative, but it removes the insulation an ACL would provide.

Q: How does ubiquitous language reduce defects in a regulated reporting pipeline?
A: By ensuring the term used by compliance experts, in the contract, and in the code are identical, it removes the silent mistranslations that produce wrong filings. When "valuation point" means exactly one thing from expert to schema, there is no gap for a developer to misinterpret. In a CSSF-facing Annex IV context, that precision is the difference between a correct submission and a regulatory conversation.

Q: What does it mean to say a bounded context owns its data?
A: It means one context is the authoritative source and accountable steward for a given set of facts, and other contexts consume that data through explicit, contracted interfaces rather than reaching into its model. Ownership ties responsibility for quality, definition, and evolution to a single domain. This single-owner principle is what keeps a shared concept from fragmenting into three divergent versions.

Q: A new integration is proposed where client reporting reads directly from fund accounting's internal tables. What DDD objection do you raise?
A: That it bypasses a deliberate context boundary and couples client reporting to fund accounting's internal model, so any internal change breaks the consumer and the accounting model gets pressured to serve reporting needs. The right pattern is an explicit published language or an upstream/downstream contract, possibly with an ACL on the consumer side. Direct table reads turn two clean contexts into a hidden ball of mud.

Q: How does a Partnership relationship differ from Customer–Supplier in terms of planning?
A: In Partnership both teams plan jointly and share roadmaps because neither is clearly upstream; coordination is mutual and continuous. In Customer–Supplier there is a clear upstream supplier whose roadmap the downstream customer influences through negotiation, but the supplier still leads. Partnership implies tighter, symmetric coupling and is reserved for genuinely aligned goals.

Q: Why is "Separate Ways" sometimes the most cost-effective relationship?
A: Because integration carries real ongoing cost — interfaces, contracts, translation, coordination — and when two contexts only marginally overlap, duplicating the small bit of functionality is cheaper than connecting them. Recognising this prevents over-integration. The discipline is admitting that not every shared concept justifies a maintained connection.

Q: What is the role of domain experts in EventStorming and why must they be in the room?
A: Domain experts supply the real meaning, sequence, and edge cases of the business process, which engineers cannot reliably infer. Their presence is what makes the resulting model match reality and what surfaces the language and hotspots that matter. EventStorming is explicitly designed to put experts and technical people at the same wall to build shared understanding.

Q: How does the concept of aggregate consistency boundary influence where you put a data contract?
A: Data contracts naturally align with the boundary of what a context guarantees to be internally consistent, so the consistency boundary informs what a product can promise atomically versus eventually. If a NAV strike is an aggregate, the contract can guarantee internal NAV consistency; cross-context joins can only promise eventual consistency. Knowing the boundary tells you which guarantees are honest to put in the contract.

Q: What is the practical first step to cluster EventStorming events into bounded contexts?
A: Walk the timeline and group adjacent events that share the same language, actors, and invariants, watching for points where vocabulary or ownership shifts — those shifts are candidate boundaries. Five to seven clusters for a fund administrator is a reasonable target. The pivotal events and hotspots you marked help reveal where to draw the lines.

Q: Why classify each clustered subdomain as core, supporting, or generic right after clustering?
A: Because the classification immediately drives investment and build-versus-buy decisions for each context, turning the discovery output into actionable strategy. Knowing custody/valuation is core and ledger is generic tells you where to spend modelling effort and where to procure. Doing it right after clustering keeps the domain understanding fresh and the rationale tied to the discovered flow.

Q: What does the syllabus mean by EventStorming being "your BA superpower, formalized"?
A: It means the business-analysis instinct of walking a process and asking "what happens next, and what could go wrong" is turned into a structured, repeatable, collaborative method with explicit notation. The formalisation — orange events, purple hotspots, pivotal markers — makes the elicitation systematic and shareable. It scales the analyst's intuition into a workshop that produces durable artefacts.

Q: How would you decide whether a "fee accrual" dataset is a source-aligned or aggregate data product?
A: If fee accrual is a fact produced natively by one context (say fund accounting computes it as part of valuation), it is source-aligned to that context. If the accrual dataset is built by combining inputs from several contexts — fee schedules, positions, valuations — it is aggregate. The deciding question is whether one context authoritatively originates it or whether it is composed across domains.

Q: What is the danger of making a data product consumer-aligned too early?
A: Shaping data tightly to one consumer's needs before the underlying source and aggregate products are stable couples you to a specific use case and forces rework when needs change or new consumers appear. Consumer-aligned products are appropriately downstream of stable source data. Premature consumer alignment can also leak presentation concerns back into the producing context.

Q: How does the EMT-as-published-language pattern protect a fund manufacturer's internal model?
A: Because distributors integrate against the standardised EMT template, not against the manufacturer's internal schema, the manufacturer can refactor internals freely as long as it keeps emitting a valid EMT. The published language is the only contract crossing the boundary. This decoupling is exactly why standardised templates exist: stable exchange without exposing private models.

Q: On the context map, what relationship would you assign between the regulatory reporting context and an external regulator's schema?
A: Often Conformist, since the regulator's schema is an authoritative external standard your reporting context must adopt as-is with no influence and frequently no translation. You speak the regulator's published language directly. Where you want insulation from schema churn, you might place an ACL, but conformity to a regulator is the common and accepted stance.

Q: Why is "context mapping" considered a strategic rather than tactical activity?
A: Because it operates at the level of whole contexts and the relationships between them — organisational and integration strategy — rather than the internal code patterns of any one context. It shapes team boundaries, ownership, and data flows across the landscape. Tactical DDD (entities, aggregates as code) lives *inside* a context; context mapping governs *between* contexts.

Q: How do you justify naming NAV calculation the core subdomain when defending build-versus-buy?
A: By showing it is where the hard, differentiating problem lives: accurate, timely, defensible valuation under complex instruments, fees, corporate actions, and regulation is what a fund administrator is paid to get right. That difficulty and differentiation is the definition of core, so it warrants custom build and the best modelling. Generic capabilities around it can be bought, concentrating advantage where it matters.

Q: What concrete invariant might an ACL enforce when wrapping a legacy custody feed?
A: It can normalise legacy status codes and units into your canonical model — mapping, say, a legacy "settled/partially settled" enumeration and currency conventions into your domain's position and settlement concepts — so downstream logic only ever sees clean, canonical values. The ACL guarantees that whatever shape the legacy feed takes, your model's invariants about positions hold. That translation is the corruption prevention the ACL exists for.

Q: Why is it a mistake to draw a single enterprise-wide canonical model of "fund" across all contexts?
A: Because forcing one model across contexts with genuinely different invariants produces an over-generalised, lowest-common-denominator model that serves none well and recreates the big-ball-of-mud coupling. Different contexts legitimately need different "fund" models. DDD instead lets each context keep its own model and translates at boundaries via published languages and ACLs.

Q: What does "model consistency" mean as the criterion for a bounded context?
A: Model consistency means that within the boundary, all the concepts, rules, and terms fit together coherently without contradiction, so the model is valid everywhere inside. The boundary is drawn exactly where keeping that single model consistent stops being possible or sensible. Outside the boundary a different consistent model takes over, which is why translation is needed across it.

Q: How does EventStorming's left-to-right timeline help reveal missing steps?
A: Laying events in temporal order exposes gaps where the flow jumps — if "order accepted" is followed directly by "NAV struck" with nothing between, participants immediately ask what happened to cash, dealing, and settlement. The visual sequence makes omissions obvious. This is why the timeline format is so effective for discovery: absence is as visible as presence.

Q: Why must a data architect resist letting microservice boundaries dictate bounded contexts?
A: Because microservice boundaries are driven by deployment, scaling, and team-topology concerns that may not align with domain meaning, and letting them dictate contexts splits or merges models for the wrong reasons. The context should be found from the domain first; services are then chosen to fit. Reversing the order produces models fractured along infrastructure seams.

Q: In the subscription-order-to-NAV flow, which events are the natural pivotal events and why?
A: "Order accepted", "trade settled", and "NAV struck" are natural pivotal events because each marks a major state transition and a handoff between contexts — acceptance commits the order, settlement transfers responsibility to accounting, and the strike produces the authoritative valuation. They divide the flow into TA, dealing/custody, and fund-accounting segments. Marking them structures the timeline and exposes candidate boundaries.

Q: How does annotating data-product ownership on the context map support data governance?
A: It makes explicit, per product, which single context is accountable for definition, quality, and evolution, and how it relates to consumers — the foundation of clear governance and contracts. Governance can then assign owners, SLOs, and contracts against a real map rather than guesswork. Without the annotation, ownership stays ambiguous and the same data fragments into competing versions.

Q: A reviewer asks "is this an aggregate or a separate context?" — how do you decide?
A: Ask whether the cluster needs its own consistency boundary within one model (an aggregate) or its own *language and model* distinct from neighbours (a context). If the invariants and vocabulary are shared with the surrounding model and you only need transactional consistency, it is an aggregate; if the language and meaning genuinely diverge, it is a context. The distinguishing test is language/model divergence versus mere consistency scope.

Q: Why is "published language" often preferred over point-to-point Customer–Supplier when many consumers exist?
A: Because a single published language lets many downstream consumers integrate against one stable, documented format instead of each negotiating a bespoke supplier relationship, which scales far better. It decouples the producer from the specific needs of each consumer. EMT distributing to many distributors is exactly this: one template, many consumers, no per-distributor model.

Q: How does strategic DDD reduce the risk that "integrations rot into a big ball of mud"?
A: By forcing each integration to be a named, deliberate relationship on a context map — with explicit direction, translation, and ownership — rather than an ad hoc connection. Each edge carries a chosen pattern (ACL, published language, conformist) that constrains how models couple. That deliberate structure is what keeps the landscape legible instead of degenerating into undifferentiated mud.

Q: What three things must your context map withstand for lesson 8.4.1 to be "done"?
A: It must let you defend a boundary like "why is fund accounting not part of TA" with a domain answer about language and invariants, name the fund administrator's core subdomain with a build-versus-buy justification, and point to one ACL on the map and explain the exact model corruption it prevents. Each is a domain-reasoned defence, not an org-chart or hand-wave answer. Together they prove you can cut and justify boundaries that survive scrutiny.

Q: What is the Shared Kernel context-mapping relationship and what is its main risk?
A: Shared Kernel is a relationship where two contexts deliberately share a small subset of the model — code, schema, or a kernel of concepts — and agree to co-own and co-evolve it. Its main risk is high coupling: neither team can change the shared part unilaterally, so every modification needs cross-team coordination, which can become a bottleneck. It suits only a few closely cooperating teams; for most fund-data integrations a published language is safer because it shares a contract, not a model.

Q: In Context Mapper DSL, how do you write a Shared Kernel relationship?
A: As a symmetric edge using the `[SK]<->[SK]` arrow notation, for example `FundAccountingContext [SK]<->[SK] ValuationContext`, or with the explicit `Shared-Kernel` keyword between the two context names. The `<->` arrow signals symmetry, so it does not matter which context is on which side. You can attach `{ implementationTechnology = "Java Library" }` to record how the kernel is physically shared.

Q: How does Shared Kernel differ from Partnership on a context map?
A: Both are symmetric (`<->`) relationships between cooperating teams, but Shared Kernel means they literally co-own a shared *part of the model*, whereas Partnership means they coordinate roadmaps and succeed-or-fail together without necessarily sharing model artefacts. Shared Kernel is tighter and riskier because a change to the kernel touches both codebases directly. Partnership is about aligned planning; Shared Kernel is about a jointly maintained chunk of the model.

Q: In Context Mapper DSL, how is a Partnership relationship written and why does side not matter?
A: It is written as `ContractsContext [P]<->[P] ClaimsContext` or with the `Partnership` keyword between the two names, optionally with a relationship name after a colon like `: ContractClaimRelationship`. Side does not matter because the `<->` arrows mark it as a symmetric relationship with no upstream or downstream. This contrasts with the asymmetric `[U]`/`[D]` Customer–Supplier edge where direction is meaningful.

Q: Two contexts each maintain their own copy of "instrument reference data" and never sync. Which context-map relationship is this, and is it defensible?
A: This is Separate Ways — they deliberately do not integrate and each solves the overlapping need independently. It is defensible when the overlap is small and the integration cost (sync, contracts, conflict resolution) exceeds the benefit. It becomes indefensible if the two copies must agree for correctness, such as both feeding the same NAV; then the duplication causes reconciliation breaks and you need a single owner instead.

Q: A downstream NAV-reporting context keeps breaking whenever custody changes its feed. First thing to check through a DDD lens?
A: First confirm the edge's relationship type and whether any insulation exists: if reporting is a Conformist directly consuming custody's raw model, every upstream change propagates with no buffer, which explains the breakage. The fix is usually to introduce an Anticorruption Layer on the reporting side so custody's structural changes are absorbed by translation rather than reaching domain logic. Check whether a published language or contract should sit on that edge instead of raw coupling.

Q: How would you tell whether two teams should be Partnership or Customer–Supplier on a context map?
A: Ask who absorbs the cost of change and whether influence is mutual: if one context clearly supplies the other and the consumer negotiates its needs into the supplier's roadmap, it is Customer–Supplier with a defined upstream. If neither leads and both must co-plan because failure is shared, it is Partnership. Partnership demands genuinely aligned goals and tighter coordination, so do not reach for it just because two teams talk often.

Q: What is the EventStorming "command" sticky and what colour is it conventionally?
A: A command represents an intention or request that triggers a domain event — written in imperative form like "place order" or "strike NAV" — and is conventionally a blue sticky placed immediately before the orange event it causes. Commands appear in the design-level (process-modelling) variant rather than the pure big-picture discovery. They make explicit who or what initiates each event, complementing the past-tense events.

Q: In design-level EventStorming, what colour sticky represents an aggregate and where does it sit?
A: An aggregate is conventionally a large pale-yellow sticky, placed between the command and the event it produces, representing the unit of consistency that receives the command and emits the event. It clusters the commands and events that belong to one consistency boundary. For a NAV strike, the valuation aggregate would receive a "strike NAV" command and emit a "NAV struck" event.

Q: What colour sticky marks an external system in EventStorming, and why distinguish it?
A: External systems are conventionally pink stickies, distinguishing parts of the flow driven by systems outside your domain — a pricing vendor feed or a SWIFT network gateway — from internal actors and aggregates. Marking them shows where you depend on something you do not control, which often coincides with a context boundary needing an ACL or published language. The colour makes external dependencies visible on the wall.

Q: What does a lilac (purple) policy sticky represent in EventStorming, distinct from a hotspot?
A: A policy sticky — conventionally lilac — represents a reactive rule of the form "whenever event X happens, then command Y is triggered", capturing business logic that links an event to a subsequent action. It is distinct from a hotspot, which is an unresolved question or risk, even though both use purple-ish colours; policies are decisions, hotspots are open issues. For example "whenever cash is received, then place the dealing trade" is a policy.

Q: What does a green read-model sticky capture in EventStorming?
A: A green read model represents the view or information an actor needs to decide on a command — the user's window into the system's state at that point in the flow. It answers "what does the person look at before issuing this command?". For instance, a dealer might consult a read model of available cash and cut-off times before issuing the "place trade" command.

Q: How would you use EventStorming to decide whether "FX hedging" deserves its own bounded context?
A: Lay out the FX-related events and watch whether the language, actors, and invariants around them diverge from the surrounding valuation flow — distinct terms like "hedge ratio", "rolling", and a separate desk suggest a separate context. If the FX events are tightly interwoven with valuation and share its vocabulary, it may instead be an aggregate inside fund accounting. The test is language and invariant divergence, surfaced by the vocabulary shift along the timeline.

Q: A workshop produces 40 events but zero hotspots. What does that suggest about the session?
A: It usually suggests the session was not probing hard enough or the right domain experts and dissenting voices were absent, because real domains almost always contain ambiguity, disagreement, and unknowns. A healthy big-picture EventStorming surfaces honest hotspots as a sign of genuine discovery. Zero hotspots is a smell that the group rubber-stamped a happy path rather than exploring edge cases and handoffs.

Q: How do you handle an event that two groups insist happens at different points in the timeline?
A: Treat the disagreement as a hotspot, place the event with a purple marker at the contested position, and capture both orderings rather than forcing a premature resolution. The divergence often reveals two different processes, two contexts, or a genuine business ambiguity worth investigating later. Resolving it on the spot risks baking in one group's assumption; flagging it preserves the real signal.

Q: When clustering events into contexts, you find one event that plausibly belongs to two clusters. How do you proceed?
A: Examine which cluster's language and invariants the event truly serves and whether it is actually a pivotal handoff sitting on the boundary between the two — boundary events naturally feel like they belong to both. Often the right move is to assign authoritative ownership to one context and let the other consume it via a contract. "Trade settled" is the classic case: it lives on the custody/fund-accounting seam and is owned upstream, consumed downstream.

Q: How is the EventStorming pivotal event "NAV struck" likely to relate to an aggregate consistency boundary?
A: "NAV struck" is the moment the valuation aggregate enforces its invariant — NAV per share consistent with valued positions, fees, and shares in issue — atomically, so the pivotal event coincides with the aggregate's consistency boundary. The strike is exactly where everything inside the boundary must agree. This is why pivotal events are useful: they frequently mark both context seams and aggregate boundaries.

Q: A proposed aggregate spans "investor register" and "fund valuation" in one transaction. What invariant test exposes this as too large?
A: Ask whether any single business rule genuinely requires the investor register and the fund valuation to change atomically together — there is none; an investor joining the register need not be transactionally consistent with the NAV strike. Because no shared invariant binds them, the aggregate is too large and should be split, with the link handled by eventual consistency across contexts. Oversized aggregates hurt scalability and usually signal two contexts merged by accident.

Q: How does choosing a smaller aggregate boundary affect what your data contract can promise?
A: A smaller aggregate means fewer facts are guaranteed atomically consistent, so the contract can only promise strong consistency within that narrow scope and must label cross-aggregate relationships as eventually consistent. This keeps the contract honest: you do not over-promise atomicity you cannot deliver. For a NAV product, the contract guarantees internal NAV-component consistency but only eventual alignment with downstream client-reporting figures.

Q: Why can two source-aligned data products legitimately publish conflicting values for "position quantity" at the same instant?
A: Because they may belong to different contexts with different invariants and valuation points — custody's settled position versus a trade-date position in dealing — so the "same" quantity is actually two different facts measured under different rules. Each is authoritative within its own context. The DDD instinct is to confirm which context and valuation basis each value comes from before declaring a data-quality defect.

Q: A consumer wants both raw orders and a join of orders-with-settlements in one dataset. Which alignment types are involved and how do you respond?
A: Raw orders are a source-aligned product owned by transfer agency; the join of orders with settlements is an aggregate product that integrates TA and custody facts. You respond by serving them as two distinct products with clear ownership rather than collapsing them, then optionally a consumer-aligned product shaped for that consumer. Mixing raw source facts and cross-domain joins into one product blurs ownership and alignment intent.

Q: How would you spot that a "consumer-aligned" data product has begun leaking presentation logic back into the producing context?
A: Watch for the producing context's model acquiring fields, formats, or rounding that exist only to serve one downstream report — for instance fund accounting adding a "statement display NAV" rounded for the client PDF. That is presentation concern migrating upstream, a sign the consumer-aligned shaping was pushed too far back. The fix is to keep the producer's model pure and do the report-specific shaping in the consumer-aligned layer.

Q: How does the data-mesh principle of domain ownership change who is paged when a NAV product's freshness SLO breaks?
A: Under domain ownership the context that authoritatively produces the NAV — fund accounting — owns the product end to end, so its team is accountable and paged, not a central data team. Ownership ties operational responsibility to domain authority. This is the practical consequence of mapping data-product boundaries onto bounded contexts: the producer of the truth also operates and supports the product.

Q: A central platform team wants to own all data products "for consistency". What DDD/data-mesh objection do you raise?
A: That centralising ownership recreates the bottleneck and divorces accountability from domain knowledge, contradicting domain-oriented decentralised ownership — the central team cannot know each context's invariants well enough to steward the data. Data mesh deliberately pushes ownership to the domains that originate the facts. The platform team should provide self-serve infrastructure, not own the products themselves.

Q: How would you justify making "regulatory reporting" a supporting subdomain rather than core for a typical fund administrator?
A: Regulatory reporting is mandatory and skill-intensive but largely reshapes facts the core valuation and accounting contexts already produce into prescribed formats, rather than creating competitive advantage. Its difficulty is in compliance and mapping, not differentiating domain logic. That makes it supporting — important and carefully built, but not where the firm wins, so it does not warrant the deepest custom modelling that NAV calculation does.

Q: Could the same capability be core for one firm and generic for another? Give a fund-industry example.
A: Yes — subdomain classification is relative to the business model, not absolute. Transfer-agency register-keeping is core for a specialist TA whose differentiation is investor servicing, but generic for a manager who outsources it and treats it as a commodity to buy. The same code does the work; what differs is whether it is the firm's competitive edge, which determines build versus buy.

Q: What strategic mistake does "buying your core subdomain" represent for a fund administrator?
A: It surrenders the very capability that differentiates the firm — for example outsourcing NAV calculation to a generic off-the-shelf engine and inheriting its limits on complex instruments and corporate actions. You give away your advantage and become indistinguishable from competitors using the same product. The investment logic is to build the core precisely because that is where custom modelling creates the edge you sell.

Q: How does misclassifying a generic subdomain as core waste budget concretely?
A: You pour your best engineers and deep custom modelling into a solved commodity — say building a bespoke double-entry general ledger from scratch — producing something a mature off-the-shelf engine already does better and cheaper. The differentiation budget is spent where it cannot create advantage. The result is high build and maintenance cost with zero competitive return, the mirror image of buying your core.

Q: How would you explain to a sceptical stakeholder why one global "customer" model across all contexts is harmful?
A: Show that "customer" legitimately differs by context — an investor in TA, a counterparty in custody, a billing party in finance — each with different identifiers, invariants, and lifecycles, so a single global model becomes a bloated lowest-common-denominator that fits none. Forcing it recreates tight coupling and the big-ball-of-mud problem. Each context should keep its own model and translate at boundaries, not collapse into one schema.

Q: A team wants to add a `legacy_status_code` column to your clean domain table "just to be safe". What DDD principle pushes back?
A: The anticorruption-layer principle: legacy codes belong outside your model and must be translated at the boundary, not stored as first-class fields where downstream logic will start branching on them and erode your ubiquitous language. Letting the legacy code in is exactly the model corruption an ACL exists to prevent. Keep the legacy enumeration in the ACL's mapping, and persist only your canonical status.

Q: How would an ACL handle an upstream legacy feed that sometimes sends an unknown status code?
A: The ACL should fail fast or route the unknown code to an explicit error or quarantine path rather than silently mapping it to a default canonical value, so corruption never reaches the domain. Mapping unknowns to a guessed canonical status would let bad data masquerade as valid. The ACL's job is to guarantee that only recognised, translated values cross into your model, surfacing the gap as a contract violation against the upstream.

Q: What is the cost trade-off between an ACL and a Conformist relationship, expressed in maintenance terms?
A: An ACL costs ongoing translation-layer maintenance — you must update the mapping whenever upstream changes — but buys insulation so the rest of your model is untouched. Conformist costs nothing to build but means every upstream change ripples directly into your model and code. You choose ACL when the upstream is volatile or its model is unacceptable, and Conformist when the upstream is stable and its model is good enough to live with.

Q: When is Conformist genuinely the better engineering choice over an ACL for a regulatory schema?
A: When the regulator's published schema is authoritative, stable, and you have no influence over it, conforming directly avoids paying for a translation layer that only re-expresses a format you must speak anyway. You gain simplicity and a single source of truth for the format. The risk — direct exposure to schema churn — is acceptable precisely because such schemas change rarely and on published timelines.

Q: How would you encode in Context Mapper DSL a custody-to-fund-accounting edge where custody offers an open host service and fund accounting defends with an ACL?
A: As `FundAccountingContext [D,ACL]<-[U,OHS] CustodyContext`, where `[U,OHS]` marks custody as the upstream open host service and `[D,ACL]` marks fund accounting as the downstream defending with an anticorruption layer. The `<-` arrow encodes that influence flows from upstream to downstream. You can append `{ implementationTechnology = "REST" }` to record the transport.

Q: In Context Mapper DSL, what is the difference between the `<->` and `<-` operators on a relationship?
A: The `<->` operator denotes a symmetric relationship — Partnership or Shared Kernel — where neither side is upstream and the order of contexts is irrelevant. The `<-` operator denotes an asymmetric upstream/downstream relationship — Customer–Supplier or generic Upstream-Downstream — where direction encodes who influences whom, with the upstream on the arrow's source side. Choosing the right operator is what makes the map's dependency direction explicit and reviewable.

Q: How does committing a Context Mapper CML file enable a "diffable" architecture review?
A: Because the map is plain text, a change such as turning a Conformist edge into an ACL appears as a line-level diff in the pull request, so a reviewer sees exactly what relationship changed and can tie it to an ADR. This turns architecture into a reviewable, version-controlled artefact instead of a stale diagram. The CML file becomes a first-class design document that evolves under the same git workflow as code.

Q: You inherit a context map drawn as a static Visio diagram that no longer matches reality. What is the DDD-aligned remediation?
A: Re-derive the real contexts from current language and data flows — ideally via a fresh EventStorming — then encode the corrected map in a versionable form like Context Mapper CML committed alongside ADRs, so it stays in sync going forward. The static diagram drifted precisely because it was not diffable or owned. Making the map code, with each edge a deliberate named relationship, prevents the same rot.

Q: How would you detect, from data alone, that a hidden bounded-context boundary runs through a single shared table?
A: Look for columns or rows that are populated and interpreted differently by different consumers, status enumerations that mean different things to different teams, or a "type" discriminator that effectively splits the table into two models. That divergence in meaning over one schema is the data signature of two contexts crammed together. The remedy is to separate the models and translate at the boundary rather than overloading one table.

Q: A field called `nav` appears in three tables with three different precisions and as-of times. What does this reveal and how do you resolve it?
A: It reveals that "NAV" is being used across multiple bounded contexts without explicit boundaries — fund accounting's struck NAV, an indicative reporting NAV, and a distributor-facing figure — each legitimately different. The resolution is not to force one column but to name each precisely, assign each to its owning context, and document the published-language translation between them. The ambiguity is a context-mapping problem, not a single-source-of-truth bug.

Q: How would you respond to "let's just have one source of truth for NAV across the whole firm"?
A: Clarify that there is one authoritative *struck* NAV owned by fund accounting, but presented and indicative NAVs in other contexts are legitimately different objects, so "one source of truth" must mean one owner per fact, not one identical number everywhere. Collapsing them ignores differences in valuation point, rounding, and status. The correct framing is single authoritative ownership plus explicit, contracted translation, not a single universal value.

Q: A new consumer asks to read directly from fund accounting's `positions` working table for "performance". How do you redirect them with DDD reasoning?
A: Decline the direct read because it couples the consumer to an internal, unstable model and pressures fund accounting to keep that table report-friendly, then offer a contracted data product or published-language interface instead. Internal working tables are implementation, not contract. Serving them via an explicit source-aligned or consumer-aligned product preserves both contexts' independence and prevents a hidden ball of mud.

Q: How does ubiquitous language discipline help when onboarding a new data engineer to the fund-accounting context?
A: Because class names, table names, and columns mirror the experts' exact terms — "valuation point", "shares in issue", "struck NAV" — the engineer can read the schema and the domain conversation as one vocabulary, with no private translation glossary to learn. This shortens onboarding and reduces misinterpretation defects. The alignment is what lets the code be read as domain documentation rather than a cipher.

Q: Two columns, `value_date` and `settlement_date`, appear in the same context for the same concept. What ubiquitous-language rule is violated and what is the fix?
A: The rule "one concept, one term inside a context" is violated by using two synonyms for the same idea, which invites confusion about whether they ever differ. The fix is to agree the single canonical term with domain experts and rename consistently across code, schema, and conversation. If the two genuinely denote different facts, then the violation is the opposite — one term hiding two concepts — and they must be disambiguated.

Q: How would you use a context map to plan a phased decommission of a legacy transfer-agency platform?
A: Mark the legacy platform as an upstream context behind an ACL on the map, then plan to migrate each downstream consumer to the new TA context one edge at a time, keeping the ACL stable so consumers do not see the change. The map shows exactly which edges depend on the legacy and in what direction, so you sequence the cutover by dependency. The ACL is what lets you swap the upstream behind a stable downstream-facing model.

Q: A regulator publishes a breaking change to an XSD you consume as a Conformist. What does the context map tell you about blast radius?
A: Because you are Conformist with no insulating layer, the map shows the change flows straight into your reporting context and any downstream contexts that depend on it, defining the blast radius along those edges. Every context on the dependency path must change in lockstep. The map lets you enumerate exactly which products and consumers are affected and decide whether to retrofit an ACL to contain future churn.

Q: How would you defend, in a design review, treating EMT as a published language rather than letting each distributor read your internal schema?
A: Argue that one stable, documented EMT contract lets many distributors integrate without coupling to your private model, so you can refactor internals freely as long as you keep emitting valid EMT — far more scalable than bespoke per-distributor reads. Direct schema access would multiply tight couplings and freeze your internals. The published language is the single crossing contract that decouples producer evolution from consumer stability.

Q: What is the difference between a context map's "Open Host Service" and a "Published Language", and can a context have both?
A: An Open Host Service is the upstream's published *protocol/interface* offered to many consumers, while a Published Language is the shared *format/vocabulary* those messages are expressed in; one is the door, the other is the language spoken through it. A context can and often does have both, which Context Mapper writes as `[U,OHS,PL]`. A fund manufacturer exposing an EMT endpoint offers an OHS speaking the EMT published language.

Q: How would you decide which context owns the "share class" concept when TA, fund accounting, and client reporting all reference it?
A: Identify which context is the authoritative source of the share class's defining facts and lifecycle — typically the one that creates and governs share-class setup, often fund accounting or a product-master context — and make it the single owner, with the others consuming via contract. Multiple references are fine; multiple owners are not. Ownership follows domain authority over the fact's definition and evolution, not how many contexts happen to read it.

Q: How would you use the core/supporting/generic classification to prioritise where to apply the richest data contracts first?
A: Apply the strictest, most fully specified contracts to the core subdomain's products first — for a fund administrator that is the NAV and valuation outputs — because that is where correctness creates competitive advantage and where errors are most costly. Supporting products get lighter contracts proportional to their stability, and generic ones often rely on the vendor's own guarantees. The classification turns finite contracting effort into a risk- and value-weighted plan rather than treating all products equally.

Q: A stakeholder argues custody and fund accounting should merge into one context "because they share instruments". Why is shared data not sufficient justification?
A: Shared reference to instruments does not imply a shared model: custody speaks of holdings, settlement, and safekeeping while fund accounting speaks of valued positions, NAV, and accounting periods, so the invariants and language diverge even over the same instruments. A bounded context is justified by model and language consistency, not by overlapping data. The correct treatment is two contexts with a Customer–Supplier edge passing positions, not a merge that would collapse two distinct models.
