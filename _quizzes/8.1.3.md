# Phase 8 · 8.1.3 Industry frameworks (TOGAF & ArchiMate, working fluency) — 150 self-test questions

Q: What does TOGAF stand for, and who maintains it?
A: TOGAF stands for The Open Group Architecture Framework, maintained by The Open Group, an industry standards consortium. It is the dominant enterprise-architecture (EA) framework in EU financial institutions, which is why a data architect needs working fluency to engage EA boards on their own terms. The current edition you should reference is TOGAF 10 (the "TOGAF Standard, 10th Edition"), restructured into Fundamental Content plus a Library of guides.

Q: What is the ADM in TOGAF, in one sentence?
A: The ADM is the Architecture Development Method — TOGAF's iterative, phased process for developing and governing an enterprise architecture from vision through change management. It is the beating heart of TOGAF: most of the framework's value to a practitioner is the ADM's phase structure and the deliverables each phase produces. You move around the cycle repeatedly rather than running it once start-to-finish.

Q: Name the lettered phases of the TOGAF ADM in order from A to H.
A: The phases are A Architecture Vision, B Business Architecture, C Information Systems Architectures, D Technology Architecture, E Opportunities and Solutions, F Migration Planning, G Implementation Governance, and H Architecture Change Management. They form a cycle, with Phase H feeding back to the start. A Preliminary Phase precedes A, and Requirements Management sits continuously at the centre of the cycle.

Q: What happens in the TOGAF Preliminary Phase, before Phase A?
A: The Preliminary Phase prepares and initiates the architecture capability: it tailors TOGAF into an organization-specific framework, establishes architecture principles, selects tools, and sets up the governance structure. It is "how we will do architecture here" rather than "what architecture we will build". Skipping it is why some EA functions never agree on principles or repository conventions and re-argue them on every engagement.

Q: Which two ADM phases are most directly the data architect's home, per this lesson?
A: Phases B (Business Architecture) and C (Information Systems Architectures), because Phase C explicitly contains the Data Architecture (alongside Application Architecture). This lesson tells you to focus your study on B/C, where your data products, models, and context maps belong. Phases E and F (Opportunities/Solutions and Migration Planning) are covered separately via Appendix A.10 roadmapping.

Q: What does Phase C of the ADM cover, and why does it matter to a data architect?
A: Phase C is Information Systems Architectures, which TOGAF splits into Data Architecture and Application Architecture. This is where your logical/physical data models, data-product definitions, and the data-flow relationships formally land in the framework. It matters because if you can name Phase C, your data designs enter the enterprise repository in the slot EA reviewers expect, rather than as orphan diagrams.

Q: What is produced in Phase A, Architecture Vision?
A: Phase A defines the scope and high-level Architecture Vision, identifies stakeholders and their concerns, secures sponsorship, and produces the Statement of Architecture Work that authorizes the engagement. It is deliberately high-altitude — an executive-readable target — not detailed models. Think of it as the "why and what, at 30,000 feet" that the later phases flesh out.

Q: What is the focus of Phase B, Business Architecture?
A: Phase B develops the Business Architecture that supports the agreed Architecture Vision: business capabilities, value streams, organization, business services, and processes. For a fund administrator this is where capabilities like "NAV calculation", "transfer agency", and "regulatory reporting" are modeled. Data architects care because business objects defined here (NAV, order, holding) are what your data objects later realize.

Q: What does Phase D, Technology Architecture, produce?
A: Phase D defines the technology infrastructure needed to support the data and application architectures: compute, storage, networks, platform services, and the technology standards. In your capstone this is the cluster, object-store, and database layer. It is the layer your ArchiMate technology view and C4 deployment/container thinking map onto.

Q: What is the purpose of Phase E, Opportunities and Solutions?
A: Phase E identifies the major work packages and solution building blocks needed to move from baseline to target, grouping changes into deliverable increments and Transition Architectures. It is the first phase that thinks in terms of implementation projects rather than pure architecture. It feeds Phase F, which sequences those increments into a plan.

Q: What does Phase F, Migration Planning, deliver?
A: Phase F produces the detailed Implementation and Migration Plan: sequencing the Transition Architectures, costing, prioritizing by business value and risk, and agreeing the roadmap. This is the TOGAF phase your Appendix A.10 roadmapping and strangler-fig migration work slot into. It is where "we will replace the legacy TA reporting mart" becomes a dated, gated plan.

Q: What is Phase G, Implementation Governance, for?
A: Phase G governs implementation against the architecture: it provides architectural oversight of delivery, issues Architecture Contracts, and ensures projects conform to the target (or raise dispensations). It is the bridge between the paper architecture and what actually gets built. A data architect uses it to ensure a delivery team does not silently diverge from the contracted data model.

Q: What does Phase H, Architecture Change Management, handle?
A: Phase H manages changes to the deployed architecture over its life: monitoring technology and business change, triaging change requests, and deciding whether a change is incremental (handled in-cycle) or warrants a new ADM iteration. It closes the loop back to the Preliminary/Phase A start. It is the difference between a living architecture and a one-off diagram that rots.

Q: Where does Requirements Management sit in the ADM, and why is it drawn in the centre?
A: Requirements Management is a continuous process at the hub of the ADM cycle, not a numbered phase, because every phase both generates and consumes requirements. It is the mechanism that feeds new and changed requirements into whichever phase is active. Drawing it in the centre signals that requirements flow in and out of all phases throughout the engagement.

Q: An EA reviewer asks "which ADM phase does your DCAM assessment and roadmap belong to?" How do you answer?
A: A maturity assessment that establishes the baseline and target governance state supports the Preliminary Phase and Phase A (capability and vision), while the resulting roadmap belongs to Phases E and F (Opportunities and Solutions, then Migration Planning). The point of the "Done-when" check is that you can place your existing artifacts in the correct phase without looking it up. Answering crisply signals fluency to the board.

Q: In which ADM phase would your DDD context map most naturally be placed?
A: A context map describing business/data boundaries supports Business Architecture (Phase B) and the Data Architecture portion of Phase C, since it defines language boundaries and which context owns which data. It is a strategic boundary artifact, so it lives upstream of technology. Being able to say this on demand is exactly the "place any artifact in the correct ADM phase" competency the lesson targets.

Q: In which ADM phase would a technology standard (e.g. "Iceberg is our table format") live?
A: A technology standard belongs in the Technology Architecture (Phase D) and is recorded in the architecture repository's Standards Information Base. Standards are reference content the ADM consumes, not a phase deliverable per engagement. Knowing this lets you answer the lesson's challenge to place a technology standard in the right phase instantly.

Q: What is the TOGAF Architecture Repository, conceptually?
A: The Architecture Repository is the structured store where all architecture work products formally live — reference models, standards, the architecture landscape, governance logs, and reusable building blocks. It is what turns scattered diagrams into a governed, discoverable asset. For an architect, "where does this artifact live?" should always have a repository answer, not a personal-folder answer.

Q: What is the "architecture landscape" within the TOGAF repository?
A: The Architecture Landscape is the view of the architectures currently in existence or planned, organized at different levels of detail — typically Strategic, Segment, and Capability. It lets you place a specific design in the wider estate rather than treating it in isolation. It is how a board sees whether your NAV product fits the broader information-systems landscape.

Q: What is a building block in TOGAF, and what are the two main kinds?
A: A building block is a reusable component of capability that can be combined with others to deliver architectures. TOGAF distinguishes Architecture Building Blocks (ABBs — abstract, capability-defining) from Solution Building Blocks (SBBs — concrete implementations that realize ABBs). For data work, "a contracted gold data product" is an ABB; the specific PostgREST-plus-Kong implementation is the SBB.

Q: What is the Standards Information Base (SIB) in the TOGAF repository?
A: The Standards Information Base captures the standards with which new architectures must comply — technology, data, and process standards the organization has adopted. It is the authoritative answer to "what are we allowed to use?". Your build-vs-buy decisions and technology choices register here so future projects inherit them rather than re-litigating each one.

Q: This lesson says to take some of TOGAF "seriously" and hold the rest "lightly". What should you take seriously?
A: Take seriously the deliverable discipline (knowing what each phase produces and producing it well) and the governance hooks (the repository, Architecture Contracts, and conformance review). These are the parts that make your work land and stick in a regulated firm. They are also the parts a board actually checks.

Q: What should you hold lightly about TOGAF, and why is that defensible?
A: Hold lightly the full ceremony — running every phase formally, producing every catalog/matrix/diagram, and treating the framework as a compliance checklist. On a small programme that overhead buys nothing and slows delivery. It is defensible because TOGAF itself is designed to be tailored: the Preliminary Phase exists precisely to scope down the method to what the organization needs.

Q: On a 10-person programme, which TOGAF artifacts would you skip, and how do you justify it?
A: You would skip the heavyweight catalogs/matrices, the elaborate Architecture Vision ceremony, and most formal Transition Architecture documentation, keeping only a lightweight vision, the key data/technology models, ADRs, and a thin governance gate. The justification is proportionality: the cost of producing dozens of formal artifacts exceeds the coordination benefit at that team size. TOGAF's tailoring mandate makes this an on-framework choice, not a violation.

Q: What is the relationship between an ADR log and TOGAF, given you already keep ADRs?
A: ADRs (Architecture Decision Records) are a lightweight, developer-friendly way to satisfy TOGAF's decision-governance intent without its full document set — each ADR captures a decision, context, and consequences. They map naturally to the repository's governance log and to Phase G conformance evidence. This is exactly "hold the ceremony lightly, keep the governance hook" in practice.

Q: What is ArchiMate, and how does it relate to TOGAF?
A: ArchiMate is an open, visual modeling language for enterprise architecture, also maintained by The Open Group, designed to express architectures across business, application, and technology layers. It is complementary to TOGAF: TOGAF is the method (the ADM and repository), ArchiMate is the notation you draw the models in. Together they cover "how we work" and "how we depict it".

Q: Which version of ArchiMate does this lesson target, and what are its core layers?
A: The lesson targets ArchiMate 3.2, whose core comprises three layers: the Business layer, the Application layer, and the Technology layer. These three are the ones you must be fluent in for data work. (The full 3.2 language adds further layers/aspects such as Strategy, Physical, Implementation & Migration, and Motivation, but the core three carry most of a data architecture.)

Q: What does the Business layer of ArchiMate model?
A: The Business layer models the products and services offered to customers, realized by business processes performed by business actors and roles, and the business objects they act on. For a fund administrator it captures capabilities like NAV striking and TA processing, plus business objects such as order, holding, and NAV. It is the "what the business does and means" layer that the technology layers ultimately serve.

Q: What does the Application layer of ArchiMate model?
A: The Application layer models the application components and the application services they expose to support the business — the software systems and their behaviors and interfaces. Here live your data products as application/data services and the components that produce them. Data objects (the logical data) are the Application-layer passive structure elements.

Q: What does the Technology layer of ArchiMate model?
A: The Technology layer models the infrastructure: nodes, devices, system software, networks, technology services, and the artifacts (physical data such as files and database tables) deployed on them. This is where your Kubernetes nodes, object store, and Postgres live as nodes and system software. Artifacts here realize the Application-layer data objects.

Q: ArchiMate splits every layer using "active structure", "behavior", and "passive structure". What do these three mean?
A: Active structure elements are the things that display behavior (e.g. business role, application component, node); behavior elements are what they do (e.g. business process, application function, technology service); passive structure elements are what behavior acts on (e.g. business object, data object, artifact). This active/behavior/passive triad repeats consistently across all three layers. It is the grid that makes ArchiMate learnable once you see it.

Q: What is a "serving" relationship in ArchiMate, with a data example?
A: A serving relationship models that one element provides functionality to (serves) another — for example an application service serving a business process, or a data API serving a distributor's reporting process. It captures dependency in the direction of value provision. In a fund context, "NAV API service serves the distributor onboarding process" is a serving relationship.

Q: What does a "realization" relationship express in ArchiMate?
A: Realization expresses that a more concrete element implements (makes real) a more abstract one — the source realizes the target. Examples: a data object realizes a business object, and an artifact realizes a data object. It always runs from concrete to abstract, which is the direction beginners most often draw backwards.

Q: What does an "assignment" relationship express in ArchiMate?
A: Assignment expresses an allocation of responsibility — an active structure element is assigned to perform a behavior, or a behavior is allocated to a structure. For example, a business role is assigned to a business process, or a node is assigned to (hosts) a system software. It is the "this thing performs / hosts that thing" link.

Q: Name the core "dependency" relationships in ArchiMate beyond serving and realization.
A: Beyond serving and realization, the core relationships include assignment (allocation of responsibility), access (behavior reading/writing a passive object), influence, and the structural composition/aggregation; triggering and flow capture dynamic/behavioral dependencies, and association is the generic catch-all. You do not need all of them daily, but you must recognize serving, realization, assignment, access, composition, and triggering on sight. These are the "core relationship set" the lesson asks you to know.

Q: What is the "access" relationship used for, and why does it matter for data modeling?
A: Access models a behavior element reading from, writing to, or read/writing a passive structure element — e.g. an application function that writes a data object, or a business process that reads a business object. It matters because it is how you show data lineage at the modeling level: which process touches which data, and in which direction. The arrowhead style indicates read, write, or both.

Q: What is the difference between composition and aggregation in ArchiMate?
A: Composition means the parts cannot exist without the whole (a strong "is made of" with exclusive ownership), while aggregation means the whole groups parts that can exist independently. For data, a "Fund NAV product" might compose its mandatory schema parts but aggregate optional reference datasets. The distinction matters when a reviewer asks whether a component is intrinsic to a product or merely grouped under it.

Q: What is the three-element data realization chain this lesson singles out?
A: The chain is business object ↔ data object ↔ artifact: a data object (Application layer) realizes a business object (Business layer), and an artifact (Technology layer) realizes the data object. It is the spine of a data architecture in ArchiMate — the same information expressed at business meaning, logical data, and physical deployment. Drawing it correctly is the lesson's central data-modeling skill.

Q: In ArchiMate, which way does the realization arrow point between a data object and a business object?
A: The realization runs from the data object (concrete, Application layer) to the business object (abstract, Business layer) — the data object realizes the business object. A common beginner error is to point it from business to data. Remember: realization always goes concrete-to-abstract, "the source implements the target".

Q: Give a fund-data example of the business object / data object / artifact chain.
A: The business object "NAV" (the business concept of net asset value per share class) is realized by a data object "nav_fact" (your logical gold model), which is in turn realized by an artifact such as the `nav_fact` Iceberg/Delta table or a Parquet file on object storage. Same NAV, three altitudes: meaning, logical, physical. This is precisely the modeling the lesson's "Do" step requires for products like NAV, order, and holding.

Q: What is a "representation" in ArchiMate, and how does it differ from a data object?
A: A representation is a perceptible carrier of a business object — a document, message, form, or file as seen by a human, such as a printed NAV statement or a published KID. It differs from a data object, which is the machine-oriented logical data inside the application layer. An EMT file delivered to a distributor is closer to a representation/artifact than to the internal data object that produced it.

Q: What ArchiMate element would you use for an EMT or EPT file delivered to distributors?
A: At the technology level an EMT/EPT file is an artifact (a physical data file deployed/transferred), and conceptually it is a representation of the underlying business objects (the fund/share-class data). Modeling it as an artifact that realizes the relevant data object, exchanged via an application or technology service, captures both its physical nature and its meaning. This lets you show the published-language boundary to distributors explicitly.

Q: How does ArchiMate let "realization cross layers" for data, in one sentence?
A: Realization crosses layers because a Technology-layer artifact realizes an Application-layer data object, which in turn realizes a Business-layer business object — stacking three layers into one vertical chain for the same information. This vertical traceability is what lets a reviewer follow a number from the business meaning down to the physical file. It is the core reason ArchiMate suits data architecture.

Q: What is Archi, and why does this lesson use it?
A: Archi is the free, open-source (FOSS) ArchiMate modeling tool, cross-platform (Linux, macOS, Windows) and the de-facto entry point for hands-on ArchiMate 3.2. The lesson uses it because it implements the full language at zero cost, so you can model your whole capstone without a licensed EA suite. Its commercial counterparts are tools like Sparx EA and BiZZdesign.

Q: In Archi, what is the relationship between the model and the views?
A: Archi follows "one model, many views": there is a single underlying repository of elements and relationships, and views are diagrams that project selected elements for a particular purpose. The same element (e.g. the NAV data object) can appear in several views, and editing it once updates it everywhere. This is the key mental model that separates ArchiMate tools from drawing tools like Visio.

Q: Why does "one model, many views" matter compared with drawing diagrams in a tool like Visio?
A: Because in a single-model tool an element is a real object with relationships, not a duplicated picture — so consistency is enforced and a change propagates to every view automatically. In a drawing tool, the "same" box on two diagrams is two unrelated shapes that drift apart. For governance and lineage this difference is decisive: the model is queryable and consistent, not a folder of stale pictures.

Q: What is a viewpoint in ArchiMate/Archi, and how does it relate to a view?
A: A viewpoint is a predefined selection of element and relationship types aimed at a specific stakeholder concern; a view (diagram) is an instance created according to a viewpoint. The viewpoint acts as a lens that restricts what you may draw so the diagram stays audience-appropriate. For example, a "layered" viewpoint shows all three layers, while a "technology" viewpoint hides business detail.

Q: Why are viewpoints described as "audience-specific projections"?
A: Because the same single model can be projected through different viewpoints to show each stakeholder only what they care about — a COO sees business capabilities to data products, a security reviewer sees zones and PII-bearing artifacts. The underlying elements are shared; only the projection changes. This lets you tell different true stories to different audiences without maintaining different models.

Q: The lesson asks for a "COO viewpoint". What should that view contain?
A: A COO viewpoint is a business-application view showing which business capabilities each data product serves — for example NAV calculation, TA, and regulatory reporting capabilities, each linked by serving relationships to the data products that support them. It deliberately omits technology detail the COO does not need. Its purpose is to answer "which parts of the business does each product enable?".

Q: The lesson asks for a "security viewpoint". What should that view show?
A: A security viewpoint is a technology view showing the security zones, the API gateway, and where PII-bearing artifacts physically live — nodes, networks, the Kong/Keycloak boundary, and the storage holding personal data. Its job is to let a security reviewer reason about exposure and data residence. It hides business-capability detail to keep the focus on protection boundaries.

Q: When modeling your capstone in Archi, which ArchiMate elements represent your data products?
A: Data products are typically modeled as Application-layer application/data services (the consumable interface) backed by application components, with the underlying logical data as data objects. The physical tables/files behind them are technology artifacts. This lets you show the product as something consumers are served by, realized down to storage.

Q: How would you model a Kubernetes cluster and object storage in ArchiMate's technology layer?
A: Model the cluster as nodes (with system software for the runtime/orchestrator) and the object storage as a node or technology service, with your tables/files as artifacts deployed on them. Networks and the gateway are technology elements too. The goal is a technology view that a platform engineer recognizes and that the data objects can be traced down onto via realization.

Q: What does it mean that ArchiMate is "layered" but TOGAF is "phased"?
A: ArchiMate organizes content by architectural layer (business/application/technology) — a structural decomposition of what exists. TOGAF organizes work by ADM phase — a process decomposition of how you develop architecture over time. They are orthogonal and complementary: you produce ArchiMate models (layered) as deliverables within TOGAF phases (process).

Q: What is the C4 model, and who created it?
A: The C4 model is a lightweight notation for visualizing software architecture, created by Simon Brown, named for its four levels: Context, Container, Component, and Code. It targets developers and is far simpler than UML or full ArchiMate. The lesson treats it as the developer-facing notation you translate to and from ArchiMate.

Q: Name the four levels of the C4 model and what each shows.
A: System Context shows your system as a box among users and external systems; Container shows the deployable/runnable units inside the system (apps, services, databases); Component shows the major structural pieces inside one container; and Code (optional, rarely drawn) shows classes/functions inside a component. The four are linked by a "zoom-in" relationship — each level opens up the box above it. Most teams stop at Container and Component.

Q: In C4, what precisely is a "container"?
A: A C4 container is a separately runnable or deployable unit that executes code or stores data — e.g. a web application, an API service, a database, a message broker, or a single-page app. It is not a Docker container specifically, though a Docker container can host one. For your NAV product, PostgREST, Kong, the Postgres database, and the Delta-sharing server are each C4 containers.

Q: How does a C4 Container diagram differ from an ArchiMate application view?
A: A C4 container diagram is a developer-oriented, deployment-flavored picture of the runnable units of one system and their interactions, whereas an ArchiMate application view is an enterprise-oriented projection of application services/components and their relationships across the wider estate. C4 zooms into a single system for builders; ArchiMate places systems in the organization for governance. They overlap most at the application/container boundary, which is why the lesson maps between them.

Q: Roughly how does a C4 Container map to ArchiMate elements?
A: A C4 container maps approximately to an ArchiMate application component (for software) or a node/system-software-plus-artifact (for databases and infrastructure-ish containers), with the interactions becoming serving or flow relationships. A C4 database container, for instance, maps to a data object realized by a technology artifact on a node. The mapping is approximate, not one-to-one, which is exactly why you record your mapping rules.

Q: How does a C4 System Context diagram correspond to ArchiMate?
A: A C4 System Context — your system plus external users and systems — corresponds in ArchiMate to a high-level view mixing business actors/roles and application/technology services at the system boundary, often near a layered or cooperation viewpoint. It answers "who and what does this system interact with?". It is the altitude an executive or COO viewpoint also tends to occupy.

Q: How does a C4 Component map to ArchiMate?
A: A C4 component — a grouping of related functionality inside a container — maps roughly to an ArchiMate application component or application function within the owning component. Because C4 components live inside one container, the ArchiMate side stays within the application layer at finer granularity. The mapping again is approximate; you note where the abstractions do not line up cleanly.

Q: Why does the C4 "Code" level usually have no ArchiMate equivalent worth drawing?
A: Because the Code level depicts classes/functions inside a component, which is implementation detail below the altitude ArchiMate addresses; enterprise architecture stops at logical/technology elements, not source code. Most teams skip the Code level even in C4, letting the IDE be the source of truth. So in practice you map Context/Container/Component and let Code go unmodeled.

Q: The lesson asks you to "record the mapping rules you used" when translating ArchiMate to C4. Why is that important?
A: Because the ArchiMate-to-C4 correspondence is approximate and judgment-based (e.g. does a database container become a data object, an artifact, or a node?), so the value is in a consistent, written rule set anyone can re-apply. Without recorded rules, two people translate the same model differently and the diagrams diverge. The rules are the reusable artifact, more than any single translated diagram.

Q: A developer audience and an EA board want the same system explained. Which notation for which, and why?
A: Use C4 for the developer/delivery audience — it is lean, code-adjacent, and shows runnable containers they recognize — and ArchiMate for the EA board, which expects layered enterprise notation that fits the repository and governance views. The skill the lesson certifies is translating any element between the two live. Picking the audience's native notation is what stops your good design from being "translated by someone who can".

Q: What does the lesson mean by "your designs lose governance-board arguments to a well-drawn ArchiMate view of a worse idea"?
A: It means that in EA forums, the proposal expressed fluently in the board's own notation tends to win regardless of underlying merit, because reviewers evaluate what they can read and place in the repository. If your superior design arrives in an unfamiliar form, it gets discounted or re-drawn by someone whose idea then carries the day. Working fluency in TOGAF/ArchiMate is therefore self-defense for good architecture, not box-ticking.

Q: Why is TOGAF/ArchiMate called the "lingua franca" of EU financial-institution EA departments?
A: Because most large EU banks, asset managers, and fund administrators run their enterprise-architecture function on TOGAF method plus ArchiMate notation and an EA repository tool, so it is the shared language governance conversations happen in. An architect who cannot speak it depends on a translator and loses control of how their work is represented. Fluency lets your data architecture land "on its own terms".

Q: What is the danger of treating TOGAF as a rigid waterfall rather than an iterative cycle?
A: Treating the ADM as a one-pass waterfall produces a huge up-front document set that is stale before delivery and resists change, which is the caricature that gives EA a bad name. The ADM is explicitly iterative and feedback-driven (Phase H loops back), and Requirements Management feeds change continuously. The correct posture is to iterate at an appropriate altitude, not to "boil the ocean" once.

Q: What is an Architecture Contract in TOGAF, and where is it used?
A: An Architecture Contract is a formal agreement between architecture/development partners on the deliverables, quality, and conformance expected, used primarily in Phase G Implementation Governance. It is the governance hook that holds delivery to the agreed architecture. For data, it is conceptually adjacent to your ODCS data contracts, though TOGAF's contract is about the architecture engagement, not the dataset.

Q: How does a TOGAF Architecture Contract differ from an ODCS data contract?
A: A TOGAF Architecture Contract governs an architecture/development engagement's conformance and deliverables between architects and implementers, whereas an ODCS (Open Data Contract Standard) data contract specifies a dataset's schema, semantics, quality, SLAs, and ownership for consumers. One governs "did you build to the architecture?"; the other governs "can a consumer rely on this data product?". Confusing them in a review signals a gap in fluency.

Q: What are "architecture principles" in TOGAF, and where are they established?
A: Architecture principles are durable, general rules and guidelines that constrain and inform design decisions (e.g. "buy generic, build core"; "data is a managed asset"), established in the Preliminary Phase and refined in Phase A. They are the value statements every later decision must respect. Good principles end repeated arguments by encoding the answer once.

Q: What is a Transition Architecture in TOGAF?
A: A Transition Architecture is an intermediate, deliverable target state between the baseline and the final target — a "you are here" milestone on the migration path. It lets a long programme deliver value incrementally rather than in one big-bang. It is defined in Phase E and sequenced in Phase F, and it pairs naturally with a strangler-fig migration plan.

Q: How do "baseline" and "target" architectures relate in the ADM?
A: The baseline architecture is the current "as-is" state and the target architecture is the desired "to-be" state; the ADM's job in B/C/D is to define both, and in E/F to plan the journey between them via Transition Architectures. The gap between baseline and target is what drives the work packages. A data architect uses this to frame "current legacy mart" vs "target gold product".

Q: What is gap analysis in the ADM, and in which phases is it used?
A: Gap analysis compares the baseline and target architectures within a domain to identify what is missing, redundant, or to be replaced, and it is performed in Phases B, C, and D for their respective domains. The gaps it surfaces become the building blocks and work packages in Phase E. For data, a gap might be "no contracted NAV product exists yet" — a concrete, plannable item.

Q: What is an ArchiMate "service" and why is it central to layering?
A: A service is an explicitly defined, externally visible unit of functionality that one part of the architecture offers to another (business service, application service, technology service), and it is central because services are the clean seams between layers. The Application layer serves the Business layer via application services; the Technology layer serves the Application layer via technology services. Modeling at the service boundary keeps layers loosely coupled.

Q: Why model "application services" rather than wiring business processes straight to technology in ArchiMate?
A: Because the application service is the contract between what the business needs and how technology delivers it, so business behavior depends on the stable service rather than on volatile implementation detail. This mirrors good engineering: the business process is served by a "NAV calculation service", not coupled to a specific database. It keeps the model resilient to technology change underneath.

Q: What is a business actor versus a business role in ArchiMate?
A: A business actor is an entity (person, organization, or unit) capable of behavior, while a business role is the named responsibility or capacity an actor takes on; an actor is assigned to a role, and the role is assigned to perform a process. For example, a fund-accounting analyst (actor) acts in the "NAV approver" role (role) assigned to the approval process. Separating them lets responsibilities move between people without redrawing processes.

Q: Why does the lesson pair the ArchiMate exercise with your Kimball/data-model work?
A: Because the ArchiMate data objects you draw should correspond to real logical data models (your dimensional/gold models), so the picture is anchored to actual designs rather than decoration. The business object / data object / artifact chain only tells the truth if the data object matches a model and the artifact matches a real table/file. This grounding is what makes the model defensible in a review.

Q: An ArchiMate model has a serving relationship pointing the wrong way. What is the quickest sanity check?
A: Ask "which element provides functionality to which?" — the arrow runs from the provider toward the consumer (the served element), so a NAV API service serves a distributor process, not the reverse. If you have drawn the consumer serving the provider, flip it. Serving direction errors are among the most common ArchiMate mistakes in reviews.

Q: You drew a business object realizing a data object. Why is that wrong, and how do you fix it?
A: It is wrong because realization runs concrete-to-abstract, and the data object (Application layer, logical) is the more concrete element realizing the more abstract business object (Business layer, meaning) — so the arrow must point from data object to business object. Fix it by reversing the realization. Remember the rule "the source implements the target" to avoid this each time.

Q: In Archi, you change an element in one view but a second view still looks "right" — is that a bug?
A: No — that is the "one model, many views" behavior working correctly: the element is a single shared object, so a property change is reflected wherever it appears, while view-specific layout (position, which views include it) is independent. If a rename did not propagate, you likely edited a label-only override or a different element. The fix is to confirm you edited the model element, not a visual duplicate.

Q: How do TOGAF building blocks (ABB/SBB) relate to ArchiMate elements?
A: Architecture Building Blocks (capability-level abstractions) map well to ArchiMate elements at the service/component level, while Solution Building Blocks (concrete implementations) map to specific application components and technology artifacts/nodes. ArchiMate gives you the notation to draw what TOGAF defines as building blocks. The two frameworks are designed to interlock this way.

Q: What ArchiMate viewpoint best fits a "layered" walkthrough for an EA board?
A: The Layered viewpoint, which shows all three core layers stacked with the realization/serving relationships between them, is the natural choice for walking a board top-to-bottom from business capability to physical artifact. It demonstrates vertical traceability — meaning to logical to physical — which is exactly the data story you want to tell. It is the most common "explain the whole thing" view.

Q: For a fund administrator, what might the Business layer of your ArchiMate model contain?
A: It would contain business capabilities/services such as transfer agency, fund accounting, NAV calculation, regulatory reporting, and client reporting, plus business objects like order, holding, NAV, and ISIN-identified instrument. These are the meaning-level anchors that your data objects realize. They line up with the candidate bounded contexts from the DDD lesson (8.4.1).

Q: How would you model a transfer-agency (TA) feed arriving from a legacy system in ArchiMate?
A: Model the legacy TA system as an application component (or node, if you are emphasizing infrastructure) exposing an application service consumed via a serving relationship, with the inbound file as an artifact realizing the relevant data object. If you want to mirror the DDD anti-corruption layer, add a component that mediates and translates between the legacy model and your own. This keeps the legacy model's corruption visible and contained.

Q: How does ArchiMate let you show where PII-bearing data physically lives?
A: By placing the relevant artifacts (the physical files/tables holding personal data) on specific technology nodes within a security/technology view, optionally tagged or grouped by zone, so a reviewer can see exactly which storage holds PII and behind which boundary. The access relationships then show which behaviors read/write that data. This is the substance of the lesson's security viewpoint.

Q: Why is being able to "place any artifact in the correct ADM phase without looking it up" a stated competency?
A: Because in a live EA review you cannot pause to consult a manual; fluency means instantly situating a context map (B/C), a technology standard (D/SIB), or a roadmap (E/F) in the framework, which signals you genuinely operate in TOGAF rather than reciting it. It is the difference between speaking the language and reading from a phrasebook. The lesson lists it as an explicit "Done-when" check.

Q: What is the risk of "mesh-washing"-style relabeling, applied to TOGAF/ArchiMate?
A: The analogous risk is "framework theatre" — producing TOGAF-shaped documents and ArchiMate-shaped diagrams that look compliant but carry no real decisions or governance, the equivalent of folder-renaming. The artifacts exist to make decisions land and stick; if they encode nothing, the ceremony is pure cost. Holding the framework "lightly" is partly a defense against this.

Q: How does the "deliverable discipline" of TOGAF help in a regulated Luxembourg context?
A: A CSSF-supervised firm must show traceability and governance, so producing the right deliverables (vision, data/technology models, conformance evidence, decision records) and storing them in a repository gives auditors and the regulator a defensible paper trail. The discipline turns "we have an architecture" into "here is the documented, governed architecture and the decisions behind it". That is exactly what survives a regulatory review.

Q: How does ArchiMate's vertical traceability help you "trace one EMT figure to source via lineage" (a capstone criterion)?
A: The business object / data object / artifact chain plus access relationships let you walk from the EMT representation up to the business object it conveys and down through the data object to the physical artifact and the processes that wrote it. That vertical-plus-behavioral trace is lineage expressed in the model. It complements your runtime lineage (e.g. OpenMetadata) with an architectural one a board can read.

Q: What is the difference between the "triggering" and "flow" relationships in ArchiMate?
A: Triggering models a temporal/causal sequence between behaviors — one behavior causes the next to start (e.g. "order accepted" triggers "NAV impact recalculation"). Flow models the transfer of something (information, value, goods) from one element to another (e.g. NAV data flowing from the gold mart to the EMT generator). Triggering is about "what happens next"; flow is about "what moves between them", and the two often appear together on a process or data-pipeline view.

Q: What is the difference between a view and a viewpoint, stated as a one-line rule?
A: A viewpoint is the reusable specification of what an audience may see (the lens); a view is a concrete diagram created through that lens (the photo). One viewpoint can produce many views, each drawn for a specific situation. Confusing the two is a common fluency slip in reviews.

Q: Why does the lesson say to model your "entire capstone platform" in one Archi model rather than many diagrams?
A: Because a single model gives you consistency, reuse, and queryability across every view — the COO view, the security view, and the layered view all reference the same NAV data object, so they cannot disagree. Many separate diagrams would drift and contradict each other. The one-model discipline is the architectural-integrity payoff of using an ArchiMate tool at all.

Q: How would you represent your gold "Fund NAV & Flows" data product across all three ArchiMate layers?
A: As a business object/service "NAV & Flows" in the Business layer (meaning and the product offered), a data object plus an application/data service and component in the Application layer (logical data and consumable interface), and artifacts on nodes in the Technology layer (the physical tables/files and where they run). Realization links the layers vertically and serving links the product to its consumers. This is the full vertical the lesson wants demonstrable.

Q: Where does the API gateway (Kong) appear in your ArchiMate model, and in which viewpoint is it prominent?
A: The gateway appears in the Technology layer as a node/system software exposing a technology service (and possibly an application interface for the API), and it is prominent in the security viewpoint because it is the auth/quota boundary in front of the NAV API. It mediates access to the data artifacts. Showing it lets a security reviewer see where token validation and rate limiting sit.

Q: What is the relationship between your DCAM assessment + roadmap and the TOGAF repository/landscape?
A: The DCAM maturity assessment populates the capability/baseline picture of the architecture landscape, and the resulting roadmap registers as Phase E/F migration content; both formally live in the architecture repository as governed artifacts. This is how a data-governance assessment stops being a one-off slide deck and becomes a repository asset. It also lets you place it in the right ADM phase on demand.

Q: In a C4 container diagram of your NAV product, which containers would you expect to see?
A: You would expect the PostgREST (or FastAPI) API service, the Kong gateway, the Postgres/gold database, possibly the Delta-sharing server, and the Superset/Streamlit clients as external or internal containers, with users and the distributor environment as external actors at the context boundary. Each is a separately runnable/deployable unit. This is the developer-facing picture you then translate into ArchiMate.

Q: When translating that C4 container diagram to ArchiMate, what becomes of the Postgres database container?
A: The database container typically becomes an ArchiMate data object (the logical gold data) realized by a technology artifact (the physical database/tables) hosted on a node, with system software for the DBMS — so one C4 box expands into several ArchiMate elements across layers. This expansion is exactly why the mapping is approximate and the rules need recording. It shows ArchiMate's finer layered granularity versus C4's deployable-unit altitude.

Q: Why might you present C4 to a delivery squad and ArchiMate to the same project's steering committee?
A: Because the delivery squad reasons in runnable containers and code (C4's strengths and their daily reality), while the steering committee reasons in business capabilities, governance, and enterprise fit (ArchiMate's strengths and the repository's language). Same architecture, two audiences, two native notations. The fluency to switch live is the lesson's headline skill.

Q: What is the single most important "what I will actually use" takeaway from TOGAF for a data architect?
A: That the durable value is the ADM phase vocabulary (especially B/C deliverables), repository/landscape discipline, and the governance hooks (principles, Architecture Contracts, conformance) — and that you should tailor away the rest. Knowing where your artifacts live and which phase produces them lets you operate inside any TOGAF shop. The full ceremony is optional; the deliverable-and-governance backbone is not.

Q: What is the single most important ArchiMate skill for a data architect, and why?
A: Drawing the business object / data object / artifact realization chain correctly across the three core layers, because it is how a data architecture earns vertical traceability from meaning to physical storage. Get that chain right (concrete realizes abstract, serving runs provider-to-consumer) and the model tells the truth a board can follow. Everything else in the language builds on that triad.

Q: Final synthesis — how do TOGAF, ArchiMate, and C4 divide the work between them?
A: TOGAF is the method and governance frame (the ADM phases, repository, and conformance) that says how architecture is developed and where it lives; ArchiMate is the enterprise notation (three layers, core relationships, viewpoints) you draw the models in for governance audiences; C4 is the lighter developer notation (Context/Container/Component/Code) you map to and from for delivery teams. Working fluency means producing the right TOGAF deliverable, modeled in ArchiMate, and translated to C4 for whoever is in the room — so your data architecture lands on its own terms.

Q: ArchiMate has a `plateau` element — what is it, and how does it map to TOGAF?
A: A `plateau` is an Implementation & Migration layer element representing a relatively stable state of the architecture over a limited period, and it maps directly to TOGAF's baseline, transition, and target architecture states. You model the journey as a sequence of plateaus (baseline → transition 1 → transition n → target), which is how a strangler-fig migration of a legacy TA mart is drawn in ArchiMate. It is the element that gives Phase E/F roadmapping a native ArchiMate representation.

Q: How is the ArchiMate `gap` element used between plateaus, and what relationship connects them?
A: A `gap` element is associated with two plateaus (e.g. baseline and a transition, or two consecutive transitions) and records the differences identified in gap analysis — what must be added, removed, or changed. It is associated to the plateaus via an `association` relationship and is realized by `deliverable` and `work package` elements that close it. This is the ArchiMate-native way to capture the Phase B/C/D gap-analysis output that feeds Phase E work packages.

Q: In ArchiMate, what is the difference between a `work package` and a `deliverable`?
A: A `work package` is a series of actions with a defined start and end designed to achieve a goal (an implementation project), while a `deliverable` is the precisely-defined outcome that the work package produces. A work package realizes a deliverable, and a deliverable can in turn realize a gap or a piece of architecture. In fund terms, "migrate NAV mart to gold product" is a work package whose deliverable is the contracted `nav_fact` product that closes the "no NAV product" gap.

Q: ArchiMate relationships are ranked by "strength" — what is the ordering, and why does it matter?
A: Structural relationships are stronger than dependency relationships, with composition the strongest, then aggregation and assignment (structural), then realization, serving, access, and influence (dependency, in decreasing strength). It matters because the derivation rules use this ranking: when you abstract away an intermediate element, the derived relationship takes the weakest of the two combined. Knowing the order lets you predict what a tool will derive rather than being surprised by it.

Q: State the ArchiMate derived-relationship rule in one sentence, with a data example.
A: If element `a` relates to `b` and `b` relates to `c` via two valid dependency relationships, a derived relationship from `a` to `c` is the weakest of the two. For example, if a NAV application service `serves` a reporting process and the reporting process `accesses` a NAV data object, the derived link between service and data object is the weaker `access`. This is what lets a layered view be abstracted into a high-level COO view without manually redrawing every hop.

Q: What is a `junction` in ArchiMate, and when do you need the OR versus the AND form?
A: A `junction` is a connector that joins multiple relationships of the same type, using an AND junction when all paths apply together and an OR junction when the paths are alternatives. You need OR when, say, an "order accepted" trigger leads to either a "subscription" or a "redemption" process, and AND when a NAV-publish behavior triggers both EMT and EPT generation. Without junctions you would draw ambiguous fan-out that a tool cannot validate.

Q: A reviewer says your layered view is "too busy". Which ArchiMate feature reduces clutter without losing meaning?
A: Use derived relationships plus a more restrictive viewpoint to abstract away intermediate elements — the model still holds the full detail, but the view shows only the abstracted hops the audience cares about. For example, hide the application functions and show only "business capability served by data product" derived serving links. This is the one-model-many-views payoff: you declutter the projection, not the underlying truth.

Q: C4 has supplementary diagrams beyond Context/Container/Component/Code. Name them and their purpose.
A: The three supplementary C4 diagrams are the System Landscape diagram (all systems across the enterprise, above context), the Dynamic diagram (how containers/components collaborate for one use case, like a sequence diagram in C4 shapes), and the Deployment diagram (mapping containers to infrastructure nodes). The Deployment diagram is the one that most resembles an ArchiMate technology view. They let C4 stretch toward landscape and runtime concerns that the four core levels skip.

Q: Which C4 supplementary diagram maps most cleanly onto an ArchiMate technology view, and why?
A: The C4 Deployment diagram, because it maps containers onto deployment nodes (servers, Kubernetes, cloud infrastructure) — the same job ArchiMate's technology layer does with nodes, system software, and artifacts. A C4 deployment node becomes an ArchiMate node, and a container instance running on it becomes an artifact deployed on that node. This is the cleanest of the C4-to-ArchiMate mappings, which is why deployment diagrams translate with the least judgment.

Q: How does a C4 System Landscape diagram relate to the TOGAF architecture landscape?
A: A C4 System Landscape shows all software systems across the organization and their relationships, which corresponds to the Strategic/Segment level of TOGAF's architecture landscape — the wide view of what exists. It is the C4 altitude closest to enterprise architecture rather than single-system design. You would use it to place your NAV system among the TA, fund-accounting, and reporting systems before zooming into any one.

Q: A C4 Dynamic diagram shows a NAV-publish use case. What ArchiMate relationships express the same thing?
A: A C4 Dynamic diagram's numbered collaboration sequence maps to ArchiMate `triggering` relationships (temporal order — striking triggers validation triggers publish) combined with `flow` relationships (the NAV figures moving between behaviors). Together they reproduce the sequence on a behavioral/process view. The difference is C4 numbers the steps explicitly, while ArchiMate uses the triggering chain to imply order.

Q: An EA reviewer asks where your ODCS data contract sits in the TOGAF repository. How do you answer?
A: An ODCS data contract is a Solution Building Block-level artifact (a concrete, contracted data-product specification) that registers in the architecture repository, with its schema/SLA constraints also feeding the Standards Information Base if they become organization-wide rules. The architecture-level intent it serves lives in Phase C Data Architecture, and its conformance is checked in Phase G. So it is repository content realizing a Phase C ABB, governed at Phase G.

Q: What is the difference between an Architecture Definition Document and an Architecture Requirements Specification in TOGAF?
A: The Architecture Definition Document is the deliverable that describes the architecture itself (the models and their rationale across phases B–D), while the Architecture Requirements Specification captures the quantitative/qualitative requirements the architecture must meet. The first is "what the architecture is"; the second is "what it must satisfy", fed continuously by Requirements Management. A reviewer who hears you separate them knows you have read the ADM rather than skimmed it.

Q: What is a "dispensation" in TOGAF governance, and when would a data architect request one?
A: A dispensation is a time-bound, governed exception granted in Phase G when a project cannot or should not conform to the target architecture, recorded so the divergence is visible and revisited rather than hidden. A data architect requests one when, say, a regulatory deadline forces shipping a NAV feed off a legacy mart before the gold product is ready. The dispensation makes the technical debt an explicit, dated decision instead of silent non-conformance.

Q: Compare a TOGAF dispensation with an ArchiMate `gap` — are they the same thing?
A: No: a `gap` is a modeled difference between two plateaus (an analysis result that drives planned work), whereas a dispensation is a governance decision to permit a temporary deviation from the agreed target. A gap says "here is what is missing on the planned path"; a dispensation says "we are knowingly off the path for now, with permission". Conflating them in a review confuses planning artifacts with governance exceptions.

Q: You must show that a NAV figure on a distributor's EMT can be traced to source. Which ArchiMate construct proves it?
A: The vertical realization chain (artifact realizes data object realizes business object) combined with `access` relationships from the behaviors that wrote each step, ending at the EMT artifact as a representation. Walking that chain plus the read/write accesses is architectural lineage a reviewer can follow from the published EMT figure back to the gold table and the process that produced it. It complements runtime lineage from a catalog like OpenMetadata.

Q: An ArchiMate `access` relationship needs to show "read and write". How is each direction notated?
A: An `access` relationship is drawn as a dotted line with an open arrowhead: the arrowhead toward the data object means write, toward the behavior means read, arrowheads at both ends mean read/write, and no arrowhead means unspecified access. So a NAV calculation function that reads holdings and writes the NAV fact shows a read access to the holdings object and a write access to the NAV object. The arrowhead direction is the single detail beginners most often omit.

Q: How would you model a strangler-fig migration of a legacy TA reporting mart in ArchiMate?
A: Model it as a sequence of plateaus: a baseline plateau with the legacy mart as an artifact, one or more transition plateaus where new gold artifacts coexist with shrinking legacy ones, and a target plateau with the legacy mart removed. Gaps between plateaus are realized by work packages that incrementally redirect consumers from legacy to gold. This makes the incremental "strangle" visible as governed, dated transitions rather than a big-bang cutover.

Q: A new joiner draws every data product as an application component. What is the more precise modeling, and why?
A: A data product is better modeled as an application (or data) service — the consumable, externally visible interface — backed by an application component that implements it, with the logical data as a data object and the physical store as an artifact. The service is what consumers depend on; the component is the swappable implementation. Modeling at the service boundary keeps the product stable when you re-platform the component underneath.

Q: Two Archi views show the "NAV data object" but one rename did not propagate. What went wrong?
A: Almost certainly the second view contains a different element that merely shares the label, or you renamed a view-local visual element rather than the model element — in true one-model-many-views behavior a rename always propagates. Check the model tree: if there are two distinct `Data Object` entries named "NAV", you have an accidental duplicate to merge. The fix is to delete the duplicate and re-point its view occurrence at the canonical element.

Q: How do you export an Archi model so another EA tool (Sparx, BiZZdesign) can import it?
A: Export it in the Open Group ArchiMate Model Exchange File Format — an XML format defined by The Open Group specifically for tool-to-tool interchange that preserves elements, relationships, and properties. In Archi this is "Export Model to Open Exchange File", producing a `.xml` other conforming tools can read. Native `.archimate` files are tool-specific, so the exchange format is the answer whenever portability or vendor-independence is the concern.

Q: You need to push your Archi elements into a CMDB or ServiceNow. What is the supported path?
A: Use Archi's CSV export (which produces separate files for elements, relationships, and properties) or jArchi scripting to emit JSON/CSV, then have an external script (Python via `uv run`, or PowerShell) call the target system's API. Archi has no direct CMDB connector, so file-based exchange plus an integration script is the standard pattern. jArchi also lets you automate validation and naming-convention checks before export.

Q: What is jArchi, and give one governance task you would automate with it.
A: jArchi is Archi's JavaScript scripting plugin (built on GraalVM) that gives programmatic access to the model for automation. A useful governance task is enforcing naming conventions and validation — e.g. a script that flags any data object lacking a realizing artifact, or any artifact holding PII not placed behind the gateway node. This turns "model hygiene" from manual review into a repeatable check you run before publishing.

Q: An EA board wants a one-page HTML report from your model. How do you generate it without a commercial tool?
A: Export the model to the ArchiMate Open Exchange Format (XML) and transform it with an XSLT stylesheet to a standalone HTML report, or use Archi's built-in HTML report generator, or a jArchi script that walks the model and writes HTML. All three are free and reproducible. This keeps the repository's content publishable to non-Archi stakeholders without buying a licensed EA suite.

Q: A reviewer claims your ArchiMate `serving` and a UML dependency are interchangeable. Correct them.
A: They are not: ArchiMate `serving` is a specific dependency meaning "provides functionality to", with a defined direction (provider to consumer) and layered semantics, whereas a UML dependency is a generic "depends on" with no service semantics. ArchiMate's relationship set is deliberately constrained and typed so models stay analyzable and derivable. Treating serving as a loose UML arrow loses exactly the typing that makes ArchiMate queryable.

Q: When is ArchiMate the wrong tool and a plain Kimball model or ERD the right one?
A: When the audience needs the actual attribute-level logical/physical data model — column names, keys, grain, conformed dimensions — ArchiMate is the wrong altitude; it models the data object as a single element, not its internal schema. There you reach for a dimensional model or ERD. ArchiMate places the data object in the enterprise and traces its realization; it does not replace the detailed model the data object stands for.

Q: A C4 container diagram has the Kong gateway and PostgREST as separate containers. Should they be one ArchiMate element?
A: No — keep them distinct: Kong maps to a technology node/system software exposing a technology (and application interface) service that enforces auth and quotas, while PostgREST maps to an application component exposing the data service. They have different responsibilities (boundary vs data access), so collapsing them would hide the security boundary the gateway represents. The serving chain runs consumer → Kong service → PostgREST service → data object.

Q: How do you show in ArchiMate that the gold NAV product is consumed by an external distributor system?
A: Model the distributor as a business actor/role or external application component outside your boundary, served by your NAV application/data service via a `serving` relationship, with the EMT/EPT files as artifacts flowing to them. A `flow` relationship can carry the data, and the serving relationship shows the dependency direction. This makes the published-language boundary to the distributor explicit on the view.

Q: An EA review asks "what is your baseline and what is your target for the NAV capability?" Where in ArchiMate do these live?
A: Both are modeled as plateaus in the Implementation & Migration layer: a baseline plateau capturing today's legacy NAV mart and a target plateau capturing the contracted gold product, with the gap between them recording the work. The core-layer elements (data objects, artifacts, services) are referenced from each plateau to show what differs. This directly answers TOGAF's baseline-vs-target framing in native ArchiMate.

Q: What is the cleanest way to model "the same NAV figure at three altitudes" so a board can follow it?
A: Use a layered viewpoint showing the business object "NAV" (meaning), the data object `nav_fact` (logical) realizing it, and the artifact (the physical Iceberg/Delta table) realizing the data object, stacked vertically with realization arrows pointing upward (concrete to abstract). Add the serving link from the application service to the consuming process. The vertical stack is the single most persuasive data-architecture picture for a governance audience.

Q: A junior models "Postgres" as a single ArchiMate element. What is the more correct decomposition?
A: Decompose it into a node (the host/cluster), system software (the PostgreSQL DBMS) assigned to that node, and artifacts (the physical tables/files) deployed on it, with the logical content as a data object realized by those artifacts. One word "Postgres" actually spans node, system software, artifact, and data object across the technology and application layers. Collapsing them loses the realization traceability a data architecture needs.

Q: How do TOGAF's catalogs, matrices, and diagrams differ as artifact types?
A: A catalog is a list of one kind of element (e.g. a data-entity catalog), a matrix shows relationships between two element types (e.g. data-entity to business-function CRUD matrix), and a diagram is a visual rendering of elements and relationships. They are three views of the same repository content at increasing visual richness. On a small programme you skip the heavyweight matrices and keep a thin catalog plus the key diagrams.

Q: A data-entity/business-function CRUD matrix is requested. What does it show and which ADM phase owns it?
A: It maps each data entity against each business function and marks Create/Read/Update/Delete, revealing ownership, redundancy, and which function is the system of record. It is a Phase C (Data Architecture, with Phase B business functions) artifact and is the classic input for deciding data-product boundaries. For a fund admin it shows, for instance, that fund accounting creates NAV while distribution only reads it.

Q: What is the relationship between an ArchiMate `application collaboration` and a `serving` relationship?
A: An `application collaboration` is an aggregate of two or more application components that cooperate to perform collective behavior (an `application interaction`), whereas `serving` is the dependency one element provides to another. You use a collaboration when several components jointly deliver a capability — e.g. the PostgREST component and the gateway component collaborating to deliver the secured NAV API. Collaboration models the joint structure; serving models who consumes the result.

Q: Your gold product depends on a reference-data context you do not own. How do you model that boundary in ArchiMate?
A: Model the external reference-data context as a separate application component/service outside your boundary, served to your product via a `serving` relationship, and if you must translate its model, add a mediating component acting as a DDD anti-corruption layer between them. This keeps the foreign model's semantics from leaking into your data objects. It mirrors the context-map relationship from lesson 8.4.1 in ArchiMate notation.

Q: On a 10-person programme, which single TOGAF governance hook would you keep above all others, and why?
A: Keep Phase G conformance via ADRs and a thin Architecture Contract / dispensation log, because that is the hook that stops delivery silently diverging from the agreed data model — the failure mode that actually hurts. The vision ceremony and full catalogs can go; a record of decisions and a gate cannot. In a CSSF-supervised firm this lightweight governance trail is also what an auditor will ask to see.

Q: How does ArchiMate's `influence` relationship differ from `serving`, and where might you use it?
A: `Influence` is the weakest dependency relationship, modeling that one element affects the achievement of another (often a motivation element like a driver, goal, or requirement) in a positive or negative, possibly weighted way, whereas `serving` is concrete functionality provision. You use influence on a motivation view — e.g. the DORA driver positively influences the "resilient data platform" goal that the security viewpoint realizes. It captures soft, directional effect rather than runtime service.

Q: Where do regulatory drivers like DORA or UCITS fit in an ArchiMate model?
A: They fit in the Motivation aspect as `driver` and `assessment` elements, with `requirement`/`constraint` elements derived from them, linked by `influence` and `realization` to the core architecture that satisfies them. So DORA becomes a driver influencing requirements like "ICT incident reporting" that your technology architecture realizes. Modeling regulators as motivation elements lets a board see which design choices exist because of which regulation.

Q: A reviewer asks you to prove the COO and security viewpoints describe the same system. How does the tool let you?
A: Because both viewpoints are projections of one underlying model, the shared elements (e.g. the NAV data object, the gold artifact) are literally the same objects in both views, so you can select an element in the COO view and have the tool show its other occurrences, including in the security view. Consistency is structural, not promised. In a drawing tool you could only assert the two diagrams agree; in Archi you can demonstrate it.

Q: How would you model rate limiting and token validation at the gateway in ArchiMate?
A: Model them as technology (or application) functions assigned to the gateway node/system software, serving the consuming application services — a "token validation" function and a "rate limiting" function that every NAV API call passes through. They sit in front of the data-access service on the security viewpoint. This shows a security reviewer exactly where authentication and quota enforcement live relative to the PII-bearing artifacts.

Q: What goes wrong if you model the EMT file as a business object instead of an artifact?
A: You lose the physical-deployment and representation semantics: a business object is meaning-level (the fund/share-class concept), whereas the EMT is a concrete file exchanged with distributors, which is an artifact (technology) and conceptually a representation. Modeling it as a business object would break the realization chain and hide where the file physically lives and crosses the boundary. The fix is artifact-realizes-data-object-realizes-business-object, with the EMT as the artifact/representation.

Q: A board confuses your ArchiMate application service with a microservice. How do you clarify the distinction?
A: An ArchiMate application service is an abstract, externally visible unit of functionality (a behavior contract), not a deployable thing — the deployable runtime unit is closer to a C4 container or an ArchiMate artifact on a node. A single microservice might expose several application services, and one application service might be realized across several components. The service is the "what is offered"; the container/artifact is the "what runs".

Q: Which ArchiMate viewpoint would you use to show a regulator the ICT resilience of the NAV platform for DORA?
A: A technology/infrastructure viewpoint augmented with motivation elements — showing the nodes, networks, redundancy, and the DORA driver/requirements they realize — so the regulator sees both the resilient infrastructure and which DORA obligations it satisfies. It is essentially the security viewpoint extended with resilience and motivation. The point is to answer "show us the architecture that meets the regulation" in one governed projection.

Q: How do you decide whether a database belongs as a C4 container or gets pushed to a deployment diagram?
A: In a C4 Container diagram the database is a first-class container (a deployable/stateful unit consumers interact with), shown alongside the API and gateway; in a Deployment diagram it instead appears as a container instance mapped onto an infrastructure node. So it appears in both, at different altitudes: logical structure in the container diagram, runtime placement in the deployment diagram. Knowing which diagram answers the question keeps each one focused.

Q: A migration plan must satisfy T+1 settlement constraints. How would you reflect that constraint in ArchiMate?

This makes "the architecture must support T+1" an explicit, traceable requirement rather than an unstated assumption.

Q: How does ArchiMate's `specialization` relationship help model fund-data variants like share-class-specific NAV?
A: A `specialization` relationship expresses that one element is a more specific kind of another (the source specializes the target), letting you model, for example, a "hedged share-class NAV" data object as a specialization of the generic "NAV" data object. It is the ArchiMate equivalent of inheritance and keeps shared structure defined once on the parent. This is cleaner than copying the NAV object per share class and lets a view show the family hierarchy at a glance.

Q: An EA board wants to see motivation, then capability, then realization in one walkthrough. Which sequence of viewpoints delivers that?
A: Walk a Motivation viewpoint first (drivers like DORA/UCITS, goals, requirements), then a Capability/Strategy viewpoint (the business capabilities such as NAV calculation and TA), then a Layered viewpoint that realizes those capabilities down to data objects and artifacts. Because all three are projections of one model, the requirements named at the start trace through to the physical artifacts at the end. This "why → what → how" sequence is the most persuasive board narrative and showcases ArchiMate's full traceability.
