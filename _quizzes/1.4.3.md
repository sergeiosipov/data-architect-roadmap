# Phase 2 · 1.4.3 Medallion architecture — 150 self-test questions

Q: What is the medallion architecture in one sentence?
A: It is a data-design pattern that organizes data into progressively refined layers — conventionally named bronze, silver, and gold — where each layer increases data quality and consumption-readiness over the one below it. The term was popularized by Databricks, and the colours are a naming convention, not a fixed product feature; the substance is the per-layer contract, not the medal name.

Q: What does the bronze layer guarantee?
A: Bronze holds raw, ingested data in a form that is immutable and replayable — typically a near-verbatim copy of what arrived from the source, with ingestion metadata attached. Its core promise is that you can always re-derive everything downstream by reprocessing bronze, so it acts as the landing zone and the replay buffer of the whole lakehouse.

Q: What does the silver layer guarantee?
A: Silver holds data that is conformed (consistent types, naming, and grain), deduplicated, and has passed data-quality checks. It is the cleaned, integrated, query-trustworthy version of the data — joined across sources and standardized — but it is not yet shaped for any specific consumer or report.

Q: What does the gold layer guarantee?
A: Gold holds consumption-modeled data: aggregates, business-level metrics, dimensional marts, or feature tables shaped for a specific downstream use such as a dashboard, a regulatory file, or a machine-learning model. Its promise is fitness-for-purpose for named consumers, so a gold table is allowed to be denormalized and opinionated in ways silver is not.

Q: Why are bronze, silver, and gold called a "default layering" rather than a law?
A: They are a sensible starting template that fits most lakehouses, because almost every estate needs a raw landing zone, a cleaned/conformed zone, and a consumption zone. But the number of layers should follow the estate's real needs — some justify two layers, some four — so treating three as mandatory is cargo-cult architecture rather than design.

Q: Why should you not query the bronze layer directly for reporting?
A: Bronze is raw and untrusted: it contains duplicates, malformed records, schema drift, and source quirks that no data-quality gate has cleaned. Querying it directly means every consumer re-implements deduplication and validation inconsistently, so two reports off the same bronze table can legitimately disagree — the classic symptom of skipping silver.

Q: Give a concrete fund example of a bronze object.
A: A raw NAV feed file exactly as the fund accounting system or pricing vendor delivered it — for example a daily CSV or fixed-width file of per-share-class NAVs, landed unchanged with metadata like source name, file name, and ingestion timestamp. You keep it verbatim so an auditor can compare the platform's figures back to the exact bytes the source sent.

Q: Give a concrete fund example of a silver object.
A: A conformed NAV table at one row per fund share class per valuation date, with standardized identifiers (ISIN, currency, share-class code), deduplicated re-sends, and DQ checks applied (NAV positive, date valid, ISIN well-formed). It integrates the raw feed into a clean, queryable shape that any internal consumer can trust without re-cleaning.

Q: Give a concrete fund example of a gold object.
A: An EMT-shaped output table — the European MiFID Template fields populated per share class from conformed silver data, ready to export to distributors. It is consumption-modeled: the columns, naming, and granularity match exactly what the EMT file requires, even though that shape is awkward for general querying.

Q: What is "schema-on-read"?
A: Schema-on-read means data is stored in raw form and a schema is applied only when the data is queried, so the store tolerates whatever structure arrives. It favors flexibility and cheap ingestion — you do not reject or reshape data at write time — at the cost of every reader having to interpret and validate the data itself.

Q: What is "schema-on-write"?
A: Schema-on-write means a schema is enforced at the moment data is written, so non-conforming records are rejected or quarantined before they land. It favors trust and consistency — every reader sees a guaranteed structure — at the cost of stricter ingestion and the need to handle source changes explicitly.

Q: Where in the medallion does the schema-on-read to schema-on-write transition happen?
A: At the bronze-to-silver boundary. Bronze is schema-on-read — it tolerates drift and stores what arrives — and silver is schema-on-write — it enforces a contract and stops tolerating drift. That boundary is the single most important contract in the architecture because it is where you decide to stop absorbing chaos and start guaranteeing structure.

Q: Why does bronze deliberately tolerate schema drift?
A: Because the source can change its layout, add columns, or send malformed batches at any time, and a raw landing zone that rejected those changes would drop data you may legally need to retain or replay. Bronze absorbs the chaos so that nothing is ever lost at the door; the cleanup and enforcement are silver's job, not bronze's.

Q: What does "reprocessable" or "replayable" mean for bronze?
A: It means you can re-run all downstream transformations from bronze and reproduce silver and gold deterministically, because bronze is an immutable, complete record of the inputs. This is what lets you fix a bug in silver logic and simply rebuild, rather than going back to the source systems to re-extract.

Q: Why is immutability so important for the bronze layer specifically?
A: Immutability makes bronze a reliable replay buffer and an audit anchor: if records were edited in place, you could never reproduce a past pipeline run or prove what the source actually sent. In a regulated fund platform, that immutable raw copy is often the evidence that answers "what did the vendor deliver on 2026-03-31?".

Q: Which layer is typically the "system of record" for raw inputs?
A: Bronze is the system of record for what was ingested — it is the authoritative, immutable copy of source deliveries inside the platform. Note this is distinct from the upstream source system, which is the system of record for the business fact; bronze is the platform's faithful record of having received it.

Q: Is silver rebuildable from bronze, or is it a source of truth?
A: Silver is normally rebuildable from bronze: given immutable bronze plus the silver transformation code, you can regenerate silver deterministically. The exception is any data created in silver that has no upstream source — manual overrides or master-data enrichments — which must be stored as their own system of record so a rebuild does not erase them.

Q: Is gold rebuildable from silver?
A: Yes, in the normal case gold is fully derived from silver by aggregation and modeling, so it can be dropped and rebuilt at will. Anything in gold that is not derivable — a human-curated label, an analyst's adjustment — is a system of record and must be persisted separately so a rebuild from silver does not lose it.

Q: What is the practical test for "rebuildable vs system of record" at any layer?
A: Ask: if I delete this table and re-run the pipeline from the layer below, do I get it back identically? If yes, it is rebuildable and can be treated as a cache; if no — because it holds inputs that exist nowhere else — it is a system of record and needs its own retention, backup, and lineage discipline.

Q: Why is mislabeling a system-of-record table as "rebuildable" dangerous?
A: Because someone will eventually truncate or reprocess it assuming it can be regenerated, and the unique data it held — manual corrections, externally-keyed enrichment — is gone permanently. The damage is silent until a consumer or auditor asks for a figure that no longer exists, which in a fund context can be a regulatory breach.

Q: What is a "layer contract"?
A: A layer contract is the explicit, written agreement about what a layer guarantees: its schema policy, data-quality gate, retention, owner, and the promise it makes to downstream consumers. Making these contracts explicit standards — instead of tribal folklore — is the architect's core job, because an undocumented boundary is exactly where lineage and trust break.

Q: Name five columns you would put on a one-page layer-contract sheet.
A: Owner (who is accountable), schema policy (drift tolerated vs enforced), DQ gate (which checks must pass to enter), retention (how long data is kept), and consumer promise (the SLA offered downstream). One row per layer makes the boundaries legible and turns folklore into a reviewable standard.

Q: Why is an undocumented layer boundary a lineage risk in a regulated platform?
A: Because when an auditor asks "where did this NAV figure come from?", you must trace it through each transformation, and an undocumented boundary is a gap in that chain where nobody can say what was changed or guaranteed. Lineage breaks at exactly the points where the contract was tribal knowledge rather than a written, enforced standard.

Q: What happens to silver if its contract is left implicit?
A: It degrades into a dumping ground — people write whatever shape is convenient, skip DQ gates, and overload the grain — yet everyone keeps querying it because it is "the cleaned layer." The result is a layer nobody trusts but everybody depends on, which is the worst possible state for a layer that is supposed to be the trusted middle.

Q: Who typically "owns" each medallion layer?
A: Ownership is usually assigned to a team accountable for that layer's contract: ingestion/platform engineers own bronze, the data/analytics-engineering team owns silver's conforming and DQ logic, and the consuming domain or analytics team owns gold's business models. The point is that each layer has a single accountable owner who can be held to its promise.

Q: What is a "consumer promise" or SLA for a layer?
A: It is the commitment a layer makes to whatever reads from it — for example freshness (silver is updated within N hours of ingestion), stability (gold's schema will not change without notice), and quality (every silver row passed its DQ gate). Downstream teams build on these promises, so changing one without warning breaks consumers.

Q: "Who can break what" — what does this phrase capture about layer ownership?
A: It captures that each layer's owner is allowed to change that layer's internals freely but must not violate the contract the layer exposes upward. Bronze owners can change ingestion mechanics but must keep bronze replayable; silver owners can rewrite cleaning logic but must keep the conformed schema and DQ guarantees stable for consumers.

Q: Why is "schema-on-read everywhere" a poor design for a regulated lakehouse?
A: Because if no layer ever enforces structure, every consumer independently interprets and validates raw data, so reports diverge and there is no single trusted shape to audit against. Regulated fund reporting needs a defensible, repeatable figure, which requires a schema-on-write boundary where the platform commits to a contract.

Q: Why is "schema-on-write at the very front door" also usually wrong?
A: Because rejecting non-conforming data at ingestion means a source layout change can drop records you were legally required to retain, and you lose the ability to replay history with corrected logic. Bronze should accept everything; enforcement belongs at bronze-to-silver, where you can quarantine bad rows without having lost them.

Q: When might a two-layer (bronze + gold) design be justified?
A: When the conforming and consumption steps are trivial or collapse into one — for example a small estate with a single clean source where there is little to integrate or dedupe, so a separate silver adds ceremony without value. You still keep an immutable raw layer and a consumption layer; you just skip a middle that would have been a pass-through.

Q: When might a four-layer design be justified?
A: When one of the standard layers does too much and benefits from a split — for example a "raw bronze" plus a "cleansed/typed staging" before conformed silver, or a separate semantic/metrics layer above gold. Estates with many heterogeneous sources or a strict typed-staging step often need the extra layer to keep each contract single-purpose.

Q: What is the danger of blindly applying three layers to every project?
A: You create empty pass-through layers that add latency, storage, and cost without adding guarantees, and you train the team to treat the layer count as the goal instead of the contracts. The number of layers should follow the data quality work that genuinely needs doing, not a slide deck's default of three.

Q: How do you decide the right number of layers for an estate?
A: Identify the distinct contracts the data must satisfy — immutable replay, conformed/validated, consumption-shaped, and any additional one like typed staging or a metrics layer — and create a layer per genuine contract, no more. If two proposed layers would carry the same contract, merge them; if one layer is straining under two contracts, split it.

Q: A late-arriving NAV correction arrives three days after the original. Which layer does it enter first?
A: Bronze first — it is new raw input, so it lands immutably in bronze like any other delivery, tagged with its own ingestion timestamp and (ideally) a business effective date. You never edit the original bronze record; the correction is an additional immutable fact, preserving the full history of what the source sent and when.

Q: How does a late NAV correction propagate from bronze upward?
A: Silver's transformation must recognize the correction (typically by effective date and a supersession rule) and update the conformed NAV for that share class and valuation date, then gold rebuilds the affected outputs such as the EMT file. The key is that the correction flows through the same DQ and conforming logic, not as a manual patch in gold.

Q: Why should you not "fix" a NAV correction by editing gold directly?
A: Because a direct gold edit breaks the rebuildable property — the next reprocessing from silver would overwrite your fix — and it bypasses the DQ gate and lineage, so the corrected figure has no traceable origin. Corrections enter at bronze and propagate through the contracts, keeping gold a pure derivation of the layers below.

Q: What is "conforming" in the context of the silver layer?
A: Conforming means making data from different sources consistent in structure and semantics: aligning column names, data types, units, code lists, and grain so that records from multiple feeds can be joined and compared. For funds, it means one canonical share-class identifier, one currency representation, and one valuation-date convention across all sources.

Q: Why is deduplication a silver responsibility rather than a bronze one?
A: Because bronze must preserve every delivery verbatim — including duplicate re-sends — to remain replayable and auditable, so it cannot drop rows. Silver is where you apply the business rule for what counts as a duplicate and collapse them, producing the single conformed record that consumers should see.

Q: What kinds of data-quality checks belong at the silver gate?
A: Structural and business validity: types and non-null constraints, referential integrity (every ISIN exists in the instrument master), range and plausibility checks (NAV positive, date within the period), and uniqueness on the conformed grain. Records that fail are quarantined or routed to an error table rather than silently passing into trusted silver.

Q: What does it mean to "quarantine" a record at the silver gate?
A: It means routing a record that failed a DQ check into a separate error/dead-letter table instead of admitting it to silver or dropping it. Quarantine preserves the failed record for investigation and reprocessing while keeping silver clean, so the trust guarantee of silver holds without losing the problematic data.

Q: Why does putting DQ gates in silver make lineage answerable?
A: Because the gate is the documented, single place where "this record was validated against these rules" is recorded, so when asked why a figure is trusted you can point to the exact checks it passed. Scattered, ad-hoc validation in many consumers gives you no single answer; a silver gate gives you one auditable checkpoint.

Q: For your contract sheet, which layer owns the rule "NAV must be positive" and why?
A: Silver owns it, because the rule is a conformity/validity check that gates data into the trusted layer, and the contract says only validated records may enter silver. Bronze must still store a negative NAV verbatim if the source sent one, but it is silver's gate that decides such a record does not become part of the trusted set.

Q: Which layer owns the rule "every NAV row must reference a known ISIN"?
A: Silver, as a referential-integrity DQ check against the instrument master. Bronze cannot enforce it without rejecting raw data, and gold consumes already-conformed data, so the natural home for cross-reference validation is the silver gate where conforming and integration happen.

Q: What is the cost of letting each consumer apply its own DQ rules instead of a shared silver gate?
A: You get inconsistent results — two dashboards filtering NAVs differently disagree — duplicated effort, and no single auditable definition of "valid." Centralizing DQ at the silver contract gives every consumer the same trusted base, which is both cheaper and the only defensible posture under regulatory scrutiny.

Q: Why is gold allowed to be denormalized when silver is kept conformed and normalized?
A: Because gold is optimized for consumption — fast dashboards, fixed report shapes, ML features — and denormalization or pre-aggregation serves those query patterns. Silver stays normalized and at fine grain so it can serve many different gold models; pushing the denormalization down into silver would lock it to one consumer's shape.

Q: Can there be multiple gold tables built from the same silver?
A: Yes, and that is the intended design: one conformed silver dataset fans out into many gold products — an EMT file, a board NAV dashboard, a regulatory return, a risk feature table — each shaped for its consumer. This fan-out is why silver must stay consumer-neutral and well-conformed.

Q: What is the relationship between the medallion layers and an ACID table format like Iceberg or Delta?
A: The table format provides the per-table guarantees — atomic commits, snapshots, schema evolution — that make each medallion layer reliable, while medallion is the logical organization across tables. You implement bronze/silver/gold as sets of Iceberg or Delta tables; the format gives transactionality, the medallion gives the layering contracts.

Q: Is the medallion architecture specific to Databricks?
A: No. Databricks coined and popularized the bronze/silver/gold naming, but the underlying idea — raw, cleaned, and consumption layers — predates it and applies on any lakehouse or warehouse stack such as Iceberg + Trino + dbt. The contracts are portable; only the colour names are Databricks branding.

Q: How does dbt typically map onto medallion layers?
A: dbt staging models often correspond to the bronze-to-silver cleaning step, intermediate models to further silver conforming, and mart models to gold consumption shapes. dbt's tests enforce the DQ gates and its documentation and lineage graph make the layer contracts and dependencies explicit, which is exactly what the architecture demands.

Q: What is the risk of skipping the silver layer and building gold straight from bronze?
A: You embed cleaning, dedup, and conforming logic inside each gold model, so that logic is duplicated and diverges across consumers, and there is no shared trusted dataset. The first time two gold outputs disagree on a NAV, you discover the missing single source of conformed truth — which is what silver exists to be.

Q: "Why not query bronze directly?" — answer with consequences, not dogma.
A: Because bronze contains duplicates, malformed rows, schema drift, and source-specific quirks that no gate has cleaned, so a query against it can double-count a re-sent NAV, choke on a changed column, or include a record the source later corrected. The consequence is wrong, non-reproducible numbers that no auditor would accept — not a rule violation, a correctness failure.

Q: How does the medallion architecture support answering an auditor's "where did this NAV come from?"
A: Each layer's explicit contract plus lineage lets you trace the figure from the gold report, back through the silver conforming and DQ checks, to the immutable bronze file the source delivered. Because boundaries are documented standards and bronze is immutable, every hop in that chain is reproducible and evidenced rather than tribal recollection.

Q: What metadata do you typically attach to bronze records on ingestion?
A: Source system identifier, original file name, ingestion timestamp, a batch or load id, and often a hash of the payload for integrity. This metadata is what makes bronze auditable and replayable — you can prove which delivery a record came from and reconstruct exactly which batch fed a downstream rebuild.

Q: What is the difference between "source system of record" and "bronze as system of record"?
A: The source system (e.g. the fund accounting platform) is the system of record for the business fact — it is where the NAV is authoritatively produced. Bronze is the platform's system of record for the ingested copy — the authoritative evidence of what the source delivered to the lakehouse and when. Both can be systems of record for different things.

Q: Why should retention policy differ per layer?
A: Because each layer carries a different obligation: bronze often needs long, possibly regulatory, retention as the immutable evidence trail; silver may be kept long enough to rebuild gold and serve trusted queries; gold may be rebuildable and so kept only as long as consumers need it. Putting retention on the contract sheet forces these decisions to be explicit.

Q: A new pricing vendor adds three columns to its NAV feed. Which layer absorbs the change first and how?
A: Bronze absorbs it first by tolerating the drift — it stores the wider file as-is without breaking, because bronze is schema-on-read. Silver then decides whether to map, ignore, or reject the new columns under its schema-on-write contract, so the change is handled deliberately at the enforcement boundary rather than crashing ingestion.

Q: Why is the bronze-to-silver boundary the place to handle a source schema change?
A: Because bronze deliberately accepted the change without loss, and silver is where the contract is enforced, so the change must be reconciled exactly at that gate — mapping new fields, defaulting missing ones, or quarantining. Handling it anywhere else either drops data (if at ingestion) or scatters the fix across consumers (if in gold).

Q: What does "DQ gate" mean as a property of a layer boundary?
A: It means a set of data-quality assertions that records must satisfy to cross from one layer to the next, typically the bronze-to-silver boundary. The gate is a contract: passing it is what earns a record the trust guarantee of the higher layer, and failing it routes the record to quarantine rather than into trusted data.

Q: How does medallion layering reduce the "everyone reinvents cleaning" problem?
A: By making silver the single place where cleaning, dedup, and conforming happen once, so all consumers build on the same trusted base instead of each re-cleaning bronze differently. The layer contract turns cleaning from scattered, inconsistent code into one owned, tested, documented step.

Q: Why might silver be modeled close to third-normal-form while gold is dimensional?
A: Silver aims to be a flexible, consumer-neutral integration layer, and a normalized model avoids baking in any one consumer's shape, so 3NF or a normalized vault keeps it reusable. Gold serves specific analytics, so a dimensional (star) model optimizes the actual query patterns of dashboards and reports.

Q: What is the relationship between medallion and the staging area concept from classic warehousing?
A: Bronze plays the role of the raw landing/staging area, silver corresponds to the cleansed and integrated core, and gold to the presentation/mart layer. Medallion is essentially the lakehouse-era renaming and re-grounding of the long-standing land-cleanse-present pattern, with explicit ACID-table contracts.

Q: How would you reprocess silver after fixing a deduplication bug?
A: Re-run the silver transformation over the immutable bronze data with the corrected logic, regenerating the conformed tables, then rebuild dependent gold outputs. Because bronze is replayable and silver is rebuildable, no re-extraction from the source is needed — that replay property is precisely why bronze must be immutable.

Q: What can you NOT rebuild by reprocessing, and why does it matter?
A: You cannot rebuild any data that exists only inside the platform — manual corrections, human-assigned classifications, externally-keyed enrichments with no stored source — because there is nothing below to derive it from. Those are systems of record and must be persisted and backed up independently, or a reprocess will silently erase them.

Q: Why is "silver is a dumping ground that nobody trusts but everybody queries" the failure mode to avoid?
A: Because it means the trusted middle layer lost its contract: quality is inconsistent, grain is muddled, and yet consumers still depend on it, so bad data flows everywhere while the team has nowhere clean to point. The fix is an explicit, enforced silver contract — schema policy, DQ gate, owner — restored as a standard.

Q: In a Luxembourg fund-administration context, why is the immutable bronze layer particularly valuable?
A: Because regulated fund administration must be able to evidence the exact data received from sources for NAV production and reporting, often for years, and an immutable bronze copy is that evidence. It underpins audit, supports DORA-style operational-resilience requirements, and lets you reconstruct any past figure from the original deliveries.

Q: How does layering help when DORA or an auditor demands reconstruction of a past NAV calculation?
A: You replay from immutable bronze (the exact source files), through the versioned silver conforming and DQ logic, to the gold output, reproducing the figure deterministically. The explicit per-layer contracts and lineage make each step traceable, turning "we think it came from here" into a reproducible, evidenced chain.

Q: What is wrong with treating gold tables as the system of record for business facts?
A: Gold is a derived, consumption-shaped layer that should be rebuildable from silver, so making it the system of record means a rebuild could erase or alter "authoritative" data and bypass the DQ and lineage of the layers below. Systems of record belong at bronze (ingested copy) or in dedicated SoR tables, not in derived gold.

Q: How do you keep silver "consumer-neutral"?
A: By conforming and normalizing it to a canonical model driven by the data's intrinsic structure, not by any single report's needs, and by pushing report-specific shaping into gold. If a request would denormalize silver to suit one consumer, that work belongs in a gold model so silver remains reusable by all.

Q: What is "schema enforcement" in a lakehouse table format and how does it serve silver?
A: It is the table format's ability to reject writes that violate the table's declared schema, so silver tables can refuse non-conforming rows at write time. This implements silver's schema-on-write contract at the storage level, complementing application-level DQ checks with a hard structural guarantee.

Q: Why might you add a typed "staging" layer between bronze and silver, making four layers?
A: To separate the contract "raw, untyped, drift-tolerant" (bronze) from "parsed and strongly typed but not yet conformed/deduped" (staging), so each layer has a single clear responsibility. This is common when parsing complex source formats is heavy enough to deserve its own owned, testable step before conforming.

Q: Why might you add a separate "metrics" or "semantic" layer above gold?
A: To centralize business metric definitions (a single canonical "total net assets" formula) so every dashboard computes them identically, rather than each gold mart redefining them. When metric consistency across many consumers is a hard requirement, a dedicated semantic layer is the fourth layer that carries that contract.

Q: How would you defend a two-layer design to a skeptic who insists on three?
A: By showing that in this specific estate the conforming step is trivial — one clean source, no integration, minimal dedup — so a separate silver would be a pass-through that adds latency and cost without a distinct contract. The argument is from the actual data-quality work needed, not from rejecting the pattern.

Q: How would you defend a four-layer design against a skeptic who insists three is enough?
A: By identifying a contract that one standard layer is overloaded with — for example heavy typed parsing crammed into bronze-to-silver, or metric definitions scattered across gold marts — and showing that splitting it gives each layer a single, testable responsibility. The fourth layer earns its place by carrying a distinct contract.

Q: What is the role of lineage tooling in a medallion lakehouse?
A: Lineage records how each table and column derives from those below, so you can trace a gold figure back to its bronze source and assess the impact of a change. It operationalizes the layer contracts: lineage is the machine-readable form of "this came from that," which is exactly what audits and impact analysis require.

Q: How do you handle personally identifiable or restricted data across the layers?
A: Typically you minimize or mask sensitive fields as they move up — bronze may hold the raw sensitive copy under tight access controls and retention, while silver and gold expose masked, tokenized, or aggregated forms per the consumer's need-to-know. The layer contract should state the data-protection policy alongside schema and DQ.

Q: Why is "every consumer queries silver directly" sometimes acceptable and sometimes not?
A: It is acceptable when consumers genuinely need fine-grained conformed data and a gold model would just be a thin view — silver is trusted, so reading it is fine. It is not acceptable when consumers need stable, consumption-specific shapes or aggregates, because then they each rebuild gold logic ad hoc, reintroducing inconsistency.

Q: What does it mean that "bronze is the source of truth for ingested data but not for the business fact"?
A: It means bronze authoritatively records what the platform received — the exact bytes and timing — but the authoritative business value (the official NAV) is owned by the upstream source system that produced it. If bronze and source disagree, the source defines the fact and bronze defines what was delivered; both roles are real and distinct.

Q: How should a contract sheet treat a manual NAV override entered by an operator?
A: It must be marked as a system of record, stored in its own override table with its own retention and lineage, and applied at a defined layer (often silver) with provenance recorded. Because it is not derivable from any lower layer, treating it as rebuildable would lose it on the next reprocess — a serious risk in regulated data.

Q: Why does the architect's job include making layer contracts "explicit standards instead of tribal folklore"?
A: Because contracts that live only in people's heads are inconsistent, unenforceable, and invisible to audit, so boundaries drift and lineage breaks at exactly those points. Writing them as standards — owner, schema policy, DQ gate, retention, consumer promise — makes them reviewable, enforceable, and survivable beyond the current team.

Q: A gold EMT file shows a NAV that disagrees with the fund accounting system. What is the first thing to check?
A: Trace the figure down the layers: confirm gold derives correctly from silver, that silver conformed and deduplicated the right bronze record, and that bronze matches the source file. The disagreement is almost always a contract violation at one boundary — a missed dedup, a wrong effective-date rule, or a stale bronze ingest — and the layering tells you where to look.

Q: Why is per-layer ownership essential to enforcing SLAs?
A: Because an SLA — freshness, schema stability, quality — is only meaningful if someone is accountable for meeting it; without a named owner the promise is nobody's job and silently lapses. Assigning each layer a single owner turns its consumer promise from an aspiration into an enforceable commitment.

Q: How does medallion layering interact with idempotent pipelines?
A: Idempotent transformations let you re-run any layer's build over the same input and get the same output without duplication, which is what makes "rebuildable from below" practical and safe. Combined with immutable bronze, idempotency is how you reprocess confidently after a bug fix — re-running is harmless and deterministic.

Q: What is the consequence of putting DQ checks in gold instead of silver?
A: Bad data has already passed through silver and been consumed by other gold products before the gold-level check catches it, so each gold model re-implements and re-runs validation and they can disagree. Gating at silver stops bad data once, at the boundary into the trusted layer, for all consumers simultaneously.

Q: How do you reason about cost when adding layers?
A: Each layer adds storage and compute (rebuilding and persisting another copy) and pipeline latency, so a layer must buy a contract worth that cost. A pass-through layer with no distinct guarantee is pure overhead, which is the cost argument against cargo-culting three layers when two suffice.

Q: Why keep both the original NAV and its later correction in bronze rather than overwriting?
A: Because overwriting would destroy bronze's immutability and the audit trail of what was delivered when, breaking replayability and the evidence an auditor needs. Keeping both — original and correction as distinct immutable facts — lets silver apply the supersession rule while preserving the full history of source behaviour.

Q: What is a "supersession rule" for late-arriving corrections in silver?
A: It is the logic that decides which version of a record is current when multiple deliveries exist for the same key and date — typically the latest by ingestion or by business effective date wins, with the prior version retained or flagged. It lives in silver because it is a conforming decision, and it must be documented as part of silver's contract.

Q: Why is it a problem if two gold reports compute "total net assets" differently from the same silver?
A: Because it means the business-metric definition is not centralized, so each gold model embeds its own formula and they drift. The fix is to define the metric once — in gold's shared logic or a dedicated semantic layer — so the layer contract guarantees one canonical definition for all consumers.

Q: What belongs in the "schema policy" cell of a layer contract?
A: Whether the layer tolerates drift or enforces a fixed schema, and the rule for handling source changes — for bronze, "accept any schema, store as-is"; for silver, "enforce the conformed schema, quarantine non-conforming, evolve only via change control." It makes the schema-on-read versus schema-on-write decision an explicit, reviewable standard.

Q: How would you describe the consumer promise of the gold layer to a downstream BI team?
A: That gold offers stable, documented, consumption-ready tables with a committed freshness and a schema that will not change without notice through change control, and whose figures are derived from validated silver. The BI team can build dashboards on that promise without re-cleaning or re-validating data themselves.

Q: Why does "schema-on-write start at silver" reduce downstream surprises?
A: Because once silver enforces a fixed, validated schema, every consumer reads a guaranteed structure, so a source change cannot ripple unannounced into a dashboard or a regulatory file. Surprises are absorbed and reconciled at the silver gate, which is the whole point of moving enforcement to that boundary.

Q: When is it acceptable for bronze to be the layer a consumer reads, and what is the caveat?
A: Only for narrow, expert use cases like debugging ingestion, reconciling against the source, or one-off forensic queries where the reader explicitly wants the raw, unvalidated truth. The caveat is that such reads must not feed reporting or downstream products, because bronze carries no quality or schema guarantee.

Q: How do you document "what is rebuildable" so reprocessing is safe?
A: On the contract sheet, mark each layer's tables as rebuildable (derivable from the layer below) or system-of-record (unique inputs held nowhere else), and back up only the latter independently. This makes a reprocess a low-risk operation, because the team knows exactly what would be regenerated versus what must never be truncated.

Q: A reprocess of silver silently dropped a set of manual NAV adjustments. What was the design error?
A: The manual adjustments were stored only inside silver and treated as rebuildable, when they were actually a system of record with no upstream source to regenerate them. The error is failing to separate system-of-record data from derived data; those overrides needed their own persisted table that the reprocess reads from, not rebuilds.

Q: Why is layering "the default of every lakehouse you will design" but still requires deliberate contracts?
A: Because the pattern is near-universal — almost every estate needs raw, conformed, and consumption zones — but its value comes entirely from the contracts at each boundary, which are estate-specific and must be designed, not copied. Adopting the layers without designing their contracts gives you the shape of medallion with none of its guarantees.

Q: How does medallion layering help bound the blast radius of a bad source feed?
A: The bad data lands in bronze (contained, immutable) and is stopped or quarantined at the silver DQ gate, so it never reaches trusted silver or any gold consumer. The layering localizes the damage to bronze and the error table, and the gate is the documented place where the bad feed was caught and can be diagnosed.

Q: What is the architect's answer to "let's just dump everything in one big cleaned table"?
A: That collapsing raw, conformed, and consumption into one table erases the contracts — you lose the immutable replay buffer, the single validation gate, and the consumer-neutral integration layer — so cleaning, audit, and reuse all degrade. The layers exist to separate distinct guarantees; merging them trades all of those for apparent simplicity.

Q: How do you decide which DQ rules are "blocking" (quarantine) versus "warning" at the silver gate?
A: Blocking rules guard invariants that would make data untrustworthy or unjoinable — null keys, unknown ISINs, impossible NAVs — and failing them quarantines the record. Warning rules flag suspicious-but-usable conditions (an unusually large day-over-day NAV move) for review without blocking, and the contract should state which class each rule is in.

Q: Why must the layer-contract sheet name a single owner per layer rather than "the data team"?
A: Because diffuse ownership means no individual is accountable when the contract is breached, so SLAs slip and decisions stall. A single named owner per layer is who downstream consumers escalate to and who authorizes contract changes, making the promise enforceable rather than aspirational.

Q: How does the schema-on-read to schema-on-write transition affect how you onboard a new source?
A: You land the new source raw in bronze immediately (schema-on-read accepts it), then do the deliberate work of mapping it into silver's enforced schema and DQ gate before consumers see it. This lets you start capturing data on day one without risk, while controlling exactly when and how it becomes trusted.

Q: Summarize the one-line contract for each of the three classic layers.
A: Bronze: immutable, replayable raw copy of what was ingested, drift tolerated, the platform's record of receipt. Silver: conformed, deduplicated, DQ-validated single trusted dataset, schema enforced, consumer-neutral. Gold: consumption-modeled, denormalized, consumer-specific outputs with stable schemas and committed SLAs, fully derived from silver.

Q: On Delta Lake, how would you reprocess a gold table from a known-good past state of silver after a bad batch?
A: Use Delta time travel to read silver as of the good version with `SELECT * FROM silver_nav VERSION AS OF 123` (or `TIMESTAMP AS OF '2026-03-31'`), or restore the table itself with `RESTORE TABLE silver_nav VERSION AS OF 123`. The version number comes from `DESCRIBE HISTORY silver_nav`, and reprocessing reads the file set that snapshot referenced, so gold can be rebuilt deterministically without re-extracting from the source.

Q: On Apache Iceberg, which procedure rolls a silver table back to a prior snapshot, and how do you call it?
A: Use `CALL system.rollback_to_snapshot('db.silver_nav', 8675309)` (or `rollback_to_timestamp`), where the snapshot id comes from the table's `history`/`snapshots` metadata. It is a metadata-only operation that re-points the current table state at that snapshot, which is why Iceberg-backed medallion layers make safe reprocessing cheap.

Q: Why can aggressive `expire_snapshots` on Iceberg break bronze's audit promise, and how do you reconcile that?
A: `CALL system.expire_snapshots` permanently deletes data files uniquely owned by old snapshots, so a short expiry window destroys the historical states an auditor may need to replay. Reconcile it by setting bronze's snapshot retention to match the regulatory retention on the contract sheet — for example years for raw NAV deliveries — so maintenance never collides with the immutability obligation.

Q: A nightly silver build doubled every NAV row. What is the first thing to check?
A: Check the deduplication/supersession join in the silver transform for a fan-out — a non-unique key on the bronze side (a vendor re-sent the file, or the instrument-master join matched two rows) multiplies output rows. Confirm by counting distinct keys versus row count on the conformed grain; the fix is restoring uniqueness on the share-class-plus-valuation-date key, not deleting the extra bronze deliveries.

Q: How does partitioning a bronze table by ingestion date help reprocessing?
A: Partitioning by ingestion date (e.g. `dt=2026-03-31`) lets a reprocess prune to just the affected batch instead of scanning all history, so re-running silver after a bad feed touches only that partition's files. It also makes idempotent overwrite-by-partition safe, because rewriting one partition cannot disturb other days' immutable data.

Q: Why partition gold by business date rather than ingestion date when bronze is partitioned by ingestion date?
A: Gold serves consumers who query by valuation/business date (a board pack for 2026-03-31), so partitioning on that key prunes their queries efficiently. Bronze partitions on ingestion date because its job is to record arrivals; a late correction for an old business date lands in a new ingestion partition, so the two partitioning schemes intentionally differ by layer.

Q: A consumer complains gold is stale at 09:00 but the silver SLA is "fresh by 07:00." Where do you look first?
A: Check whether the gold build job actually ran and completed after silver landed, and whether its own freshness SLA covers 09:00 — silver meeting its SLA does not imply gold did. The contract sheet should give gold its own freshness promise; a gap between silver-at-07:00 and gold-at-09:00 means the missing or mis-scheduled step is the gold refresh, not silver.

Q: How do you make a silver MERGE idempotent so a re-run does not create duplicates?
A: Key the `MERGE` on the conformed business key (share class plus valuation date) with `WHEN MATCHED THEN UPDATE` and `WHEN NOT MATCHED THEN INSERT`, so re-processing the same bronze batch updates in place rather than appending. The gotcha is a non-unique merge key: if two source rows share the key, the merge errors or non-deterministically picks one, so dedup must happen before the merge.

Q: What is a dead-letter table and where does it sit in the medallion?
A: A dead-letter (or quarantine) table is the error sink at the bronze-to-silver gate that captures records failing a blocking DQ check, with the failure reason attached. It sits beside silver, not inside it, so silver stays trusted while no record is silently dropped — the failed row is retained for investigation and later reprocessing once the issue is fixed.

Q: How would you design reprocessing of quarantined records once a source bug is fixed?
A: Re-run the silver gate over the dead-letter table (or over the original bronze batch, since bronze is immutable and replayable), so corrected records now pass and merge into silver, then rebuild dependent gold. Because bronze still holds the verbatim originals, you never lose the chance to admit a record that was wrongly rejected by an earlier rule.

Q: An auditor asks you to prove a specific gold NAV figure used the vendor file delivered on 2026-03-31. What artefacts do you produce?
A: The bronze record with its ingestion metadata (source id, file name, ingestion timestamp, payload hash, batch id), the silver row showing it passed the documented DQ gate, and the gold derivation, tied together by lineage. The immutable bronze copy plus the hash is the evidence that the exact bytes you replayed match what the vendor sent.

Q: Why store a payload hash on bronze ingestion, and how is it used later?
A: A content hash (e.g. SHA-256 of the raw file) lets you prove the stored bronze copy is byte-identical to what the source delivered and detect silent corruption or tampering. Later, during a replay or audit, you recompute the hash and compare; a mismatch means the evidence chain is broken, which in a regulated context is a control finding.

Q: How does medallion layering interact with GDPR "right to erasure" given bronze immutability?
A: Erasure conflicts with bronze's append-only ideal, so you handle it with a deletion mechanism the table format supports — a `DELETE` on the Delta/Iceberg table that rewrites affected files, or crypto-shredding by destroying the key for tokenized fields. The contract sheet must record this exception, because "immutable" means no logical edits, not an inability to comply with a legal erasure obligation.

Q: Why is crypto-shredding often preferred over physically deleting rows from bronze for PII erasure?
A: Crypto-shredding encrypts sensitive fields per subject and erases by destroying that subject's key, so the immutable file set and its hashes stay intact while the data becomes unrecoverable. Physically rewriting bronze files to delete rows disturbs the audit trail and replay snapshots, whereas key destruction satisfies erasure without touching the layout the auditor relies on.

Q: An EMT gold file is rejected by a distributor for a missing ongoing-charges field. Where in the medallion do you trace the gap?
A: Trace upward: confirm gold mapped the field, then check silver actually conformed and held it, then check bronze received it from the source — the null usually originates where the field was never delivered or never mapped at the silver gate. The layering localizes the fault to a specific boundary rather than leaving you guessing across the whole pipeline.

Q: How does an EET (European ESG Template) gold output differ in source dependency from an EMT output?
A: An EET draws on ESG/sustainability data (SFDR indicators, PAI metrics) that often come from different vendors than the pricing/NAV feeds behind EMT, so its silver inputs span additional conformed sources. The gold contract is the same shape-for-a-named-consumer pattern, but its lineage fans in from more silver tables, which raises the DQ and freshness coordination burden.

Q: Why keep EMT, EET, and EPT as separate gold tables rather than one wide template table?
A: Each template serves a different regulatory consumer with its own fields, cadence, and validation rules (EMT for MiFID costs, EPT for PRIIPS, EET for ESG), so separate gold tables keep each contract single-purpose and independently releasable. Merging them couples unrelated release schedules and forces a change in one template to risk-test all three.

Q: A backfill of two years of gold ran but silver only retains 90 days. What design error does this expose?
A: Gold needed to be rebuilt from silver, but silver's retention was set shorter than the period gold must cover, so the lower layer cannot supply the history — the rebuildable chain is broken. The fix is on the contract sheet: silver's retention must be at least as long as the longest gold rebuild horizon, or the backfill must replay from bronze instead.

Q: How do you decide silver retention versus bronze retention on the contract sheet?
A: Bronze retention is driven by legal/regulatory evidence obligations (often years), while silver retention is driven by the longest window over which you must rebuild gold quickly without replaying all of bronze. Silver can be shorter than bronze if you accept slower bronze replays for old periods; the decision is a cost-versus-rebuild-speed trade made explicitly per layer.

Q: What dbt command runs the DQ tests that enforce a silver gate, and how does it block bad data?
A: `dbt test` (or `dbt build`, which interleaves `run` and `test`) executes the schema and data tests — `unique`, `not_null`, `relationships`, `accepted_values` — defined on the silver models. With tests at `severity: error`, a failure fails the build so downstream gold models do not run, which is how the gate stops bad data propagating.

Q: How do dbt's `severity: error` versus `severity: warn` map onto blocking versus warning DQ rules?
A: `severity: error` makes a failed test fail the run, implementing a blocking rule that quarantines or halts (null key, unknown ISIN), while `severity: warn` logs the failure without failing the build, implementing a warning rule (an unusually large NAV move). Setting severity per test on the silver model is how the contract's blocking-versus-warning classification becomes executable.

Q: Why is `dbt build` preferable to `dbt run` followed separately by `dbt test` for medallion pipelines?
A: `dbt build` runs and tests each model in dependency order, so a failing silver test stops its downstream gold models from building on bad data, whereas `dbt run` then `dbt test` would materialize all of gold first and only discover the failure afterward. Interleaving makes the DQ gate actually gate rather than merely report.

Q: How does `dbt source freshness` support a layer's freshness SLA?
A: `dbt source freshness` checks the max loaded-at timestamp of a source (bronze) against `warn_after`/`error_after` thresholds, so you can detect a stale or missing feed before building silver. It turns the freshness clause of the contract into an automated check that fails the pipeline when bronze did not arrive on time.

Q: A silver `not_null` test on `isin` passes but gold still has null ISINs. What likely happened?
A: A gold model probably introduced the nulls after silver — a left join to a dimension that did not match, or a union with an unconformed source — so the silver guarantee held but gold broke it downstream. Add a `not_null` test on the gold model too; the silver gate only guarantees silver, and joins in gold can reintroduce nulls.

Q: How do you prevent schema drift in a source from silently widening silver?
A: Pin silver to an explicit, declared schema (column list and types) so new bronze columns are ignored unless deliberately mapped, and let the table format's schema enforcement reject unexpected writes. Drift is then surfaced as a controlled decision at the gate — map, default, or quarantine — rather than columns leaking through into the trusted layer unnoticed.

Q: When is `mergeSchema`/schema evolution appropriate in a medallion table, and when is it dangerous?
A: Schema evolution is appropriate on bronze, where the contract is to tolerate drift and capture whatever arrives. It is dangerous on silver, because auto-evolving the trusted schema means a source change silently alters the contract consumers depend on; silver schema changes belong in change control, not in an `mergeSchema` flag.

Q: How would you implement a slowly-changing dimension for share-class attributes across the layers?
A: Land raw attribute snapshots in bronze, build the SCD (type 2 with effective-from/effective-to and a current flag) in silver as part of conforming, and expose the current or as-of view in gold. Silver owns the historization logic because it is a conforming decision, keeping a single canonical history that every gold consumer reads consistently.

Q: Why does an as-of join for point-in-time NAV reporting belong in gold rather than silver?
A: The as-of join (pick the NAV effective on a given report date) is a consumption shaping decision tied to a specific report's date semantics, so it belongs in gold. Silver keeps the full conformed history at fine grain; baking one report's as-of logic into silver would lock the trusted layer to that consumer's view and break neutrality.

Q: How do you size compute differently per medallion layer?
A: Bronze ingestion is I/O-bound and bursty, so it scales with arrival volume; silver is transformation-heavy (joins, dedup, DQ) and is usually the most compute-intensive; gold aggregates are smaller and often cached or incremental. Right-sizing each layer's job separately avoids paying silver-scale compute for a trivial gold refresh.

Q: What is the trade-off between full-refresh and incremental builds of a silver table?
A: Full refresh re-derives the whole table from bronze each run — simplest and always consistent but expensive at scale — while incremental processes only new/changed partitions, which is cheaper but must handle late-arriving corrections and idempotency carefully. The choice is per table: incremental for large append-mostly feeds, full refresh where correctness-on-rebuild matters more than cost.

Q: How do late-arriving NAV corrections complicate an incremental silver build, and how do you handle them?
A: An incremental build keyed only on ingestion date would miss that a correction changes an old business date's conformed row, so you must reprocess the affected business-date partitions, not just the latest. Handle it by deriving the impacted partitions from the correction's effective date and rebuilding those, which is why effective date — not just arrival time — must be captured at bronze.

Q: A gold dashboard double-counts a NAV after a vendor re-sent the same file. Which layer's contract was violated and how do you fix it?
A: Silver's dedup contract was violated — bronze correctly stored both deliveries, but silver failed to collapse the re-send into one conformed row. Fix it by enforcing uniqueness on the conformed grain in silver (dedup on share-class-plus-date, keeping the latest by ingestion), so gold counts each NAV once regardless of re-sends.

Q: Why might you expose silver to consumers as read-only views rather than direct table access?
A: A view layer lets you enforce column-level masking, hide in-flight or quarantined data, and stabilize the consumer-facing shape even if the underlying silver table is refactored. It preserves silver's trust guarantees and access controls without freezing its physical model, which matters when restricted fund data has need-to-know constraints.

Q: How does column-level lineage differ from table-level lineage for impact analysis in a medallion estate?
A: Table-level lineage tells you which tables feed a gold output; column-level lineage tells you which specific source columns flow into a given gold field, so you can assess that changing one bronze column only affects, say, the EMT ongoing-charges field. Column lineage makes impact analysis precise enough to avoid over-broad re-testing when a single field changes.

Q: What is the risk of building gold incrementally from a silver table that gets retroactively corrected?
A: If gold only appended new rows and silver later corrected an old period, gold keeps the stale figure because its incremental logic never revisited that partition. The fix is to make gold reprocess the partitions silver changed (driven by a change-data signal or effective date), so corrections actually propagate instead of being stranded in an old gold partition.

Q: How would you detect that a gold table has silently diverged from silver?
A: Run a reconciliation check — recompute the gold aggregate directly from silver and compare to the materialized gold value, alerting on drift beyond tolerance. Divergence usually means a manual edit to gold or an incremental build that missed a correction; the reconciliation is the control that catches a broken rebuildable property before a consumer does.

Q: Why is "test gold against silver" a weaker control than "gate at silver"?
A: Testing gold only catches errors after bad data already entered the trusted layer and possibly several gold products, so each gold table re-validates and they can disagree. Gating at the silver boundary stops bad data once for all consumers; gold tests are a useful backstop but not a substitute for the single authoritative gate.

Q: How does CDC (change data capture) ingestion fit the bronze contract?
A: CDC streams of inserts/updates/deletes land in bronze as an immutable, append-only log of change events with their LSN/commit timestamp, preserving the full sequence verbatim. Silver then materializes current state by applying the changes in order; bronze keeps the raw change log so you can replay to any point, which is exactly its replay-buffer role.

Q: With CDC, why must silver apply changes in commit order rather than arrival order?
A: Out-of-order application could apply an older update after a newer one and produce a stale current state, so silver orders by the source's commit sequence (LSN or commit timestamp) per key. Bronze preserves enough ordering metadata to do this; getting it wrong yields a conformed row that disagrees with the source's actual latest value.

Q: How do you handle a hard delete in the source under medallion immutability?
A: Capture the delete as a tombstone event in bronze (an immutable record that the source removed the key) rather than physically erasing the prior bronze rows, then have silver mark the conformed record as deleted/inactive. This preserves the audit trail of "it existed, then was deleted" while keeping silver's current state correct.

Q: What is the difference between a "soft delete" flag in silver and physically removing the row?
A: A soft delete sets an `is_deleted`/`valid_to` flag so the record stays queryable for history and audit while being excluded from current-state views, whereas a physical removal loses the fact that it ever existed. In regulated fund data, soft deletes in silver preserve the as-of reconstructability that hard deletes would destroy.

Q: How would you back-pressure or alert when a bronze feed fails to arrive entirely?
A: Use a freshness/heartbeat check (e.g. `dbt source freshness` or an orchestrator sensor) that fails when no file lands by the expected cut-off, alerting the owner before silver builds on missing data. Building silver anyway would silently produce a stale or incomplete trusted dataset, so the contract's freshness clause must trigger an alert rather than a default-to-empty.

Q: Why might you keep a separate "rejects rate" metric per source at the silver gate?
A: A rising quarantine/reject rate is an early signal that a source changed format or quality before any single record visibly breaks reporting, so tracking rejects per source per run gives you a leading indicator. Funds use it operationally: a spike in rejected NAV rows from a vendor triggers investigation before the bad batch reaches a regulatory file.

Q: How do you choose between storing gold as materialized tables versus views?
A: Materialize gold when the aggregation is expensive, consumers need fast or high-concurrency reads, or you need a stable snapshot for a regulatory submission; use views when the transform is cheap and you want gold to always reflect the latest silver without a rebuild step. The trade is compute/storage and freshness-control versus simplicity and always-current results.

Q: Why does a regulatory submission usually want a materialized, versioned gold snapshot rather than a live view?
A: A submission must be reproducible exactly as filed, so you materialize and version the gold table at submission time, freezing the figures even as silver continues to evolve with later corrections. A live view would change underneath you, making it impossible to evidence "this is precisely what we reported on the filing date."

Q: How does the medallion pattern apply when ingesting a streaming feed instead of daily files?
A: Bronze becomes an append-only stream landing (raw events with event-time and ingestion-time), silver applies windowed dedup/conforming and DQ on the stream or in micro-batches, and gold serves aggregates or a serving table. The contracts are identical — immutable raw, conformed trusted, consumption-shaped — only the cadence and the handling of event-time skew change.

Q: What new failure mode does event-time versus processing-time skew introduce at the silver layer?
A: Late events can arrive after a window has been finalized, so silver may under-count or need to re-emit a corrected aggregate, requiring watermarks and allowed-lateness handling. The contract must state how late data is treated (reprocess the window versus drop), because for NAV-type data dropping late corrections would breach the reconstructability promise.

Q: How would you justify collapsing bronze and a typed-staging layer into one for a simple, already-typed Parquet source?
A: If the source already arrives strongly typed and parsed (clean Parquet), the typed-staging contract adds nothing — there is no heavy parsing to isolate — so a separate staging layer would be a pass-through. You keep immutable bronze plus conformed silver; the staging split only earns its place when parsing complex or untyped formats is a distinct, testable burden.

Q: Summarize how a single late-arriving NAV correction exercises every medallion contract end to end.
A: It lands immutably in bronze with its own ingestion timestamp and effective date (bronze's receipt contract), silver applies the supersession and dedup rules and re-passes the DQ gate to update the conformed row for that business date (silver's trusted-conforming contract), and the affected gold outputs such as the EMT file rebuild from silver (gold's derived, SLA-bound contract). At no point is gold edited directly, so the correction is fully traceable and reproducible across all three boundaries.

Q: How does a T+1 settlement cycle pressure the freshness clauses on your medallion contract sheet?
A: Shortening settlement to one day after trade compresses the window in which bronze must ingest, silver must conform and pass DQ, and gold must produce downstream files, so each layer's freshness SLA must be tightened and the build chain shortened. The practical effect is that slow full-refresh silver rebuilds may have to become incremental, and the contract sheet must commit to cut-off times that leave room for same-day correction and reprocessing before the T+1 deadline.
