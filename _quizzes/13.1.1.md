# Phase 8 · 13.1.1 + 13.1.2 Data contracts (ODCS, enforcement) — 150 self-test questions

Q: What is a data contract in one sentence, and what problem does it solve?
A: A data contract is an explicit, negotiated, machine-readable agreement between a data producer and its consumers that specifies the schema, semantics, quality guarantees, and SLAs of a dataset. It solves the problem of implicit, undocumented expectations, where a producer can change a feed and only discover the breakage when a consumer's pipeline fails downstream. For a fund platform it turns "the NAV mart exists" into "the NAV mart guarantees these fields, this precision, and this freshness, owned by this team."

Q: Why is "operationalizes the governance work of Phase 6" a fair description of data contracts?
A: Phase 6 produced governed datasets — catalogued, classified, quality-measured — but governance metadata sitting in a catalog does not stop a producer from breaking a feed. A data contract takes those same guarantees and makes them a versioned, testable, enforced artifact that lives in CI. It is the mechanism that converts "this dataset is governed" into "a consumer can rely on it and a producer cannot silently break it."

Q: Name the components of contract anatomy as one negotiated document.
A: A complete contract covers schema (the fields and types), semantics (what each field actually means), quality guarantees (the checks that must hold), SLAs (freshness, availability, support response), ownership (a named accountable team), and terms of use (licensing, allowed usage, access). The point is that these are negotiated together as one agreement, not assembled piecemeal. Leaving any one out — most commonly semantics or ownership — produces a contract that looks complete but fails the first real dispute.

Q: Why are "semantics" treated as a first-class part of the contract, separate from "schema"?
A: Schema tells you a column is named `nav_per_share` and is a decimal, but semantics tells you it is the dealing-NAV per share in the share-class currency, rounded to the class's published precision, as of the valuation point. Two producers can ship identical schemas with incompatible meanings, so schema alone does not let a consumer rely on the data. In fund data this is acute: a "NAV" field can mean dealing NAV, official NAV, or gross NAV, and only documented semantics resolves which.

Q: In the EU fund-data world, what is the "implicit contract" problem with EMT files?
A: EMT files have flowed between fund manufacturers and distributors for years against a published FinDatEx field specification, but the actual feeds carry undocumented local conventions, optional-field usage, and tolerances that were never written down as an enforced agreement. Because the contract is implicit, a manufacturer can change behaviour and the breakage surfaces only as a failed distributor load or a corrected NAV report. Explicit contracts turn those silent breaks into pre-merge CI failures on the producer side.

Q: What does ODCS stand for and who governs it?
A: ODCS stands for the Open Data Contract Standard, a YAML-based specification for writing data contracts. It is stewarded by Bitol, a project under the Linux Foundation (specifically the LF AI & Data umbrella), which makes it a vendor-neutral open standard rather than one vendor's format. Choosing a governed open standard matters because your contracts outlive any single tool that reads them.

Q: What is the `kind` value at the top of an ODCS document, and why does it exist?
A: The top-level `kind` is `DataContract`. It exists so tools can distinguish an ODCS contract from other YAML documents and so the standard can later introduce other kinds without ambiguity. It mirrors the Kubernetes-style `apiVersion` plus `kind` discriminator pattern that makes machine parsing unambiguous.

Q: What `apiVersion` does current ODCS use, and why version the spec itself?
A: Current ODCS uses an `apiVersion` of `v3.1.0` (the v3 line). Versioning the spec itself lets tooling know which fields and structures to expect, so a parser written for v3 does not misread a v2 contract. It is distinct from the contract's own `version` field, which versions your dataset, not the standard.

Q: List the major top-level blocks of an ODCS contract.
A: The major blocks are fundamentals (id, name, version, status, domain, dataProduct, description, tenant), `schema`, `quality` (often nested under schema), `slaProperties`, `team`, `roles`, `support`, `servers`, `price`, `tags`, and `customProperties`. Together they cover the full anatomy: what the data is, what is guaranteed, who owns it, how to reach them, and where it lives. Optional blocks like `price` and `customProperties` let the contract carry organisation-specific terms without breaking the standard.

Q: What does the ODCS `status` field capture and what are typical values?
A: The `status` field captures the lifecycle state of the contract, with typical values such as `draft`, `active`, `proposed`, `deprecated`, and `retired`. It lets consumers see at a glance whether a contract is safe to depend on or being phased out. A consumer should treat a `deprecated` contract as a signal to plan migration before the announced retirement date.

Q: How is the `schema` block structured in ODCS, including logical vs physical types?
A: The `schema` block lists one or more schema objects (think tables/topics), each with a `physicalName`, `physicalType`, and a list of `properties` (columns). Each property carries a `name`, a `logicalType` (the abstract type like string, integer, date), a `physicalType` (the platform-specific type like `varchar` or `decimal(18,8)`), plus flags like `required`, `primaryKey`, and `classification`. Separating logical from physical type lets the same semantic contract map onto Postgres, BigQuery, or Kafka without rewriting the meaning.

Q: How are quality guarantees expressed in ODCS?
A: Quality is expressed as a list of quality rules attached to a schema or a property, each typically carrying a `dimension` (e.g. completeness, validity, uniqueness, accuracy), a `type`/`method`, a threshold such as `mustBe`, `mustBeGreaterThan`, or `mustBeBetween`, a `severity`, and an optional `schedule`. This lets the contract state machine-checkable guarantees like "completeness of `isin` must be 100%" rather than prose. Tools then execute these rules against live data to prove the guarantee holds.

Q: What goes in the ODCS `slaProperties` block, and how does an SLA property reference a field?
A: `slaProperties` is a list of SLA entries, each with a `property` name (e.g. `frequency`, `latency`, `generalAvailability`), a `value`, a `unit`, and an optional `element` reference pointing at the specific schema field the SLA applies to. The `element` link lets you say, for instance, that the freshness SLA applies to the `nav_date` column specifically. This is how a contract expresses "this mart is refreshed daily and is available within two hours of the valuation point."

Q: What do the `team` and `roles` blocks in ODCS provide?
A: The `team` block names the owning team and its members (each with a username, role, and join/leave dates), establishing the accountable humans. The `roles` block defines named access roles, each with an access level and approver lists such as `firstLevelApprovers` and `secondLevelApprovers`, describing who must approve access grants. Together they answer "who owns this and who can grant access" — the ownership leg of contract anatomy.

Q: What is the purpose of the ODCS `servers` block?
A: The `servers` block lists where the data physically lives, with entries carrying a `type` (e.g. `postgres`, `bigquery`, `s3`, `kafka`, `snowflake`), and connection details like `host`, `port`, `database`, and `schema`. This is what lets a validation tool actually connect and run tests against the live table the contract describes. Without it, a contract is documentation only; with it, the contract is testable.

Q: What is `datacontract-cli` and which specification does it natively support?
A: `datacontract-cli` is an open-source command-line tool (installable via `pip install datacontract-cli`) for authoring, validating, and testing data contracts. It natively supports the Open Data Contract Standard (ODCS), so you can lint ODCS files, connect to data sources, run schema and quality tests, and export to other formats. It is the hands-on enforcement tool that turns a written contract into a CI gate.

Q: What is the default contract filename `datacontract-cli` looks for?
A: The default filename is `datacontract.yaml`, so commands like `datacontract test` will pick it up without an explicit path argument. You can pass a different path as a positional argument, for example `datacontract test nav_mart.odcs.yaml`. A common gotcha after a recent CLI release: the old `--file` flag was removed in favour of the positional argument, so `datacontract test --file ...` now errors.

Q: What does `datacontract lint` do, and what does it not do?
A: `datacontract lint` validates that the contract document is well-formed and conforms to the standard's JSON Schema — correct keys, required fields present, valid value types. It does not connect to any data source or check that real data matches the contract; it is purely a static check of the YAML. Lint catching nothing does not mean your data is correct, only that the contract file itself is structurally valid.

Q: What does `datacontract test` do, and what does it need to run?
A: `datacontract test` executes the contract's schema and quality checks against live data, verifying field presence, data types, missing values, uniqueness, and custom quality rules. To run it needs a `servers` entry (or a `--server` reference) so it can connect to the actual table, plus any credentials supplied via environment variables. It is the command that proves the data conforms, not just the document.

Q: Which data sources can `datacontract test` connect to?
A: It supports a wide range, including PostgreSQL, Snowflake, BigQuery, Databricks, Redshift, MySQL, SQL Server, Oracle, Trino, Athena, Kafka, S3/GCS/Azure object storage, and local files like Parquet, CSV, JSON, and Delta. This breadth lets one contract format gate both a warehouse mart and a Kafka topic. For a lakehouse NAV mart you would typically point it at the Postgres/Trino/Databricks server hosting the gold table.

Q: How does `datacontract-cli` detect breaking changes between two versions of a contract?
A: It compares an old and a new contract and reports the differences; the `breaking` command focuses specifically on changes that would break consumers, and the `changelog` command produces a categorized list (error, warning, info). The `breaking` command exits with a non-zero exit code (1) when it finds breaking differences, printing a message like "found breaking differences between the data contracts." That non-zero exit is exactly what makes it usable as a hard CI gate on a pull request.

Q: Exact answer — what does `datacontract breaking old.odcs.yaml new.odcs.yaml` do on a breaking change?
A: It compares the two contract versions, identifies changes that break consumers (such as removing a field or tightening a type), and exits with code 1 so the CI job fails. The non-zero exit is the mechanism: a green CI requires no breaking diff, so the producer cannot merge a silent break. A compatible change, such as adding a nullable column, produces no breaking diff and the command exits 0.

Q: What does `datacontract export` do and roughly how many formats does it support?
A: `datacontract export` converts a contract into other artifacts — it supports 20-plus targets including SQL DDL, dbt models, Avro, JSON Schema, Protobuf, HTML, Markdown, and BigQuery schema. This lets one ODCS source generate the warehouse DDL and the Kafka Avro schema from a single source of truth. Generating downstream artifacts from the contract, rather than maintaining them by hand, is how you keep enforcement points in sync.

Q: What does `datacontract import` do and why is it the backbone of contract-extracted adoption?
A: `datacontract import` builds a starting contract from an existing source — SQL DDL, a dbt manifest, Avro, BigQuery/Snowflake/Glue/Unity Catalog metadata, or a sample CSV/Parquet file. It is the backbone of contract-extracted adoption because it lets you retro-fit a contract onto an existing feed in minutes instead of writing it from scratch. The imported skeleton still needs human work to add semantics, quality rules, and SLAs, which the source cannot supply.

Q: What does the `datacontract ci` command add over plain `datacontract test`?
A: The `ci` command wraps `test` with CI-friendly output: GitHub Actions annotations, a Markdown summary, and machine-readable JSON, so failures show up inline on a pull request rather than buried in logs. It is meant to be the entrypoint in a GitHub Actions workflow. Using it instead of bare `test` makes the contract result visible to reviewers at the point of review.

Q: What does `datacontract init` do?
A: `datacontract init` scaffolds a new contract file (defaulting to `datacontract.yaml`), optionally from a template, giving you a valid skeleton to fill in. It is the greenfield starting point for contract-first adoption, the mirror image of `import` for the contract-extracted path. Starting from `init` plus a real template avoids structural mistakes that `lint` would otherwise catch later.

Q: Name the four enforcement points emphasised for data contracts in this lesson.
A: The four are producer-side CI schema diff (catching breaks before merge), a schema registry on Kafka (rejecting incompatible producer schemas at registration), a Write-Audit-Publish gate in the lakehouse (blocking bad data before it becomes visible), and dbt model contracts in the warehouse (failing the producer's build on type drift). Each sits at a different layer and catches a different class of failure. A robust platform uses several together rather than relying on one.

Q: At which enforcement point is a breaking schema change caught earliest, and why does earliest matter?
A: The producer-side CI schema diff catches it earliest — before the change ever merges, by running `datacontract breaking` (or `changelog`) on the pull request. Earliest matters because the cost of a break rises sharply the further it travels: a CI failure costs the producer minutes, while the same break discovered as a failed distributor load costs reconciliation, corrections, and reputation. The whole philosophy is "shift the failure left onto the producer."

Q: What does a Kafka schema registry enforce that producer CI does not?
A: The schema registry enforces compatibility at the moment a producer tries to register a new schema version at runtime, rejecting an incompatible schema so the bad producer cannot publish at all. CI catches changes that go through code review, but the registry also catches producers that bypass the contract repo or deploy out of band. It is a runtime backstop on the wire, complementary to the design-time CI check.

Q: What does the Write-Audit-Publish (WAP) gate catch that schema-level enforcement misses?
A: The WAP gate catches bad data values that pass schema validation but violate quality or plausibility rules — for example a NAV that is the right type and present but is negative or moved 40% overnight. Schema checks only see structure; WAP writes to a staging copy, audits the actual values against quality rules, and only publishes if they pass. It is the enforcement point for the "quality guarantees" leg of the contract, not the schema leg.

Q: How do dbt model contracts enforce a contract on the warehouse side?
A: dbt model contracts are turned on with `contract: { enforced: true }` in a model's config, plus a full column list with `name` and `data_type` (and optional `constraints`). At build time dbt runs a preflight check comparing the model's actual output columns and types against the contract, and emits the column types and constraints in the DDL; if the output does not match, the model fails to build. This means a producer who changes a type breaks their own dbt build rather than the consumer's load.

Q: What must you declare for every column once `contract: enforced: true` is set in dbt, and why?
A: You must declare every column the model outputs, each with at least a `name` and a `data_type`. dbt requires the full set because a contract is an exhaustive promise; an undeclared column would be an unstated guarantee, so dbt fails rather than letting a column slip through silently. This forces the producer to consciously acknowledge every field they expose.

Q: Which dbt materializations support model contracts, and which do not?
A: Contracts are fully supported on `table` and `incremental` materializations, and partially on `view` (column names and data types, but not `constraints`). They are not supported on `ephemeral`, `materialized view`, Python models, custom materializations, or BigQuery models using recursive CTEs. A gold NAV mart you want to contract should therefore be materialized as a `table` or `incremental`, not `ephemeral`.

Q: In dbt, which contract constraint is actually enforced by most warehouses, and which are metadata-only?
A: `not_null` is enforced at build time on most platforms because warehouses honour it in DDL, so a null in a `not_null` column fails the build. `primary_key`, `unique`, and `foreign_key` are usually definable but not actively enforced by cloud warehouses — they are emitted as metadata for documentation and query optimisation only. The gotcha is assuming a declared `primary_key` constraint guarantees uniqueness; on Snowflake or BigQuery it does not, so you still need a uniqueness quality test.

Q: What does dbt's contract "preflight check" do, and is column order significant?
A: The preflight check runs the model's `SELECT` and compares the returned column names and data types against the contract before writing anything. It is order-agnostic — the columns must match by name and type, but they need not be in the same order as the contract lists them. If a name or type differs, dbt fails the run with a clear contract-mismatch error, which is the early warning that the model drifted from its promise.

Q: What is contract-first adoption?
A: Contract-first (greenfield) adoption means the contract is negotiated and written before the dataset is built, so the schema, semantics, and guarantees are agreed up front with the consumers. The producer then builds to satisfy a contract that already exists. It wins when you are creating a new data product and have the leverage to bring producers and consumers to the table before any code is written.

Q: What is contract-extracted adoption?
A: Contract-extracted adoption means retro-fitting a contract onto an existing feed by inspecting what the feed currently produces — typically using `datacontract import` against the live schema — and codifying the de-facto behaviour. It wins when you cannot rewrite an entrenched feed and need governance over it now, accepting that you are documenting reality rather than negotiating an ideal. The risk is enshrining existing bad behaviour, so extraction should be followed by deliberate cleanup.

Q: When does contract-first win over contract-extracted, and vice versa?
A: Contract-first wins for new products where you have negotiating leverage and want the cleanest possible interface from day one. Contract-extracted wins for legacy, high-volume, or politically entrenched feeds where rewriting is infeasible and the immediate need is visibility and a stop on silent breaks. A pragmatic platform uses extraction to get coverage fast, then migrates strategic feeds toward first-class contracts over time.

Q: Why can contract-extracted adoption "enshrine bad behaviour," and how do you mitigate it?
A: Because extraction codifies whatever the feed currently does — including loose types, undocumented nulls, and accidental conventions — turning accidents into guaranteed contract terms. You mitigate it by treating the extracted contract as a v0 baseline, then deliberately tightening it: adding semantics, raising quality thresholds, and marking dubious fields deprecated. The contract should converge toward what consumers actually need, not freeze the status quo forever.

Q: How does semantic versioning (semver) apply to data contracts?
A: Semver gives a contract version a MAJOR.MINOR.PATCH number where MAJOR is a breaking change (remove or rename a field, tighten a type), MINOR is a backward-compatible addition (a new nullable column), and PATCH is a non-structural fix (clarified description, corrected quality threshold). Consumers read the version delta to know whether they must act: a MAJOR bump means migrate, a MINOR means they can ignore it safely. This is the same evolution discipline as your 1.9.7 schema-evolution policy, now productized as a consumer-facing promise.

Q: Is adding a new nullable column a major or minor version bump, and why?
A: It is a MINOR bump because it is backward-compatible: existing consumers that do not select the new column are unaffected, and those that do simply gain data. Nothing they relied on was removed or changed. The new column must be nullable (or defaulted) so that historical rows and existing writers are not invalidated.

Q: Is renaming a column a major or minor change, and why is "rename" especially dangerous?
A: Renaming a column is a MAJOR, breaking change because every consumer referencing the old name breaks instantly. Rename is especially dangerous because it often looks cosmetic to the producer yet is indistinguishable, to a consumer, from "old column deleted and a new one added." The safe migration is to add the new column, deprecate the old one across a notice period, and only remove it in a later major version.

Q: What belongs in a deprecation policy for a data contract?
A: A deprecation policy specifies the notice period (how long before removal), the communication channel, the migration path consumers should follow, and the target version/date of removal. It exists so consumers have a guaranteed runway to adapt before a breaking change lands. Without a stated notice period, even a well-versioned break is a surprise; the policy is the promise that there will always be lead time.

Q: What three things must a concrete deprecation notice contain?
A: A usable deprecation notice must contain the version (which contract version introduces or removes the change), the timeline (the deprecation date and the removal/end-of-life date), and the migration path (exactly what the consumer must do to move). For example: "v2.0.0, effective 2026-09-01, removes `nav`; migrate to `nav_per_share` (same value, explicit name); old field removed 2026-12-01." Anything vaguer than this leaves consumers guessing.

Q: How do data contracts relate to your earlier 1.9.7 schema-evolution policy?
A: It is the same policy, now productized and externalised. The 1.9.7 work defined how a schema may evolve internally (compatible vs breaking changes); the contract takes that exact discipline and publishes it as a consumer-facing guarantee with versions, notice periods, and migration paths. The mechanism is unchanged — the audience and the enforcement (CI, registry) are what is new.

Q: Walk through writing an ODCS contract for the gold NAV mart — what must the schema block capture?
A: It must list every gold-NAV field with both logical and physical types and per-field semantics: `isin` as the share-class identifier (string, exactly 12 chars), `nav_per_share` with its exact decimal precision and currency, `nav_date` as the valuation date, plus flags for required and primary-key fields. Each field's semantics should pin down meaning that the type alone cannot — for instance that `nav_per_share` is dealing NAV in class currency at the valuation point. This is the schema-plus-semantics core that distinguishes a contract from a bare DDL.

Q: For the NAV mart contract, how would you express the freshness guarantee?
A: As an `slaProperties` entry — for example a `frequency` of daily and a `latency`/freshness property stating the mart is available within a set number of hours of the valuation point, with the `element` referencing `nav_date`. This makes "the NAV is fresh" a checkable promise rather than an assumption. A monitoring job or `datacontract test` can then assert the freshness SLO is met each day.

Q: How would a NAV-mart contract encode the ISIN field's guarantee?
A: With a quality rule asserting `isin` is required (completeness 100%), unique per share class per valuation date, and valid against the ISIN format — 12 characters, two-letter country prefix, nine alphanumerics, and a check digit. Encoding the format as a validity rule means a malformed ISIN fails `datacontract test` rather than reaching a distributor. The semantics note should also state ISIN identifies the share class, not the fund or sub-fund, which is a common confusion.

Q: How does the EMT-output contract differ in audience from the NAV-mart contract?
A: The NAV-mart contract is internal — its consumers are your own downstream marts and reports. The EMT-output contract treats external distributors as the consumers and the FinDatEx EMT field definitions as the published, shared language of the agreement. That external audience raises the stakes: a break propagates to third parties' loaders, so the contract's semantics must match the FinDatEx specification field-for-field rather than your internal naming.

Q: Why use the FinDatEx field semantics as the "published language" in the EMT contract?
A: Because distributors and manufacturers across the EU already speak FinDatEx EMT, so aligning your contract's semantics to that specification means consumers need no translation. Inventing your own field meanings would force every distributor to map them, reintroducing exactly the ambiguity the contract is meant to remove. Treating FinDatEx as the canonical vocabulary makes your contract interoperable by construction.

Q: How would you wire `datacontract test` into CI for both the NAV and EMT contracts?
A: Add a CI job that, on every merge to the contract or model repo, runs `datacontract test` (or `datacontract ci`) for each contract against the live lakehouse tables, using server entries and credentials from CI secrets. The job must be a required check so a red result blocks the merge. This is what makes "the consumer knows exactly what is guaranteed" demonstrable in CI rather than merely asserted in a wiki.

Q: How do you demonstrate that a breaking change is caught pre-merge?
A: Open a pull request that renames a NAV column (say `nav_per_share` to `nav`), and let the CI run `datacontract breaking` against the previous contract version; it detects the removed field, exits 1, and fails the required check, blocking the merge. The demonstration is the red CI status on the PR, visible to reviewers. This is concrete proof that a producer cannot silently break the contract.

Q: How do you demonstrate that a compatible change passes with a version bump?
A: Open a pull request that adds a new nullable column and bumps the contract version from, say, 1.1.0 to 1.2.0; `datacontract breaking` finds no breaking diff and exits 0, dbt's contract still builds because existing columns are unchanged, and CI goes green. The green status plus the MINOR bump shows the change is additive and safe. Pairing the passing check with the correct semver bump is what makes the evolution legible to consumers.

Q: Why add dbt model contracts in addition to the `datacontract` CI check — aren't they redundant?
A: They catch breaks at different moments and on different artifacts. The `datacontract` CI check compares contract files and tests data; the dbt model contract fails the producer's actual model build the instant the SQL drifts from the declared types, even if the contract file was not touched. So a producer who changes only the model's SQL — not the contract — is still caught by dbt. Layering both closes the gap where one is bypassed.

Q: A producer changes a gold model's column type but forgets to update the contract file — which enforcement point catches it?
A: The dbt model contract catches it: the model's preflight check sees the actual output type no longer matches the declared `data_type` and fails the build. The `datacontract breaking` check might pass because the contract file itself was not changed, so it would not see a diff. This is precisely why dbt model contracts are not redundant — they bind the promise to the running code, not just the document.

Q: A distributor reports a failed EMT load this morning. As the architect, what is the first thing you check?
A: First check whether the change that broke them should have been caught by the contract: did a producer ship a schema or semantic change to the EMT output, and if so, why did CI not block it? Compare the current EMT contract version and the live output against the previous version with `datacontract changelog`. If the break slipped through, the root cause is a gap in enforcement (a missing required check or an out-of-band deploy), not just the immediate data error.

Q: `datacontract test` fails with a connection error but `lint` passed. What does that tell you?
A: It tells you the contract document is structurally valid (lint's job) but the tool could not reach the live data source. The fault is in the `servers` block or the credentials — wrong host/port/database, a missing or expired secret, or network/firewall access from the CI runner. Fix the connection configuration; the contract itself is fine, since lint already validated its structure.

Q: `datacontract test` reports a completeness failure on `isin`. What does that mean and what do you do first?
A: It means the `isin` field has nulls or missing values that violate the contract's completeness rule (it must be 100% populated). First inspect the upstream data to find which rows are missing the ISIN — typically a join that dropped the security master mapping or a new instrument not yet mapped. The contract is doing its job by stopping an EMT/NAV output that would have shipped unidentifiable share classes to distributors.

Q: A teammate set `contract: enforced: true` in dbt but the build fails immediately on a column count mismatch. Why?
A: Because dbt contracts require every output column to be declared; if the model emits a column not listed in the contract's `columns`, or omits one that is listed, the preflight check fails. The fix is to make the declared column list exactly match the model's actual `SELECT` output. This strictness is intentional — a partial declaration would be an incomplete promise.

Q: A producer's dbt build passes locally but the contract's `datacontract test` fails in CI. What is the likely cause?
A: The dbt contract validates structure (column names and types) at build time, but `datacontract test` also validates data values and quality rules against live data. So the model can be structurally correct yet contain values that violate a quality guarantee — a negative NAV, a duplicate ISIN, a stale `nav_date`. The two checks cover schema versus data; a green dbt build does not imply green data tests.

Q: What is the relationship between a WAP gate and the quality rules written in the contract?
A: The contract's quality block defines the rules; the WAP gate is one place those rules get executed before data is published. In Write-Audit-Publish, data is written to a staging location, the contract's quality rules are run as the audit, and only a passing audit triggers the publish to the consumer-visible table. The contract is the spec, the WAP gate is one enforcement of it at write time.

Q: Why is "both demonstrated in CI, not asserted" the bar for the done-when criteria?
A: Because a contract that only lives in documentation gives consumers no real guarantee — a producer can ignore prose. The criterion demands that you actually show, with a passing/failing pipeline, that a consumer's guarantees hold and that a producer's break is blocked. Proof in CI is enforceable; an assertion in a README is not. The whole value proposition collapses if enforcement is theoretical.

Q: Explain where each enforcement point catches something the others miss, in one synthesis.
A: Producer CI schema diff catches design-time breaking changes on reviewed PRs but misses out-of-band deploys and data-value problems. The Kafka schema registry catches incompatible producers at runtime on the wire but does not see warehouse models or value-level quality. The WAP gate catches bad data values that pass schema checks but does not prevent a schema change from merging. dbt model contracts catch type drift in the warehouse model build but only for warehouse models, not streams. Each plugs a different hole, so layering them gives defence in depth.

Q: What are the seven Confluent Schema Registry compatibility types?
A: They are `BACKWARD`, `BACKWARD_TRANSITIVE`, `FORWARD`, `FORWARD_TRANSITIVE`, `FULL`, `FULL_TRANSITIVE`, and `NONE`. They control which schema evolutions the registry will accept when a producer registers a new version. Choosing the right one is itself a contract decision: it encodes your compatibility promise to consumers at the topic level.

Q: What does `BACKWARD` compatibility allow, and what is the default?
A: `BACKWARD` — the default mode — allows the new schema to delete fields and add optional fields, so that consumers using the new schema can still read data produced under the old schema. It protects consumers who upgrade first. The default being `BACKWARD` means out of the box the registry assumes you upgrade consumers before producers.

Q: What does `FORWARD` compatibility allow and who does it protect?
A: `FORWARD` allows adding fields and deleting optional fields, so that consumers still on the old schema can read data written with the new schema. It protects consumers who upgrade last — producers can move ahead while old consumers keep working. The choice between `BACKWARD` and `FORWARD` mirrors whether your consumers or producers upgrade first.

Q: What is the difference between `FULL` and `FULL_TRANSITIVE` compatibility?
A: `FULL` requires the new schema to be both backward- and forward-compatible with the immediately previous version (so only adding or removing optional fields). `FULL_TRANSITIVE` requires that compatibility against all previous versions in the topic's history, not just the latest one. Transitive modes are stricter and protect consumers reading a long backlog of historical messages spanning many schema versions.

Q: What happens when a producer registers an incompatible schema under `BACKWARD` compatibility?
A: The Schema Registry runs the compatibility check before accepting the version and rejects the registration, so the producer cannot publish under the incompatible schema. This is runtime enforcement of the contract on the wire — the equivalent of a failed CI check, but at register time. The producer must either make the change compatible or coordinate a deliberate compatibility-mode change.

Q: Why might you set a Kafka topic to `NONE` compatibility, and what is the risk?
A: `NONE` disables compatibility checks entirely, allowing any schema change to register — sometimes used during a deliberate, coordinated breaking migration where all producers and consumers cut over together. The risk is that you have removed the registry's safety net, so an accidental incompatible change ships silently and breaks consumers. It should be a temporary, intentional state, never a default.

Q: How does a Kafka schema registry's compatibility setting relate to the contract's versioning policy?
A: The compatibility mode is the runtime enforcement of the same compatible-vs-breaking distinction your semver versioning policy describes. A `BACKWARD`-compatible schema change corresponds to a MINOR bump; an incompatible one corresponds to a MAJOR bump that the registry will reject unless you explicitly relax compatibility. The registry mechanically enforces, at register time, the promise your version numbers communicate to consumers.

Q: What is the difference between `datacontract changelog` and `datacontract breaking`?
A: `changelog` produces a full, human-readable list of all differences between two contract versions, categorized as error, warning, and info. `breaking` zeroes in on the consumer-breaking subset and is designed to fail CI — it exits non-zero when breaking differences exist. Use `changelog` to communicate what changed and `breaking` as the hard gate that blocks a merge.

Q: Why would you generate warehouse DDL from the contract with `datacontract export` rather than writing it by hand?
A: Generating DDL from the contract keeps the warehouse table definition and the contract in lockstep, so there is a single source of truth and no manual drift between the documented promise and the deployed table. Hand-written DDL inevitably diverges from the contract over time, reintroducing the implicit-expectation problem. The export turns the contract into the authoritative definition the warehouse is built from.

Q: How can `datacontract import` accelerate contract-extracted adoption of a legacy EMT feed?
A: Point `datacontract import` at the existing EMT table's DDL, a sample Parquet/CSV, or the warehouse catalog, and it emits a skeleton ODCS contract reflecting the current columns and types. You then enrich it with FinDatEx semantics, quality rules, and SLAs that the source could not supply. This gets a legacy feed under contract governance in minutes, after which you tighten it deliberately.

Q: What does "terms of use" in a contract cover, and why does it matter for fund data?
A: Terms of use cover licensing, permitted usage, redistribution rights, and any access restrictions on the dataset. It matters for fund data because some inputs (index data, ratings, certain security-master fields) carry vendor licensing that restricts who may receive derived outputs. Encoding terms in the contract prevents a consumer from unknowingly redistributing licence-restricted data downstream to a distributor.

Q: What is Gable, and where does it sit relative to `datacontract-cli`?
A: Gable is a commercial data-contract platform that adds code-level lineage, contract workflow, and producer-impact analysis on top of the contract concept. Relative to the FOSS `datacontract-cli`, it is the buy option you evaluate at build-vs-buy level — it integrates earlier in the producer's code rather than only testing the output table. The lesson treats `datacontract-cli` as the hands-on tool and Gable as the commercial comparator.

Q: What do DataHub contracts add beyond a plain ODCS file?
A: DataHub contracts attach the contract to a catalog entity and tie it into DataHub's lineage and discovery surface, so the contract is visible alongside the asset and its upstream/downstream lineage. The value commercial/platform contracts add is workflow and integration — proposal, approval, and impact visualization — not a different fundamental concept. You still write essentially the same guarantees; the platform changes where they live and how they are operated.

Q: Why is producer accountability the cultural heart of data contracts, beyond the tooling?
A: Because the entire point is to move the cost of a break from the consumer who suffers it to the producer who causes it. Tooling (CI gates, registries) is how you enforce that, but the cultural shift is producers accepting that their dataset is a product with an SLA they are answerable for. Without that accountability, teams route around the gates and contracts become decorative.

Q: How does a data contract change the answer to "can I rename this column?"
A: Before contracts the answer was a guess about who might be affected; with a contract the architect points to the contract and CI, and the answer becomes "renaming is a breaking MAJOR change, so you publish a deprecation notice, add the new column, keep the old one for the notice period, then remove it in a later version." The contract turns a political negotiation into a defined, enforceable process. The producer cannot just rename and merge.

Q: Why is a "named owner" in a contract more than a formality?
A: Because guarantees without an accountable owner are unenforceable — when the contract is breached, someone must be answerable and reachable to fix it. The named team and support channel are how a consumer escalates and how the platform assigns responsibility for SLA breaches. An "ownerless" contract collapses the first time a guarantee is missed and no one is on the hook.

Q: A consumer wants to "subscribe" to the NAV product knowing exactly what is guaranteed. What does the contract give them?
A: It gives them a single document listing the exact fields and types, the meaning of each field, the quality guarantees that are tested in CI, the freshness/availability SLA, the owning team and support channel, and the versioning and deprecation policy. They can rely on every clause because CI proves it holds and blocks breaks. That is the difference between subscribing to a governed product and merely reading from a table.

Q: How does an immutable, versioned contract history support audit and regulatory review?
A: Because every contract version is stored and tagged, an auditor can see exactly what was guaranteed at any point in time and when each change took effect, with the deprecation notices that accompanied breaking changes. In a regulated Luxembourg fund-admin context this provides a defensible trail showing that data-product changes were controlled, communicated, and approved. The contract becomes evidence of governance, not just an engineering artifact.

Q: For a Luxembourg fund administrator, why are explicit contracts on EMT/EPT outputs a regulatory-adjacent concern?
A: Because EMT and EPT feed downstream regulatory and distribution deliverables (MiFID target-market data, PRIIPs KID inputs), a silent break can propagate into mis-stated investor disclosures or failed distributor onboarding — outcomes a regulator cares about. An explicit, enforced contract gives demonstrable control over those outputs and a clear ownership and change trail. It moves "we think the EMT is right" toward "we can prove what we guaranteed and that we could not break it silently."

Q: Where does `nav_per_share` precision belong in the contract, and why is it load-bearing?
A: It belongs both in the `physicalType` (e.g. `decimal(18,8)` or the share-class's published precision) and in the field semantics describing the rounding convention and currency. It is load-bearing because NAV precision drives dealing prices and investor allocations — truncating or over-rounding changes the money. Pinning precision in the contract means a producer cannot quietly change it and silently alter published prices.

Q: Why is `status: active` versus `status: deprecated` on a contract a signal a consumer must act on?
A: Because `deprecated` declares the contract is on a path to retirement, so a consumer depending on it must plan migration to the successor before the end-of-life date. Treating `deprecated` as "still works, ignore it" leads to a guaranteed future break when the contract is finally retired. The status field is part of the SLA: it promises lead time, but only if consumers read and act on it.

Q: What is the danger of letting schema validation pass while semantics drift, and how do contracts address it?
A: The danger is a "silent semantic break" — the column keeps its name and type but its meaning changes (say `nav` switches from dealing NAV to official NAV), so every check is green while every consumer is now wrong. Contracts address it by documenting semantics explicitly and treating a semantic change as a versioned, communicated change even when the schema is unchanged. The fix is process plus documentation, since no type check can detect a meaning shift.

Q: How would you set the Kafka compatibility mode for an EMT-event topic consumed by many distributors, and why?
A: Use `BACKWARD` (or `BACKWARD_TRANSITIVE` if distributors may replay historical messages), so you can add optional fields and remove deprecated ones while every distributor's existing consumer keeps reading. Distributors upgrade on their own schedules, so you must not require them to move in lockstep with your producers. The transitive variant is safer when consumers read a long backlog spanning several schema versions.

Q: Why test contracts against the live lakehouse tables rather than only against a sample?
A: Because the guarantee a consumer relies on is about the real production data, not a curated sample — value-level rules like completeness, uniqueness, and freshness can only be proven against the live table. A sample might pass while production has nulls, duplicates, or stale partitions. Testing live in CI is what makes the consumer's reliance justified rather than aspirational.

Q: What does it mean that `primaryKey: true` in an ODCS property does not, by itself, guarantee uniqueness in the warehouse?
A: It declares the intended key for documentation and tooling, but most cloud warehouses do not enforce primary-key uniqueness at the storage level. So a declared `primaryKey` can still contain duplicates unless you back it with an actual uniqueness quality rule that `datacontract test` (or dbt) executes. The gotcha is trusting the declaration; the enforcement must be an explicit test.

Q: How do contract quality `severity` levels affect CI behaviour?
A: Severity (e.g. error vs warning) determines whether a failing quality rule should block the pipeline or merely flag it. An `error`-severity completeness failure on `isin` should fail the run and stop publication; a `warning`-severity rule might log and continue. Setting severity deliberately lets you make critical guarantees hard gates while keeping advisory checks non-blocking.

Q: Why might you keep a deprecated column in the schema during the notice period rather than removing it immediately?
A: Because removing it immediately is itself the breaking change you are giving notice about; keeping it lets existing consumers keep working while they migrate to the replacement. During the period both the old and new columns coexist, the old one marked deprecated, so no consumer breaks before the announced removal date. Removing on day one would defeat the purpose of the notice period entirely.

Q: How does a contract make a "compatible change" safe even though the dataset changed?
A: A compatible change (a new nullable column, a clarified description, a relaxed quality threshold) does not alter or remove anything an existing consumer relied on, so their pipelines keep working unchanged. The contract proves this by the absence of a breaking diff and a MINOR version bump, which signals to consumers that no action is required. Safety here means "nothing a consumer depended on was touched," verified mechanically.

Q: In a contract-first negotiation for a new fund-data product, who are the parties at the table and what do they each bring?
A: The producer team brings what the source systems can actually deliver and at what frequency; the consumers (downstream marts, reporting, distributors) bring the fields, semantics, precision, and freshness they need; and the architect brokers a contract both can sustain. The negotiation surfaces conflicts — a consumer wanting intraday NAV the producer can only deliver daily — before any code is written. Resolving those up front is the entire advantage of contract-first.

Q: Why is the `support` block (contact/communication channel) in ODCS operationally important?
A: Because when an SLA is breached or a consumer hits an ambiguity, they need a defined channel to reach the owner — a Slack channel, email, or ticket queue named in the contract. Without it, escalation is ad hoc and slow, which undermines the SLA promise. The `support` block operationalises ownership: it is how the named owner is actually reached.

Q: How would you handle a contract change that is compatible at the schema level but tightens a quality rule (e.g. completeness 99% to 100%)?
A: Tightening a quality guarantee is potentially breaking for the producer even if the schema is unchanged, because data that previously passed may now fail and block publication. Treat it as at least a MINOR change with a heads-up to the producer, and ideally a transition window where the stricter rule runs as a warning before becoming an error. The contract's job is to make the producer aware before the stricter gate starts failing their builds.

Q: What is the ODCS `authoritativeDefinitions` field for, and give a fund-data example?
A: `authoritativeDefinitions` links a schema, property, or whole contract to an external source of truth — a URL pointing at the canonical definition, business glossary entry, or standard. For a fund-data contract you would point the `isin` property's `authoritativeDefinitions` at the ISO 6166 standard, or an EMT field at the FinDatEx specification page. This anchors the contract's semantics to an authority instead of an internal paraphrase, so disputes about meaning resolve against the published standard.

Q: What do the ODCS `domain` and `dataProduct` fundamental fields express?
A: `domain` names the business domain the contract belongs to (e.g. `fund-accounting`), and `dataProduct` names the specific data product the contract describes (e.g. `nav-gold-mart`). They place the contract within a data-mesh-style ownership and discovery structure so consumers can find products by domain. They are organisational metadata, not enforcement, but they make a large contract estate navigable.

Q: How would you use `customProperties` in an ODCS contract for a regulated fund context?
A: `customProperties` is a free-form key-value block for organisation-specific terms the standard does not model natively — for example a data-classification tag, a DORA-relevant criticality rating, a retention period, or an internal cost-centre. It lets you carry regulatory and operational metadata inside the same contract without forking the standard. The trade-off is that `customProperties` are not enforced by generic tooling, so any checks on them are yours to build.

Q: A transfer agency consumes your share-class register feed. How does a contract reduce reconciliation breaks there?
A: The contract pins the register feed's keys (ISIN per share class, investor/account identifiers), the meaning of holding and units fields, and the freshness SLA, all tested in CI against the live table. Because a producer cannot silently change the key or units semantics without failing the breaking check, the transfer agency's nightly reconciliation stops inheriting surprise changes. Reconciliation breaks then point to genuine data differences rather than undocumented feed changes.

Q: How does a contract handle identifier fields like LEI or ISIN that must conform to an external standard?
A: The field carries a validity quality rule encoding the standard's format — ISIN as 12 chars with country prefix and check digit, LEI as a 20-character ISO 17442 code with its own check digits — plus an `authoritativeDefinitions` link to the standard. `datacontract test` then fails any value that does not conform, so a malformed LEI never reaches a regulatory output. Encoding the external standard as a checkable rule is how the contract enforces conformance rather than merely documenting it.

Q: Summarise the end-to-end flow from "producer wants to rename a NAV column" to a safe outcome under contracts.
A: The producer opens a PR with the rename; `datacontract breaking` detects a removed field and fails CI (exit 1), blocking the merge. The producer instead adds the new column, keeps the old one, bumps the version, and issues a deprecation notice with timeline and migration path; the additive PR passes CI and dbt builds cleanly. After the notice period elapses, a follow-up MAJOR-version PR removes the old column, and consumers who migrated are unaffected. At every step the contract, not goodwill, is what prevents a silent break.

Q: What are the four `type` values an ODCS v3 quality rule can take, and how do they differ?
A: The four are `text` (human-readable prose describing the guarantee, not yet executable), `library` (a predefined metric like `nullValues` or `rowCount` compared with an operator, no SQL needed), `sql` (your own query returning a number or boolean for comparison), and `custom` (a tool-native block handed to an engine like Soda or Great Expectations). `library` is the everyday workhorse because it needs no SQL; `sql` covers checks the library cannot express; `custom` lets you reuse existing Soda/GE rules verbatim. A NAV-mart contract typically mixes `library` for completeness/uniqueness and `sql` for cross-field plausibility.

Q: Write a `library`-type ODCS quality rule that asserts the `isin` column has zero nulls.
A: It is a property-level rule on `isin` with `type: library`, `rule: nullValues`, and `mustBe: 0` (optionally `unit: rows`), meaning the count of null values must equal zero. The `library` rule names a built-in metric so the tool computes the count and applies the operator without you writing SQL. The equivalent percentage form uses `unit: percent` so you can say nulls `mustBe: 0` as a fraction of rows instead of an absolute count.

Q: In an ODCS `sql`-type quality rule, what do the `{object}` and `{property}` placeholders do?
A: Inside the `query` string, `{object}` is substituted with the schema object's physical table name and `{property}` with the property's physical column name before the SQL is sent to the server, so one rule template is portable across renames and environments. For example `SELECT COUNT(*) FROM {object} WHERE {property} < 0` checks for negative NAV without hard-coding the table. This keeps the rule decoupled from physical naming, which matters when the same contract maps to dev and prod schemas.

Q: Which ODCS `library` metrics exist at the property level versus the schema level?
A: Property-level metrics include `nullValues`, `missingValues`, `invalidValues`, and `duplicateValues`, each evaluated against one column; schema-level metrics include `rowCount` and `duplicateValues` evaluated across the whole object. So "the table has at least one row" is a schema-level `rowCount` rule with `mustBeGreaterThan: 0`, while "`isin` has no nulls" is a property-level `nullValues` rule. Knowing the level matters because attaching a `rowCount` rule under a property is a structural error `datacontract lint` will flag.

Q: List the comparison operators available on an ODCS `library` or `sql` quality rule.
A: They are `mustBe` (=), `mustNotBe` (not equal), `mustBeGreaterThan` (>), `mustBeGreaterOrEqualTo` (>=), `mustBeLessThan` (<), `mustBeLessOrEqualTo` (<=), `mustBeBetween` (inclusive range, takes a two-element list), and `mustNotBeBetween` (outside a range). You pick exactly one operator per rule and give it the threshold value or list. A freshness-style check might use `mustBeBetween: [0, 24]` on an "hours since valuation" SQL metric to bound staleness on both sides.

Q: For a `custom`-type ODCS quality rule, what do the `engine` and `implementation` fields carry?
A: `engine` names the external quality tool — values such as `soda`, `greatExpectations`, or `montecarlo` — and `implementation` carries an opaque block of that tool's native rule code, which ODCS does not parse but passes through to the engine at test time. This lets a team reuse an existing SodaCL or Great Expectations suite inside the contract instead of rewriting it as library rules. The trade-off is portability: a `custom` rule only runs where that specific engine is available, unlike `library` rules which any compliant tool can execute.

Q: What is the `dimension` field on a quality rule for, and what values does ODCS suggest?
A: `dimension` tags the rule with the data-quality category it measures — ODCS lists `accuracy`, `completeness`, `conformity`, `consistency`, `coverage`, `timeliness`, and `uniqueness` — so a reader can see at a glance what aspect each check defends. It is documentation/reporting metadata, not something that changes how the rule executes. Tagging a `nullValues` rule as `completeness` and an ISIN-format rule as `conformity` lets you roll up a contract's coverage by dimension in a quality report.

Q: What is the difference between the `nullValues` and `missingValues` library metrics in ODCS?
A: `nullValues` counts SQL `NULL` occurrences in a column, whereas `missingValues` counts values that match a configured set of "treated as missing" tokens (such as empty string, `N/A`, or `-`) in addition to nulls. The distinction matters for feeds where absence is encoded as a sentinel rather than a true null — a NAV file might write `0.00` or blank for an unpriced class, which `nullValues` would miss but `missingValues` catches. Choosing the right metric prevents a column that is "100% non-null but full of placeholders" from passing a completeness check.

Q: Your `datacontract test` quality check needs a tolerance, e.g. AUM within 5% of last period. Which rule type fits and why?
A: A `sql`-type rule fits, because the comparison spans rows/periods and references a prior value, which the single-column `library` metrics cannot express. You write a `query` that computes the period-over-period percentage move and assert it `mustBeLessOrEqualTo: 5`, using `{object}` for portability. This is the contract encoding of a plausibility gate — structurally valid data with an implausible jump fails before publication.

Q: What does the ODCS `criticalDataElement` flag on a property signal, and why does it matter in a fund context?
A: `criticalDataElement: true` marks a column as a critical data element (CDE) — a field whose correctness has outsized business or regulatory impact — so governance tooling and reviewers prioritise it. In fund data, `nav_per_share`, `isin`, and `lei` are textbook CDEs because errors flow into dealing prices and regulatory filings. The flag is metadata, but it lets you, for example, require stricter quality rules or four-eyes review specifically on CDE-flagged fields.

Q: What do the ODCS `transformLogic` and `transformSourceObjects` property attributes capture?
A: `transformSourceObjects` lists the upstream objects a derived column is computed from, and `transformLogic` describes (often as an expression or prose) how it is derived. Together they give lightweight, contract-level lineage for a calculated field — for instance that `nav_per_share` comes from `total_net_assets` divided by `shares_outstanding`. This is documentation lineage inside the contract, complementary to (not a replacement for) a full lineage system.

Q: How does ODCS let you constrain a string column's format, e.g. an ISIN, without an external SQL rule?
A: Through `logicalTypeOptions` on the property: for a `string` logical type you can set `minLength` and `maxLength` (both 12 for ISIN), a `pattern` regular expression, and a `format`. So `isin` can carry `logicalTypeOptions` with `minLength: 12`, `maxLength: 12`, and a `pattern` matching two letters then nine alphanumerics then a digit. This expresses the shape declaratively in the schema, leaving only the mod-10 check-digit validation to a `sql` or `custom` quality rule.

Q: What `logicalTypeOptions` are available for a numeric ODCS property, and how would you bound a NAV?
A: For `integer`/`number` you get `minimum`, `maximum`, `exclusiveMinimum`, `exclusiveMaximum`, `multipleOf`, and `format`. To bound a NAV you would set `exclusiveMinimum: 0` so a non-positive NAV violates the schema option, and optionally a `maximum` plausibility ceiling. These declarative bounds catch out-of-range values at schema-validation time, before you even reach the row-level quality rules.

Q: What `logicalType` values does ODCS v3 allow beyond the common four, and which suits a valuation timestamp?
A: ODCS allows `string`, `date`, `timestamp`, `time`, `number`, `integer`, `object`, and `boolean`. A valuation point that needs a wall-clock instant should use `timestamp` (date plus time), not `date`, so the valuation moment is captured rather than just the calendar day. For `timestamp`/`time`/`date` types `logicalTypeOptions` adds `format`, `timezone`, and `defaultTimezone`, which lets you pin the valuation time to a specific zone.

Q: What is `dataGranularityDescription` on an ODCS schema object, and why include it for a NAV mart?
A: `dataGranularityDescription` is a free-text statement of the object's grain — what one row represents — for example "one row per share class per valuation date." It removes a major source of misuse, since a consumer who assumes fund-level grain on a share-class-grained mart will double-count or mis-aggregate. Stating the grain explicitly is a semantics control that no type system captures.

Q: What does the ODCS `partitioned` / `partitionKeyPosition` pair on a property declare?
A: `partitioned: true` marks a property as a partition key of the physical object, and `partitionKeyPosition` gives its ordinal position when multiple columns form the partitioning, mirroring how `primaryKey`/`primaryKeyPosition` work. For a NAV mart partitioned by `nav_date`, you set `partitioned: true` and `partitionKeyPosition: 1` on `nav_date`. This documents the physical layout so consumers know which predicate prunes partitions for efficient reads.

Q: How do `primaryKey` and `primaryKeyPosition` work together for a composite key in ODCS?
A: Each component column sets `primaryKey: true` and a `primaryKeyPosition` giving its order within the key (1, 2, ...), so the standard can reconstruct a multi-column key. For a NAV mart keyed on share class plus date you set `primaryKey: true` with `primaryKeyPosition: 1` on `isin` and `primaryKeyPosition: 2` on `nav_date`. As with single keys, this is declarative intent — uniqueness must still be backed by a quality rule because warehouses do not enforce it.

Q: What is the ODCS `authoritativeDefinitions` `type` field, and give two values you would use for a fund contract.
A: Each `authoritativeDefinitions` entry pairs a `url` with a `type` describing what the link is, such as `businessDefinition`, `transformationImplementation`, `videoTutorial`, or `implementation`. For a fund contract you might use `businessDefinition` linking the `isin` property to the ISO 6166 description and another entry linking an EMT field to the FinDatEx specification page. The `type` lets tooling and readers know whether the link is a glossary definition, code, or example.

Q: Beyond `frequency` and `latency`, name three other ODCS `slaProperties` properties and what they express.
A: `generalAvailability` (the date/time the data product becomes available for general use), `endOfSupport` and `endOfLife` (when support stops and when the product is decommissioned), and `retention` (how long data is kept). There are also `timeOfAvailability` (the daily time the data is ready) and `latency` (delay from source event to availability). For a NAV mart you would combine `timeOfAvailability` (ready by, say, 18:00) with `retention` (e.g. ten years for regulatory record-keeping).

Q: How does an ODCS `slaProperties` entry bind to a specific column, and why use it for NAV freshness?
A: An SLA entry can carry an `element` reference in the form `object.property` (e.g. `nav_mart.nav_date`), tying the SLA to that field rather than the whole object. You use it for freshness so the latency SLA is anchored to `nav_date`, making clear the clock is measured from the valuation date the column carries. Without the `element` link, "fresh within two hours" is ambiguous about two hours from what.

Q: A teammate runs `datacontract test --output-format junit --output results.xml`. What is the effect and where would you use it?
A: It runs the contract's tests and writes the results as a JUnit-format XML file at `results.xml`, which most CI systems (Jenkins, GitLab, GitHub Actions test reporters) ingest natively to display pass/fail per check. You wire it into CI so individual contract checks surface as test cases in the pipeline's test report, not just as console text. The alternative `--output-format json` emits machine-readable JSON for custom dashboards.

Q: What does the `datacontract catalog` command produce, and how do you point it at many contracts?
A: `datacontract catalog` generates a static HTML catalog from a set of contract files, giving a browsable index of your data products. You select the inputs with the `--files` glob (e.g. `--files "contracts/**/*.odcs.yaml"`) and an `--output` directory for the generated site. This is a cheap way to publish a human-readable directory of contracts alongside the enforcement tooling.

Q: How does `datacontract-cli` receive database credentials, and what is the variable naming pattern?
A: Credentials come from environment variables following the pattern `DATACONTRACT_<SERVERTYPE>_<PARAMETER>`, for example `DATACONTRACT_POSTGRES_USERNAME` and `DATACONTRACT_POSTGRES_PASSWORD`, or `DATACONTRACT_SNOWFLAKE_PASSWORD`, or `DATACONTRACT_DATABRICKS_TOKEN`. The tool also loads a local `.env` file, but variables already set in the real environment take precedence over `.env`. In CI you inject these as secrets so no credential lives in the contract or the repo.

Q: `datacontract test` against BigQuery fails to authenticate. Which environment variable is the first thing to check?
A: Check `DATACONTRACT_BIGQUERY_ACCOUNT_INFO_JSON_PATH`, which points to the service-account key JSON the tool uses to authenticate to BigQuery; a missing, wrong-path, or wrong-project key is the usual cause. Confirm the file exists on the CI runner and the service account has read access to the dataset in the contract's `servers` entry. Because `lint` would still pass, an auth failure here is a connection/credentials problem, not a contract-structure problem.

Q: What does the `--fail-on` flag on `datacontract ci` control, and what are its values?
A: `--fail-on` sets the severity threshold at which the CI command returns a non-zero exit and fails the pipeline, accepting `warning`, `error` (the default), or `never`. With the default `error`, warning-severity rule failures are reported but do not break the build; setting `--fail-on warning` makes even warnings block the merge. Using `never` runs the checks for reporting without ever failing — useful for a soft-launch/observation phase before you turn a contract into a hard gate.

Q: How would you stage a new quality rule so it warns first and blocks later, using ODCS and the CLI together?
A: Set the rule's `severity` to a warning level in the contract and run CI with the default `--fail-on error`, so failures are reported but non-blocking during the transition window; once producers have adapted, raise the rule's `severity` to error (or tighten `--fail-on`) so it becomes a hard gate. This mirrors the deprecation/transition discipline applied to schema changes, but for quality thresholds. It gives producers a runway before a stricter check starts failing their builds.

Q: After upgrading `datacontract-cli`, `datacontract test --file nav.odcs.yaml` errors. What changed and what is the fix?
A: The `--file` flag was removed; the contract path is now a positional argument, so the fix is `datacontract test nav.odcs.yaml`. The CLI defaults to `datacontract.yaml` when no path is given, and any other file must be passed positionally rather than via `--file`. This is a common breakage when pinning a newer CLI version in CI without updating the invocation.

Q: How do you point `datacontract test` at one of several servers defined in the contract?
A: Use the `--server` option with the name of the server entry, e.g. `datacontract test --server production`, so the tool connects to that specific `servers` block instead of guessing. This matters when a contract defines `development`, `staging`, and `production` servers and CI must run against the right one. Pairing `--server production` with production credentials in CI secrets keeps the same contract usable across environments.

Q: How would `datacontract import --format sql --source ddl.sql` help adopt a legacy EMT feed, and what must you still add?
A: It reads the table's `CREATE TABLE` DDL and emits a skeleton ODCS contract with the columns and physical types it found, giving you a contract-extracted starting point in seconds. You must still add what DDL cannot supply: per-field semantics aligned to FinDatEx, quality rules (completeness, ISIN format), SLAs, ownership, and `authoritativeDefinitions` links. The import gets coverage fast; the human enrichment is what makes it a real contract rather than a typed schema.

Q: What does `datacontract export --format dbt` give you, and how does it tie into warehouse enforcement?
A: It generates dbt model/schema YAML from the contract — column names, data types, and contract metadata — so the dbt side stays derived from the single ODCS source of truth. Combined with `contract: enforced: true` in dbt, the exported definitions become the warehouse-side gate: a model whose SQL drifts from the exported types fails its build. This closes the loop where the contract defines the promise and dbt enforces it on the running model.

Q: How does `datacontract changelog` categorise differences, and how do you use that output?
A: `changelog` compares two contract versions and labels each difference as `error` (breaking), `warning` (potentially impactful), or `info` (benign), producing a human-readable list. You use it to author the consumer-facing change communication and to decide the semver bump — any `error` entry means a MAJOR bump and a deprecation process. Where `breaking` is the silent gate that fails CI, `changelog` is the narrative you send to consumers.

Q: Can `datacontract breaking` give a false sense of safety, and how?
A: Yes — it only compares the two contract documents, so if a producer changes the model's SQL or the live data's meaning without editing the contract file, `breaking` sees no diff and exits 0. The break is then caught (if at all) by the dbt model contract or `datacontract test` against live data, not by `breaking`. This is exactly why the lesson layers enforcement points rather than trusting the document-diff check alone.

Q: When `contract: enforced: true` is on, what two-step process does dbt run at build time?
A: dbt first runs a preflight check that executes the model's query and compares the returned column names and data types against the contracted columns (order-agnostic); if they match, it then builds the relation with the column names, types, and constraints emitted into the DDL. A mismatch in the preflight fails the run before any table is written. So the contract is verified before the build and re-asserted in the resulting DDL.

Q: On which warehouses is a dbt `not_null` constraint actually enforced at build time, and where is it metadata-only?
A: `not_null` is genuinely enforced on platforms like PostgreSQL, Snowflake, and Redshift, where the constraint is honoured in the DDL so a null fails the build; on Spark/Databricks it is enforced only post-build, and `primary_key`/`unique`/`foreign_key` are metadata-only on most cloud warehouses (Postgres being the notable exception that enforces them). The gotcha is assuming a declared `unique` or `primary_key` blocks duplicates on Snowflake or BigQuery — it does not, so you still need a uniqueness quality test.

Q: What is a dbt `check` constraint, and where can you rely on it?
A: A `check` constraint declares a boolean condition a column's values must satisfy (e.g. `nav_per_share > 0`) in the model's contract. It is supported and enforced only on a subset of platforms — notably PostgreSQL and Databricks — and not on Spark or Athena, so it is not a portable substitute for a quality test. Where supported it pushes a value-level guarantee into the DDL; elsewhere you must express the same check as a `datacontract`/dbt-test rule instead.

Q: For an `incremental` dbt model under a contract, what does `on_schema_change` need to be, and why?
A: It should be set to `append_new_columns` or `fail` rather than `ignore`, so that a schema change is either handled explicitly or stops the run instead of silently diverging from the contract. With `ignore`, the incremental model could drift from its declared columns without surfacing the mismatch. Pairing the contract with `on_schema_change: fail` makes any unexpected structural change a hard build failure.

Q: Under Confluent Schema Registry's default `TopicNameStrategy`, what subject name is a value schema registered under?
A: With `TopicNameStrategy` (the default), the value schema for topic `nav-events` is registered under the subject `nav-events-value`, and a key schema under `nav-events-key`. The compatibility mode is enforced per subject, so the topic's value evolution is governed independently of its key. Knowing the `-value`/`-key` suffix convention is essential when you query or set compatibility on the right subject.

Q: Which REST endpoint tests a candidate schema for compatibility without registering it, and how do you see why it failed?
A: `POST /compatibility/subjects/{subject}/versions/{version}` checks an input schema against that subject version under the subject's configured compatibility level and returns whether it is compatible, without registering anything. Appending `?verbose=true` makes the response include the reason(s) it failed, which is invaluable for diagnosing a rejected producer schema. Using `latest` (or `-1`) as the version checks against the most recently registered schema.

Q: A producer's Kafka deploy fails at startup with a `409`-style schema rejection. What is happening and what are the options?
A: The producer tried to auto-register a new schema version that violates the subject's compatibility mode, so the registry refused it (an HTTP 409 conflict), and the producer cannot publish. The options are to make the change compatible (e.g. add an optional field instead of a required one under `BACKWARD`), or deliberately and temporarily relax the subject's compatibility before a coordinated migration, then restore it. This is the registry doing its job — the runtime equivalent of a failed CI gate.

Q: How does a Kafka schema registry catch a break that producer-side contract CI would miss entirely?
A: The registry enforces at register time on the wire, so it stops a producer that deployed out of band, bypassed the contract repo, or auto-registered a schema that never went through the pull-request CI check. Producer CI only sees changes that flow through code review; the registry is the runtime backstop for everything else. This is why the lesson treats them as complementary rather than redundant enforcement points.

Q: How does a WAP gate decide whether to publish, and where do the contract's quality rules fit in?
A: In Write-Audit-Publish, data is first written to a staging/branch location, then audited by running the contract's quality rules against it, and only if the audit passes is the data published (atomically swapped or branch-merged) to the consumer-visible table. The contract's `quality` block is the spec the audit step executes; a failing rule aborts the publish so bad values never become visible. On a table-format lakehouse this maps cleanly onto a branch or staging snapshot that is promoted only on a green audit.

Q: A row with a structurally valid but negative `nav_per_share` reaches the audit step. Which enforcement layer should stop it, and how would you encode it?
A: The WAP gate should stop it, because the value is type-correct (so schema and dbt contract pass) but violates a plausibility rule. You encode it as an ODCS `sql`-type quality rule like `SELECT COUNT(*) FROM {object} WHERE {property} <= 0` with `mustBe: 0` on `nav_per_share`, or as an `exclusiveMinimum: 0` schema option, run as the audit before publish. The negative NAV then fails the audit and the publish is aborted, keeping an impossible price out of the consumer-visible mart.

Q: Two producers ship identical ODCS schemas but the field meaning differs — which checks pass and what is the only real defence?
A: Every structural check passes — `lint`, `datacontract breaking` (no diff), the dbt contract, and the schema-registry compatibility check — because the types and names are identical; nothing mechanical detects a meaning change. The only real defence is treating semantics as first-class: documenting each field's meaning, `authoritativeDefinitions`, and grain, and handling a meaning change as a versioned, communicated change even with an unchanged schema. This silent semantic break is why the contract bundles semantics with schema rather than relying on types alone.

Q: How would you express, in an ODCS contract, that the share-class register feed retains data for ten years for CSSF record-keeping?
A: As an `slaProperties` entry with `property: retention` and a `value`/`unit` of ten years, optionally with a `customProperties` note referencing the regulatory basis. This turns a record-keeping obligation into a stated, auditable term of the data product rather than tribal knowledge. Because generic tooling does not enforce retention automatically, you still build the actual lifecycle policy — but the contract documents the promise an auditor can check against.

Q: How does an EU AI Act consideration touch a data contract feeding an AI/ML system in the fund context?
A: Where a contracted dataset feeds an AI system that the EU AI Act would classify as high-risk, the contract becomes part of the data-governance evidence the Act expects — documented provenance, quality guarantees, and the meaning/limitations of each field used as a feature. You would surface AI-relevant flags (e.g. permitted-use terms, bias-sensitive fields) via `customProperties` and tie semantics to `authoritativeDefinitions`. The contract does not by itself make a system compliant, but its enforced quality and lineage are exactly the kind of controlled data foundation the Act's high-risk obligations rely on.

Q: A T+1 settlement move shortens the window for the NAV/holdings feed. How does the contract's SLA block adapt, and what breaks if it does not?
A: You tighten the `slaProperties` freshness/`latency` and `timeOfAvailability` so the contracted mart is guaranteed ready earlier, matching the compressed T+1 timeline, and you make the freshness quality check fail if the partition is late. If the SLA is not tightened, the data may be technically correct but arrive after the settlement cut-off, so downstream reconciliation and instructions miss the window even though no schema or value check failed. The lesson here is that under T+1 the timeliness SLA, not just schema and values, becomes a hard business gate the contract must enforce.

Q: How would you stand up a contract on a Kafka EMT-event topic end-to-end across the registry and the CLI?
A: Author the ODCS contract with the object's `physicalType: topic` and a `kafka` entry in `servers`, then `datacontract export --format avro` to generate the Avro schema, register it on the subject (`nav-events-value`) with the chosen compatibility mode, and run `datacontract test` against the topic in CI. The registry then rejects incompatible producer schemas at runtime while the CLI gates contract changes at design time. One ODCS source drives both the wire schema and the CI check, keeping them in lockstep.

Q: A consumer complains the NAV mart "was empty this morning" though no test failed. Which contract control was missing and how do you add it?
A: A schema-level `rowCount` quality rule was missing — an empty-but-valid table passes every type and null check, so freshness/non-emptiness was never asserted. Add a `library` rule with `rule: rowCount` and `mustBeGreaterThan: 0` (or a per-partition variant keyed on today's `nav_date`), run as part of `datacontract test`/the WAP audit. Now a zero-row load fails the gate instead of silently shipping an empty mart to consumers.

Q: How do you prove "a producer cannot break the EMT contract silently" using exactly the commands in this lesson?
A: Open a PR that makes a breaking EMT change and show CI run `datacontract breaking old.odcs.yaml new.odcs.yaml` exit 1 and block the merge; separately show that a SQL-only drift (no contract edit) is caught by the dbt enforced contract's preflight failing the build, and that an out-of-band Avro change is rejected by the registry's `POST /compatibility/...` check. The proof is three red gates on three bypass paths, all visible in CI rather than asserted. Demonstrating each path closes the "but what if they go around it" objection concretely.

Q: Why might you keep both an ODCS `sql` quality rule and a dbt `check` constraint for the same NAV > 0 guarantee?
A: They enforce at different moments and survive different bypasses: the dbt `check` constraint (where supported, e.g. Postgres/Databricks) blocks a bad value at the producer's model build, while the ODCS `sql` rule runs in `datacontract test`/the WAP audit against live data even on platforms where `check` is unsupported or in pipelines that bypass dbt. Relying only on the dbt constraint leaves BigQuery/Snowflake/Spark paths unguarded; relying only on the test misses the build-time catch. Layering both gives the same defence-in-depth the lesson's four enforcement points provide.
