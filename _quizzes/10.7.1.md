# Phase 6 · 10.7.1 Retention & lifecycle — 100 self-test questions

Q: What is data retention in one sentence, as a governance discipline?
A: Data retention is the policy and mechanism that decides how long each class of record is kept and when it is destroyed, expressed as a schedule and enforced by lifecycle automation. It exists to satisfy two opposing pressures: regulators that demand records be kept for a minimum period, and privacy law that demands personal data not be kept longer than necessary. For a fund platform the architect's job is to reconcile these per record class so deletion is provable and defensible.

Q: What is the core tension this lesson is built around?
A: Regulated retention (for example 10-year fund record-keeping) pulls toward keeping data, while GDPR data minimisation pulls toward deleting it as soon as the purpose ends. Lifecycle design is where the architect reconciles them by assigning each record class its own retention period and deletion mechanism. Get it wrong in one direction and you breach GDPR by holding personal data too long; wrong in the other and you breach CSSF expectations by expiring a record a regulator still wants.

Q: Why is a single blanket retention period for the whole platform a bad design?
A: Different record classes have wildly different legal and business retention needs: KYC personal data may need deleting soon after a relationship ends, while order and NAV records may need a decade. A blanket period either over-retains personal data (a GDPR breach) or under-retains regulated records (a CSSF breach). The architecture must therefore carry a per-class schedule, not one global number.

Q: What is a retention schedule?
A: A retention schedule is a documented mapping from each record class to a retention period, a trigger event that starts the clock, and the disposition action at the end (typically deletion). It is the authoritative source the lifecycle rules implement. Without it, your storage lifecycle rules are just guesses with no auditable justification behind them.

Q: Name the five record classes this lesson uses as worked examples.
A: The five are orders, NAV, investor KYC, logs, and EMT outputs. They are deliberately chosen to span very different regimes: transactional fund records, calculated valuations, personal data, operational telemetry, and regulatory disclosure files. Each gets its own retention period and deletion mechanism rather than a shared one.

Q: Why do investor KYC records sit at the opposite end of the spectrum from order records on retention?
A: KYC is dense personal data, so GDPR minimisation pushes to delete it once the legal basis (the client relationship plus any AML record-keeping window) ends, often around five years after the relationship closes. Order records are transactional fund records a regulator may want for the full statutory window, commonly ten years. Treating them with one rule guarantees you breach one regime or the other.

Q: How long must Luxembourg fund records typically be retained, and why does this drive design?
A: A common requirement is around ten years for fund accounting and transfer-agency records, reflecting AML/CTF and commercial-law record-keeping obligations. This long floor drives the lifecycle design because it forces cheap long-term storage tiers and snapshot retention windows that can survive a decade. The architect must prove the record still exists and is readable at year nine, not just at year one.

Q: In retention terms, how does a NAV record differ from an order record?
A: A NAV is a calculated, published valuation; an order is an investor's instruction. Both are fund records with long retention floors, but NAV history also feeds restatement and performance-attribution lineage, so its retention is tied to reproducibility, not just record-keeping. Orders are tied to the transaction trail and AML evidence. Both typically land in the multi-year regulated bucket, but for different reasons.

Q: Why are application and access logs a distinct retention class?
A: Logs are operational and security telemetry, not business records, so their retention is usually shorter (months to a year or two) and driven by incident-investigation and security needs rather than fund record-keeping. They also frequently contain personal data (IPs, user IDs, account references) caught by GDPR. Treating logs as if they were ten-year fund records both wastes money and over-retains personal data.

Q: Why must EMT outputs have their own retention rule?
A: EMT files are regulatory disclosure artefacts (the European MiFID Template carrying cost-and-charges and target-market data) that you produced and distributed, so you must be able to prove what you published and when. Their retention is tied to the disclosure obligation and to disputes about what figures a distributor received. That is a different driver from the underlying order or NAV data, so it earns its own class.

Q: What standard or appendix does this lesson tie the retention schedule back to?
A: The Learn bullet ties retention-schedule design to A.19, the records-management/retention appendix item in the plan. The point is that the schedule is not invented per platform; it derives from a documented records-management policy that lists record classes and their statutory periods. The lifecycle rules are the technical implementation of that policy.

Q: What three things must a complete retention-schedule entry specify?
A: A retention period (how long), a trigger that starts the clock (the event from which the period is measured, such as account closure or trade date), and a disposition action at expiry (almost always deletion, occasionally anonymisation). Without the trigger you cannot compute the expiry date; without the disposition the schedule never actually frees anything. The architect writes all three for every class.

Q: Why is the trigger event, not just the period, critical for KYC retention?
A: KYC retention is usually measured from the end of the business relationship, not from when the document was collected, so the clock only starts when the account closes. If you implement it as days-after-creation you delete active clients' KYC, and if you never model the closure event you keep it forever. The trigger event is therefore as load-bearing as the number of years.

Q: What is the architect's litmus-test question for any retention design, per the Done-when criteria?
A: You must be able to answer "when does this data actually disappear, everywhere?" with a concrete per-class answer you can prove. "Everywhere" is the hard part: the same record can live in the table, in old snapshots, in compacted files, in backups, and in logs. A design that only deletes the live copy fails this test.

Q: What does storage-tier lifecycle management mean for object storage?
A: It means automatically moving objects between cost/performance tiers as they age, typically hot to cool to archive, and eventually deleting them, based on rules rather than manual action. The economics: hot tier is cheap to read but expensive to store, archive is cheap to store but slow and costly to read. Lifecycle rules let infrequently-accessed older data drift to cheaper tiers without an engineer touching it.

Q: Describe the hot, cool, and archive tier economics in Azure Blob Storage.
A: Hot has the highest storage cost but the lowest access cost and instant availability; cool has lower storage cost, higher access cost, and a minimum-retention penalty if you delete early; archive has the lowest storage cost but is offline, requiring rehydration that can take hours and costs more to read. The design moves data to colder tiers as access frequency drops, trading retrieval latency and read price for storage savings.

Q: Azure recently added a tier between hot and cool — what is it and where does it sit?
A: The cold tier sits between cool and archive: cheaper to store than cool, more expensive to access, and still online (unlike archive). Lifecycle rules can target it with the `tierToCold` action. It suits data accessed rarely but where you cannot tolerate archive's hours-long rehydration.

Q: What is the key difference between the archive tier and the cool/cold tiers regarding access?
A: Archive is offline: a blob in archive cannot be read until it is rehydrated to an online tier, which can take many hours for standard priority. Cool and cold are online, so reads work immediately, just at a higher per-operation cost. This is why archive is only appropriate for data you are confident you will rarely, if ever, need quickly.

Q: Can an Azure Blob lifecycle policy rehydrate a blob from archive back to hot automatically?
A: No. The documentation states explicitly that lifecycle management policies cannot be used to rehydrate blobs to an online tier. Rehydration from archive is a separate, manual or programmatic operation. A lifecycle rule can move data into archive, but getting it back out is not something the lifecycle engine does for you.

Q: What is the `enableAutoTierToHotFromCool` action in an Azure lifecycle rule?
A: It is an action that automatically moves a blob back from the cool tier to the hot tier when the blob is accessed, so frequently-read data does not stay stranded in cool. It requires last-access-time tracking to be enabled on the account. It is the one lifecycle action that warms data up rather than cooling it down, and it only works between cool and hot, not from archive.

Q: What three blob properties can Azure lifecycle rule conditions be based on?
A: Creation Time, Last Modified Time, and Last Accessed Time (the last only if access-time tracking is enabled). These map to the condition keys `daysAfterCreationGreaterThan`, `daysAfterModificationGreaterThan`, and `daysAfterLastAccessTimeGreaterThan`. Choosing the wrong property is a common bug: tiering on modification time keeps frequently-read-but-never-written data hot far too long unless you use last-access time.

Q: Why does last-access-time-based tiering require enabling access tracking, and what does it cost?
A: Conditions like `daysAfterLastAccessTimeGreaterThan` only work if blob last-access-time tracking is turned on for the account, because otherwise the timestamp is not maintained. Each last-access update is billed in the "other operations" category, but at most once per object per 24 hours even if accessed thousands of times. So the cost is bounded, but it is not free and must be enabled deliberately.

Q: How are filters used in an Azure lifecycle rule, and what is their logical combination?
A: Filters limit a rule's actions to a subset of blobs using name prefixes and blob index tags; if more than one filter is defined they are combined with a logical AND. Filters can only include blobs, never exclude them. This is how you point a rule at, say, the KYC prefix without touching the orders prefix in the same container.

Q: What are the documented limits on prefixes and blob index tags per Azure lifecycle rule?
A: Each rule can have up to 10 case-sensitive prefixes and up to 10 blob index tag conditions. If your record classes need more discriminators than that within one rule, you split them across multiple rules. The case-sensitivity of prefixes is a frequent gotcha when paths do not match the rule.

Q: How long can it take for an Azure lifecycle policy change to take effect, and why does that matter operationally?
A: When you add or edit policy rules, it can take up to 24 hours for the changes to go into effect and for the first execution to start. This matters because you cannot expect an immediate deletion or tier change after editing a policy; testing must allow for that latency. A new deletion rule that "isn't working" five minutes after you saved it is usually just inside this window.

Q: A delete action in an Azure lifecycle policy is not deleting blobs in a soft-delete-enabled account. Why?
A: With soft delete enabled, the lifecycle delete action puts the object into a soft-deleted state rather than purging it, and it is retained for the soft-delete retention period before final removal. So the blob is "deleted" logically but still occupies storage and is recoverable until the soft-delete window lapses. For a true retention-deadline guarantee you must account for this extra window on top of the rule's days.

Q: Why will an Azure lifecycle delete action fail to remove a blob in an immutable container?
A: An immutable (WORM) policy allows objects to be created and read but not modified or deleted for the immutability period. A lifecycle delete cannot override that, so the delete action simply will not work on those blobs. Immutable storage is how legal hold and tamper-evidence are often implemented, and by design it blocks deletion until the hold or retention interval ends.

Q: Which system containers does Azure lifecycle management explicitly not affect?
A: It does not affect system containers such as `$logs` and `$web`. This is a gotcha if you assumed a single account-wide rule would also age out the `$logs` container's analytics logs; those need a separate retention mechanism. It is a concrete example of why "delete everywhere" requires checking each store, not just the main containers.

Q: Are Azure lifecycle management policies themselves billed, and what is billable?
A: The lifecycle policies are free of charge, but you are billed for the standard operation cost of the `Set Blob Tier` API calls the rules trigger. Delete operations are free. So tier transitions have a per-operation cost at scale, while expirations do not, which can influence how aggressively you tier versus delete.

Q: How does Azure Blob lifecycle map onto MinIO for the FOSS hands-on in this lesson?
A: MinIO implements S3-compatible object lifecycle management with transition (tiering to a configured remote tier) and expiration (deletion) rules, configured via `mc ilm rule add`. It is the local, free stand-in for Azure Blob lifecycle so you can practise tiering and expiry without a cloud account. The concepts (rule, filter by prefix, transition, expiration, days) carry across directly.

Q: What MinIO command family configures object lifecycle rules?
A: The `mc ilm` command family manages Information Lifecycle Management rules, for example `mc ilm rule add` to add a rule and `mc ilm rule ls` to list them. You specify expiry with options like `--expire-days` and transitions with `--transition-days` and `--transition-tier`. It mirrors S3 lifecycle configuration and the same prefix/tag filtering ideas as Azure.

Q: In MinIO, what must exist before you can write a transition (tiering) lifecycle rule?
A: You must first configure a remote storage tier with `mc ilm tier add`, defining where transitioned objects go (for example another S3-compatible or cloud endpoint). The lifecycle rule's `--transition-tier` then references that named tier. Without a configured tier, a transition rule has nowhere to move objects to.

Q: What is the difference between a transition rule and an expiration rule in object-storage lifecycle?
A: A transition rule moves an object to a different (usually colder/cheaper) storage tier but keeps the object; an expiration rule deletes the object entirely. Transition is about cost optimisation while preserving the data; expiration is about retention enforcement and actually freeing the record. A complete schedule typically uses transitions to age data through tiers and an expiration at the very end.

Q: For a NAV file class with a ten-year floor, sketch a sensible tiering-and-expiry rule shape.
A: Keep it hot while it is current and being read for reporting, transition to cool after, say, 90 days, transition to archive/cold after a year, and expire only after the full ten-year statutory window. The early tiering saves storage cost on data that is rarely read after the period closes, while the late expiration honours the regulatory floor. The exact day thresholds come from the retention schedule, not from a default.

Q: What is a legal hold in retention terms?
A: A legal hold is an instruction to suspend deletion of specified records because they are relevant to litigation, a regulatory investigation, or an audit, even if their retention period has expired. It overrides the normal lifecycle expiry so evidence is not destroyed while it is needed. The architecture must be able to freeze deletion for a subset of data on demand and lift it later.

Q: Why does a legal hold complicate an otherwise clean retention schedule?
A: A clean schedule deletes on a deterministic date, but a legal hold must be able to suspend that deletion for an arbitrary subset of records for an indeterminate time. So the lifecycle mechanism needs a way to exclude held records from expiry without rewriting the whole schedule. If your only deletion mechanism is a blanket time-based rule with no exception path, you cannot honour a hold.

Q: How is legal hold commonly implemented in object storage like Azure Blob?
A: Through immutability/WORM features — a legal-hold or time-based-retention immutability policy that prevents deletion regardless of lifecycle rules. Because a lifecycle delete action cannot remove an immutable blob, the hold is enforced at the storage layer, not just by skipping a rule. Lifting the hold removes the immutability and lets normal expiry resume.

Q: What is the risk of implementing legal hold only as "we paused the deletion job"?
A: Pausing the job stops new deletions but does not protect data already past its expiry from a future run, and it is easy to forget to re-pause after a deploy, a config reset, or a different deletion path (backups, snapshot expiry) firing. A robust hold marks the specific records as non-deletable at the storage layer so every deletion path respects it. Relying on a paused cron job is fragile and not auditable.

Q: A regulator opens an investigation into 2021 NAV calculations and your schedule would delete them next month. What must happen?
A: A legal hold must be placed on the 2021 NAV records (and their supporting lineage) to suspend expiry before the deletion date passes, across every store they live in — live tables, snapshots, backups, and logs. The hold stays until the investigation closes, then is lifted so normal retention resumes. The key risk is a deletion path you forgot about firing before the hold was fully applied.

Q: How does legal hold interact with GDPR's right to erasure?
A: A legal hold can justify lawfully refusing or deferring an erasure request, because GDPR allows retention where needed for the establishment, exercise, or defence of legal claims or to comply with a legal obligation. So a held record is not deleted on a data-subject request until the hold lifts. The architect must be able to flag held records so the erasure pipeline skips them and the refusal is documented.

Q: What is an Iceberg snapshot?
A: An Iceberg snapshot is an immutable point-in-time view of a table's data, recorded each time the table is committed (insert, update, delete, compaction). It references the manifest lists and data files valid at that moment, enabling time travel and rollback. Crucially, old snapshots keep pointing at old data files, which is why data you logically deleted can still be physically reachable through them.

Q: Why is Iceberg snapshot expiry a different layer of control from business retention?
A: Business retention says how long the record must exist as a record; snapshot expiry controls how long Iceberg keeps old historical versions of the table around for time travel and rollback. They are different layers: you can keep the current row for ten years while expiring the dozens of intermediate snapshots after days. Conflating them either bloats storage with endless snapshots or, worse, lets a deleted row survive in an unexpired snapshot.

Q: What does the Iceberg `expire_snapshots` procedure do?
A: It removes snapshots older than a cutoff and deletes the data files, manifest files, and manifest lists that are uniquely referenced by those expired snapshots and not needed by any remaining snapshot. This is what physically reclaims space after deletes and rewrites and is what actually removes data that was logically deleted in an earlier commit. Until you expire the snapshot that still references a row, that row's file is not gone.

Q: What are the main parameters of `expire_snapshots` in Spark?
A: The key parameters are `table` (required), `older_than` (expire snapshots made before this timestamp), and `retain_last` (keep at least this many of the most recent snapshots regardless of age). There is also `snapshot_ids` to expire specific snapshots, and `max_concurrent_deletes`/`stream_results` for performance. If `older_than` and `retain_last` are omitted, the table's own expiration properties are used.

Q: What is the default value of `retain_last` in `expire_snapshots`, and why does that default exist?
A: The default is 1, because Iceberg never lets you expire all snapshots — there must always be at least one to represent the current table state. So even an aggressive expiry keeps the latest snapshot. You raise `retain_last` when you want to guarantee a minimum number of recent versions are kept for rollback regardless of their age.

Q: What is `history.expire.max-snapshot-age-ms` and its default?
A: It is the table property setting the maximum age a snapshot can reach before it is eligible for expiry, with a default of 432000000 ms, which is 5 days. When `expire_snapshots` is run without an explicit `older_than`, this property supplies the cutoff. It is the table-level default that keeps roughly five days of history unless overridden.

Q: What is `history.expire.min-snapshots-to-keep` and its default?
A: It is the table property setting the minimum number of snapshots to retain regardless of age, defaulting to 1. It works with `max-snapshot-age-ms`: even if snapshots are older than the age cutoff, at least this many recent ones survive. It is the table-property equivalent of the `retain_last` procedure argument.

Q: Why is running `expire_snapshots` essential for "deletion that actually deletes" in Iceberg?
A: An Iceberg DELETE or overwrite does not erase the underlying data files; it writes a new snapshot that no longer references those rows, while old snapshots still point at the old files. The data is only physically removed when every snapshot referencing it is expired and `expire_snapshots` deletes the now-orphaned files. So a GDPR erasure in Iceberg is not complete until snapshot expiry runs and clears the historical references.

Q: A row was deleted from an Iceberg table to satisfy a GDPR erasure, yet time travel still returns it. Why, and what is the fix?
A: Time travel returns it because an earlier snapshot still references the data file containing that row; the DELETE only created a new snapshot without it. The fix is to expire the snapshots that predate the deletion (`expire_snapshots` with an appropriate `older_than`/`retain_last`) so those files are removed and that historical version is no longer queryable. Until then the erasure is incomplete.

Q: What does the Iceberg `remove_orphan_files` procedure do?
A: It deletes files in the table's storage location that are not referenced by any table metadata and are older than a retention threshold, reclaiming space lost to failed jobs, aborted commits, or files left behind by tooling. It complements `expire_snapshots`: snapshot expiry removes files no longer referenced by metadata it tracks, while orphan removal sweeps up files metadata never pointed to. It is run periodically as table maintenance.

Q: What is the default retention threshold for `remove_orphan_files`, and why is it deliberately conservative?
A: The default is 3 days. It is conservative because a recently written file might belong to a commit still in progress or not yet visible; deleting such a file would corrupt the table. The 3-day buffer ensures you only remove files old enough to be safely orphaned, which is why you should not casually shrink it to clean up faster.

Q: What is the danger of setting `remove_orphan_files`'s `older_than` too aggressively?
A: You risk deleting files that belong to an in-flight or recently committed write that has not fully propagated, which can corrupt the table or make snapshots unreadable. That is exactly why the 3-day default exists. If you must shorten it, you should be certain no concurrent writers are active and ideally run it in `dry_run` mode first to see what would be deleted.

Q: How does `rewrite_data_files` (compaction) relate to retention and deletion?
A: Compaction rewrites many small files (and applies pending deletes) into fewer, larger files, improving query performance and physically removing rows marked deleted from the new files. But it creates a new snapshot and leaves the old files referenced by older snapshots, so it does not free space by itself. You still need `expire_snapshots` afterward to drop those old snapshots and reclaim the storage.

Q: Why might storage usage temporarily grow after a compaction, and how is that resolved?
A: Compaction writes new combined files while the pre-compaction files remain, still referenced by earlier snapshots, so for a window you are storing both copies. The growth is resolved by expiring the snapshots that reference the old files, after which `expire_snapshots` deletes them. This is the classic "compact then expire" maintenance pairing.

Q: Why is "deletion that actually deletes" the hardest requirement in this lesson?
A: Because a record can persist in many places after you delete the live copy: old Iceberg snapshots, pre-compaction data files, object-storage versions or soft-deleted blobs, backups, and logs. Truly honouring an erasure means clearing every one of those, each with its own mechanism and timeline. The Done-when test — "when does this data disappear, everywhere?" — is hard precisely because "everywhere" spans all those layers.

Q: List the layers you must clear for an Iceberg-on-object-storage record to be truly gone.
A: The live table rows (DELETE), the historical snapshots that still reference the file (`expire_snapshots`), any orphaned files (`remove_orphan_files`), the object-storage versions or soft-delete copies, the backups containing old table state, and any logs that captured the data. Each has its own deletion control. Missing any one means the record is not actually gone.

Q: Why are backups a notorious blind spot in "deletion that actually deletes"?
A: Backups are immutable point-in-time copies, so a record deleted from production still lives in every backup taken before the deletion. Unless your backup rotation expires those backups within a defensible window, a "deleted" personal record persists for the full backup retention. The schedule must therefore state a backup retention period and how erasure is reconciled with it, not assume backups are out of scope.

Q: How do you reconcile a GDPR erasure with the existence of older backups?
A: The accepted approach is to document that backups age out on a defined, reasonable cycle and that the erased record will be gone once the last backup predating the deletion expires, rather than attempting to surgically edit backups. You then ensure the deletion is re-applied or never restored if a backup is ever recovered. The key is a defensible, documented retention window for backups, not pretending they do not hold the data.

Q: Why must logs be part of the deletion story, not an afterthought?
A: Logs frequently capture personal data — account numbers, investor IDs, IP addresses, request payloads — so deleting the database row while leaving the log line means the personal data still exists. Logs therefore need their own retention rule and deletion mechanism. The litmus-test answer "everywhere" explicitly includes logs, which is why the lesson lists logs as a record class.

Q: An auditor asks "prove this investor's data is gone after their erasure request." What evidence would you show?
A: You would show: the row deleted from the live table, the snapshot-expiry job that removed the historical files referencing it, the object-storage lifecycle/soft-delete window that has lapsed, the backup rotation window that has expired (or a documented date by which it will), and the log retention that has aged out the relevant entries. Together these prove the data is gone everywhere, each with a timestamped job or policy. That bundle is the per-class, provable answer the Done-when criteria demand.

Q: What two configuration artefacts does the Done-when criteria require you to show?
A: You must show that Iceberg snapshot expiry is configured (the maintenance job or table properties controlling `expire_snapshots`) and that storage-tier rules are configured (the object-storage lifecycle policy). Together they evidence that both the table-history layer and the storage-economics layer are governed. One without the other leaves either snapshots or storage unmanaged.

Q: Why does GDPR's storage-limitation principle map directly to this lesson?
A: GDPR's storage-limitation principle requires personal data be kept in identifiable form no longer than necessary for the purpose, which is exactly what a retention schedule with real deletion enforces. Lifecycle automation is the technical control that makes the principle operational rather than aspirational. Without enforced expiry you are asserting compliance you cannot prove.

Q: How can anonymisation serve as an alternative disposition to deletion?
A: Instead of deleting a record at expiry you can irreversibly strip or pseudonymise its personal fields so the residual data is no longer personal data under GDPR, letting you keep aggregate or non-identifying information. This satisfies minimisation while preserving analytical value. The catch is it must be irreversible — reversible pseudonymisation still counts as personal data and does not discharge the obligation.

Q: Why is retention design fundamentally an architecture decision, not just an ops task?
A: Because it determines storage layout (which classes live where), tier strategy, snapshot-maintenance cadence, backup policy, and the deletion mechanisms across every layer — choices that are expensive to change later. The architect must reconcile conflicting legal regimes and make the result provable to an auditor. Treating it as a downstream ops chore typically produces unprovable, non-compliant deletion.

Q: What is the economic argument for tiering even data you must keep for ten years?
A: Most of that ten-year data is read rarely after the period closes, so leaving it in the hot tier wastes money on premium storage you do not benefit from. Tiering it to cool, cold, or archive cuts storage cost dramatically while still satisfying the retention floor. You pay more only on the rare retrieval, which for cold archival records is an acceptable trade.

Q: What is the risk of over-aggressive tiering to archive for fund records?
A: Archive is offline and rehydration can take many hours and cost more, so if a regulator or auditor asks for a record on short notice you may not produce it quickly. For records with any realistic chance of urgent retrieval, cool or cold (online) is safer than archive despite the higher storage cost. Tier choice should reflect realistic access urgency, not just storage price.

Q: Why is "creation time" sometimes the wrong basis for a tiering condition?
A: Creation time ignores whether the data is still actively read, so a hot, frequently-queried object created long ago would be tiered down purely because it is old, hurting performance. Last-access time is usually the better basis for tiering decisions, while creation or modification time may suit deletion deadlines. Matching the condition to the intent (cost vs. retention) is the design skill.

Q: How does last-access-based tiering risk surprising you with cold data that should be warm?
A: If access tracking has gaps, or if internal/system reads do not update last-access time, a regularly-needed object can appear idle and get tiered down, then suffer slow first reads. You mitigate this with `enableAutoTierToHotFromCool` so a read warms it back up. Understanding what does and does not count as an "access" for the timestamp is essential before relying on it.

Q: What is the difference between logical deletion and physical deletion, and why does retention care?
A: Logical deletion makes a record invisible to normal queries (a new Iceberg snapshot, a soft-delete flag, a tombstone) while the bytes remain on disk; physical deletion removes the bytes. Retention and GDPR care about physical deletion, because personal data still on disk is still "kept." This is why snapshot expiry, orphan-file removal, and backup aging matter — they turn logical deletes into physical ones.

Q: Why is object versioning a double-edged sword for retention?
A: Versioning protects against accidental overwrite by keeping previous versions, which is great for resilience but means a "deleted" or overwritten object still exists as an older version. For retention you must add lifecycle rules that expire noncurrent versions too, or personal data persists in version history. Many real over-retention incidents come from forgetting noncurrent-version expiry.

Q: In Azure lifecycle, how do you handle previous versions and snapshots of blobs for deletion?
A: Lifecycle rules can target current versions, previous (noncurrent) versions, and blob snapshots separately, with their own conditions and delete actions. So you write a rule that deletes noncurrent versions after N days in addition to handling current versions. Omitting the noncurrent-version rule is how data survives long after you thought it was deleted.

Q: Why might a retention schedule specify different periods for the same data in different systems?
A: Because the same fund record may be required for ten years in the system of record but only needed for, say, two years in a downstream analytics copy whose purpose is shorter-lived. Minimisation says the analytics copy should expire sooner even though the master is kept. The schedule reflects purpose per system, not one number applied blindly everywhere.

Q: What is the relationship between a retention schedule and a records-management policy?
A: The records-management policy (referenced here as A.19) is the governing document that classifies records and assigns statutory/business retention periods; the retention schedule is the operational table derived from it, and the lifecycle rules implement that schedule technically. So the chain is policy → schedule → enforced rules. An auditor traces a deletion back up that chain to a documented justification.

Q: Why is "we'll just keep everything forever, storage is cheap" not a defensible position in a regulated fund context?
A: Keeping personal data with no defined end breaches GDPR's storage-limitation and minimisation principles regardless of storage cost, and it enlarges breach exposure and discovery burden. Cheap storage addresses cost, not legality. The regulator expects defined retention and actual deletion, so "keep everything" is a compliance failure, not a cost optimisation.

Q: Why is "delete everything as soon as possible" equally indefensible?
A: Fund records carry minimum retention obligations (often ten years for AML and commercial-law reasons), so premature deletion destroys records the CSSF or an auditor still expects, which is itself a breach. Minimisation does not override a legal duty to retain. The correct posture is per-class periods that satisfy both the floor and the ceiling, which is the whole point of the schedule.

Q: How would you set retention for the "logs" class given they may contain personal data?
A: Set a relatively short period driven by security and incident-investigation needs (commonly months up to a year or two), apply it to all log stores including system log containers, and ensure the deletion mechanism actually purges them rather than just rotating them logically. Because logs hold personal data, the same minimisation logic as KYC applies — keep only as long as needed. Verify deletion reaches every log sink, not just the primary one.

Q: A lifecycle delete rule on your MinIO bucket "ran" but objects are still present. What do you check first?
A: First check whether bucket versioning is enabled, because a delete on a versioned bucket typically just adds a delete marker, leaving prior versions in place; you need an expiry rule for noncurrent versions. Then confirm the rule's prefix/filter actually matches the object keys (case and path), and that enough time has passed for the lifecycle scan to run. Versioning and a mismatched prefix are the two most common causes.

Q: Your Azure delete rule has been live for an hour with no deletions. Is this a bug?
A: Probably not yet — policy changes can take up to 24 hours to take effect and for the first execution to start, so an hour is well inside the expected latency. You would wait out the window before treating it as a fault. If it still has not run after 24 hours, then you investigate the filter, conditions, soft-delete, or immutability blockers.

Q: Iceberg `expire_snapshots` ran but storage did not shrink. What are the likely causes?
A: Likely causes: the snapshots you expected to expire were younger than the cutoff (so still retained), `retain_last`/`min-snapshots-to-keep` is holding them, the files are still referenced by a non-expired snapshot, or the freed files are actually orphans needing `remove_orphan_files`. You verify which snapshots remain and whether files are still metadata-referenced. Expiry only deletes files uniquely owned by the expired snapshots.

Q: You ran `remove_orphan_files` with a short `older_than` and a concurrent write was happening — what is the danger?
A: You may have deleted files belonging to the in-flight commit before it became visible, which can corrupt the table or break snapshots that later reference those files. This is exactly why the 3-day default exists and why you avoid shortening it while writers are active. Running with `dry_run` first and quiescing writers are the safeguards.

Q: How do you prove to an auditor that snapshot expiry is configured for an Iceberg table?
A: Show the table properties `history.expire.max-snapshot-age-ms` and `history.expire.min-snapshots-to-keep`, and the scheduled maintenance job that calls `expire_snapshots` (with its cadence and `older_than`/`retain_last` arguments) plus its run history. That evidences both the policy (properties) and the enforcement (the job actually running). The Done-when criteria explicitly want this shown.

Q: Why is snapshot expiry cadence a balance, not "as often as possible"?
A: Expire too rarely and old snapshots accumulate, bloating storage and leaving logically-deleted (including erased) data physically reachable for time travel; expire too aggressively and you lose the rollback/time-travel window you may need to recover from a bad write. The cadence balances storage and deletion compliance against operational safety. Fund platforms often keep only days of history while expiring frequently to bound the deletion lag.

Q: How does the time-travel window interact with GDPR erasure timelines?
A: Whatever your snapshot retention window is, it is also the maximum lag between a logical erasure and the data physically disappearing from time travel, because the erased rows remain reachable through unexpired snapshots until they age out. So a 30-day snapshot window means erased data can linger up to ~30 days in history. You must be able to defend that lag as reasonable or shorten the window for personal-data tables.

Q: Why might you set a shorter snapshot-retention window on tables holding personal data than on purely operational tables?
A: Shorter snapshot retention bounds how long erased or expired personal data remains reachable through time travel, tightening GDPR compliance, at the cost of a smaller rollback window. Operational tables without personal data can afford longer history for safety. Tuning `max-snapshot-age-ms` per table by data sensitivity is a concrete application of minimisation.

Q: What does it mean that snapshot expiry and storage-tier lifecycle are "different layers, different controls"?
A: Snapshot expiry is a table-format control (Iceberg metadata governing historical versions), while storage-tier lifecycle is an object-store control (Azure/MinIO governing where bytes live and when they are deleted). They operate independently: expiring a snapshot removes metadata references and the uniquely-owned files, but the underlying object store still applies its own tiering/expiry to whatever remains. A complete design configures both, because neither alone guarantees deletion everywhere.

Q: If Iceberg sits on top of object storage, can the object-store lifecycle rule safely delete old data files directly?
A: No — you must not let a naive object-store expiry rule delete Iceberg data files by age, because files are referenced by metadata regardless of their age, and deleting a still-referenced file corrupts the table. Deletion of Iceberg files must go through `expire_snapshots`/`remove_orphan_files`, which respect metadata. The object-store lifecycle is for non-Iceberg objects (raw landing files, exports) or for tiering, not for blindly expiring table files.

Q: So what is the object-store lifecycle's proper role for an Iceberg table's bucket?
A: It is appropriate for tiering whole objects to colder storage by age (a cost optimisation that does not delete) and for expiring non-table objects, but deletion of table data must be driven by Iceberg maintenance, not a blind age-based delete rule. Mixing the two without care is how people corrupt tables. The clean separation: object store tiers, Iceberg deletes.

Q: For the EMT-outputs class, why is proving "what we published and when" central to its retention design?
A: Because EMT files are regulatory disclosures sent to distributors; disputes hinge on exactly which figures a recipient received on a given date. Retention must keep the published artefact (and ideally a record of distribution) for the disclosure-obligation window, immutably enough that it cannot be quietly altered. This is closer to an evidentiary archive than to mutable operational data, which shapes both the retention period and the storage controls.

Q: Why might EMT outputs warrant immutable (WORM) storage rather than ordinary tiering?
A: Because their value is evidentiary — proving the exact content you disclosed — so they must be tamper-evident and undeleteable for the retention window, which WORM/immutability provides. Ordinary mutable storage cannot prove a file was not edited after the fact. Immutability also doubles as a built-in legal-hold mechanism for that class.

Q: How does an ISIN-keyed dataset complicate per-class retention?
A: A single dataset keyed by ISIN may mix fund-record data (long retention) with references that tie into investor-level personal data (shorter, minimisation-driven retention), so a per-row or per-column distinction may be needed rather than per-table. You cannot always apply one retention period to a whole ISIN-keyed table without over- or under-retaining part of it. This pushes retention granularity down to record class within the table, not just the table.

Q: How does a transfer-agency register stress the retention design?
A: A TA register holds investor positions and transaction history — both long-retention fund records and dense personal data — and it must support a closed investor's eventual KYC erasure while preserving the transaction trail regulators require. So you may keep the transaction record (possibly with personal fields anonymised) for ten years while deleting standalone KYC sooner. Reconciling "delete the person, keep the transaction" is a classic TA retention puzzle.

Q: Why does the LEI/entity reference data generally have different retention drivers than investor personal data?
A: LEI and legal-entity reference data identify organisations, not natural persons, so it is largely outside GDPR's personal-data minimisation pressure and is retained for as long as it is referenced by records (often long-lived, slowly-changing reference data). Investor KYC is natural-person personal data driven hard by minimisation. Mixing the two under one rule misapplies GDPR where it does not bite and ignores it where it does.

Q: How do SWIFT/payment message records typically map onto retention classes?
A: Settlement and payment instruction messages are transactional financial records carrying AML relevance, so they usually fall into a long retention bucket similar to orders, and they often contain personal/account data triggering minimisation at the end. They also may be subject to financial-messaging record-keeping rules. They illustrate that a single message type can sit in the "long retention plus eventual personal-data deletion" pattern.

Q: Under DORA, why does deletion/retention design also touch operational-resilience evidence?
A: DORA emphasises ICT risk management and incident handling, which means logs, incident records, and audit trails must be retained long enough to support investigation and reporting, while still respecting personal-data minimisation. So your log-retention class is partly governed by resilience/evidence needs, not just by minimisation. The schedule must reflect that some logs need keeping for resilience even as you minimise personal data within them.

Q: How would you set retention for the "orders" class in a Luxembourg UCITS context?
A: Treat orders as long-retention fund records, typically a ten-year floor measured from the relevant trade or relationship event, stored cost-effectively via tiering toward cold/archive as they age, and deleted (or personal fields anonymised) only after that window. Because they carry investor personal data, plan the eventual minimisation of personal fields even while keeping the transactional record. The exact period comes from the records-management policy, not a guess.

Q: What is the difference between retention floor and retention ceiling, and how does the schedule encode both?
A: The floor is the minimum you must keep (regulatory, e.g. ten years); the ceiling is the maximum you may keep personal data before minimisation forces deletion. The schedule encodes a concrete period that satisfies the floor and triggers deletion at or before the ceiling. Where the two conflict for the same field (a long-retention record containing personal data), you resolve it with anonymisation or field-level handling rather than picking one regime to ignore.

Q: Why is per-column or field-level retention sometimes required, not just per-table?
A: Because one record can mix long-retention transactional fields with personal data that minimisation says to delete sooner, so deleting the whole row is wrong but keeping the personal fields is also wrong. Field-level handling — anonymising or nulling personal columns at one date while retaining the rest until the regulatory date — resolves this. It is more work but is the only correct answer when floor and ceiling collide within a row.

Q: How do you avoid the "we deleted it from prod but it's still in the dev replica" failure?
A: The retention schedule must cover every copy, including masked or unmasked dev/test replicas, and either propagate deletions to them or ensure they are short-lived and regularly rebuilt from already-minimised sources. Personal data in a forgotten replica is still a breach. "Everywhere" in the litmus test explicitly includes non-production copies.

Q: Why should test/dev environments ideally use masked or synthetic data for personal fields?
A: Masking or synthesising personal fields means dev/test copies are not personal data, which removes them from the erasure-propagation and minimisation burden and shrinks breach exposure. Then a prod erasure does not require chasing the same person through every lower environment. It is a design move that simplifies the "delete everywhere" problem by ensuring personal data never reaches those environments.

Q: What single end-to-end statement should your retention design let you make for each record class?
A: For each class you should be able to state: this data is retained for X, measured from event Y, then deleted by mechanism Z across the live table, snapshots, object versions, backups, and logs, with legal hold able to suspend it, and here is the job/policy proving each step. That is the complete, provable, per-class answer to "when does this data disappear, everywhere?" Producing it for all five classes is exactly what the lesson's Done-when criteria require.
