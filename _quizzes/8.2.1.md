# Phase 1 · 8.2.1 + 8.2.2 + 8.2.3 Conceptual → logical → physical modeling — 150 self-test questions

Q: What are the three levels of the conceptual → logical → physical modeling discipline?
A: The conceptual model names the business things and their relationships in business language with no keys or types; the logical model adds candidate keys, full attributes, and normalization while staying engine-neutral; the physical model is engine-specific DDL with data types, indexes, partitioning, and constraints. The chain is the architect's signature artifact: each level refines the previous one and is traceable back to it. Skipping a level is how you end up with an `ISIN`-as-primary-key database that cannot survive a corporate action.

Q: What belongs in a conceptual data model and what does NOT?
A: A conceptual model contains the major business entities (fund, share class, investor, order, NAV) and the relationships between them, expressed in business terms. It deliberately excludes attributes' data types, primary/foreign keys, normalization decisions, and anything engine-specific — those are logical or physical concerns. The test is that a business stakeholder who has never seen a database should be able to read and validate it.

Q: What is the "when to stop" rule for conceptual entity modeling?
A: You stop adding entities when every distinct business thing the stakeholders talk about is named and no new entity would change the shape of the relationships — roughly one page of entities and relationships. Per DMBOK ch. 5, the conceptual level is about scope and shared vocabulary, not completeness of detail, so resist the urge to add attributes or resolve many-to-many relationships there. Over-modeling at the conceptual level is a common smell: if you are arguing about a foreign key, you have drifted into the logical model.

Q: In the fund domain, why is "share class" a separate entity from "fund" rather than an attribute of it?
A: A single fund typically issues multiple share classes (e.g. retail vs institutional, distributing vs accumulating, hedged vs unhedged), each with its own `ISIN`, fee schedule, and NAV per share. Modeling share class as its own entity captures the one-to-many relationship and lets each class carry its own attributes; folding it into the fund as an attribute would force repeating-group or multi-valued columns that violate first normal form. This is exactly the kind of entity boundary a conceptual model must get right before any keys are assigned.

Q: What is a candidate key?
A: A candidate key is any minimal set of attributes whose values uniquely identify each row in a relation, where "minimal" means no attribute can be removed without losing uniqueness. A table can have several candidate keys; you pick one as the primary key and the rest become alternate keys, usually enforced by `UNIQUE` constraints. Identifying all candidate keys is a logical-modeling step that drives normalization, because functional dependencies are defined relative to keys.

Q: What is the difference between a natural key and a surrogate key?
A: A natural key is a candidate key drawn from real-world business data (an `ISIN`, a passport number, an email), while a surrogate key is a system-generated identifier with no business meaning (an auto-increment integer, a `UUID`, a `BIGINT` from a sequence). Surrogates are stable and opaque, so they survive business-rule changes; natural keys are meaningful but can change, be reissued, or be unknown at insert time. The choice between them is one of the central logical-design decisions you must justify in writing.

Q: Why is an `ISIN` an attribute and not a primary key for a security or share class?
A: `ISIN`s can be reused or reassigned after a security is delisted, are sometimes unavailable when a record is first created, and a single economic instrument can carry different identifiers across markets — so an `ISIN` is not guaranteed stable or always present, which a primary key must be. Making it the PK welds every foreign key in the database to a value that a corporate action (merger, rename, redomicile) can change, forcing painful cascading updates. The architect's answer is a surrogate primary key with the `ISIN` stored as a `UNIQUE`, indexed business attribute.

Q: A colleague proposes `ISIN` as the primary key of the `share_class` table. What is your first objection?
A: That `ISIN` is not guaranteed immutable: corporate actions and reissuance can change it, and the PK should never change because every foreign key depends on it. I would propose a surrogate `share_class_id` as the PK and keep `ISIN` as a `UNIQUE NOT NULL` (or `UNIQUE` allowing nulls for not-yet-assigned classes) attribute. This isolates the join graph from business-identifier churn while still enforcing the one-`ISIN`-per-class rule.

Q: What is a composite key?
A: A composite (compound) key is a candidate or primary key made of two or more columns that together provide uniqueness, because no single column is unique on its own. A classic example is a NAV history table keyed by (`share_class_id`, `nav_date`): neither column alone is unique, but the pair is. Composite keys are common at the logical level but are often replaced by a surrogate at the physical level when the key is wide or referenced by many child tables.

Q: What does normalization aim to achieve?
A: Normalization organizes columns and tables to minimize redundancy and the update/insert/delete anomalies that redundancy causes, by ensuring each fact is stored in exactly one place. It proceeds through normal forms (1NF, 2NF, 3NF, BCNF, ...) that each forbid a specific class of dependency. The goal at the logical level is a structure where business rules are enforced by keys and constraints rather than by hoping every copy of a fact stays in sync.

Q: What does first normal form (1NF) forbid?
A: 1NF requires every column to hold a single atomic value per row and forbids repeating groups, multi-valued columns, and arrays-as-columns used to dodge a child table. For example, storing three sub-fund `ISIN`s in one comma-separated column violates 1NF; you split them into separate rows of a child table. 1NF is the precondition for all the higher forms, because functional dependencies are only well defined over atomic columns.

Q: What does second normal form (2NF) forbid?
A: 2NF applies to tables with a composite primary key and forbids partial dependencies — where a non-key column depends on only part of the key rather than the whole key. If a NAV-line table keyed by (`order_id`, `line_no`) stores `order_date`, that date depends on `order_id` alone, violating 2NF; it belongs in the `order` table. A table already in 1NF with a single-column key is automatically in 2NF.

Q: What does third normal form (3NF) forbid?
A: 3NF forbids transitive dependencies: a non-key column depending on another non-key column rather than directly on the key. If a `share_class` table stores `fund_name` (which depends on `fund_id`, itself a non-key attribute), that is transitive and `fund_name` belongs in the `fund` table. The informal mnemonic is that every non-key attribute depends on "the key, the whole key, and nothing but the key".

Q: What does Boyce-Codd normal form (BCNF) forbid that 3NF allows?
A: BCNF strengthens 3NF by requiring that for every non-trivial functional dependency `X → Y`, `X` must be a superkey — closing the loophole where a non-prime attribute determines part of a candidate key. The cases where 3NF and BCNF differ involve overlapping candidate keys, which are rare but real. In practice most well-designed transactional schemas reach BCNF naturally once they are in 3NF.

Q: What is a functional dependency, and why does it matter for normalization?
A: A functional dependency `A → B` means that for any given value of `A`, the value of `B` is uniquely determined — knowing the `share_class_id` determines the `fund_id`. Normalization is defined entirely in terms of functional dependencies relative to keys: each normal form forbids a particular shape of dependency. You cannot defend a table's normal form without first writing down its functional dependencies.

Q: What is denormalization and when is it legitimate?
A: Denormalization deliberately reintroduces redundancy — duplicated columns, pre-joined tables, stored aggregates — to improve read performance or simplify queries, accepting the cost of keeping copies in sync. It is legitimate when measured read patterns demand it and the redundancy is documented and controlled (e.g. maintained by triggers or batch jobs), not when it happens by accident. The discipline is that every denormalization must be a recorded, justified decision, never silent drift from a normalized baseline.

Q: How should a deliberate denormalization be documented in the model?
A: You record which normalized form the table departs from, exactly which redundancy was introduced, the read pattern that justifies it, and the mechanism that keeps the duplicated data consistent. The "Done when" bar for this lesson is being able to name each table's normal form and point to where you denormalized deliberately and why. Undocumented denormalization is indistinguishable from a modeling mistake during review.

Q: A reviewer finds the same `fund_name` stored in three tables. What two questions decide whether it is acceptable?
A: First: is this a documented, deliberate denormalization with a stated read-pattern justification, or accidental duplication? Second: what mechanism keeps the three copies consistent, and what happens on a rename? If there is no documentation and no sync mechanism, it is an update anomaly waiting to happen and should be normalized back to a single `fund` row that the others reference by `fund_id`.

Q: What changes between the logical and physical model regarding keys?
A: At the logical level keys are conceptual: you state candidate keys and choose a primary key by attribute set. At the physical level those become concrete DDL — a `BIGINT GENERATED ALWAYS AS IDENTITY` surrogate, a `PRIMARY KEY` constraint, `UNIQUE` constraints for alternate keys, and `FOREIGN KEY` constraints with chosen `ON DELETE`/`ON UPDATE` actions. The physical model also picks index types and storage details the logical model deliberately ignored.

Q: At the physical level, why does data-type choice matter beyond just "holding the value"?
A: Data types determine storage size, index efficiency, comparison/sort behavior, and what invalid values the engine rejects for free. Choosing `NUMERIC(18,6)` for a NAV instead of `FLOAT` avoids binary rounding errors that are unacceptable in financial calculations; choosing `DATE` instead of `TEXT` for `nav_date` enables range partitioning and correct ordering. Type choice is a physical decision tuned to the target engine, which is why a logical model should not pin it down.

Q: Why is `NUMERIC`/`DECIMAL` preferred over `FLOAT`/`DOUBLE PRECISION` for monetary and NAV values in Postgres?
A: `NUMERIC` stores exact base-10 values with a defined precision and scale, so sums and comparisons of money are exact, whereas `FLOAT`/`DOUBLE PRECISION` are binary floating point and cannot represent values like `0.1` exactly, producing accumulating rounding errors. In a regulated NAV context those errors can break reconciliation to the cent and fail audit. The cost is that `NUMERIC` arithmetic is slower and the column is larger, which is an acceptable trade for correctness here.

Q: What is the difference between `CHAR(n)`, `VARCHAR(n)`, and `TEXT` in Postgres, and which would you use for an `ISIN`?
A: `CHAR(n)` is blank-padded to a fixed length, `VARCHAR(n)` enforces a maximum length, and `TEXT` is unbounded; in Postgres there is no performance penalty for `TEXT` versus `VARCHAR`. An `ISIN` is always exactly 12 characters, so `CHAR(12)` documents that fixed width, though many teams use `VARCHAR(12)` plus a `CHECK` constraint to avoid the padding semantics. Either way you add a `CHECK` that enforces the 12-character ISO 6166 format.

Q: What is declarative table partitioning in PostgreSQL?
A: Declarative partitioning splits one logical table into multiple physical child tables ("partitions") using `CREATE TABLE ... PARTITION BY`, with each partition created via `CREATE TABLE ... PARTITION OF ... FOR VALUES ...`. Postgres routes inserts to the correct partition automatically and can prune irrelevant partitions from query plans. It is a physical-design technique for very large tables like NAV history, introduced as the modern replacement for the old inheritance-plus-trigger approach.

Q: What are the three declarative partitioning methods in PostgreSQL?
A: Range partitioning (`PARTITION BY RANGE`) splits on non-overlapping value ranges, with lower bound inclusive and upper bound exclusive — ideal for `nav_date`. List partitioning (`PARTITION BY LIST`) assigns explicit key values to each partition, useful for something like fund domicile. Hash partitioning (`PARTITION BY HASH`) spreads rows across partitions by a modulus and remainder for even distribution when no natural range or list exists.

Q: Which partitioning method fits a NAV-history table and why?
A: Range partitioning on `nav_date` (typically monthly or yearly) fits best, because NAV history grows by date and almost every query filters or aggregates by date range. Range partitions let the planner prune to just the relevant months and make archiving old data a fast `DETACH PARTITION` rather than a huge `DELETE`. This is the canonical example the PostgreSQL partitioning docs use for the design.

Q: Show the syntax to declare a range-partitioned NAV table and one monthly partition.
A: You declare the parent with `CREATE TABLE nav_history (...) PARTITION BY RANGE (nav_date);` and add a partition with `CREATE TABLE nav_history_2026m06 PARTITION OF nav_history FOR VALUES FROM ('2026-06-01') TO ('2026-07-01');`. The upper bound `'2026-07-01'` is exclusive, so it does not overlap the next month's partition. New rows whose `nav_date` falls in June 2026 are routed to that child table automatically.

Q: In PostgreSQL, what constraint must a primary key on a partitioned table satisfy?
A: The primary (or unique) key's columns must include all of the partition key columns, and the partition key must not use expressions or function calls. This is because each partition has its own local index that can only enforce uniqueness within itself, so the partition boundaries plus the included key columns are what guarantee global uniqueness. For a `nav_history` partitioned by `nav_date`, the PK must therefore be a composite like (`share_class_id`, `nav_date`), not `share_class_id` alone.

Q: A `CREATE TABLE ... PARTITION BY RANGE (nav_date)` fails when you also declare `PRIMARY KEY (share_class_id)`. Why?
A: Postgres rejects it because the partition key column `nav_date` is not part of the primary key, and on a partitioned table the unique/primary key must include every partition-key column. The fix is to make the key composite — `PRIMARY KEY (share_class_id, nav_date)` — or to choose a different partition key that is already in the PK. This is a hard limitation of how per-partition indexes enforce uniqueness, not a bug.

Q: What is a `DEFAULT` partition and what is the catch when attaching new partitions later?
A: A `DEFAULT` partition catches any row whose key matches no defined partition, declared with `CREATE TABLE ... PARTITION OF parent DEFAULT`. The catch is that to `ATTACH` a new partition whose range might already contain rows sitting in the default partition, Postgres must scan the default partition to verify none of those rows belong to the new range, which can be slow and takes a strong lock. Many designs avoid a default partition for time-series tables so future-month partitions attach cheaply.

Q: What is the practical difference between `ATTACH PARTITION` and `DETACH PARTITION CONCURRENTLY`?
A: `ALTER TABLE ... ATTACH PARTITION` takes only a `SHARE UPDATE EXCLUSIVE` lock on the parent, so it is light, but it validates that incoming rows respect the partition bound. Plain `DETACH PARTITION` requires an `ACCESS EXCLUSIVE` lock that blocks all access, whereas adding `CONCURRENTLY` lowers it to `SHARE UPDATE EXCLUSIVE` so reads and writes continue. For archiving a month of NAV history, `DETACH PARTITION ... CONCURRENTLY` lets you remove old data without an outage.

Q: Why is `DETACH PARTITION` a better archival strategy than `DELETE` for old NAV rows?
A: `DETACH PARTITION` is a near-instant metadata operation that turns a partition into a standalone table, whereas `DELETE` of millions of rows generates WAL, bloats the table, and requires a `VACUUM` to reclaim space. After detaching you can dump the standalone table to cold storage or `DROP` it cheaply. This is one of the main reasons to range-partition append-only history in the first place.

Q: What is partition pruning and what enables it?
A: Partition pruning is the planner (and executor) eliminating partitions that cannot contain matching rows, so a query touching only June 2026 reads only that month's partition. It is enabled when the query's `WHERE` clause filters on the partition key with a constant or parameter the planner can compare to partition bounds. Pruning is the main performance payoff of partitioning; a query with no partition-key predicate must scan every partition.

Q: How does choosing an index differ at the physical level versus what the logical model says?
A: The logical model declares uniqueness and relationships; the physical model decides which of those become indexes and adds non-unique indexes to support query access paths. You add indexes for foreign keys (so joins and cascade checks are fast), for frequent filter/sort columns, and choose the index type — B-tree for ranges and equality, GIN for arrays/JSONB, BRIN for huge naturally-ordered tables. Indexes are pure physical tuning: they change performance, never the logical meaning of the data.

Q: Why is a `BRIN` index sometimes a good fit for a large NAV-history table?
A: A `BRIN` (Block Range Index) stores summary min/max values per block range rather than per row, so it is tiny and cheap to maintain, and it works well when the data is physically ordered by the indexed column — which `nav_date` typically is in an append-only history. For range scans over date it can prune large swathes of the table at a fraction of a B-tree's size. The trade-off is that `BRIN` is far less effective if the column's physical order is random.

Q: On a partitioned table, does `CREATE INDEX` on the parent create a global index?
A: No — PostgreSQL has no global indexes; an index on a partitioned table is "virtual" and Postgres creates a matching local index on each partition. The benefit is that each partition's index is independent, so detaching a partition takes its index with it; the cost is that uniqueness can only be enforced per partition unless the partition key is part of the unique constraint. This is why unique constraints on partitioned tables must include the partition key.

Q: What is forward engineering in data modeling?
A: Forward engineering is generating the physical DDL (and ultimately the database) from a logical or physical model — going model → schema. A modeling tool like DBeaver, erwin, or ER/Studio produces `CREATE TABLE` statements with the types, keys, and constraints the model specifies. It is half of the round-trip; the value is that the running schema is a faithful image of the reviewed model rather than hand-typed drift.

Q: What is reverse engineering in data modeling?
A: Reverse engineering builds a model by reading an existing database's catalog — going schema → model — so you can diagram and review a database you did not author. Tools such as DBeaver's ERD view or erwin connect to the database and reconstruct entities, columns, keys, and relationships from the system catalog. It is essential for documenting legacy fund-admin systems where the only authoritative artifact is the live schema.

Q: What does "round-tripping without drift" mean and why is it the goal?
A: Round-tripping means you can forward-engineer a model to DDL, later reverse-engineer that DDL back into a model, and diff the two to confirm they still agree. "Without drift" means the model and the database have not silently diverged — no column added in production that the model lacks, no constraint in the model missing from the database. Keeping them in sync is what makes the model a trustworthy review artifact rather than stale documentation.

Q: How would you detect drift between your logical model and the deployed Postgres schema?
A: Reverse-engineer the live schema (e.g. in DBeaver) into a model and diff it against your maintained logical model, comparing entities, columns, types, keys, and constraints. Differences flag drift: a column present in production but not the model, or a relationship the model claims that the database does not enforce. The lesson's "Do" step is exactly this — reverse-engineer the physical model and diff against the logical model.

Q: What is Crow's Foot notation and what does it express?
A: Crow's Foot is an ERD notation where relationship cardinality is drawn at the line ends: a "crow's foot" (three prongs) means "many", a single bar means "one", a circle means "zero/optional", so combinations express minimum and maximum participation. For example a bar-plus-crow's-foot on the child end reads "one and only one ... to many". It is the most widely used notation in practical database modeling and the one you are expected to write fluently.

Q: In Crow's Foot notation, how is a mandatory-one-to-many relationship drawn?
A: The "one" parent end is marked with a single perpendicular bar (exactly one), and the "many" child end is marked with a crow's foot preceded by a bar to mean "one or more" (mandatory many) or by a circle to mean "zero or more" (optional many). So fund-to-share-class, where a fund must have at least one share class and each share class belongs to exactly one fund, is bar on the fund end and bar-crow's-foot on the share-class end. Reading the symbols at each end gives you both cardinality and optionality.

Q: What is IDEF1X notation and how does it differ from Crow's Foot?
A: IDEF1X is a formal ERD notation (a U.S. federal standard, common in defense and government data modeling) that distinguishes identifying from non-identifying relationships and uses solid versus dashed lines and rounded versus square-cornered entity boxes to show dependency. Unlike Crow's Foot it encodes whether a child entity's primary key includes the parent's key (identifying = solid line, rounded child box). The lesson asks you to *read* IDEF1X and *write* Crow's Foot, so you need passive fluency in IDEF1X's dependency semantics.

Q: In IDEF1X, what is the difference between an identifying and a non-identifying relationship?
A: In an identifying relationship the parent's primary key migrates into the child's primary key, making the child existence-dependent on the parent; IDEF1X draws this as a solid line with a rounded-corner (dependent) child entity. In a non-identifying relationship the parent's key becomes a non-key foreign-key attribute in the child, drawn as a dashed line with a square-corner (independent) entity. This distinction is central to IDEF1X and is why it is favored where key-dependency rigor matters.

Q: Why must an architect be fluent in more than one modeling notation?
A: Different stakeholders and tools speak different notations — a vendor may hand you IDEF1X diagrams, a DBA Crow's Foot ERDs, a BA BPMN — and the architect is the one who reads all of them without a translator. The lesson sets the bar at writing Crow's Foot and reading IDEF1X. Notation is just a rendering of the same underlying entities, keys, and cardinalities, so fluency is about recognizing the same model in different clothes.

Q: What is an associative entity (junction table) and when do you introduce one?
A: An associative entity resolves a many-to-many relationship into two one-to-many relationships by creating a new entity that holds a foreign key to each side, plus any attributes of the association itself. You introduce it whenever the conceptual model shows a many-to-many — e.g. an investor holding many share classes and a share class held by many investors becomes a `holding` entity. At the logical level this is mandatory, because a relational schema cannot directly represent many-to-many.

Q: Why can a conceptual many-to-many relationship not survive into the physical relational model unchanged?
A: A relational table cannot store a direct many-to-many link in a single foreign key, so the relationship must be resolved into a junction/associative table carrying one foreign key per side. The junction table's primary key is typically the composite of the two foreign keys (or a surrogate), and it is the natural home for relationship attributes like position quantity or as-of date. Leaving a raw many-to-many in the logical model is an unimplementable model.

Q: What is referential integrity and how is it enforced at the physical level?
A: Referential integrity is the rule that every foreign-key value must match an existing primary-key value in the referenced table (or be null), so you never point at a non-existent parent. Postgres enforces it with `FOREIGN KEY` constraints, optionally with `ON DELETE`/`ON UPDATE` actions like `RESTRICT`, `CASCADE`, or `SET NULL`. Enforcing it in the database, not just application code, is what guarantees an order can never reference a deleted share class.

Q: What does `ON DELETE CASCADE` do, and why would you be cautious with it on fund reference data?
A: `ON DELETE CASCADE` automatically deletes child rows when their parent row is deleted, propagating the delete down the relationship. On fund reference data this is dangerous because deleting a fund would silently erase all its share classes, NAV history, and orders — destroying records you are regulated to retain. For reference and historical data you usually prefer `ON DELETE RESTRICT` (or soft-delete flags) so accidental deletes fail loudly.

Q: Why is the surrogate-key-versus-natural-key decision recorded "in writing" in this lesson's Done-when criteria?
A: Because the architect must be able to state a reason for every surrogate-key choice — including why `ISIN` is not the PK — and an unwritten rationale is unreviewable and forgotten. Writing it down makes the decision auditable, which a regulated fund administrator's governance expects, and forces you to actually have a reason rather than a habit. The bar is that a colleague reading the model can see why each key is what it is.

Q: What is the test that the lesson uses to judge whether a logical model is good enough?
A: The test is: can you hand the logical model to a colleague and have them implement it without asking you any questions? If they need to ask what a key is, what a relationship means, or what normal form a table is in, the model is underspecified. This is the lesson's primary "Done when" criterion and it forces completeness and clarity at the logical level.

Q: Why does the lesson say a logical model should be engine-neutral?
A: Because welding the logical model to one engine's types, partitioning, or index syntax means you cannot retarget it and you blur the line between logical meaning and physical tuning. An engine-neutral logical model states entities, keys, attributes, relationships, and normal forms; the choice of `NUMERIC(18,6)` versus another engine's decimal type belongs only in the physical model. Keeping them separate is what lets the same logical design drive Postgres, Snowflake, or Synapse physical models.

Q: What is a degenerate dimension's logical-modeling analogue — a degenerate identifier — and how does it relate to keys here?
A: A degenerate identifier is a business identifier like an `order_number` that you keep as an attribute for traceability but do not promote to a primary key, because it may not be stable or unique across the whole system. It relates to this lesson's key discipline: like `ISIN`, it is meaningful business data carried as an attribute while a surrogate provides the stable PK. Recognizing which identifiers are decoration versus keys is core to logical key design.

Q: Why is "premature surrogate key on every table" itself a modeling smell?
A: Adding a surrogate to a table that already has a small, stable, single-column natural key (like a 2-letter country code or a currency `ISO 4217` code) adds a join and an opaque integer for no benefit. The decision should be justified case by case: surrogates earn their place where natural keys are wide, unstable, or absent. The discipline is reasoned key selection, not a blanket rule in either direction.

Q: How do you decide whether to store an attribute as a column or promote it to its own entity?
A: Promote it to its own entity when it has its own attributes, participates in its own relationships, or repeats across rows — i.e. it is a "thing" not just a "fact about a thing". A fund's domicile is a single coded value, so it can be a column (or a small reference table); a fund's set of distributors each with their own agreements is an entity. The conceptual model should reveal this before keys are assigned.

Q: What is a reference (lookup) table and why model coded values that way?
A: A reference table holds the allowed values of a coded attribute — currencies, countries, fund types, order statuses — usually with a short code as the key and a human-readable description. Modeling them this way enforces referential integrity on the codes, centralizes the descriptions, and lets you add metadata (active flag, effective dates) per code. It also prevents the data-quality drift you get when free-text values like "EUR", "Euro", and "eur" coexist.

Q: At the physical level, why might you choose `GENERATED ALWAYS AS IDENTITY` over a `SERIAL` column in Postgres?
A: `GENERATED ALWAYS AS IDENTITY` is the SQL-standard syntax and, with `ALWAYS`, prevents an application from accidentally supplying its own value for the surrogate key, which protects key integrity. `SERIAL` is older Postgres-specific sugar that creates a sequence and a default but does not block manual inserts and has quirkier ownership semantics. For a surrogate primary key you want the stricter, standard `IDENTITY` form.

Q: What is the difference between a primary key and a unique constraint at the physical level?
A: A primary key is a single chosen candidate key that is `NOT NULL` and uniquely identifies the row, and a table has at most one; a `UNIQUE` constraint enforces uniqueness on an alternate key and may allow nulls (treated as distinct in Postgres). Both create an underlying unique index. You designate the surrogate as the primary key and add a `UNIQUE` constraint on the natural key like `ISIN` so both are enforced.

Q: Why might `UNIQUE` on a nullable `ISIN` column behave differently than you expect in Postgres?
A: In standard Postgres a `UNIQUE` constraint treats `NULL` values as distinct, so multiple rows with `ISIN IS NULL` are all allowed — useful when a share class exists before its `ISIN` is assigned. If you need at most one null, you would use `NULLS NOT DISTINCT` (available in modern Postgres) or a partial unique index. Knowing this is the difference between "one un-ISIN'd class allowed" and "many" — a real fund-onboarding edge case.

Q: How does the conceptual model constrain what the logical model can do?
A: The conceptual model fixes the entities and the relationships' cardinality, so the logical model must produce keys and tables that faithfully represent those things and links — it cannot invent or drop a business relationship. If the conceptual model says one investor places many orders, the logical model must give `order` a foreign key to `investor`, not the reverse. Traceability runs downward: each logical element should map to something the conceptual model named.

Q: What is the danger of modeling NAV as an attribute of share class rather than as its own time-series entity?
A: NAV is a value that changes daily, so storing a single `nav` column on `share_class` keeps only the latest value and throws away the history regulators and investors require. Modeling NAV as its own entity keyed by (`share_class_id`, `nav_date`) preserves the full periodic time series and lets you answer "what was the NAV on this date". This is a conceptual error that, if uncaught, poisons every downstream physical design.

Q: Why is (`share_class_id`, `nav_date`) a natural composite candidate key for NAV history?
A: Because exactly one official NAV is struck per share class per valuation date, that pair uniquely identifies a NAV record and neither column alone does. It also doubles conveniently as the basis for range partitioning by `nav_date` while still being a valid composite primary key (since the partition key is included). Whether you keep it as the PK or add a surrogate is a physical decision, but the candidate key is clear at the logical level.

Q: In a transfer-agency context, why might an investor entity need a surrogate key even though it has identifiers like an account number?
A: Account numbers can be reformatted on system migrations, reissued, or differ across the registers an investor holds, so they are not guaranteed stable or globally unique — making them poor primary keys. A surrogate `investor_id` gives a stable internal identity that all the investor's accounts, orders, and holdings can reference unchanged. The account number stays as a `UNIQUE` business attribute for lookup and external reconciliation.

Q: What is the relationship between a logical model's normal form and its update anomalies?
A: Lower normal forms permit redundant storage of a fact, which creates update anomalies (change it in one place, others go stale), insertion anomalies (cannot record one fact without another), and deletion anomalies (deleting a row loses unrelated facts). Reaching 3NF/BCNF removes the functional-dependency redundancies that cause these. That is the whole point of stating each table's normal form — it tells the reviewer which anomalies are structurally impossible.

Q: Give an insertion anomaly example in a poorly normalized fund table.
A: If `fund` and `share_class` data live in one table, you cannot record a newly registered fund that has not yet launched any share class without inventing a dummy share-class row, because the share-class columns cannot be null in a meaningful key. Splitting into `fund` and `share_class` lets the fund exist independently of its classes. The anomaly disappears once the transitive/partial dependency is removed.

Q: How does an optional (zero-or-one) relationship translate to physical DDL?
A: An optional relationship lets the foreign-key column be nullable, so a row may or may not reference the parent — for example an order that has not yet been allocated to a settlement batch has a null `batch_id` — while the `FOREIGN KEY` still validates any non-null value against the parent. A mandatory relationship instead makes that column `NOT NULL`, forcing every child to have a parent. This is how the ERD's cardinality and optionality become enforced database constraints rather than mere drawing.

Q: What is a "self-referencing" relationship and where might it appear in fund data?
A: A self-referencing (recursive) relationship is a foreign key from a table back to itself, modeling a hierarchy among rows of the same entity. In fund data a master-feeder structure (a feeder fund investing into a master fund) or an umbrella-and-sub-fund hierarchy is naturally a `parent_fund_id` foreign key on the `fund` table. It is the relational way to represent a tree of funds.

Q: Why should the physical model's choices be "per target engine" rather than universal?
A: Because engines differ in available types, index kinds, partitioning syntax, and performance characteristics — Postgres has `BRIN` and declarative range partitioning, a columnar mart engine wants different storage and clustering. A physical model tuned for Postgres is not optimal or even valid on Snowflake or Synapse. The logical model is shared; the physical model is re-derived for each engine it targets.

Q: What is the practical workflow described in the lesson for producing and validating the physical model?
A: You write the physical model as Postgres DDL with types, constraints, and declarative partitioning for NAV history, then reverse-engineer that schema in DBeaver and diff the reconstructed model against your logical model. The diff confirms the physical model is a faithful, drift-free implementation of the logical design. It closes the round-trip and is the lesson's hands-on "Do" step.

Q: What is a domain (in the relational/data-modeling sense) and how does it relate to data types?
A: A domain is the set of permitted values for an attribute — its type plus any constraints, like "a positive `NUMERIC(18,6)`" or "a 12-character `ISIN` matching a checksum pattern". Postgres lets you formalize this with `CREATE DOMAIN`, reusing the same constrained type across many tables. At the physical level the domain becomes a column type plus `CHECK` constraints; conceptually it is the meaning behind the type.

Q: How could a `CHECK` constraint help enforce `ISIN` validity at the physical level?
A: A `CHECK` constraint can enforce the structural rules of an `ISIN` — exactly 12 characters, first two being an ISO 3166 country code, the rest alphanumeric, and the final digit a Luhn-style check digit over ISO 6166. For example `CHECK (char_length(isin) = 12)` rejects malformed values at write time. Pushing this into the database catches bad reference data at the source rather than discovering it downstream during NAV distribution.

Q: Why does the architect care about "the decisions you didn't write down" in a model review?
A: Because the riskiest modeling choices — why this is a surrogate key, why this table is denormalized, why NAV history is partitioned this way — are invisible if undocumented, and a reviewer (or a future you, or an auditor) cannot reconstruct the intent. The whole three-level discipline exists to make reasoning reviewable. An undocumented decision is a maintenance landmine, especially in a regulated fund-admin system.

Q: A model passes review but production queries are slow on NAV history. Which level is most likely at fault?
A: The physical level, since the logical model can be correct while the physical implementation lacks partitioning, the right indexes, or appropriate data types. The first checks are whether `nav_history` is partitioned by `nav_date`, whether queries actually filter on the partition key to allow pruning, and whether foreign-key and filter columns are indexed. Logical correctness and physical performance are independent concerns, which is exactly why the levels are separated.

Q: What does it mean that a logical model is "implementation-independent"?
A: It means the logical model expresses keys, attributes, relationships, and normal forms without committing to a storage engine, file layout, or index strategy — anyone could implement it on any relational system. Implementation independence is what makes the model a durable communication and review artifact rather than a transcript of one database. The physical model is where independence ends and engine-specific choices begin.

Q: How many entities is "too many" at the conceptual level, and how do you tell?
A: There is no fixed number, but the signal is that you have started modeling things the business does not name or arguing about attributes and keys — that means you have slid into logical detail. The DMBOK guidance frames the conceptual model as scope and shared vocabulary: a page of the major business things. If stakeholders cannot validate it at a glance, it has too much detail, not too many real entities.

Q: Why is "fund" alone insufficient and you typically also need "legal entity" or "umbrella" in a Luxembourg context?
A: Luxembourg funds are often umbrella structures (a single legal entity, e.g. a SICAV, containing multiple sub-funds/compartments), so "fund" is ambiguous between the legal wrapper and the sub-fund that strikes a NAV. Modeling the umbrella and sub-fund as related entities captures that one legal entity issues many sub-funds, each with its own share classes and NAVs. Collapsing them loses the structure that drives reporting and ring-fencing of assets.

Q: What is the role of a `valid_from`/`valid_to` pair, and is it a logical or physical concern?
A: A `valid_from`/`valid_to` pair records the period during which a row's values are correct, enabling temporal/point-in-time queries — it is a logical modeling decision (you are modeling history of an entity) realized with physical date/timestamp columns and constraints. For slowly changing reference data like fund attributes, it lets you answer "what was true as of this date". The columns are physical; the decision to keep history is logical.

Q: What is a "weak entity" and how does it relate to IDEF1X identifying relationships?
A: A weak entity cannot exist without its parent and its primary key includes the parent's key — a NAV record cannot exist without its share class. In IDEF1X this is exactly an identifying relationship, drawn with a solid line and a rounded-corner (dependent) child box. Recognizing weak entities is what tells you whether to migrate the parent key into the child's primary key.

Q: How do you express a one-to-one relationship and when is it appropriate?
A: A one-to-one relationship pairs each row of one entity with at most one row of another, physically enforced by a `UNIQUE` `FOREIGN KEY` (or sharing the primary key). It is appropriate to split rarely-used or security-sensitive attributes into a separate table, or to model an optional specialization (e.g. extra attributes only hedged share classes carry). If the two always coexist and are always read together, a one-to-one split may be unnecessary normalization.

Q: What is supertype/subtype (generalization) modeling and where does it appear in fund products?
A: Supertype/subtype modeling captures "is-a" relationships: a general entity (`security`) with specialized subtypes (`equity`, `bond`, `fund_unit`) that share common attributes but add their own. It appears with heterogeneous fund products where different instrument types share identity and pricing but differ in detail. Physically you implement it as a single table with a type discriminator, a table per subtype, or a shared supertype table with subtype tables linked by the same key.

Q: Why is a single huge "wide" table usually the wrong logical model for diverse fund products?
A: A wide table forces every product type to carry every other type's columns, leaving most of them null, which obscures which attributes are mandatory for which type and invites data-quality errors. It also typically hides functional dependencies that proper normalization or supertype/subtype modeling would separate. The fix is to model the shared attributes once and the type-specific attributes in their own structures.

Q: What does "the partition key must be part of every unique constraint" force you to reconsider about a NAV table's keys?
A: It forces you to confirm that `nav_date` (the partition key) is included in the primary/unique key, so a composite like (`share_class_id`, `nav_date`) is required rather than a bare surrogate or `share_class_id` alone. If your logical model assumed a single-column surrogate PK, the physical partitioning decision pushes back and you reconcile the two. It is a concrete case where physical and logical levels must be negotiated, not assumed independent.

Q: Why might you NOT partition a table even though it is large?
A: Partitioning adds planning overhead and only pays off when queries filter on the partition key (enabling pruning) or when you need partition-level operations like fast archival; a large table queried by arbitrary columns gains little and can even slow down. You also incur the unique-constraint-must-include-partition-key restriction. The decision is driven by the access pattern, so you partition NAV history (date-filtered, archived by date) but not a small dimension table.

Q: What is the difference between a "model" and a "schema" in this discipline?
A: A model is a deliberately abstracted representation (conceptual, logical, or physical) used for understanding and review; a schema is the concrete catalog of tables, columns, and constraints that exists in a running database. The physical model is the closest to the schema and forward-engineers into it, while the schema reverse-engineers back into a physical model. Keeping the model and schema in sync (no drift) is the whole point of round-tripping.

Q: How does naming discipline in the conceptual model pay off later?
A: Clear, business-agreed entity names (fund, share class, investor, order, NAV) become the vocabulary for tables, columns, ADRs, and conversations, so consistent naming reduces ambiguity across every downstream artifact. If the conceptual model calls it "share class", the physical table should not surprise readers by calling it `unit_series`. Naming is a governance concern as much as a technical one, especially where multiple teams and regulators read the same model.

Q: Why is it dangerous to let the physical model's denormalizations leak back into how people understand the data?
A: Because a denormalized physical structure (duplicated `fund_name`, pre-joined reporting table) is a performance artifact, not the source of truth, and treating it as authoritative invites people to update the wrong copy or reason about anomalies as if they were intended. The logical model remains the canonical statement of meaning. This is why denormalizations are documented as departures from the logical baseline, not as the model itself.

Q: When reverse-engineering a legacy fund-admin database, what early sign suggests the original design skipped the logical level?
A: A telltale sign is business identifiers used directly as primary and foreign keys everywhere — `ISIN`, account numbers, fund codes — with no surrogate keys, so the join graph is welded to mutable business data. You also often see wide tables, repeating-group columns (violating 1NF), and no documented normal forms. These are the failure modes the three-level discipline exists to prevent.

Q: What is the value of stating each table's normal form explicitly in the model documentation?
A: It tells a reviewer exactly which anomalies are structurally impossible and which redundancies are deliberate, turning "trust me, it's normalized" into a checkable claim. It also surfaces denormalizations as exceptions that must be justified rather than hiding them. The lesson's Done-when bar is naming the normal form of each table and where you denormalized and why.

Q: Why does the lesson tie the three-level discipline to "every review you'll run"?
A: Because the conceptual/logical/physical chain is the architect's standard lens: you review whether the conceptual model matches the business, whether the logical model's keys and normal forms are sound, and whether the physical model implements them faithfully and performantly. Each review level checks a different class of error, and a model that traces cleanly across all three is the reviewable artifact governance expects. Without the chain, reviews degrade into ad-hoc opinion.

Q: How would you justify a surrogate key for a `fund` table in one written sentence?
A: "Funds are identified externally by codes and `ISIN`s at the share-class level that can change through corporate actions, so `fund` uses an internal stable surrogate `fund_id` as its primary key, with external identifiers kept as `UNIQUE` business attributes." That sentence states the instability of natural keys, the chosen surrogate, and where the natural keys live. Being able to write exactly this for every table is the lesson's key-justification bar.

Q: What is the risk of choosing `nav_date` partitions that are too small (e.g. daily)?
A: Daily partitions on a long-lived NAV history can create thousands of partitions, inflating planning time, catalog size, and per-partition index overhead, sometimes hurting more than helping. Conversely, partitions too large (e.g. all-time) defeat pruning and archival. Monthly or yearly partitions are the usual balance for NAV history, chosen to match query and retention patterns.

Q: Why is the physical-level choice of `TIMESTAMP WITH TIME ZONE` vs `DATE` for valuation points a meaningful decision?
A: A NAV is struck for a valuation *date* (a business day), so `DATE` is usually correct and avoids spurious time-zone arithmetic, whereas event timestamps like order receipt need `TIMESTAMPTZ` to be unambiguous across time zones. Using a timestamp where a date suffices can break range partitioning boundaries and comparisons; using a date where a true instant is needed loses information. Matching the type to the real temporal semantics is a physical-design responsibility.

Q: What does "round-trip engineering" buy a regulated organization specifically?
A: It gives an auditable, current model that provably matches the deployed database, so questions like "why is NAV history partitioned this way?" can be answered from a reviewed artifact rather than tribal memory. Drift detection means a column added in production without a model change is caught. For a fund administrator under regimes like DORA and UCITS reporting obligations, a trustworthy model is part of demonstrable IT governance.

Q: How does normalization interact with the decision to use surrogate keys?
A: Normalization is about functional dependencies and is independent of whether keys are natural or surrogate — a fully normalized schema can use surrogates throughout. Surrogates do not "fix" a normalization problem; a table with a surrogate PK can still hide a transitive dependency among its non-key columns. You normalize based on dependencies first, then choose key representation for each resulting table.

Q: What is the difference between a candidate key and a superkey?
A: A superkey is any set of attributes that uniquely identifies a row, possibly with redundant extra columns; a candidate key is a *minimal* superkey, with no removable attribute. Every candidate key is a superkey, but not every superkey is minimal. Normal-form definitions (especially BCNF's "X must be a superkey") rely on this distinction, so getting it right matters when you defend a table's form.

Q: When would deliberate denormalization into a reporting table be the right call for fund data?
A: When NAV/holdings reporting queries repeatedly join the same normalized tables under tight SLA, pre-joining them into a denormalized, periodically-refreshed reporting structure can meet performance needs the normalized model cannot. The key is that it is an additional, documented, derived structure refreshed from the normalized source of truth — not a replacement for it. You record the read pattern that justifies it and the refresh mechanism that keeps it consistent.

Q: What is the first thing to check when a `FOREIGN KEY` creation fails with a violation error?
A: Check that every value in the referencing column already exists in the referenced table's key column (and that the referenced column is itself a primary key or `UNIQUE`), because Postgres validates existing rows when adding the constraint. Orphan rows — a child pointing at a parent that was deleted or never existed — are the usual cause. You either clean the orphans or correct the references before the constraint can be added.

Q: Why must the referenced column of a `FOREIGN KEY` be a primary key or have a unique constraint?
A: Because a foreign key must point at a uniquely identifiable parent row; if the target column allowed duplicates, "the referenced row" would be ambiguous and referential integrity could not be enforced. Postgres therefore requires the referenced columns to be backed by a primary key or unique constraint. This is why you reference `share_class_id` (the surrogate PK) rather than a non-unique business attribute.

Q: How does the three-level discipline help prevent the "ISIN-as-primary-key" failure mode described in the lesson's Why?
A: The conceptual level names `ISIN` as a property of a share class, not the share class itself; the logical level identifies it as a candidate/natural key and forces an explicit, written decision about whether it should be the primary key; that decision, made with stability in mind, leads to a surrogate PK with `ISIN` as an attribute. Each level provides a checkpoint where the bad choice would be caught. Skipping the logical key analysis is precisely how the failure slips through.

Q: After a corporate action changes a share class's `ISIN`, what should happen in a well-modeled database versus an `ISIN`-keyed one?
A: In a well-modeled database the `ISIN` is just an attribute, so you update one column on one `share_class` row and every foreign-key reference (orders, NAV history, holdings) remains valid because they point at the unchanged surrogate `share_class_id`. In an `ISIN`-keyed database you must cascade the change through every referencing table, risking missed references and downtime. This single scenario is the strongest practical argument for the surrogate-key discipline.

Q: What is the final self-review test the lesson recommends and why wait a week?
A: After a week you re-read the conceptual → logical → physical chain against one question: could a colleague implement it from the logical model alone? Waiting a week removes the author's short-term memory of unstated assumptions, so gaps that were "obvious" at the time now stand out as genuinely missing. It is a cheap way to simulate the outside reviewer the model must ultimately satisfy.

Q: An `INSERT` into `nav_history` fails with `SQLSTATE 23503` — what does that code mean and what do you check first?
A: `23503` is `foreign_key_violation`: the row's foreign-key value (e.g. its `share_class_id`) has no matching key in the referenced parent table, so Postgres refuses the insert. First check whether the parent `share_class` row actually exists and was committed in the same transaction ordering — a common cause is inserting the child before the parent, or referencing a surrogate id that was never created. The fix is to insert the parent first, correct the id, or wrap both inserts so the parent is visible when the child is checked.

Q: A bulk load into `share_class` aborts with `SQLSTATE 23505` — what failed and how do you find the offending value?
A: `23505` is `unique_violation`: a row collides with a `PRIMARY KEY` or `UNIQUE` constraint, typically a duplicate `ISIN` in the batch or one already present in the table. The error detail line names the constraint and prints the conflicting key value (e.g. `Key (isin)=(LU0123456789) already exists`), so you read `DETAIL:` to locate it. You then de-duplicate the source, or use `INSERT ... ON CONFLICT (isin) DO NOTHING`/`DO UPDATE` if upsert semantics are intended.

Q: What do `SQLSTATE 23502` and `SQLSTATE 23514` signify, and give a fund-data trigger for each?
A: `23502` is `not_null_violation` — a `NOT NULL` column received `NULL`, e.g. inserting a NAV row with no `nav_date`. `23514` is `check_violation` — a `CHECK` constraint failed, e.g. an `ISIN` that is not 12 characters or a negative `nav_per_share` rejected by `CHECK (nav_per_share >= 0)`. All four `23xxx` codes are the integrity-constraint class, so testing the SQLSTATE rather than the localized message is the portable way to branch on them.

Q: Why test the SQLSTATE code rather than the error message text when handling constraint failures in a loader?
A: SQLSTATE codes like `23505` are stable across Postgres versions and unaffected by the server's locale, whereas the human-readable message is localized and can be reworded between releases. A loader that branches on `if sqlstate == '23505'` keeps working when the database language is French or the message wording changes, while one that string-matches `"duplicate key"` silently breaks. This is why the docs explicitly recommend code-based handling for any program that must react to specific conditions.

Q: In a composite foreign key, what is the difference between `MATCH SIMPLE` and `MATCH FULL` when some referencing columns are null?
A: `MATCH SIMPLE` (the Postgres default) lets the row skip the foreign-key check if ANY of its referencing columns is null, so a partially-null composite FK is silently unchecked. `MATCH FULL` only lets the row skip the check if ALL referencing columns are null; a mix of null and non-null is guaranteed to fail. For a composite reference like (`register_id`, `account_id`) you usually want `MATCH FULL` so a half-populated link cannot slip past integrity checks.

Q: Is `MATCH PARTIAL` available in PostgreSQL, and what should you do if a design seems to need it?
A: No — `MATCH PARTIAL` is defined in the SQL standard but is not implemented in PostgreSQL; only `MATCH SIMPLE` and `MATCH FULL` exist. If a model appears to require partial matching, that is usually a sign the composite key should be split or the nullability rethought, because the semantics it wants (some columns matched, others ignored) are not enforceable. You enforce the intent with `MATCH FULL` plus `CHECK` constraints or a redesign rather than expecting `MATCH PARTIAL`.

Q: What does `DEFERRABLE INITIALLY DEFERRED` on a foreign key let you do, and when is it useful in fund data?
A: It postpones the foreign-key check from after each statement to transaction commit, so you can temporarily have rows that violate the constraint mid-transaction. This is useful for loading mutually-referencing or circular reference data — e.g. a master-feeder `fund` hierarchy where parent and child rows reference each other — and for reordering bulk loads without sequencing every insert. You declare it `... REFERENCES parent(id) DEFERRABLE INITIALLY DEFERRED`, and can flip a deferrable constraint per-transaction with `SET CONSTRAINTS ... IMMEDIATE`.

Q: Which constraint types accept the `DEFERRABLE` clause in Postgres, and what is the default?
A: Only `UNIQUE`, `PRIMARY KEY`, `EXCLUDE`, and `REFERENCES` (foreign key) constraints accept `DEFERRABLE`; `CHECK` and `NOT NULL` constraints are always checked immediately and cannot be deferred. The default for every constraint is `NOT DEFERRABLE`, checked right after each statement. You must explicitly declare `DEFERRABLE` (and optionally `INITIALLY DEFERRED`) at creation time, because you cannot defer a constraint that was not created as deferrable.

Q: You need a single un-`ISIN`'d share class allowed but no duplicate real `ISIN`s — how do you enforce that exactly?
A: A plain `UNIQUE (isin)` allows many null `ISIN`s because Postgres treats nulls as distinct, so it cannot cap the un-assigned class at one. Use `UNIQUE NULLS NOT DISTINCT (isin)` (Postgres 15+) so at most one `NULL` is permitted while real `ISIN`s stay unique, or a partial unique index `CREATE UNIQUE INDEX ON share_class (isin) WHERE isin IS NOT NULL` if you instead want unlimited nulls but unique non-nulls. The choice depends on whether the business rule is "one pending class" or "many pending classes".

Q: How would you write a partial unique index to enforce "only one current NAV row per share class" alongside full NAV history?
A: Keep the full history in `nav_history` keyed by (`share_class_id`, `nav_date`), add an `is_current` boolean, and enforce singularity with `CREATE UNIQUE INDEX ON nav_history (share_class_id) WHERE is_current` so only rows with `is_current = true` participate in the uniqueness check. The partial predicate means historical rows (with `is_current = false`) are exempt, so you never collide on them. This is the standard pattern for "current flag" designs without a separate current-only table.

Q: What is a partial index and why might you use one on a large fund table?
A: A partial index indexes only the rows matching a `WHERE` predicate, declared as `CREATE INDEX ... WHERE condition`, so it is smaller and cheaper to maintain than a full index. On a huge `order` table you might index only `WHERE status = 'PENDING'` because that is the slice queries hot-path, leaving settled orders un-indexed by that index. The catch is the planner only uses it when the query's predicate provably implies the index's predicate.

Q: How does an expression index help enforce a case-insensitive unique `ISIN`, and what is the gotcha?
A: You build `CREATE UNIQUE INDEX ON share_class (upper(isin))` so two rows differing only in case collide, indexing the computed value rather than the raw column. The gotcha is the index is only used when the query expresses the same expression — `WHERE upper(isin) = upper($1)` hits it, but `WHERE isin = $1` does not. For `ISIN` specifically the cleaner answer is a `CHECK` enforcing upper-case at write time, so the stored value is already canonical.

Q: How could a `CHECK` constraint enforce that the first two characters of an `ISIN` are a valid letter pair (country code)?
A: A regex `CHECK` such as `CHECK (isin ~ '^[A-Z]{2}[A-Z0-9]{9}[0-9]$')` requires two leading letters (the ISO 3166 alpha-2 country prefix), nine alphanumeric NSIN characters, and a trailing numeric check digit — the full ISO 6166 shape. It rejects lower-case, wrong length, and a non-digit check position at write time. It does not validate that the letters are a real country or that the Luhn digit is correct, which needs a function-based check.

Q: At a high level, how is an `ISIN` check digit computed, and why can't a simple `CHECK` length constraint catch a typo in it?
A: The 12th character is a Luhn (modulus-10 double-add-double) check digit over the first 11 positions, where letters are first expanded to numbers `A=10` through `Z=35`, alternate digits are doubled, the digit-sum is taken modulo 10, and the complement to 10 is the check digit. A length-only `CHECK` confirms 12 characters but a single mistyped character usually still yields 12 characters, so it passes length while being an invalid `ISIN`. Catching that requires a `CHECK` calling a Luhn-validation function, not just `char_length(isin) = 12`.

Q: How would you implement full `ISIN` validity (including the check digit) as a database constraint?
A: Write an `IMMUTABLE` PL/pgSQL or SQL function `is_valid_isin(text) returns boolean` that maps letters to numbers, runs the Luhn modulus-10 algorithm, and compares the computed check digit to the 12th character, then attach `CHECK (is_valid_isin(isin))` to the column. Marking it `IMMUTABLE` lets the planner and constraint system reuse it safely. This pushes the full ISO 6166 rule into the database so malformed identifiers are rejected at the source rather than during NAV distribution.

Q: A reviewer asks why you used `CHAR(2)` for a country code but a surrogate elsewhere — how do you defend the natural key here?
A: An ISO 3166 alpha-2 country code is short (2 characters), globally standardized, and effectively immutable, so it is a small stable natural key with no benefit from a surrogate — adding one would just inject an opaque join. The discipline is reasoned per-table key selection: surrogates earn their place where natural keys are wide, unstable, or absent, none of which applies to a country code. You would still back it with a reference table so foreign keys enforce the closed code list.

Q: When deriving the logical model, how do you turn a many-to-many between `investor` and `share_class` carrying a position into a junction entity?
A: You introduce a `holding` associative entity with a foreign key to each side (`investor_id`, `share_class_id`) plus the relationship's own attributes such as `units_held` and `as_of_date`. Its primary key is the composite of the two foreign keys when one holding per pair is allowed, or that pair plus `as_of_date` if you keep history. This resolves the unimplementable many-to-many into two one-to-many relationships and gives the position attributes a natural home.

Q: Walk through resolving a ternary relationship (investor, share class, distributor) — does one junction table suffice?
A: A genuine ternary association where the combination of all three parties is what carries meaning is modeled as a single associative entity with three foreign keys whose composite (or a surrogate plus a unique constraint over the three) is the key. You must first confirm it is truly ternary and not two separate binary relationships in disguise, because decomposing a real ternary into binaries loses information. If the business rule is "this investor holds this class through this distributor", one junction table with all three FKs is correct.

Q: How do you decide whether a junction table's primary key should be the composite of its foreign keys or a surrogate?
A: Use the composite of the foreign keys when the pair is genuinely unique, never reused, and not referenced by many child tables — it is self-documenting and enforces the rule directly. Switch to a surrogate when the composite is wide, when other tables need to reference a single short key, or when you must keep multiple historical versions of the same pair (so the natural composite is no longer unique on its own). The decision mirrors the general natural-vs-surrogate trade-off applied to the association.

Q: In Crow's Foot, how do you read a relationship drawn with a circle-and-crow's-foot on the child end and a bar on the parent end?
A: The bar on the parent end means exactly one parent, and the circle-plus-crow's-foot on the child end means zero-or-more children, so it reads "one parent has zero or more children, each child has exactly one parent". For fund-to-order this would say a fund relates to zero or more orders while every order belongs to exactly one fund. The inner symbol nearest the entity is the maximum cardinality (crow's foot = many) and the outer symbol is the minimum (circle = zero, bar = one).

Q: How do you draw a non-identifying optional relationship in Crow's Foot versus how IDEF1X would show the same thing?
A: In Crow's Foot, optionality is shown by a circle on the relevant end and the foreign key is simply a nullable attribute in the child, with no special line style for identifying-ness. IDEF1X instead distinguishes the relationship type itself: a non-identifying relationship is a dashed line with a square-cornered (independent) child box, signalling the parent key becomes a non-key FK. So Crow's Foot encodes cardinality/optionality on the line ends while IDEF1X additionally encodes key migration through line style and box corners.

Q: A vendor hands you an IDEF1X diagram with a solid line into a rounded-corner box — what does that tell you about the keys before you read any labels?
A: The solid line marks an identifying relationship and the rounded-corner box marks a dependent (weak) entity, so you immediately know the parent's primary key migrates into the child's primary key and the child cannot exist without its parent. That is the IDEF1X signature of something like a NAV record under its share class. Reading this from the geometry alone — before labels — is exactly the passive IDEF1X fluency the lesson expects.

Q: How would you convert that IDEF1X identifying relationship into a faithful Crow's Foot ERD plus Postgres DDL?
A: In Crow's Foot you draw a mandatory one-to-many (bar on the parent, bar-crow's-foot on the child) and note in the child that the parent key is part of the primary key. In DDL the child gets the parent key inside its `PRIMARY KEY` — e.g. `PRIMARY KEY (share_class_id, nav_date)` with `share_class_id` also a `FOREIGN KEY` — preserving the existence dependency. The key migration that IDEF1X drew visually becomes the composite primary key containing the foreign key.

Q: You forward-engineer DDL from your model, deploy it, then reverse-engineer in DBeaver and the diff shows an extra index in production — is that drift, and what do you do?
A: It is drift in the broad sense (model and database disagree), but you first classify it: an index is a physical-tuning artifact, so a DBA may have added it deliberately for a hot query. You decide whether to adopt it into the maintained physical model (so the model regains parity) or remove it as unsanctioned, and you record the decision. The point of the round-trip diff is to surface the divergence for a conscious choice, not to assume every difference is an error.

Q: The reverse-engineered model shows a column `nav_ccy VARCHAR(3)` that your logical model never mentioned — how do you triage this drift?
A: This is structural drift that matters: someone added a column in production outside the model, so you check whether it is in use, what populates it, and whether it should exist. If it is a legitimate need (per-class NAV currency), you add it to the logical and physical models with its proper type and a `FOREIGN KEY` to an ISO 4217 reference table; if it is dead, you remove it. Either way the model must be reconciled so it remains the trustworthy review artifact, because a column the model lacks is exactly the failure round-tripping exists to catch.

Q: Why is a DBeaver/erwin model-to-database compare run in CI more valuable than a one-off manual diff for a regulated administrator?
A: Running the compare automatically on every change turns drift detection into a continuous control rather than a periodic hope, so an out-of-band production change is caught the same day instead of at the next audit. For a fund administrator under DORA-style governance, a CI gate that fails when the deployed schema diverges from the reviewed model is demonstrable, repeatable evidence of change control. A manual diff depends on someone remembering to run it, which is precisely the gap auditors probe.

Q: A `CREATE TABLE ... PARTITION OF nav_history FOR VALUES FROM ('2026-06-01') TO ('2026-07-01')` fails with an overlap error — what is the cause and fix?
A: The new partition's range intersects an existing partition's range (or a row already in a `DEFAULT` partition would belong here), and Postgres forbids overlapping range partitions because routing would be ambiguous. You inspect existing bounds with `\d+ nav_history` or `pg_get_partkeydef`, find the conflicting partition, and adjust the new bounds so they are disjoint — remembering the upper bound is exclusive, so `... TO ('2026-07-01')` must equal the next partition's `FROM`. If the clash is with a `DEFAULT` partition, you may need to detach it, attach the new range, then re-create the default.

Q: Adding a new monthly partition by `ATTACH` is unexpectedly slow and takes a strong lock — what design choice likely caused it?
A: The table almost certainly has a `DEFAULT` partition, so to `ATTACH` a new month Postgres must scan the entire default partition to prove none of its rows fall in the incoming range, which is slow and takes a strong lock. The remedy is to avoid a `DEFAULT` partition for time-series NAV history and instead pre-create future-month partitions, so each `ATTACH` is a cheap metadata operation. This is why the canonical NAV-history design omits a default partition.

Q: How would you automate monthly partition creation for `nav_history` so a missing future partition never causes an insert failure?
A: You schedule a job (a `cron`/`systemd` timer running `psql`, or the `pg_partman` extension's maintenance function) that pre-creates the next several months' partitions ahead of time. `pg_partman` specifically manages range partitions, creating future child tables and optionally retiring old ones on a retention policy. Pre-creating ahead matters because without a `DEFAULT` partition an insert whose `nav_date` has no partition fails with a "no partition found" error, so the automation must always stay ahead of incoming dates.

Q: What error do you get inserting a NAV row whose `nav_date` falls outside every defined partition (no default), and how is it surfaced?
A: The insert fails with `ERROR: no partition of relation "nav_history" found for row` (SQLSTATE `23514`, check_violation class), because Postgres cannot route a row whose key matches no partition and there is no catch-all. The `DETAIL` line shows the offending partition-key value. The fix is to create the partition for that date (ideally in advance via automation) or to add a `DEFAULT` partition if catching strays is acceptable — accepting the slower future `ATTACH` that implies.

Q: How does sub-partitioning work, and when might NAV history justify partitioning by range then by list?
A: You declare a partition as itself partitioned — `CREATE TABLE ... PARTITION OF nav_history FOR VALUES FROM (...) TO (...) PARTITION BY LIST (fund_domicile)` — so each monthly range partition is further split by a list key. It is justified only when queries consistently filter on both keys and the volume per cell warrants it, since each extra level multiplies partition count and planning overhead. For most NAV history a single range level on `nav_date` is enough; reach for sub-partitioning only when measured access patterns demand the second pruning dimension.

Q: You range-partition `nav_history` on `nav_date` but queries are still scanning every partition — what is the first thing to check?
A: Check that the query's `WHERE` clause filters on the partition key `nav_date` with a value the planner can compare to bounds; pruning only happens when a partition-key predicate is present. A frequent culprit is wrapping the column in a function (`WHERE date_trunc('month', nav_date) = ...`) or comparing it to a non-immutable expression, which blocks the planner from matching bounds. Rewrite the predicate as a plain range on `nav_date` (`nav_date >= '2026-06-01' AND nav_date < '2026-07-01'`) so pruning engages.

Q: How can you confirm in an `EXPLAIN` plan that partition pruning actually happened?
A: Run `EXPLAIN` (or `EXPLAIN ANALYZE`) and confirm the plan lists only the relevant child partitions as scan nodes rather than an `Append` over every partition; pruned partitions simply do not appear. `EXPLAIN ANALYZE` may also show `(never executed)` for partitions removed by runtime pruning when bounds depend on parameters. If you still see every monthly partition in the plan, pruning failed and you revisit the predicate or the partition key.

Q: What is the difference between plan-time and run-time partition pruning, and why does it matter for a parameterized NAV query?
A: Plan-time pruning happens when the partition-key value is a constant known at planning, so the planner discards partitions before execution; run-time pruning handles cases where the value comes from a bound parameter, a subquery, or a join, eliminating partitions during execution. It matters because a prepared/parameterized query (`WHERE nav_date = $1`) relies on run-time pruning, and older Postgres versions or certain plan shapes may prune less aggressively. Confirming run-time pruning via `EXPLAIN ANALYZE` ensures a parameterized NAV lookup is not silently scanning all months.

Q: When is `BRIN` the wrong choice for `nav_history` even though the table is huge?
A: `BRIN` relies on physical block-range ordering correlating with the indexed column, so if `nav_history` is heavily updated, reorganized, or loaded out of date order, the min/max per block range become loose and `BRIN` stops pruning effectively. In that case range scans degrade toward a full scan and a B-tree on `nav_date` is more reliable. `BRIN` shines specifically on append-only, naturally date-ordered history; break that assumption and its tiny size no longer compensates for poor selectivity.

Q: A foreign-key column on `order` has no index and deletes from `share_class` are slow — what is the mechanism and fix?
A: Postgres does not auto-create an index on the referencing side of a foreign key, so every parent delete or key update must scan the child table to check for referencing rows, which is slow on a large `order` table. The fix is to add an index on the FK column, `CREATE INDEX ON "order" (share_class_id)`, so the referential-integrity check and any cascade become index lookups. Indexing foreign keys is a standard physical-tuning step precisely because the parent-side cost is otherwise hidden.

Q: Why might `ON DELETE SET NULL` be the right action for `order.batch_id` but wrong for `nav_history.share_class_id`?
A: `ON DELETE SET NULL` is appropriate when the child can meaningfully exist without the parent — an order whose settlement batch is removed simply becomes un-batched, so nulling `batch_id` is sensible and the relationship was optional anyway. For `nav_history.share_class_id` the share class is the very thing the NAV belongs to, so nulling it would orphan a NAV with no meaning and likely violate `NOT NULL`; here you want `ON DELETE RESTRICT` to forbid deleting a class that has NAV history. The action must match whether the relationship is optional or existence-defining.

Q: Compare `ON DELETE RESTRICT` and `ON DELETE NO ACTION` in Postgres — do they behave identically?
A: Both prevent deleting a parent that still has children, but they differ in timing: `RESTRICT` checks immediately and cannot be deferred, while `NO ACTION` (the default) performs the check at the end of the statement and can be deferred if the constraint is `DEFERRABLE`. So a `DEFERRABLE INITIALLY DEFERRED` `NO ACTION` foreign key allows you to delete the parent and re-insert/repoint children within the same transaction, whereas `RESTRICT` would reject the parent delete outright. For reference data you usually want `NO ACTION`/`RESTRICT` to fail loudly rather than `CASCADE`.

Q: How would you model a slowly-changing fund attribute set at the logical level so history is preserved, and is this 8.2.1 or 8.2.4 territory?
A: At the logical level you add a temporal pair `valid_from`/`valid_to` (and often a current flag) to the entity, changing its key from `fund_id` to (`fund_id`, `valid_from`) so multiple versions coexist, with each row stating the period its attribute values were correct. Modeling the decision to keep history is logical; the SCD typology (SCD2 etc.) and the point-in-time query are the dimensional-modeling treatment in 8.2.4. In 8.2.1 you justify the temporal key and its normal form; 8.2.4 builds the star around it.

Q: What is a temporal exclusion constraint and how could it stop overlapping `valid_from`/`valid_to` periods for one fund?
A: An `EXCLUDE` constraint using a range type and the `&&` (overlap) operator — `EXCLUDE USING gist (fund_id WITH =, daterange(valid_from, valid_to) WITH &&)` — forbids two rows for the same `fund_id` whose validity ranges overlap. It needs the `btree_gist` extension to mix the equality column with the range column in one GiST index. This enforces non-overlapping history at the database level instead of trusting the loader, which is exactly the rigor a regulated point-in-time model wants.

Q: How do you choose between a single-table-inheritance discriminator, a table-per-subtype, and a shared-supertype design for heterogeneous securities?
A: Single table with a `type` discriminator is simplest and fastest to query but leaves many type-specific columns null and weakens `NOT NULL` enforcement; table-per-subtype keeps each type's columns mandatory and clean but needs a `UNION` or branching to query all securities together; shared-supertype (a `security` table plus subtype tables sharing its key) balances both by holding common attributes once and type-specific attributes in linked tables. You pick based on how often you query across all types versus per type, and how strictly the type-specific mandatory columns must be enforced. For heterogeneous fund products the shared-supertype pattern is the usual compromise.

Q: In a supertype/subtype design, how do you prevent a `security` row from having both an `equity_detail` and a `bond_detail` child?
A: Add a `security_type` discriminator on the supertype and make each subtype table carry that type in its key or a `CHECK`, then enforce that the subtype FK references only same-typed rows — e.g. include `security_type` in the composite FK so `equity_detail` can only reference securities of type `'EQUITY'`. This pushes the "exactly one subtype, matching the discriminator" rule into the schema rather than application code. Without it, nothing structurally stops a single security from sprouting contradictory subtype rows.

Q: A logical model lists `fund_name` on both `fund` and `share_class` — walk through the dependency analysis that condemns it.
A: `fund_name` is functionally dependent on `fund_id`; in `share_class` the key is `share_class_id`, and `fund_id` is a non-key attribute, so `share_class_id → fund_id → fund_name` is a transitive dependency that violates 3NF. The remedy is to drop `fund_name` from `share_class` and reach it by joining through `fund_id` to `fund`. If a reviewer insists on keeping it for read speed, it must be reclassified as a documented denormalization with a stated refresh mechanism, not left as an undefended logical-model column.

Q: Give a concrete BCNF-but-not-3NF-distinguishing example using fund reference data with overlapping candidate keys.
A: Suppose a table has candidate keys (`fund_code`) and (`isin_at_launch`), and an attribute `registrar` is determined by `isin_at_launch` while `isin_at_launch` is part of a candidate key — `isin_at_launch → registrar` has a determinant that is not a superkey of the whole relation, so it violates BCNF even though 3NF (which exempts dependencies into prime attributes) may tolerate it. The fix decomposes so each determinant is a superkey of its table. These overlapping-candidate-key cases are exactly where 3NF and BCNF diverge, which is why you check determinants are superkeys, not just that non-key columns depend only on the key.

Q: How would you defend leaving a reporting table at 2NF (deliberately denormalized) in a model review?
A: You state the baseline normalized form it departs from, name the exact redundancy (e.g. `fund_name` and `share_class_name` carried alongside the NAV facts), cite the measured read pattern that justifies it (a fixed regulatory report joining the same four tables under SLA), and name the refresh mechanism keeping copies consistent (a nightly rebuild from the 3NF source of truth). The review test is that the denormalization is a recorded, justified, derived structure — not the source of truth and not silent drift. Stating all four elements turns "it's denormalized for speed" into a checkable, auditable claim.

Q: What does `pg_dump --schema-only` give you for round-trip and drift work, and what does it miss?
A: `pg_dump --schema-only` emits the DDL for tables, types, keys, indexes, and constraints, giving you a textual snapshot of the deployed schema you can diff against a previously committed baseline to detect drift in version control. It captures structure but not data, and it may not perfectly round-trip every object ordering or comment, so it complements rather than replaces a model-aware compare in DBeaver/erwin. Committing the `--schema-only` output to Git is a lightweight way to make schema changes reviewable as diffs.

Q: When migrating a non-partitioned `nav_history` to a partitioned design with minimal downtime, what is the safe high-level sequence?
A: Create the new partitioned parent and its partitions, backfill historical data into them (often by attaching the existing table as a partition or copying in batches), build matching indexes, then swap names inside a transaction so the application sees the partitioned table — verifying the partition-key predicate is included in the primary key first. You confirm the PK includes `nav_date` (or you cannot make it partitioned), validate row counts and constraints, and keep the old table until the swap is proven. The risky points are the PK-must-include-partition-key rule and ensuring no rows fall outside the defined partitions during cutover.

Q: After a corporate action splits one share class into two, why does the surrogate-key design make the data fix local, and what exactly do you touch?
A: Because `share_class_id` is an opaque stable surrogate, the new class is just one new `share_class` row with its own `ISIN`, and existing orders/NAV/holdings keep pointing at their unchanged surrogate ids — nothing cascades. You insert the new class, set any `valid_to` on the superseded class if you keep temporal history, and route post-event rows to the new id; the old `ISIN` stays as historical attribute data. In an `ISIN`-keyed design the same event would force cascading key changes across every referencing table, which is precisely the fragility the surrogate discipline removes.

Q: How would you verify a reverse-engineered physical model is normal-form-equivalent to your logical model, not just structurally similar?
A: Beyond matching tables, columns, and keys, you re-derive each reconstructed table's functional dependencies and confirm the same normal-form claim holds — that no transitive or partial dependency crept in via a denormalization the model did not record. A structural diff can pass while a column was widened or a lookup inlined, silently dropping a table from 3NF to 2NF. The check is to restate dependencies per table and compare normal-form labels, because normal form is a property of dependencies, not of the column list a diff tool sees.

Q: A `numeric` NAV column was deployed as `NUMERIC` with no precision/scale — why is that a physical-model defect and how do you correct it?
A: Unqualified `NUMERIC` in Postgres stores values of any precision and scale, so it neither documents the intended 6-decimal NAV convention nor rejects an over-precise value, defeating the point of choosing exact decimal for money. The defect is that the constraint that makes the type self-documenting and validating is missing. You correct it to `NUMERIC(18,6)` (or whatever the domain dictates) so storage, rounding to the agreed scale, and reconciliation-to-the-cent behavior are explicit and enforced rather than left to whatever the inserter happened to supply.
