# Phase 2 · 5.2.2 Orchestrated ELT (build-vs-buy) — 150 self-test questions

Q: What does "build-vs-buy" mean in the context of orchestrated ELT?
A: It is the architectural decision between paying a managed vendor like Fivetran to run and maintain ingestion connectors ("buy") versus self-hosting an open-source tool like Airbyte that you operate yourself ("build"). The "buy" side trades money for engineer-hours and reliability; the "build" side trades a lower cash bill for the ongoing labour of patching connectors, fixing schema drift, and keeping the platform alive. As an architect you must price both sides for a specific estate rather than picking on ideology.

Q: What does the acronym MAR stand for in Fivetran's pricing?
A: MAR stands for Monthly Active Rows, the unit Fivetran bills on. It counts the distinct rows synced from a source to your destination within a single calendar month, identified by their primary keys. Your bill is essentially `total MAR × spend rate`, so understanding MAR is the core of pricing the "buy" option.

Q: How is a single Monthly Active Row defined in Fivetran's model?
A: A Monthly Active Row is a distinct primary key that was inserted, updated, or deleted in your destination during the calendar month. Fivetran tracks rows by their primary key, so "active" means the row changed (or was newly created) at least once that month. Rows that exist but never change in a month do not count.

Q: If the same row is updated 50 times in one month, how many MAR does it consume?
A: Exactly one. Fivetran counts each distinct primary key only once per calendar month regardless of how many times it is updated or re-synced that month. This is why high-frequency syncing of a small, churny table does not multiply your bill the way row-by-row event billing would.

Q: Why does syncing every 15 minutes instead of every 24 hours not necessarily increase your Fivetran MAR cost?
A: Because MAR is counted per distinct row per month, not per sync. A row that changes ten times a day is still one MAR for the month whether you capture each change at 15-minute or daily cadence. Sync frequency affects freshness (and which plan tier you need), not the raw MAR count for already-changing rows.

Q: Which row operations contribute to MAR?
A: Inserts of new rows, updates to existing rows, and deletes all contribute to MAR. Deletes count when the Capture Deletes feature is enabled, because Fivetran records the deletion as a soft-delete update to that row. The common thread is that the row's state changed during the month.

Q: Does Fivetran count the initial historical backfill (first full sync) toward MAR?
A: No. Initial syncs and historical/full re-imports are excluded from billable MAR, and new connections also get a free initial window. This matters because a one-time multi-billion-row backfill of a fund's trade history would be ruinous if it were billed, so Fivetran deliberately makes the first load free and bills only ongoing change.

Q: A teammate claims "Fivetran charges per row synced." Correct them precisely.
A: Fivetran charges per distinct active row per month, not per row synced. The same row synced a thousand times in a month is one MAR; unchanged rows pulled during a re-sync are not billed; and the initial historical load is free. The mental model is "pay for change volume per month," not "pay per row movement."

Q: What is the relationship `total monthly usage × spend rate = connection monthly cost`?
A: It is Fivetran's headline cost formula: your total billable MAR for a connection multiplied by a per-row spend rate gives that connection's monthly charge. The spend rate is not flat — it declines as your volume grows and depends on your plan tier. So doubling MAR does not exactly double cost.

Q: Why does Fivetran's per-row spend rate decline as volume increases?
A: Fivetran uses tiered, consumption-based pricing where the marginal rate per MAR drops as you cross higher volume bands. The intent is volume discounting: a customer moving 50 million MAR pays a lower rate per row than one moving 50 thousand. The architectural consequence is that cost-per-row is not constant, so you cannot linearly extrapolate a small pilot's bill to production.

Q: How does MAR aggregate across multiple connections in one Fivetran account?
A: Each connection contributes its own MAR, and these sum to a total account MAR that determines which volume-discount band your spend rate falls into. Two connections syncing identical data each count their rows independently, but the combined total can push you into a cheaper marginal tier. This is why consolidating into one account can lower the effective rate.

Q: What are Fivetran's four main plan tiers?
A: Free, Standard, Enterprise, and Business Critical. They differ in features such as sync frequency, security and compliance guarantees, and support — for example Standard offers 15-minute syncs while Enterprise offers 1-minute syncs, and Business Critical adds customer-managed encryption keys and PCI DSS Level 1 certification. Higher tiers cost more per MAR but unlock the controls a regulated estate may require.

Q: What does the Fivetran Free tier offer and where is its ceiling?
A: The Free plan covers up to 500,000 MAR per month for connections (with a much smaller allowance for activations). It lets a team prototype ingestion without a contract, but a real fund-administration estate will blow past 500,000 MAR quickly, so Free is a proof-of-concept tool, not a production answer.

Q: Why might a regulated fund administrator be forced onto Fivetran's Business Critical tier specifically?
A: Because Business Critical adds controls that compliance demands — customer-managed encryption keys (so the customer controls the key material) and PCI DSS Level 1 certification, among other hardened security features. A regulated EU fund handling investor and payment data often cannot accept the lower tiers' shared-key model. The architectural point is that compliance, not data volume alone, can dictate the tier and therefore the price.

Q: What is the "long tail" of SaaS sources, and why does managed ELT win there?
A: The long tail is the large set of small, third-party SaaS systems each contributing modest data — a CRM, a ticketing tool, a marketing platform, an HR system. Managed ELT wins here because the vendor maintains hundreds of pre-built, API-aware connectors and absorbs the constant breakage when those SaaS APIs change. Building and babysitting a bespoke connector for each low-value source would cost far more engineer-time than the MAR bill.

Q: Why is connector maintenance the hidden cost of the "build" (self-hosted) option?
A: Because SaaS APIs, schemas, and auth flows change continuously, and every change can silently break a self-hosted connector that nobody is paid to watch. The cash you save on a vendor bill reappears as engineer-hours spent diagnosing failed syncs, upgrading connector versions, and handling schema drift. Pricing build-vs-buy honestly means putting a salary number on that maintenance line.

Q: How do you convert "engineer babysitting connectors" into a number for a build-vs-buy note?
A: Estimate the fraction of an engineer's time the self-hosted platform consumes — connector upgrades, sync failures, schema-drift fixes, on-call — and multiply by the loaded annual salary. For example, if Airbyte connectors absorb 20 percent of a 90,000-euro engineer, that is 18,000 euros per year of hidden "build" cost to set against Fivetran's MAR bill. Without that figure the comparison is dishonest because it pretends self-hosting is free.

Q: Define the "crossover point" in a Fivetran-vs-Airbyte decision.
A: The crossover point is the data-volume (MAR) level at which Fivetran's managed bill equals the fully-loaded cost of an engineer maintaining a self-hosted Airbyte for the same sources. Below it, managed ELT is cheaper because the MAR bill is small relative to a salary; above it, self-hosting wins because the MAR bill outgrows the fixed maintenance labour. Stating this number is the whole point of the build-vs-buy exercise.

Q: Why does the crossover point favour Fivetran at low volume and Airbyte at high volume?
A: Fivetran's cost scales with MAR (a variable cost), while self-hosted Airbyte's dominant cost is a roughly fixed engineer-maintenance line that you pay whether you move one source or twenty. At low volume the fixed salary dwarfs a tiny MAR bill, so buying is cheaper; at high volume the MAR bill grows past the fixed salary, so building is cheaper. The two cost curves cross where variable equals fixed.

Q: A source has very high MAR but a perfectly maintained Airbyte connector exists for it. Which way does the crossover lean?
A: It leans toward self-hosting (build), because the high MAR makes Fivetran's variable bill large while the maintained connector keeps Airbyte's marginal effort low. The maintenance cost — usually the killer for self-hosting — is mostly absorbed by the connector already existing and being stable, so you mainly pay infrastructure plus light oversight. High volume plus a stable connector is the classic "build" quadrant.

Q: A source has tiny MAR but a flaky, frequently-breaking API. Which way does the crossover lean?
A: It leans toward managed ELT (buy), because the MAR bill is trivial while the flaky API would otherwise consume large amounts of engineer-time to keep a self-hosted connector alive. You are effectively paying Fivetran a small MAR fee to absorb the maintenance pain you would otherwise carry. Low volume plus high breakage is the classic "buy" quadrant.

Q: Why are "core banking, mainframe, and data-sovereignty" sources the place where managed ELT typically loses?
A: Because these are the systems where the data is most sensitive and most constrained — it may be legally or contractually forbidden to send to a third-party SaaS cloud, and the source protocols (mainframe, core banking) are bespoke rather than long-tail SaaS. The managed vendor's main advantage (maintained SaaS connectors) does not apply, while its main disadvantage (data leaving your perimeter) becomes a hard blocker. So these stay in-house regardless of the MAR math.

Q: What is "data sovereignty" and why is it a hard constraint for an EU fund estate?
A: Data sovereignty is the requirement that data be stored and processed under, and physically within, a particular jurisdiction's legal control — for an EU fund, often that data must remain within the EU and under EU law. It is a hard (not soft) constraint because regulators, fund prospectuses, and client contracts can flatly prohibit certain data from crossing borders or being processed by a foreign-controlled provider. No cost saving justifies violating it, so it can veto managed ELT outright.

Q: How can Fivetran's data-residency feature partially address EU sovereignty concerns?
A: Fivetran lets you pin a destination's data-residency region — for the EU this is the Europe (Frankfurt) region — so that customer data is kept within EU borders rather than routed through the US. This satisfies a "data must stay in the EU" requirement for many sources. It does not, however, satisfy a requirement that data never touch a third-party-controlled environment at all.

Q: What is Fivetran's Hybrid Deployment model and what problem does it solve?
A: In Hybrid Deployment, a Fivetran-provided agent runs inside your own network and performs the actual data processing locally, while Fivetran's cloud acts only as the control plane that orchestrates and configures pipelines. It solves the case where you want Fivetran's managed connectors and orchestration but cannot let the data itself leave your secure perimeter. The data stays local; only metadata and control signals talk to Fivetran's SaaS.

Q: For a sovereignty-constrained source, why might Hybrid Deployment still be unacceptable to a compliance team?
A: Because even though the data stays in your network, the Fivetran control plane still orchestrates the pipeline and receives metadata, and the agent maintains constant communication with a foreign-controlled SaaS. A strict reading of sovereignty rules — "no third-party control plane touches this system" — can reject any dependency on an external orchestrator. The architect must check whether the constraint is about data location or about control and dependency.

Q: In the Do task, six internal systems and four SaaS sources are split how between build and buy?
A: The four maintained-connector SaaS sources are the natural "buy" candidates for Fivetran, because they are long-tail and the vendor absorbs their API churn. The six internal systems — core banking, transfer agency, fund accounting, etc. — are the natural "build"/in-house candidates, because some cannot leave the EU perimeter and they lack off-the-shelf maintained connectors. The note's job is to make that split explicit and justified.

Q: How would you structure the half-page build-vs-buy note for a fund admin?
A: State the estate (6 internal, 4 SaaS), estimate Fivetran MAR cost for the SaaS sources, estimate engineer-hours to self-host the internal ones, give the crossover point, and flag which internal sources are off-limits to managed ELT for sovereignty. Keep it decision-oriented: each source gets a build/buy verdict with a one-line reason. The deliverable is a recommendation an architecture board can sign, not an essay.

Q: Which internal fund-admin sources are most likely "off-limits" to any managed ELT vendor?
A: Core banking ledgers, the transfer-agency register of investor holdings, fund-accounting/NAV systems, and anything carrying personal investor data under strict residency rules. These hold the most sensitive and most regulated data, often under explicit "data must not leave the perimeter / EU" obligations. They stay self-hosted regardless of the MAR economics.

Q: Why is a transfer-agency (TA) register a poor candidate for sending to a managed SaaS cloud?
A: The TA register is the authoritative record of who owns which units of a fund — investor identities, holdings, and transactions — making it both highly sensitive personal/financial data and core to the fund's legal operation. Sending it to a third-party-controlled cloud raises GDPR, residency, and confidentiality issues that usually forbid it. It is a textbook "build, keep inside the perimeter" source.

Q: How does NAV (Net Asset Value) data flow relate to the build-vs-buy decision?
A: NAV is the per-unit valuation produced by fund accounting and is both highly sensitive and tightly controlled, so the systems that compute and store it are typically in-scope for sovereignty and stay self-hosted. Downstream, non-sensitive distribution of published NAVs to external SaaS reporting tools might be a managed-ELT candidate. The architect separates the regulated core (build) from the publishable periphery (potentially buy).

Q: Why are EMT and EPT files more of a downstream concern than a managed-ELT ingestion target?
A: EMT (European MiFID Template) and EPT (European PRIIPs Template) are standardised regulatory cost/risk disclosure files produced from internal data and exchanged with distributors. They are outputs of the regulated core rather than long-tail SaaS sources, so they are not the kind of system a Fivetran connector ingests. Their existence reinforces that the sensitive, regulated data lives in self-hosted systems that managed ELT should not touch.

Q: How do LEI and ISIN identifiers relate to deciding which sources stay in-house?
A: ISIN identifies securities and LEI identifies legal entities; both are reference keys that flow through the most sensitive internal systems (fund accounting, TA, instrument masters). The systems that are authoritative for instrument and entity data are usually part of the regulated core and stay self-hosted. They are useful as join keys but their host systems are typically build-side, not managed-ELT candidates.

Q: Why does DORA strengthen the case against routing core feeds through a managed ELT vendor?
A: DORA (the EU Digital Operational Resilience Act) imposes strict third-party ICT risk-management, oversight, and exit-planning obligations on financial entities and their critical providers. Pushing a core banking or TA feed through Fivetran turns the vendor into a regulated third-party dependency you must assess, monitor, and be able to exit. That governance burden can tip a marginal source toward staying in-house even when the MAR cost looks attractive.

Q: How do UCITS/AIFMD obligations influence the perimeter you draw around managed ELT?
A: UCITS and AIFMD govern how funds and their managers handle investor protection, depositary oversight, and data, which constrains where holdings, valuations, and investor data may be processed. Data tied to these obligations generally must remain under controlled, often EU-resident, processing. That pushes the corresponding systems into the self-hosted "build" set rather than a third-party managed cloud.

Q: What is Airbyte's role in the build-vs-buy comparison?
A: Airbyte is the open-source, self-hosted connector platform that represents the "build" option you price against Fivetran's "buy" option. Running it yourself gives you full control and keeps data inside your perimeter, at the cost of operating the platform and maintaining connectors. It is the concrete baseline for estimating the engineer-hours side of the crossover calculation.

Q: What is `abctl` and why does it matter for self-hosting Airbyte today?
A: `abctl` is Airbyte's official command-line tool for installing and managing a local/self-hosted Airbyte deployment. It is the current recommended way to stand up Airbyte because it provisions a Kubernetes-based install under the hood rather than the deprecated Docker Compose path. Knowing it exists matters because build-vs-buy estimates must reflect the real, current operational tool, not an outdated quickstart.

Q: How does `abctl` actually run Airbyte under the hood?
A: `abctl` uses kind (Kubernetes in Docker) to create a local Kubernetes cluster running inside a Docker container, then deploys Airbyte into it using Helm with the official Airbyte chart. This lets a single laptop preview what a production Kubernetes deployment looks like without you provisioning a real cluster. The operational implication is that "self-hosting Airbyte" now means "operating Kubernetes," which raises the maintenance estimate.

Q: Why was Airbyte's Docker Compose deployment deprecated, and what replaced it?
A: Docker Compose was deprecated because Kubernetes became the de-facto production standard, offering better scalability, resource management, and a clean path from local to production. It was replaced by `abctl`, which deploys to a kind-based Kubernetes cluster locally and mirrors the production Helm/Kubernetes architecture. The `--migrate` flag that once helped move off Compose has itself been removed, so the recommended path is a fresh deployment.

Q: A colleague's old runbook says "deploy Airbyte with `docker compose up`." Why is that runbook out of date?
A: Because Airbyte officially deprecated the Docker Compose deployment; it is no longer supported and the recommended install is `abctl`, which uses kind plus Helm. Following the old runbook may appear to work with stale images but leaves you on an unsupported path with no upgrade story. The current guidance is a fresh `abctl` deployment, not a Compose migration.

Q: When you self-host Airbyte for production, what deployment technology should you assume?
A: A real Kubernetes cluster deployed via the official Airbyte Helm chart, since the local `abctl`/kind setup is explicitly designed to preview that production shape. This means the maintenance estimate in a build-vs-buy note must include Kubernetes operations — upgrades, scaling, monitoring, and cluster maintenance. That is a meaningful chunk of the "engineer babysitting" cost.

Q: Name three recurring operational tasks that make self-hosted Airbyte a maintenance line item.
A: Upgrading the Airbyte platform and individual connector versions, handling connector breakages when source APIs or schemas change, and operating the underlying Kubernetes infrastructure (scaling, monitoring, resource tuning, and incident response). Each is ongoing rather than one-time. Summed and priced against an engineer's salary, they form the fixed cost on the "build" side of the crossover.

Q: Why does "free software" not mean "free platform" for Airbyte?
A: Because Airbyte OSS has no licence fee, but running it costs infrastructure plus the engineer-time to operate Kubernetes, upgrade connectors, and fix breakages. The total cost of ownership is dominated by that labour, which is exactly the figure you compare against Fivetran's MAR bill. Treating "free" as "zero cost" is the most common build-vs-buy mistake.

Q: How would you estimate Fivetran MAR cost for the four SaaS sources in the Do task?
A: Estimate each source's monthly change volume — distinct rows inserted, updated, or deleted per month — sum to a total MAR, then apply the plan's spend rate (remembering it declines at higher volume and that initial backfills are free). For four modest SaaS sources this is usually a small number of MAR, so the bill is typically low. Document your row-volume assumptions so the estimate is auditable.

Q: How would you estimate the engineer-hours to self-host the six internal sources?
A: Estimate initial build effort (connector configuration or custom connectors, Kubernetes setup) plus a recurring monthly maintenance allowance (connector and platform upgrades, schema-drift fixes, on-call), then convert hours to cost at a loaded rate. The recurring figure is what dominates over time and feeds the crossover calculation. Be explicit that some internal sources have no off-the-shelf connector and cost more to build.

Q: What does it mean for a build-vs-buy verdict if your estimated MAR bill is far below the maintenance salary?
A: It means you are well below the crossover point, so managed ELT (buy) is cheaper for that scope and the recommendation should lean toward Fivetran for the SaaS sources. The cash bill is small relative to the engineer-time you would otherwise spend, so paying the vendor is the rational choice. You would only build when volume grows enough to invert that comparison.

Q: Why can't you state a single universal crossover point that applies to every estate?
A: Because the crossover depends on your specific MAR volume, your plan's spend rate, your engineers' loaded cost, and how stable your connectors are — all of which vary by organisation. A churny high-volume estate crosses over at a different point than a low-volume one. The skill is computing the crossover for the estate in front of you, not memorising a magic number.

Q: How does connector stability shift the crossover point independent of volume?
A: Stable, well-maintained connectors lower the maintenance labour on the build side, pushing the crossover toward lower volumes (making self-hosting attractive sooner). Flaky connectors raise maintenance labour, pushing the crossover toward higher volumes (making managed ELT attractive longer). So two estates with identical MAR can have opposite verdicts purely on connector reliability.

Q: Why is "an engineer babysitting Airbyte forever" the failure mode of getting build-vs-buy wrong?
A: If you self-host below the crossover point, you commit an engineer to perpetual platform and connector maintenance for sources whose tiny MAR bill Fivetran would have absorbed cheaply. You have spent salary to save a trivial vendor fee. The note exists precisely to catch this: pay the MAR bill where it is cheaper than the labour.

Q: Why is "a surprise MAR bill" the opposite failure mode?
A: If you buy (Fivetran) above the crossover point for a high-volume, churny source, the variable MAR cost can balloon past what a fixed-cost self-hosted setup would have cost. Because MAR scales with change volume, a source that suddenly churns more can spike the bill unexpectedly. The note guards against this by self-hosting high-volume sources where the fixed maintenance cost is the cheaper, more predictable option.

Q: Why does managed ELT give more predictable operational behaviour but less predictable cost than self-hosting?
A: Managed ELT removes operational unpredictability (the vendor keeps connectors working) but introduces cost unpredictability, because MAR scales with change volume you do not fully control. Self-hosting flips this: cost is a relatively fixed salary, but operations are unpredictable because breakages land on your team. Build-vs-buy is partly a choice about which kind of risk you would rather carry.

Q: For a churny, high-MAR internal source that *could* legally use managed ELT, what is the tension?
A: The high MAR makes Fivetran's variable bill large and unpredictable, favouring self-hosting on cost grounds, even though there is no legal barrier to buying. You weigh the predictable fixed maintenance cost of building against the volatile MAR cost of buying. This is the case where pure economics, not sovereignty, decides, and high volume usually tips it toward build.

Q: Why should the build-vs-buy note flag sovereignty constraints *separately* from cost?
A: Because sovereignty is a hard veto, not a number you trade off — a source that legally cannot leave the perimeter stays in-house even if managed ELT would be cheaper. Mixing it into the cost comparison risks "saving money" by violating a regulation. The note should first remove sovereignty-blocked sources from the managed pool, then run the cost crossover only on what remains.

Q: What is the correct order of operations when classifying sources in the note?
A: First apply the sovereignty/regulatory filter to remove any source that cannot legally use managed ELT, marking it build-only. Then, for the remaining eligible sources, compute the cost crossover (MAR bill versus maintenance labour) to assign build or buy on economics. Hard constraints precede soft optimisation, so you never accidentally optimise a forbidden source onto a vendor.

Q: Why is the spend rate, not just MAR count, essential to a correct Fivetran estimate?
A: Because cost is `MAR × spend rate`, and the spend rate varies by plan tier and declines with volume. Estimating MAR alone tells you change volume but not money; you can only get the bill by applying the correct, tier-and-volume-dependent rate. Using a flat per-row rate over- or under-states cost, especially at high volume where discounts kick in.

Q: How does Fivetran's exclusion of unchanged rows during re-syncs protect you from runaway bills?
A: When Fivetran re-imports a table, rows that did not actually change are not counted as MAR, so a defensive full re-sync of a large but stable table does not generate a huge bill. Only genuine changes count. This means you are billed on real change volume, not on operational re-reads, which keeps the cost tied to business activity.

Q: What is the difference between a "connector," a "source," and a "destination" in this ingestion picture?
A: A source is the system you read from (a SaaS API, a database), a destination is where data lands (a warehouse or object store), and a connector is the configured pipe that moves data from a specific source into the destination on a sync schedule. In managed ELT the vendor supplies and maintains the connector; in self-hosting you operate it. The build-vs-buy choice is really about who owns the connector's lifecycle.

Q: Why does "maintained connectors" appear repeatedly as the deciding advantage of managed ELT?
A: Because the single largest hidden cost of self-hosting is keeping connectors working as source APIs and schemas change, and a managed vendor absorbs exactly that work across its whole customer base. For any long-tail SaaS source with a vendor-maintained connector, you are mostly paying to outsource maintenance. Where no maintained connector exists, that advantage evaporates and the calculus shifts toward building.

Q: A SaaS source has *no* maintained Fivetran connector. How does that affect the decision?
A: The main reason to buy — outsourced connector maintenance — disappears, because you would have to build or wait for a connector anyway. That pushes the source toward self-hosting (where at least you control the connector) or toward a different tool. Always check connector availability before assuming a SaaS source is a "buy"; a missing connector quietly removes the buy advantage.

Q: Why is the crossover analysis more about labour cost than software cost?
A: Because the managed software fee (MAR) and the self-hosted software fee (zero licence for OSS) are easy to state, but the decisive variable is the engineer-labour to operate and maintain the self-hosted platform. The crossover is essentially "MAR bill versus salary fraction." Whoever ignores the labour line will systematically under-cost the build option.

Q: How would you sanity-check a Fivetran MAR estimate before putting it in the note?
A: Cross-check your assumed monthly change volume against the source's real activity — for a slowly-changing reference table the MAR should be small, for a high-churn transaction table it should be large — and confirm you excluded the free initial backfill. Then confirm you applied the correct plan tier's spend rate. If the number feels surprisingly high or low, your row-volume assumption is usually the error.

Q: Why might two architects produce very different Fivetran estimates for the same source?
A: Because the estimate hinges on assumed monthly change volume and the assumed plan tier/spend rate, both of which are judgement calls. One architect might assume heavy daily churn while another assumes a mostly-static table, producing wildly different MAR. This is why documenting the row-volume and tier assumptions is essential to make the estimate defensible.

Q: What infrastructure cost must you add to self-hosted Airbyte beyond engineer labour?
A: The compute, storage, and networking to run the Kubernetes cluster (or kind environment) plus any object storage destination such as the project's MinIO, and the cost of monitoring and backups. These are smaller than the labour line but non-zero and must appear in the build-side total. Omitting them understates the build option just as omitting maintenance does.

Q: Why is data-sovereignty often more decisive than cost for an EU fund estate?
A: Because a sovereignty breach is a regulatory and legal failure that no cost saving can justify, whereas cost is a continuous trade-off you can optimise. For an EU fund, residency and third-party-control rules can flatly forbid certain data from reaching a managed cloud, ending the conversation before economics matter. The architect treats sovereignty as a gate and cost as a dial.

Q: If a SaaS source contains *no* personal or regulated data, how does that simplify the decision?
A: It removes the sovereignty gate, so the source can be decided purely on the cost crossover: buy if its MAR bill is below the maintenance labour, build if above. Non-sensitive, long-tail SaaS with a maintained connector and low MAR is the cleanest "buy." Confirming the data is non-regulated is what lets you optimise on money alone.

Q: Why is "data never leaves the secure perimeter" the key phrase to look for in a sovereignty requirement?
A: Because it tells you whether even Fivetran's Hybrid Deployment (data processed locally, control plane in the cloud) could satisfy the rule, or whether the requirement also forbids any external control-plane dependency. If the rule is strictly about data location, Hybrid or EU-residency may pass; if it forbids any third-party touch, only fully self-hosted Airbyte qualifies. Parsing that phrase precisely changes the available options.

Q: How does the "buy" option's MAR cost behave if a source suddenly starts changing far more rows?
A: The MAR rises with the increased change volume, so the bill goes up roughly in proportion (modulated by volume discounts). A source that becomes much churnier — say a daily-revalued holdings table — can produce a larger-than-budgeted bill. This volatility is exactly the "surprise MAR bill" risk that pushes high-churn sources toward predictable self-hosting.

Q: Why does the "build" option's cost stay relatively flat as volume grows?
A: Because the dominant cost is fixed engineer-maintenance labour plus modest infrastructure, neither of which scales tightly with row volume once the connector is running. Whether the connector moves a thousand or a million rows, the engineer's babysitting effort is similar. This flatness is precisely why build wins above the crossover, where Fivetran's volume-scaled bill overtakes it.

Q: In one sentence, when does managed ELT win?
A: Managed ELT wins for long-tail SaaS sources with low-to-moderate change volume and vendor-maintained connectors, where the small MAR bill is cheaper than the engineer-time to operate a connector yourself. The vendor absorbs the maintenance you would otherwise pay a salary for. Below the crossover, buy.

Q: In one sentence, when does managed ELT lose?
A: Managed ELT loses for core banking, mainframe, and data-sovereignty-constrained sources — where the data cannot leave the perimeter or no maintained connector exists — and for high-volume churny sources where the MAR bill outruns a fixed self-hosted maintenance cost. Above the crossover, or behind a sovereignty gate, build.

Q: Why are mainframe feeds specifically poor candidates for managed ELT?
A: Mainframe systems use bespoke, legacy protocols and data formats that fall outside the long-tail-SaaS connector library managed vendors specialise in, so the vendor's maintained-connector advantage rarely applies. They are also typically core, sensitive systems unlikely to be allowed to ship data externally. Both the technical and the sovereignty arguments push mainframe feeds in-house.

Q: How does a half-page note differ from a full architecture proposal in this exercise?
A: A half-page note is a tight decision artefact: estate summary, MAR estimate, engineer-hour estimate, crossover point, and sovereignty flags — enough for a board to make the build-vs-buy call. It deliberately omits implementation detail, vendor negotiation, and migration planning. The discipline is forcing the decision into a page so the crossover and the sovereignty veto are unmistakable.

Q: What is the first "Done when" criterion for this lesson?
A: That you can state the crossover point where Fivetran's cost beats an engineer maintaining Airbyte. Concretely, you must be able to identify the MAR/volume level at which the managed bill equals the fully-loaded self-hosting labour, and explain which side of it a given source falls on. It proves you can turn the build-vs-buy idea into an actual decision threshold.

Q: What is the second "Done when" criterion for this lesson?
A: That you can name which sources are off-limits to managed ELT for sovereignty reasons. You must identify, from an estate, the systems whose data legally or contractually cannot leave the perimeter — core banking, TA register, regulated investor data — and mark them build-only regardless of cost. It proves you treat sovereignty as a gate, not a tunable cost.

Q: Why is it dangerous to extrapolate a Fivetran pilot's cost linearly to production?
A: Because the spend rate declines with volume and the initial backfill is free, so a small pilot pays a higher marginal rate and skips the free historical load that production already absorbed. Linear extrapolation therefore overstates the per-row cost while ignoring tier discounts. You must model the production volume band's rate, not multiply the pilot bill.

Q: How do schema changes at the source affect the two options differently?
A: Under managed ELT the vendor's connector is built to absorb and propagate schema changes, so a new or altered column is largely handled for you. Under self-hosting, the same schema change can break your connector or silently drop data until an engineer intervenes. Schema-drift handling is a concrete slice of the maintenance burden that managed ELT removes and self-hosting adds.

Q: Why might a regulated client contractually forbid a managed ELT vendor even where law would allow it?
A: Because fund prospectuses, client mandates, and service agreements can impose confidentiality and processing restrictions stricter than statute — for example forbidding any third-party processing of investor holdings. Contracts are a source of hard constraints just like regulation. The architect must read the mandate, not only the law, before classifying a source as eligible for managed ELT.

Q: How does DORA's "exit planning" obligation specifically complicate using Fivetran for critical feeds?
A: DORA requires financial entities to maintain documented exit strategies for critical ICT third-party providers, so depending on Fivetran for a critical feed obliges you to design and test how you would leave it. That adds governance work and a real switching-cost consideration to the "buy" side. For a marginal source, that overhead can tip the decision back toward self-hosting.

Q: Why is the transfer-agency feed a likely "sovereignty veto" rather than a "cost decision"?
A: Because the TA register holds investor identities and holdings — personal and financial data under GDPR, residency, and confidentiality rules — so it is usually barred from a third-party managed cloud outright. That makes it a gate (build-only) rather than something you weigh on MAR cost. You remove it from the managed pool before any crossover math.

Q: How does choosing MinIO/object storage as the destination interact with build-vs-buy?
A: If your destination is your own MinIO/object store inside the perimeter, you keep the landing zone sovereign, which supports the "build" side and any hybrid arrangement; a fully-managed vendor writing to an external cloud would not. The destination's location is part of the sovereignty assessment, not just the source's. Keeping both source and destination inside the perimeter is what makes self-hosting compliant by construction.

Q: Why does the lesson frame this as something "as architect you must price," not just choose?
A: Because the decision is fundamentally an economic and risk trade-off, and an architect's value is producing a defensible number — the crossover — plus the hard-constraint flags, not an opinion. "Pricing it" means quantifying both the MAR bill and the maintenance labour so the board can see exactly why each source is build or buy. An unpriced preference is not an architecture decision.

Q: What concrete number must your note ultimately deliver?
A: The crossover point — the MAR/volume (or equivalently the cost) level at which Fivetran's managed bill equals the fully-loaded cost of an engineer maintaining Airbyte for the same scope. Above it, build; below it, buy. Everything else in the note (MAR estimate, engineer-hours, sovereignty flags) exists to compute and contextualise that single threshold.

Q: How would you justify recommending Fivetran for the four SaaS sources to a sceptical CFO?
A: Show that their combined MAR is small, so the monthly bill is far below the cost of the engineer-time needed to build and maintain four self-hosted connectors against churning SaaS APIs — i.e. they sit well below the crossover. Add that the vendor absorbs schema drift and connector breakage, reducing operational risk. The argument is "you save salary and risk for a small predictable fee."

Q: How would you justify keeping the six internal sources self-hosted to the same CFO?
A: Argue that several carry sovereignty/regulatory constraints that legally forbid managed ELT (a gate, not a cost choice), and that the remainder are high-value, bespoke systems whose volume or sensitivity puts them above the crossover where fixed self-hosting cost beats a volatile MAR bill. Emphasise predictability and compliance. The message is "these cannot or should not leave the perimeter, and self-hosting is also the cheaper or safer option."

Q: Why is "data sovereignty is a hard constraint" stated so forcefully in the lesson's Why?
A: Because in a regulated EU fund estate sovereignty can single-handedly veto the otherwise-cheaper managed option, so it must dominate the analysis rather than be traded away. Treating it as just another cost line is how compliance failures happen. The lesson wants you to internalise that some sources are off-limits to managed ELT no matter what the MAR math says.

Q: What happens to the crossover if you can self-host Airbyte cheaply (e.g. it co-tenants existing Kubernetes)?
A: The build side's fixed cost drops because you reuse existing infrastructure and operational capacity, lowering the crossover so self-hosting wins at lower volumes. Conversely, if standing up and operating Kubernetes is greenfield and expensive, the crossover rises and managed ELT stays attractive longer. The marginal cost of self-hosting, not just the headline, moves the threshold.

Q: Why is "MAR scales with change volume" the single most important pricing fact to internalise?
A: Because it tells you the managed bill tracks how much your data *changes* per month, not how big it is or how often you sync — so churny sources are expensive and static-but-large sources are cheap on Fivetran. This directly drives which sources are economical to buy. Misreading it (thinking you pay per row or per sync) produces wrong estimates and wrong verdicts.

Q: A reference table with a million rows never changes month to month. What is its approximate Fivetran MAR?
A: Approximately zero billable MAR after the free initial load, because MAR counts only rows that are inserted, updated, or deleted in the month, and an unchanged table has no active rows. Its sheer size does not matter once it is loaded. This is a clean "buy" candidate on cost — huge but cheap — assuming no sovereignty barrier.

Q: A small table of 10,000 rows where every row updates daily — what is its monthly MAR?
A: About 10,000 MAR for the month, because each distinct row is counted once regardless of being updated daily, so the per-row-per-month rule caps it at the number of distinct rows. Despite heavy daily churn, the bill is modest because the row count is small. This shows that change frequency does not inflate MAR; only the count of distinct changed rows does.

Q: Why can a "small" table still produce more MAR than a "large" one?
A: Because MAR depends on how many distinct rows change in a month, not table size — a small but fully-churning table can have every row active while a large but static table has none. A 10,000-row table where all rows change beats a 10-million-row table where none change. Size is a red herring; monthly change is the driver.

Q: How does the free initial-sync window affect onboarding a high-volume historical source?
A: The first full historical load and a brief new-connection window are free of MAR charges, so onboarding even a massive history does not generate a one-time bill spike. You are billed only on the ongoing monthly change after onboarding. This makes the *steady-state* change volume, not the backfill, the figure that matters for the crossover.

Q: Why should you separate "steady-state MAR" from "onboarding load" when estimating cost?
A: Because onboarding/backfill is free, the steady-state monthly change volume is what actually drives the recurring bill, and conflating the two over- or under-states cost. A source with a huge history but little monthly change is cheap in steady state; one with modest history but heavy churn is expensive. The estimate should be built on steady-state MAR.

Q: How does the build-vs-buy decision change if regulators later tighten residency rules?
A: Sources previously eligible for managed ELT may move behind the sovereignty gate and become build-only, shrinking the managed pool regardless of their attractive MAR economics. The crossover math then applies to fewer sources. This is why the note flags sovereignty explicitly and why the decision must be revisited when the regulatory perimeter moves.

Q: Why is it risky to let "the connector exists" alone decide a source is a "buy"?
A: Because connector availability removes only the maintenance-cost argument; you must still pass the sovereignty gate and check that the MAR bill is below the maintenance crossover. A maintained connector for a sovereignty-blocked source is irrelevant. Always run the gate and the cost check, not just the connector-availability check.

Q: What does it mean to "price it against self-hosted connectors," in the architect's job framing?
A: It means producing a quantified comparison — Fivetran's MAR bill versus the fully-loaded cost of self-hosting the same sources on Airbyte — and identifying the crossover where they meet. The output is a defensible recommendation per source. "Pricing against" is the active verb: you are not asked to like one tool but to compute when each is cheaper.

Q: How does Fivetran's automatic adaptation to source schema changes factor into the buy case?
A: Fivetran's connectors automatically adapt to changes in source APIs and schemas, which removes a major operational burden you would otherwise carry when self-hosting. That reliability is part of what the MAR fee buys, and it reduces the risk of silent data loss from drift. It strengthens the buy case for any eligible long-tail SaaS source.

Q: Why is the predictability of self-hosted cost attractive to a finance function even when it is higher?
A: Because a fixed, salary-driven self-hosting cost is budgetable and immune to surprise spikes, whereas a MAR bill can jump with change volume. A CFO may prefer a known higher number to a volatile lower one, especially for core sources. Predictability is a legitimate factor in build-vs-buy alongside the raw crossover.

Q: What is the danger of running self-hosted Airbyte *below* the crossover purely "to avoid vendor lock-in"?
A: You pay perpetual engineer-maintenance for sources whose tiny MAR bill Fivetran would have absorbed cheaply, spending salary to avoid a lock-in risk that may be modest for long-tail SaaS. Avoiding lock-in is valid for critical or sovereign sources but is a poor reason to self-host low-value SaaS. The note should weigh lock-in against the real maintenance cost, not assume self-hosting is free.

Q: Why is the loaded (fully-burdened) engineer cost, not the base salary, the right figure for the build side?
A: Because the true cost of an engineer includes employer taxes, benefits, tooling, management overhead, and on-call burden on top of base salary, often inflating it well above the headline number. Using only base salary understates the build side and biases the crossover toward self-hosting. A defensible note prices maintenance labour at the loaded rate so the comparison against Fivetran's MAR bill is honest.

Q: How would you summarise the whole build-vs-buy decision in a single decision rule?
A: For each source, first apply the sovereignty/regulatory gate — if the data cannot leave the perimeter, build it; otherwise compute the crossover and buy when its monthly MAR bill is below the fully-loaded cost of self-hosting it, and build when it is above. Sovereignty is a hard gate, cost is the dial. That two-step rule produces the per-source verdicts the note must deliver.

Q: As of March 2025 Fivetran changed how MAR is counted — what changed and why does it matter for the build-vs-buy note?
A: Fivetran moved to counting MAR per connection rather than aggregating it across the whole account, so each source's connection now has its own independent MAR total and its own volume-discount band. This matters because you can no longer assume consolidating connections into one account buys you a cheaper marginal rate across the board; each connection prices on its own volume. The note's per-source estimate must therefore apply the discount curve connection-by-connection, not to one combined account total.

Q: What is the `$5` base charge Fivetran added in January 2026 and which connections does it hit?
A: Fivetran applies a `$5` base charge to every connection whose monthly usage falls between 1 MAR and 1M MAR, effectively a per-connection minimum that sits on top of the per-MAR cost. It means even a near-idle connection moving a handful of rows costs at least `$5` a month, so a fund estate with many tiny long-tail SaaS connectors pays a floor regardless of how little each changes. The note should multiply that floor by the connector count, not just sum the MAR-scaled cost.

Q: Four near-idle SaaS connectors each move about 50 changed rows a month on a paid plan. Roughly what do they cost, and what dominates?
A: The per-MAR cost is trivial for 200 total rows, so the bill is dominated by the `$5` per-connection base charge — about `$20` a month across the four connections rather than the negligible MAR-scaled amount. The lesson here is that for very low-volume sources the connection floor, not the change volume, drives cost. It still sits far below an engineer's salary, so the verdict stays "buy."

Q: A teammate proposes splitting one busy source into three Fivetran connections to "spread the load." What is the billing consequence?
A: Each connection counts MAR independently and each incurs its own `$5` base charge, so splitting a source into three connections adds two extra base charges and can move each piece into a higher (less-discounted) marginal rate band than the consolidated source enjoyed. Splitting for billing reasons usually costs more, not less, under the per-connection model. Split only when there is a real operational reason (different schemas, schedules, or destinations), not to game pricing.

Q: How long is Fivetran's free trial window for a new connection, and how does it differ from the free initial backfill?
A: A new connection gets a `14`-day free trial during which its usage is not billed, which is a separate concept from the always-free initial historical load. The trial is a time-boxed onboarding grace period; the free backfill is a permanent rule that the first full sync of history never counts as MAR. Both reduce onboarding cost, but only the backfill exemption persists beyond the first two weeks.

Q: When a source table has no primary key, how does Fivetran still count MAR for it?
A: Fivetran generates a synthetic hashed key to uniquely identify each row so it can still track distinct rows for MAR counting. The consequence is that a poorly-keyed source can over-count: if the hash changes when non-key columns change, rows that are logically "the same" may be counted as new actives. For a fund feed, defining a real primary key (an ISIN, an account number) keeps MAR honest and avoids inflated bills.

Q: A reference table has no natural primary key and your MAR is surprisingly high. First thing to check?
A: Check whether Fivetran is hashing the whole row into a synthetic key, so that any change to any column makes the row look brand-new and inflates MAR. The fix is to declare a stable primary key on the genuinely identifying column(s) so updates to other fields count the existing row, not a new one. Mis-keyed sources are the classic cause of a MAR estimate that is far higher than the table's real churn would suggest.

Q: Your monthly MAR for a slowly-changing dimension table spiked with no business change. What source-side cause should you suspect first?
A: Suspect a "touch every row" operation upstream — a bulk `UPDATE` with no `WHERE`, a full re-key, a migration that rewrote `updated_at` on all rows, or a primary-key change — which makes every row look active even though the data is unchanged. Because MAR counts any inserted/updated/deleted row, a no-op mass update still bills as full-table churn. Audit the source's batch jobs before assuming Fivetran is mis-counting.

Q: Distinguish Fivetran "connection" MAR from "activation" MAR, since they have different free allowances.
A: Connection MAR measures rows synced inbound from sources into your destination (the classic ingestion direction), while activation MAR measures rows pushed outbound from the warehouse to operational tools (reverse ETL). They are billed and free-tiered separately — the Free plan allows up to `500,000` connection MAR but only `3,500` activation MAR. A build-vs-buy note covering only ingestion should price connection MAR and explicitly state it is excluding activation.

Q: How does Fivetran's per-connection volume discount interact with the build-vs-buy crossover for one high-volume source?
A: For a single high-volume source the discount applies only within that connection's own MAR, so it crosses into cheaper marginal bands as its volume grows, softening — but not eliminating — the cost rise. The crossover still favours self-hosting eventually because even discounted MAR keeps climbing with change volume while the fixed maintenance salary does not. You must apply the discount curve to that connection's volume specifically, not borrow a rate from a different-sized connection.

Q: On a native Ubuntu laptop, what is the exact command to stand up a local Airbyte for the "build" cost evaluation?
A: Run `abctl local install`, which downloads the CLI's dependencies, creates a kind Kubernetes cluster in Docker, and deploys Airbyte via Helm. You first install the CLI (for example with the official install script or by placing the `abctl` binary on your `PATH`) and ensure Docker is running. The single `abctl local install` command is what replaces the deprecated `docker compose up` quickstart.

Q: Your Ubuntu laptop has limited RAM and `abctl local install` keeps failing on resources. What flag helps and what is its trade-off?
A: Use `abctl local install --low-resource-mode`, which sets job resource requests (CPU and memory) to zero so Kubernetes schedules jobs without minimum guarantees, letting Airbyte run on a constrained machine. The trade-off is that the Connector Builder is unavailable in this mode and performance on data-heavy syncs degrades, so it is fine for a cost-evaluation preview but not for production. This directly informs how cheaply you can prototype the "build" side.

Q: After `abctl local install` completes, how do you retrieve the login credentials for the Airbyte UI?
A: Run `abctl local credentials`, which prints the auto-generated email, password, and the client credentials for the local instance. You need this because Airbyte now requires authentication even on a local kind deployment, so you cannot reach the UI without retrieving the generated password. It is a small but real operational step that belongs in any honest self-hosting runbook.

Q: Which port does `abctl local install` expose the Airbyte UI on by default, and how do you change it?
A: It exposes the UI on port `8000` by default, reachable at `http://localhost:8000`, and you override it with the `--port` flag, for example `abctl local install --port 8006`. Knowing the default matters when the laptop already runs something on `8000` and the install appears to "succeed" but the UI is unreachable. A port clash is a common first-run snag on a developer machine.

Q: You want to expose your local Airbyte on a hostname rather than localhost. Which `abctl` flag do you use?
A: Use the `--host` flag on `abctl local install` to set the fully-qualified domain name the instance should accept, for example `abctl local install --host airbyte.internal.lan`. By default `--host` is empty and the instance answers on localhost only; setting it is needed when you reach the UI through a reverse proxy or another machine. It is part of the operational surface area you are pricing on the build side.

Q: How do you cleanly remove a local Airbyte while keeping its data, versus wiping everything?
A: `abctl local uninstall` removes the Airbyte software but preserves the persisted data so you can reinstall later, whereas `abctl local uninstall --persisted` additionally deletes all data and cannot be undone. The distinction matters during a cost evaluation when you tear down and rebuild repeatedly: a plain uninstall keeps your test connectors, the `--persisted` form gives you a truly clean slate. Reaching for `--persisted` by accident destroys your configured sources.

Q: How do you check the state of a running local Airbyte and the installed CLI version with `abctl`?
A: `abctl local status` reports whether the local Airbyte deployment is up and reachable, and `abctl version` prints the CLI version. You use `status` to confirm the kind cluster and Helm release are healthy before debugging a sync, and `version` to confirm you are on a current CLI when following docs. Both are basic operational commands that a self-hosting maintainer runs routinely.

Q: Why does Airbyte now requiring authentication even on a local install raise the self-hosting maintenance estimate slightly?
A: Because mandatory auth means you must retrieve and manage credentials (`abctl local credentials`), handle password rotation, and integrate with SSO or a secrets store for any shared deployment, rather than reaching an open UI. Each of those is a small recurring operational task that did not exist in the old open-by-default Compose quickstart. It is one more line in the "engineer babysitting" total that the build-vs-buy note must reflect.

Q: A source is GDPR-relevant but not residency-blocked. Does that put it behind the sovereignty gate or in the cost decision?
A: It still passes through a hard-constraint check, but the question becomes whether a managed vendor can be a compliant GDPR processor (data-processing agreement, EU residency, adequate safeguards) rather than an absolute "never leaves the perimeter" veto. If the vendor offers an EU-resident destination and a signed DPA, the source can be eligible and decided on cost; if the mandate forbids any external processor, it is gated. The architect must read whether the rule is "EU-resident processor is acceptable" or "no third party at all."

Q: How does a Standard Contractual Clauses or adequacy requirement change whether a US-headquartered vendor like Fivetran can touch EU fund data?
A: Even with an EU-region destination, a US-headquartered provider can be subject to extraterritorial access law, so compliance may require Standard Contractual Clauses, supplementary measures, or reliance on an adequacy decision before the data is allowed to flow. If those cannot be satisfied for a given source, it stays build-only regardless of the Frankfurt-region option. This is why "data stored in the EU" is necessary but sometimes not sufficient for a sovereignty-sensitive fund feed.

Q: Under DORA, Fivetran handling a critical feed may make it a "critical ICT third-party provider." What concrete obligations follow?
A: You must include the arrangement in your register of information, perform third-party ICT risk assessment, ensure the contract contains DORA-mandated clauses (audit rights, sub-outsourcing limits, incident reporting), and maintain a tested exit strategy. The provider may itself fall under direct EU oversight if designated critical at the Union level. These obligations add governance cost to the "buy" side that the crossover note should acknowledge for any core feed.

Q: What specific contractual clause does DORA require that directly affects a managed-ELT vendor choice?
A: DORA requires that contracts with ICT third-party providers grant the financial entity (and regulators) audit and access rights, define clear service levels and incident-reporting obligations, and set out termination and exit conditions. A managed-ELT vendor that will not contractually grant audit rights or a workable exit cannot lawfully carry a critical feed. The architect should treat "does the vendor's contract meet DORA clause requirements?" as a gating question for core sources, not a negotiable nicety.

Q: T+1 settlement is being adopted in the EU. How could a move to T+1 affect the MAR profile of a settlement or positions feed?
A: Compressing settlement to one business day forces more frequent, intraday updates to positions, trades, and cash records, so the number of distinct rows that change per month rises — pushing that connection's MAR (and its discount band) higher. A feed that was comfortably "buy" under T+2 batch cycles could approach the crossover as churn grows under T+1. The note should treat settlement-cycle changes as a driver that can move a source's verdict over time.

Q: How would you future-proof a build-vs-buy note against the EU's planned move to T+1?
A: Model the affected feeds' MAR under the higher intraday change volume T+1 implies, recompute their crossover, and flag any source that flips from "buy" to "build" so the decision is revisited before the regime change. Because MAR scales with monthly change and T+1 increases change frequency for settlement-adjacent data, the safest sources to leave on managed ELT are those whose change volume is insensitive to settlement cadence. Document the assumption so the note can be re-run when the date is fixed.

Q: The EU AI Act is in force. Does it bear on the build-vs-buy decision for an ingestion pipeline, and how?
A: Plain ELT moving rows is not itself an AI system, so the AI Act does not regulate the ingestion mechanics, but it governs any downstream high-risk AI that consumes the ingested data, imposing data-governance, logging, and traceability obligations. That can argue for keeping the authoritative, well-governed datasets inside a controlled perimeter where lineage and quality are provable. The AI Act influences which sources you are comfortable handing to a third party, not the MAR math directly.

Q: How does provable data lineage interact with the build-vs-buy choice for data feeding a regulated model?
A: If downstream AI is high-risk under the AI Act, you must demonstrate the provenance and quality of its training and input data, which is easier when you control the pipeline end to end and can audit every transformation. A managed vendor can still provide lineage metadata, but you depend on its logging and retention rather than your own. For AI-feeding datasets the governance argument can nudge a marginal source toward self-hosting even when cost favours buying.

Q: A self-hosted Airbyte sync silently stopped moving data after a source schema change. What is the first thing to check?
A: Check the connection's schema/catalog status and logs for a discovered-but-not-propagated new or altered column, since Airbyte may have detected the change and paused or dropped the affected stream pending your approval of the new schema. Self-hosting puts that schema-drift decision on you, so the "fix" is to refresh the source schema and re-enable the stream. This is exactly the maintenance toil that managed ELT absorbs automatically.

Q: Managed ELT "handles schema drift automatically." Name a case where that automatic handling is itself a risk you must monitor.
A: When a source renames or drops a column, automatic propagation can silently remove or remap a column your downstream models depend on, breaking dbt transformations without a hard failure at ingestion. The vendor "handled" the drift correctly at the connector layer but the change still ripples downstream. So even on the buy side you must monitor schema-change notifications, not assume "automatic" means "no action needed."

Q: Two architects estimate the same SaaS source's MAR and disagree by 10x. Where is the disagreement almost certainly hiding?
A: Almost certainly in the assumed monthly change volume — one assumed the table mostly churns (most rows change each month) while the other assumed it is largely static — since the per-connection rate and free backfill are objective. A secondary source is whether one of them mistakenly counted the historical row count instead of monthly change. Reconciling requires agreeing on a defensible "rows changed per month" figure, ideally from the source's actual `updated_at` distribution.

Q: How would you empirically measure a Postgres source table's monthly change volume to ground a MAR estimate?
A: Query the table's change tracking — for example `SELECT count(*) FROM tbl WHERE updated_at >= now() - interval '30 days'` for updates plus inserts, and reconcile against deletes from an audit log or soft-delete flag. That count of distinct rows changed in the last month is the steady-state MAR proxy, far better than guessing from table size. Document the query and window so the estimate is reproducible and auditable.

Q: Why is the historical row count of a source the wrong input to a Fivetran cost estimate?
A: Because the initial historical load is free and the bill is driven by ongoing monthly change, so a billion-row history with little monthly churn is cheap while a small table that fully churns is expensive. Plugging the total row count into a cost formula systematically over-states the bill for static-but-large tables. The correct input is steady-state distinct rows changed per month.

Q: How would you present the crossover as a break-even chart rather than a single number to a board?
A: Plot two cost curves against monthly MAR: a roughly flat line for self-hosting (fixed loaded engineer cost plus modest infrastructure) and a rising, discount-tapered line for Fivetran (`$5` base plus per-MAR cost), and mark where they intersect as the crossover. Sources are then points on the x-axis whose vertical comparison shows the cheaper option at a glance. The chart makes "below the crossover buy, above it build" visually unarguable for non-technical approvers.

Q: A source sits almost exactly on the crossover. What non-cost tie-breakers should decide it?
A: Operational predictability (managed removes breakage risk), governance burden (DORA exit-planning and audit weigh against buy for critical feeds), schema-change frequency (favours buy), connector availability and stability, and data sensitivity. When the dollar difference is within estimation noise, these qualitative factors break the tie. A defensible note states the tie-breaker explicitly rather than pretending the cost estimate is precise to the source.

Q: Why might you self-host a source whose MAR cost is below the crossover, and is that defensible?
A: You might if it is a critical feed where DORA exit-planning, audit rights, or vendor-concentration risk make the managed dependency costly in governance terms even though the cash bill is small. That is defensible when the avoided risk is real and documented; it is not defensible if "avoiding lock-in" is invoked reflexively for low-value, easily-re-pointed SaaS. The note must justify any below-crossover build on a named risk, not a vague preference.

Q: How does vendor concentration risk (many feeds on one provider) factor into a regulated build-vs-buy note?
A: Routing many critical feeds through a single managed vendor concentrates operational risk and, under DORA, can trigger heightened third-party-concentration scrutiny and a harder exit problem if that provider fails. Even when each individual source passes the cost crossover, the aggregate dependency is a risk the note should flag. Mitigations include keeping some feeds self-hosted or splitting critical feeds across providers to preserve a viable exit.

Q: For the Frankfurt-region Fivetran destination to satisfy sovereignty, what must also be true about the control plane?
A: The data residing in Frankfurt addresses data location, but you must also confirm the control plane and any metadata it receives do not breach the rule, since the orchestration and configuration still run in Fivetran's SaaS. If the requirement is purely "data stored in the EU," Frankfurt residency may suffice; if it forbids any foreign-controlled control plane touching the system, even Frankfurt residency or Hybrid Deployment can fail. Parse data-location versus control-dependency before declaring the source eligible.

Q: In Fivetran Hybrid Deployment, what exactly crosses the perimeter and what stays inside?
A: The actual data processing runs on a Fivetran agent inside your network so the data itself stays local, while the control plane in Fivetran's cloud sends configuration and receives orchestration metadata and operational telemetry. So data stays in; control signals and metadata go out. A compliance team must decide whether that metadata-and-control dependency is acceptable for the source in question.

Q: A compliance officer says "no third-party control plane may orchestrate this system." Which options survive that rule?
A: Only fully self-hosted Airbyte (or equivalent) survives, because both standard Fivetran and its Hybrid Deployment keep an external control plane orchestrating the pipeline. EU-residency and Hybrid satisfy data-location rules but not control-dependency rules. That phrasing collapses the option set to build, removing the source from the managed pool before any cost comparison.

Q: How would you write the per-source verdict line for the transfer-agency register in the note?
A: Something like "TA register — BUILD (sovereignty gate): investor identities and holdings are GDPR personal data under EU residency and confidentiality mandates; not eligible for managed ELT regardless of MAR cost." It states the verdict, the reason class (gate vs cost), and the one-line justification a board can sign. The point is that it is removed before cost math, not weighed against a MAR number.

Q: How would you write the per-source verdict line for a marketing-analytics SaaS source?
A: Something like "Marketing SaaS — BUY (cost): non-regulated data, maintained Fivetran connector, estimated ~X MAR/month well below the self-hosting maintenance crossover; `$5` base plus low per-MAR cost." It records that the sovereignty gate is passed, names the connector, gives the MAR estimate, and ties the verdict to the crossover. That is the clean "buy" template for long-tail SaaS.

Q: NAV is published daily to distributors. Which part of that flow is a candidate for managed ELT and which is not?
A: The internal computation of NAV in fund accounting is regulated-core and stays self-hosted, while the downstream distribution of already-published, non-sensitive NAV figures to external reporting or distributor SaaS can be a managed-ELT candidate. The architect splits the regulated core (build) from the publishable periphery (potentially buy). The boundary is whether the data is still confidential internal valuation or already-public published output.

Q: An EMT file is generated nightly and sent to distributors. Why is it not a Fivetran ingestion target?
A: The EMT (European MiFID Template) is an output your internal systems produce and push to distributors, not a long-tail SaaS API you ingest from, so the managed-vendor advantage of maintained inbound connectors does not apply. It is a regulated-core deliverable, reinforcing that the sensitive data lives in self-hosted systems. Managed ELT is about pulling from sources, and EMT generation is a downstream production step.

Q: How does the EET (European ESG Template) fit the same build-vs-buy framing as EMT and EPT?
A: The EET, like the EMT and EPT, is a standardised regulatory disclosure file produced from internal fund data and exchanged with distributors, so it is an output of the regulated core rather than an ingestion source for managed ELT. Its existence reinforces that ESG, cost, and risk disclosure data all originate in self-hosted systems that should not be handed to a third-party connector. The template is a downstream artefact, not a Fivetran "source."

Q: A SWIFT message feed (e.g. settlement confirmations) is proposed for managed ELT. What two arguments push it in-house?
A: First, SWIFT financial messaging is core, highly sensitive transaction data unlikely to be permitted to leave the perimeter (sovereignty/confidentiality gate); second, it uses specialised messaging formats outside the long-tail-SaaS connector library, so the vendor's maintained-connector advantage does not apply. Both the regulatory and the technical arguments favour self-hosting. It is a textbook "build" source like core banking and mainframe feeds.

Q: How do ISIN and LEI serve as join keys without their host systems being managed-ELT candidates?
A: ISIN (security identifier) and LEI (legal-entity identifier) are stable reference keys that flow through fund accounting, the instrument master, and the TA register, so they are invaluable for joining datasets downstream. But the authoritative systems that mint and govern them are part of the regulated core and stay self-hosted. The keys travel; their host systems remain build-side, so you can join on ISIN/LEI in the warehouse without ingesting their masters via a managed vendor.

Q: Re-pricing the build side: your firm already runs a shared Kubernetes platform. How does that move the crossover?
A: Reusing existing Kubernetes capacity means Airbyte's marginal infrastructure and operational cost is low (no greenfield cluster to stand up and own), so the build side's fixed cost drops and the crossover shifts to lower MAR — self-hosting wins sooner. The maintenance line still includes connector upkeep, but the platform overhead is largely amortised. Always price self-hosting at its true marginal cost given existing capacity, not as if every estate starts from zero.

Q: Conversely, what makes the build side expensive enough to keep even high-volume sources on managed ELT longer?
A: A greenfield Kubernetes deployment that the team must learn, secure, monitor, and run on-call adds substantial fixed cost and risk, raising the crossover so managed ELT stays cheaper up to higher MAR. If no one on the team operates Kubernetes today, the honest build estimate includes hiring or training, which can dominate. The decisive variable is the firm's existing operational capability, not the software licence.

Q: Summarise the corrected MAR cost formula for one connection under the 2025/2026 model.
A: For a paid connection it is approximately `$5` base charge (when monthly usage is between 1 and 1M MAR) plus the per-connection MAR multiplied by that connection's volume-discounted spend rate, with the initial historical backfill and the 14-day trial excluded. Because counting is now per connection, the discount band is set by that one connection's volume, not the account total. The note should compute this per source and then sum across connections to get the buy-side total.

Q: `abctl local install` fails complaining it cannot reach the Docker daemon on your Ubuntu laptop. First thing to check?
A: Confirm Docker is actually running and your user can talk to the daemon — `docker ps` should succeed without `sudo`, otherwise add your user to the `docker` group and re-login — because `abctl` builds its kind cluster inside Docker and cannot proceed without daemon access. A stopped Docker service or a permissions error on `/var/run/docker.sock` is the most common first-run failure on a fresh Ubuntu machine. Fixing Docker access, not Airbyte config, is the resolution.

Q: How does dbt fit into the "Fivetran + dbt" pattern, and does it change the build-vs-buy crossover for ingestion?
A: dbt performs the in-warehouse transformation (the T) after Fivetran lands raw data, so it sits downstream of ingestion and is largely orthogonal to the build-vs-buy choice about who runs the connectors. It does not change the ingestion crossover, which is purely Fivetran MAR versus self-hosted Airbyte maintenance; dbt runs on your warehouse either way. The note should price ingestion separately and not let dbt's presence blur the connector build-vs-buy decision.
